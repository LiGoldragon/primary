# Synthesis — the reusable version-controlled Sema-state library: problem space, constraints, and prior art

This is the synthesis layer of a system-designer meta-report. Twelve sub-agent chapters ground the problem in our actual code (`sema`, `sema-engine`, `spirit`, `repository-ledger`, the version/upgrade/handover infra, the cluster), survey eighteen-plus external prior-art systems across six families, and map the Rust design space. This report ties them together. It deliberately **does not decide** the design — the psyche asked to "list everything entailed," and the kernel inversion remains the `sema`/`sema-engine` owners' call. It frames options and tradeoffs.

A grounding note up front, because it changes which chapters are authoritative: the supplied ground truth carries a stale picture of `sema`, and the grounding chapters corrected it against the live tree. I verified the load-bearing corrections directly. `sema/src/` is a single 581-line `lib.rs` (`wc -l`) — there is **no** `Slot(u64)` (it was removed and is now actively guarded against, `tests/no_legacy_surface.rs`), and the `log.rs:63-68` / `snapshot.rs:19` / `store.rs:714-758` citations in the brief are **not** `sema` files: the commit log and `SnapshotIdentifier` live in `sema-engine`, and `store.rs:714-758` is spirit's *retire* path — spirit's `state_digest` is actually at `store.rs:908-932` (doc-comment from 902). The "seed to generalise" lives one layer up from the kernel, in `sema-engine`. This relocation matters for section 2 (where the library lands) and is treated as authoritative throughout.

## 1. What we are going for

### The goal and the two Spirit records

A **reusable library** that gives any component the **option** to version-control its durable Sema state: server-backed, atomic, surviving schema churn. Two recorded psyche-intent records bound the effort:

- **Spirit `29pb`** (the durability shape): atomic, server-backed, *native* version control; state loss is unacceptable; native VC, **not** an opaque blob. This is the requirement that rules out the cheap path (Btrfs/ZFS snapshot of the `.sema` file, borg/restic of opaque bytes) as the *mechanism* — those remain legitimate defense-in-depth floors *under* the mechanism, but they are opaque-blob durability and so cannot satisfy 29pb on their own.
- **Spirit `j487`** (the reuse shape): the mechanism must be built **once** as a reusable library of generic types and traits, **not** reimplemented per component; every component opts in. The reusable-library direction is firm; the exact generic mechanism is undesigned. j487 is precisely the moment `skills/rust/storage-and-wire.md:179` anticipates — "create a dedicated Sema crate only after reuse is real and its architecture is explicitly named." The reuse is now real (spirit, repository-ledger, mind, criome all build on `sema-engine`); the architecture is the thing this report maps and does not name.

### The inherited design direction: "version the log, not the store"

From `reports/system-designer/91/7` and the system-operator audit 208, accepted as a **direction**, not a spec:

- Make the commit log **payload-bearing, hash-linked, and authoritative**; demote the redb tables to a rebuildable materialized **view**.
- A schema migration is a first-class typed log entry — `SchemaTransition(v_old_hash → v_new_hash, reducer)` plus a post-migration **checkpoint** — never a cell/byte diff. Each schema version is a different rkyv type set; the old bytes are simply not decodable under the new types.
- A **typed replay envelope** per entry: op kind | table id | record key | **schema-hash (the decoder selector)** | payload | tombstone | optional prior value | entry digest | prev digest + sequence.
- **Digests sit beside the monotonic markers, not replacing them.** A blake3 digest is not sortable, has no `next()`, answers no range query; `CommitSequence` stays the ordered cursor. Every production log surveyed (Kafka offsets, Datomic `t`, commitlog, raft-log, Yjs clocks) confirms this — the ordered cursor is always an integer; content hashes are an orthogonal identity/dedup concern layered beside it.
- **Remote durability levels**: local-committed / queued-for-mirror / server-committed, with a per-store ack policy and a named RPO if async. **The local view must NOT lag (read-after-write); only the remote mirror lags.**
- **Checkpoint protocol is first-class** (identity, schema-hash inventory, covered range, digests, retained-suffix policy, pruned-head behaviour) because compaction breaks naive suffix sync.
- **Backup = ship the log suffix** since the peer's last head, to the server.
- **Rejected as cargo-cult**: prolly trees (we are append-only Datomic-shaped, not random-mutable-keyspace Dolt-shaped) and git-as-substrate (double content-addressing). Git is at most a Stage-1 transport floor.

The single most important cross-cutting finding from the prior-art survey is that **this direction is not speculative — it is the converged state of the art, validated from multiple independent traditions**: Datomic (log-is-truth, indexes-as-derived-views), Fossil (`rebuild` regenerates the entire relational cache from authoritative content-addressed artifacts — our exact "demote tables to a rebuildable view," shipped in production), event sourcing + CQRS wholesale, and eg-walker (2024), which arrived independently at "authoritative append-only lightweight-op log + materialized view as a discardable cache." We are walking a well-trodden path. What is *novel and ours to design* is narrower than it looks (section 6).

## 2. Our constraints

### The central tension: perfect specificity vs. generic-over-record-families

This is THE design-space crux, and every prior-art chapter circles it. `skills/abstractions.md` demands perfect specificity — the type carries meaning; no generic-record, stringly-typed, or string-tag-dispatched store. j487 demands genericity over arbitrary record families across many components. These pull in opposite directions, and the survey shows the prior art **resolves this tension by choosing the side we are forbidden**:

- TerminusDB erases all type into one universal `(subject, predicate, object)` triple, pushing meaning into a dynamically-validated schema layer.
- Datomic erases values into one universal value union keyed by a runtime attribute id.
- Automerge and Yjs are *schemaless* — one generic JSON/shared-type document; fields are strings. Their reusability *is* their schemalessness.
- EventStoreDB, Kafka, Marten store events as opaque JSON/Avro/byte blobs the store never deserializes.
- The Rust event-sourcing crates (`cqrs-es`, `esrs`) achieve genericity via a stringly-typed `event_type: String` / `aggregate_type: String` selector — exactly the forbidden fallback, and proof that the easy genericity path *works mechanically* and is *precisely what we must not do*.
- okaywal (the canonical Rust append-only-log library) is generic over `&[u8]` opaque entries via a `LogManager` trait that never parses the payload — the literal line we must not cross.

The two existence proofs that genericity *can* stay typed:

1. **Irmin** (OCaml): functors over a `Contents` signature (a typed value with a typed serialization descriptor, *no* `Value = Bytes` escape hatch). Genericity by parameterizing over a small set of named roles, not by erasing content. The Rust analogue is a generic store bounded by a `RecordFamily` trait.
2. **openraft** (Rust): generic over the application's command type `D` and entry type via a `RaftTypeConfig` of *bounded associated types*; the library never inspects `D`; storage is three traits the data-bearing type implements. This is discipline-compatible genericity — no ZST namespace, no free functions, no stringly metadata. Its limitation (a single entry-type parameter cannot span schema versions within one log) is exactly what our schema-hash decoder selector adds on top.

Our own code already sits *half* on the wrong side: `sema-engine` is typed at the API (`register_table<RecordValue>`, `EngineStoredValue` blanket impl over any `Archive + Clone` type, `record.rs:80-106`) but stringly-typed at the persistence boundary — the `Catalog` stores only `TableRegistration { table_name: String }` (`catalog.rs:42-44`), and `CommitLogOperation { operation, table_name: String, key }` (`log.rs:63-68`) is metadata-only. **The library's job is to push a typed identity (the schema-hash selector) down to where today there is a bare string, without inventing a `GenericRecord` blob to carry it.** That is the whole problem in one sentence.

The likely shape the synthesis points at (without deciding): the generic *layer* owns the genuinely payload-agnostic concerns — ordering, hashing, log structure, checkpoint, sync; a per-component *typed boundary* owns decode-by-schema-hash into the real Rust record. "Erased at rest (bytes + schema-hash on disk), typed in motion (hash selects a closed-sum decoder)" via a **sealed** `RecordFamily` trait + a per-component generated **closed enum** of families is the candidate that most plausibly satisfies both the discipline and same-file atomicity. (This is highlighted as the most code-aligned candidate to **test**, not selected — see §7.) Whether resting-bytes honours the *spirit* of perfect specificity is a genuine open call, not a settled yes.

### "Name the architecture when reuse is real" (storage-and-wire.md:179)

j487 is the explicit trigger this rule was written for. But it collides with two facts the grounding chapters surface: (a) `ARCHITECTURE.md:87-90` says append-only identity/sequence allocation lands as a typed **`sema-engine`** primitive, *not* a raw `sema` surface; (b) the kernel is deliberately minimal and the "seed to generalise" already lives in `sema-engine`. So the library's **home layer is undecided and load-bearing**: a new crate linking `sema`, vs. a `sema-engine` primitive, vs. a layer beside it against the re-exported kernel handle (`Engine::storage_kernel() -> &sema::Sema` makes all three placement paths already open). The rule's counsel — few well-named crates, no broad umbrella Sema crate until the architecture is named — means the missing deliverable is the **architecture name itself**: the `RecordFamily` / `Reducer` / `SchemaHash` / `ReplayEnvelope` / `Checkpoint` / `MirrorAck` noun set.

### One-redb-file-per-component vs. shared-server boundary

The discipline is firm: one redb file per component; no shared sema daemon; each component owns its own engine handle. The cluster facts let the boundary be drawn precisely:

- The **library is linked code** that compiles into each component and respects per-component file ownership. Fully compatible with the no-shared-daemon rule.
- The **server/mirror is shared infra**: the backup-ingest target is **ouranos** (GitoliteServer + TailnetController, ThinkPad T14 Gen5 — the git/control host); bulk backup storage is **prometheus** (NixBuilder + NixCache + `criome-backup` SSID + Btrfs, GMKtec EVO-X2 — a storage target, NOT the git/control host); transport is the tailnet. **Do not swap ouranos/prometheus** — a prior synthesis made exactly that error, and two chapters re-confirm the roles against `goldragon/datom.nota`. The ingest target should stay a dumb append-of-suffixes-with-write-once-semantics endpoint (Postgres-archive-command shape), not a second engine, to honour no-shared-daemon.

**A wrinkle the poster-child already exposes:** the "one redb file per component" invariant is *already informally bent* by spirit, which runs **three** sema-engine databases per deployment — the live `.sema`, a `*.archive.sema` for retired/superseded records, and a `*.guardian.sema` (guardian journal). This does not weaken the constraint; it **sharpens the case** for the library, because folding archive + guardian into one authoritative payload-bearing log (retired records become tombstoned/superseded entries; the guardian journal becomes another family on the same log) would *restore* the one-authoritative-store invariant rather than multiply files. Whether that consolidation is in scope, or whether the three remain physically separate stores that each opt into the VC library independently, is an open design call (it interacts with the same-file/separate-file matrix in §5) — flagged, not decided.

### The no-reinvent boundary (version-projection / upgrade / version-handover)

The library composes with existing infra; it does not duplicate it. The clean one-line seam: **everything above the store boundary (type relation, wire, protocol, authority, orchestration) exists and is partly real; the durable, payload-bearing, hash-linked, server-mirrorable LOG underneath it does not exist.** Every current migration is a one-shot temp-file rewrite with no log and no server hop.

| Concern | Owned by existing infra (do NOT rebuild) | Owned by the new library |
|---|---|---|
| Schema identity primitive | `version-projection::ContractVersion([u8;32])` blake3 hash + `#hex` codec | Reuse it as the per-entry schema-hash selector |
| Adjacent-version type transform | `VersionProjection<S,T>` trait + per-component impls in `signal-X/migration.rs` | Call inside replay; never re-derive |
| Representability outcome | `ProjectionError::NotRepresentable` | Map to a durable `Divergence` record on the log |
| Write/read/subscribe policy *vocabulary* | `version-projection::policy::*` | Consume; do not redefine |
| Daemon-to-daemon handover wire + protocol | `signal-upgrade` (live) + `upgrade/handover.rs` driver | Produce the durable marker contents (cursor, schema-hash, frontier); do NOT own the socket |
| Meta authority (force-flip/rollback/quarantine) | `meta-signal-upgrade` (live) | Persist the active-version/quarantine *state*; do NOT own the authority wire |
| Migration orchestration (catalogue, attempt routing, handover driving) | `upgrade` runtime | Be its backing store; do NOT own orchestration |
| The durable payload-bearing hash-linked replayable log + mirror + checkpoint | **nobody — this is the gap** | **This is the library** |

Two live hazards in this boundary: (1) **two divergent `HandoverMarker` definitions** exist (`signal-version-handover` uses `commit_sequence`/`write_counter`/`last_record_identifier`; live `signal-upgrade` uses `state_sequence`/`mirrored_write_count`/`record_frontier`) — pick one vocabulary and reconcile the rename before standardizing. (2) `version-projection::RuntimeMigrationLookup` keys decode by `RecordKind(String)` — a stringly-typed selector living *in the exact crate the library leans on*; our typed schema-hash selector competes with it, and having both live would re-create the degeneration j487 forbids. Also note: the upgrade *daemon* is a placeholder with no durable state, and its `Engine` keeps completions/rejections in in-memory `Vec`s — so "the upgrade triad handles versioning" is only half true (orchestration real, durable state unbuilt); the upgrade runtime is itself a *consumer* of the new library, not a peer.

### The substrate constraints (grounded in the live kernel)

- `Sema::write(|txn| ...)` is one `begin_write` + `commit`, redb 4.1.0 single-writer ACID, fsync-on-commit (`lib.rs:568-573`). The engine already writes the data row + log entry + counters in **one** transaction (`engine.rs:333-347`) — this is **free atomicity** (log and view cannot diverge), the most valuable existing property and the easiest to lose.
- redb 4.1.0 `Durability` has only `None` and `Immediate` (the `Eventual` variant is gone). The "queued-for-mirror / async-RPO" level is a **library/engine concept layered above redb**, never a redb durability setting. sema hard-wires `Immediate` with no `set_durability` hook exposed.
- redb savepoints exist but require `Durability::Immediate` and are not surfaced by sema; a redb-native rollback path is unreachable without a kernel change. The direction (rebuild the view from the log) deliberately avoids depending on them.
- redb has `compact(&mut self)` but **no online-snapshot/backup API** — "ship the log suffix" is a read-the-log operation, not a file snapshot.
- **redb copy-on-write page reuse destroys removed bytes within hours** (`ARCHITECTURE.md:152-159`). Any arm relying on redb retaining old versions for history is wrong — history must live in the explicit payload-bearing log.
- Two independent guards, not one: the **format-identity guard** (`DatabaseHeader`, pins the rkyv feature set against build drift) and the **schema-version guard** (`SchemaVersion(u32)`, hard-fail on mismatch). A content-addressed schema-hash replaces/extends only the *second*; collapsing them loses build-drift protection.
- `sema` must NOT depend on `sema-engine`, Signal contracts, Kameo, tokio, NOTA, Nexus, Persona, or Criome. The library cannot push remote-mirror/async/socket concerns DOWN into `sema`; those belong at or above `sema-engine`.

### The deepest cross-cutting lessons

1. **The direction is validated; the genericity is the unsolved part.** Datomic, Fossil, event-sourcing, and eg-walker independently confirm "version the log, rebuildable view." But *not one* of these systems carries a compile-time-typed payload through a generic content store — they all go unityped/opaque/schemaless/triple/stringly. The gap our discipline forces us to close is the gap *none of the donors solved*. Irmin and openraft show it is *possible* to stay typed; neither offers a Rust pattern that is simultaneously generic-over-families, non-stringly-typed, and free-function-free. That synthesis is genuinely ours to invent.

2. **rkyv makes us strictly more fragile than every donor, which forces the typed `SchemaTransition`.** Dolt rewrites a whole table on encoding change; even its "free" cases (append a nullable column, widen a fixed field) are *full-rewrite* events for us under `pointer_width_32`/`unaligned`/`bytecheck`. Avro/Protobuf/FlatBuffers all evolve in place via tags/vtables/defaults that rkyv's bare layout omits for mmap speed. rkyv forward-compat is blocked at the Rust language level (no custom DSTs; maintainer-confirmed), and the one crate that tried schema evolution (`protoss`) is abandoned. So **essentially every schema change is a full-type-set transition** — confirming that a migration must be a typed log entry carrying a reducer, decoded by schema-hash, never a byte diff. Spirit is the existence proof: it already hand-rolls **six** frozen historical readers — `SpiritStoreV1/V2/V4/V5/V6/V7Database` (V3 is skipped; `production_migration.rs:89,94,99,104,109,114`) — plus a separate `ProductionStoredRecord` reader at `PRODUCTION_SCHEMA_VERSION = 5` (`production_migration.rs:120`), against a current live `SPIRIT_SCHEMA_VERSION` of 8 (`store.rs:47`). (Note: 8 is the *current* live schema the running `Store` reads directly, not a frozen reader; there is no `SpiritStoreV8Database`. The frozen-reader count is six, not eight, and is not a contiguous V1..V7 range.) This is exactly the per-version frozen-type-set machinery a reusable VC layer would absorb — generated and replayable instead of hand-rolled.

3. **Compaction is the danger zone, not append.** borg's append-only-mode flaw (a trusted prune permanently applies a compromised client's deletions), Kafka's explicit "compaction loses intermediate history," TerminusDB's rollup-vs-sync, Dolt's whole journal→oldgen GC machinery, Beelay's Sedimentree strata-with-checkpoints, and Litestream's L1/L2/L3 level compaction all say the same thing: **naive suffix sync breaks the moment you compact**, and the checkpoint protocol (identity, schema-hash inventory, covered range, retained-suffix policy, pruned-head redirect) is load-bearing, not optional. Sedimentree (strata referencing start/end hashes with interior checkpoints, peers reconciling on shared checkpoint boundaries) is the closest external template for our checkpoint design; the deliberate divergence is that ouranos is *trusted*, so we skip Beelay's zero-knowledge-encrypted-server cost.

4. **Digest beside cursor is forced by the code and confirmed by every log.** No surveyed system uses a content hash as the ordered cursor — Datomic `t`, Kafka offset, commitlog/raft-log offsets, Yjs integer clocks, DataScript node addresses are all integer/sequence-addressed; content hashes (where present) are layered beside for integrity/dedup. `sema-engine` has no other ordering primitive than `CommitSequence`/`SnapshotIdentifier`. The brief's instinct is structurally forced.

5. **The reusable-library boundary is the documented hard part.** noms was *vendored into Dolt* because the library boundary "became so intertwined" it was unmanageable — the most general member of this lineage abandoned the clean boundary. Doltgres had to be a separate product. Treat j487's "build once as generic types/traits" as the **high-risk deliverable**, not a given.

## 4. The reusability problem specifically

The design-space chapter maps five Rust shapes (plus a dispatch axis). Each is scored against perfect-specificity, the owning-noun rule, and how the typed replay envelope stays typed across families. None is chosen.

- **Option A — a `Versioned`/`Replayable` trait each family implements.** Schema-hash as an associated const; replay is a monomorphised fold. *Specificity: high* (no string tag, type carries meaning). *Owning noun:* the trait lives on the family, but the fold wants a separate owner — the missing noun is a `Reducer`/`Projection` (Irmin's `merge` leg minus merge). *Wall:* genericity is per-type, but a component's log interleaves *many* families (spirit has `Entry` + referents). Can't be `Vec<LogEntry<R>>` for one `R` — forces one-log-per-family or erasure at the collection boundary.

- **Option B — a generic `VersionedStore<R: RecordFamily>` (the Irmin-functor analogue).** *Specificity: high.* *Owning noun: FOUND* — `RecordFamily` is the noun the verb is asking for; the generic store hangs methods off it. *Wall:* same multi-family wall as A. Per-family stores map naturally onto *separate* logs/files, in direct tension with the single-atomic-log-per-component invariant that gives free atomicity.

- **Option C — a registry keyed by schema-hash with closed-sum decoders.** Homogeneous `Vec<ReplayEnvelope>`; each entry carries a schema-hash selector; replay looks up a typed decoder. *Specificity: MEDIUM — the knife-edge.* A **closed** enum / sealed sum of the component's families honours specificity (Git's closed-kinds move). An **open** `HashMap<SchemaHash, Box<dyn Decoder>>` with a fallback degenerates into the forbidden generic-record store. The whole specificity question runs through this one line. *Owning nouns:* `FamilyRegistry`, `SchemaHash`. *Strength:* the ONLY option that cleanly supports one interleaved atomic log over many families — which is what the existing log already is and what same-file atomicity wants. Payload is "erased at rest, typed in motion."

- **Option D — `Box<dyn>` vs monomorphised generics (a dispatch axis across A–C, not a fourth design).** Trait objects buy a heterogeneous log at the cost of an erased type and a hard collision with rkyv (`EngineStoredValue`'s `for<'serialize>` HRTB serializer bounds and associated `Archived`/`CheckBytes` types resist object-safety). The realistic shape is a closed `enum LogEntryKind` (monomorphised, named variants — specificity-honouring) rather than `Box<dyn>` (specificity-eroding). Schema-hash selects an enum variant, not a v-table.

- **Option E — sealed `RecordFamily` trait + generated per-component closed enum (the synthesis the code points at).** Combines B's per-family trait with C's single-log registry: `RecordFamily` is *sealed* (only the component's own families implement it), and a per-component closed `enum ComponentRecord { Entry(...), Referent(...) }` is generated; the envelope's schema-hash selects a variant. *Specificity: high* — sealed-trait + closed-enum is the maximal-specificity way to be generic; no open dispatch, no fallback. *Owning nouns:* `RecordFamily` (sealed), the per-component closed enum, `ReplayEnvelope`, `SchemaHash`, `Checkpoint`, `CommitSequence`. *Split:* the *reusable* part is the trait + envelope + registry + checkpoint machinery generic over `R: RecordFamily`; the closed enum is *generated per component* (a derive/macro — and macro output must emit into `impl` blocks per the no-free-fn-in-schema rule). A new schema version is a new sealed family + a `SchemaTransition` entry — exactly spirit's frozen-historical-reader pattern (six readers today; §3 lesson 2), but generated and replayable instead of hand-rolled. **E is highlighted as the most code-aligned candidate to TEST, not selected — the genericity mechanism is explicitly left open in §7.**

**The owning-noun ledger** (what a "library of generic types and traits" must find, per the no-free-function / no-ZST-namespace rule): the survey converges on `RecordFamily` (Irmin content-signature, sealed) as the central noun; `Reducer`/`Projection` (the fold, Irmin's merge-minus-merge); `SchemaHash` (the typed decoder selector that replaces `Catalog`'s bare `table_name: String`); `ReplayEnvelope` (typed-in-motion, erased-at-rest entry); `Checkpoint`, `Mirror`/`RemoteHead`, `MirrorAck`; and `CommitLogEntry` extended in place rather than replaced. The temptation to forbid: a `VersionControl` ZST with helper methods — a free-function bag in disguise. openraft's storage-trait triad and the existing `Engine`/`Catalog`/`SubscriptionRegistry`/`CommitLogEntry` nouns are the in-codebase precedent that this discipline is satisfiable.

**The unresolved placement question** (deliberately left open): where on the erasure spectrum the *resting* log payload sits — fully monomorphised per-family (A/B, breaks the single atomic log) or hash-selected into a closed sum (C/E, keeps the single atomic log but rests as bytes). Whether resting-bytes honours the *spirit* of perfect specificity is a design call for the kernel owners.

## 5. Same-file vs separate-file — the experiment matrix

The psyche wants BOTH tested. The genericity choice and the file choice are **coupled**: same-file strongly favours a single interleaved log over many families (Options C/E); separate-file makes per-family `VersionedStore<R>` (Option B) natural. So the experiment is not just "two file layouts" — it is "closed-enum single-log genericity vs per-family generic-store genericity," with the file layout as the forcing function. Run both arms with the **same multi-family payload** (spirit's `Entry` + referents) so the multi-family wall actually appears, and hold everything else constant (envelope, digest-linking, checkpoint, mirror protocol, one synthetic `SchemaTransition`). Three options, instrumented as concept-branch experiments forked off a common baseline that already landed the payload envelope + hash-linking + rebuild-from-log path:

| Dimension | A: log table in `.sema` | B: second redb file | C: append-frame file |
|---|---|---|---|
| Atomicity log↔view | **Free, total** (one txn) | Lost; needs view watermark | Lost; needs view watermark |
| Crash recovery | Inherited from redb, no replay | Replay suffix to watermark | Frame-truncate + replay |
| Read-after-write | Strongest (no lag) | Policy: sync keeps / async **breaks** | Policy: sync keeps / async **breaks** |
| Backup unit | Logical entry export | Isolated log *file* | **Byte *suffix* (best)** |
| fsyncs/commit (sync) | 1 | 2 | 2 (cheaper frame fsync) |
| Write amplification | Highest (2× into one B-tree) | 2× into two B-trees | Lowest log side (append) |
| Replay substrate | B-tree scan | B-tree scan | **Linear frame scan (fastest)** |
| New complexity | Lowest | Two-file state machine | Bespoke format (highest) |
| Audit 208 stage | Sequenced first | Sequenced last | Sequenced last (end state) |

- **Option A** (log table inside the one `.sema`, today's location + payload): free atomicity from the single redb transaction (`engine.rs:333-347`) — log and view structurally cannot diverge, zero engineering. Strongest read-after-write. Weakest shippability (the log is logically exportable, not byte-shippable). Highest write amplification. Lowest complexity. Mercurial's interleaved `.i` and DataScript's whole-DB serialize are the analogues.

- **Option B** (log in a second redb file; view disposable): atomicity *lost across files* — redb gives single-file ACID, not a two-file commit. Needs a **WAL-follower watermark** ("view applied through sequence N") and a recovery reconciliation on every open. Read-after-write becomes a *policy choice*: B-sync (apply-to-view-before-return, two fsyncs, preserves the contract) vs B-async (return after log fsync, **breaks** read-after-write — flagged probably-unacceptable for the core engine, acceptable only for a mirror). Strong log-file shippability; the view never needs backup. Datomic's log/index split and Postgres's data-dir-plus-WAL-archive are the analogues.

- **Option C** (bespoke append-frame log file, no redb for the log; view rebuilt from frames): **best shippability** — the frame file is byte-shippable as a literal suffix (`sendfile`-shaped tail copy), the cheapest possible mirror. Lowest log-side amplification (sequential append, no B-tree churn). Fastest replay substrate (linear scan), and the same code path serves crash recovery and server-side ingest validation. *Highest upfront complexity* and it collides with two hard disciplines: **no hand-rolled parsers** (frame framing must be typed encode/decode) and **every fn on a data-bearing noun** (a `FrameLog` owning file + offset + head-digest, never a free `write_frame`). Treat the constraint-fit as a *measured* experiment output. commitlog's Segment=Index+Store and Kafka's head/tail are the analogues.

**On build order:** audit 208 sequenced Option A first and Options B/C last (A as the lowest-effort starting arm, C as the end state). That is a *prior recommendation carried into this map, not a conclusion of this report* — the map ranks the arms on cost/risk axes but does not itself prescribe which to build or in what order; that choice belongs with the kernel owners alongside the genericity decision.

**The real trade the matrix must measure** (not assume): same-file pays at *compaction/backup* time (no shippable suffix, no online snapshot in redb); separate-file pays at *write/crash-recovery* time (lost free atomicity, mandatory idempotent replay + watermark). Both are first-party-validated in shipping prior art, which is exactly why testing both is reasoned rather than wasteful. Instrument all three behind one benchmark + crash harness: commit latency (isolate fsync count — the dominant variable), bytes-on-disk-per-commit, rebuild-from-log wall time, recovery time after crash-injected-at-the-fsync-boundary, bytes-shipped-per-backup, a stale-read probe for the async sub-variants, **and a degraded-mode arm** (mirror/ouranos unreachable) that measures RPO accrual and queue growth — the experiment should produce a number for "how far behind is server-committed when the single ingest point is down," because §6 flags ouranos as a single point of failure and the durability contract under that failure is otherwise unmeasured.

**Correctness oracle:** replay into a fresh view and assert its digest equals the pre-rebuild digest. Spirit's `state_digest` (`store.rs:908-932`, doc-comment from 902) is the right *fold shape* to reuse — it folds `commit_sequence` first (`store.rs:916`) then per-record `(identifier, archived bytes)` and per-referent — so the rebuild must replay to the *identical* head or the digest mismatches (watch the watermark off-by-one). **One methodological correction to spirit's shape:** spirit truncates the blake3 digest to 8 bytes / `u64` (`store.rs:930-932`) to fit the schema's `Integer` digest width. That is a 64-bit oracle — fine for spirit's marker, too weak for an experiment correctness assertion (a 64-bit collision is plausible under heavy fuzzing). The experiment oracle should compare the **full 32-byte blake3** output, not the truncated `u64`; reuse the fold, drop the truncation.

## 6. Things to watch / open questions / risks

- **The genericity knife-edge (closed sum vs open map-with-fallback) is the single line the whole specificity question runs through.** A careless `Box<dyn Decoder>` registry or a kept `RecordKind(String)` selector regresses to the exact generic-record store forbidden by `abstractions.md`. okaywal and `cqrs-es` prove the wrong path is the path of least resistance.
- **What is actually novel (and therefore unde-risked by prior art):** the *typed schema-transition layer*. Every donor's durability/atomicity/sync machinery is reusable; *none* solved typed-payload genericity over a fragile zero-copy format. The schema-hash-as-decoder-selector is well-grounded (Avro single-object encoding ships it; Axon's `(type-id, revision)` selector is the upcaster analogue) and is arguably *mandatory* for rkyv (a record with no schema tag is undecodable after any schema change). But the reducer authoring story is the genuinely hard, partially-unsolved problem — TerminusDB flags strengthening migrations experimental; Axon warns upcaster chains grow unboundedly forever unless you copy-and-transform. Budget for the reducer story being the hard part. The library can *invoke* a typed reducer; only the component can *author* one (spirit's `from_v1`..`from_v7` bodies, `production_migration.rs:1140-1190`, encode irreducible domain meaning).
- **Free atomicity is the most valuable existing property and the easiest to lose.** Any "authoritative log, rebuildable view" inversion that splits the log from the view (Options B/C) forfeits the same-redb-transaction divergence guarantee redb gives for free, and *mandates* idempotent replay plus a recorded view-applied-watermark. Verify rkyv re-encode of an identical entry is byte-identical, or the write-once/refuse-overwrite idempotency check (Postgres archive-command contract) false-positives on re-sends.
- **Compaction breaks naive suffix sync; the checkpoint protocol must be designed before choosing same-file vs separate-file**, because the two arms compact differently. The checkpoint must carry identity + schema-hash inventory + covered range so a peer pruned past another peer's head gets a checkpoint-then-suffix, not a gap. Test the pruned-head redirect explicitly in both arms. Also: a schema migration that rewrites every record's bytes re-chunks the entire log differently, so CDC dedup collapses across a `SchemaTransition` — another reason the migration is a typed log entry + checkpoint, not a byte diff.
- **Async RPO is in real tension with 29pb's "state loss unacceptable."** The resolution every local-first system uses: the *local committed view never lags* (read-after-write), only the *mirror* lags under a named per-store RPO budget. Be explicit that the durability guarantee is local-commit; gate `server-committed` on a real server-side fsync ack, never an ack that races ahead of ouranos's fsync.
- **ouranos is a single ThinkPad as both git host and ingest target — a single point of failure for the server-committed tier.** Define the queued-for-mirror behaviour and named RPO when ouranos is unreachable (and measure it: see the degraded-mode arm added to §5). Multi-target / quorum ingest (rqlite/openraft-style) is *not* a fit for our single-trusted-server case — but rejecting quorum is not the same as having a degraded-mode contract; the report's position is that the contract under ouranos-down is "queue locally, accrue RPO, never block the local view," and the matrix should produce the RPO number rather than leave it asserted. The borg append-only-prune hole says: make the server's already-sealed segments WORM/immutable (S3-Object-Lock-Compliance-mode concept) so a compromised client can never command retroactive deletion.
- **Don't double-content-address or add a third hashing scheme.** criome's blake3 `ObjectDigest` (`signal-criome/src/lib.rs:55-57`) and spirit's `state_digest` are the in-house seeds to *absorb*; `version-projection::ContractVersion([u8;32])` is the ready-made schema-identity primitive to *reuse*. Decide deliberately whether the entry digest reuses blake3 (consistent, cryptographic) or a CRC-class fingerprint (Avro-style, shorter) — `ObjectDigest` stores hex-in-String, which for a hot-path log digest doubles size vs a raw `[u8;32]`; weigh a raw-byte-backed digest type rather than copying the String shape verbatim. (Related: spirit's truncated-`u64` state digest in §5 is fine as a marker but too narrow as an integrity hash — another reason not to copy spirit's digest shape verbatim into the library.)
- **Cross-filesystem rename is not atomic.** The repository-ledger spool works because temp and final share one directory; a ship-to-server design must land the suffix on the target filesystem *before* any atomic rename into the inbox — never rename across the network or across mounts. The spool gives at-least-once (commit-then-move), so suffix ingest must be idempotent (dedupe by entry digest / sequence).
- **The HandoverMarker field-name divergence** (`signal-version-handover` vs live `signal-upgrade`) must be reconciled before the library standardizes the durable head it produces. Confirm with the sema/sema-engine + persona owners whether `signal-version-handover` is intended-dead before treating it as merely frozen.
- **Consumer-shape coverage is uneven — `mind` and `criome` are under-grounded.** spirit (the hard case: three databases, six frozen readers, hand-rolled `state_digest`) and repository-ledger (the clean case: already event-shaped, atomic spool) are examined in depth. But `mind` and `criome` are named as sema-engine consumers (§1, §2) and never grounded — no chapter checks whether `mind` carries its own hand-rolled history, what record families it would register, or whether `criome`'s `ObjectDigest` overlaps the library's digest needs. Since j487's whole point is "every component opts in," the opt-in shape of `mind` and `criome` is a real gap: the experiment payload uses spirit's families, but the library's genericity claim is only as strong as the *most awkward* consumer, which has not been surveyed. Flag for a follow-up grounding pass, not for this report to invent.
- **Two open framing questions for the owners, not for this report:** (1) the library's home layer — new crate vs `sema-engine` primitive vs beside-it (load-bearing per storage-and-wire.md:179 and `ARCHITECTURE.md:87-90`); (2) whether the log, if same-file, is a `__sema_`-prefixed *kernel*-owned table (renegotiating the `ARCHITECTURE.md:84-90` "kernel owns only metadata + headers" non-goal) or a normal *engine*-owned component table. Track which.

## 7. What this report does NOT decide

This report maps the problem space. It does **not** choose:

- **Whether to invert the kernel** ("version the log, demote tables to a view"). That is the `sema`/`sema-engine` owners' call, and the genericity choice is *orthogonal* to it — the inversion is doable under any of Options A–E and nothing in the kernel today forces it.
- **The genericity mechanism** (A vs B vs C vs E, monomorphised vs `Box<dyn>`, where resting payload sits on the erasure spectrum). The central tension is framed, not resolved; whether "erased at rest, typed in motion" satisfies the spirit of perfect specificity is left open. **Option E is highlighted as the most code-aligned candidate to test — that is a steer toward an experiment, not a selection.**
- **Same-file vs separate-file** (Options A/B/C). The psyche asked for BOTH to be tested as runnable concept-branch experiments; the matrix is instrumented, the trade is mapped, the choice is deferred to the measurements. **Audit 208's A-first/B-C-last sequencing is reported as a prior recommendation, not adopted as this report's build order.**
- **Whether to consolidate spirit's three stores** (live + archive + guardian) into one authoritative log, or let each opt into the library independently. The consolidation would *restore* the one-store invariant; it is in tension with the same-file/separate-file matrix and is left to the owners.
- **The library's home layer** (new crate vs `sema-engine` primitive vs beside-it) and **whether the log is kernel-owned or engine-owned**.
- **The digest function** (blake3 reuse vs CRC-class fingerprint vs `ContractVersion`) and whether it is raw-byte- or String-backed. (The report does take one *methodological* stance for the experiment harness — the correctness oracle must use full 32-byte blake3, not spirit's truncated `u64` — but that is an oracle choice, not the production digest decision.)
- **The reducer-authoring and async-RPO policies** beyond noting their constraints (local-view-never-lags; reducer bodies stay per-component-domain) and that the degraded-mode RPO must be *measured*, not asserted.

The deliverable is the constraint map, the prior-art digest, the owning-noun ledger, and the experiment matrix — decision *inputs*, not the decision.
