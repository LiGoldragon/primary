# Federated Encrypted Sync as Prior Art for Sema Key-Scoped Stores: Willow/Meadowcap, p2panda, Earthstar, Keyhive/Beelay, Automerge, Ditto

## Scope and verdict

The psyche's "Spirit-as-federation-of-key-scoped-stores" model is not speculative — it is the convergent design of the entire 2023-2026 local-first / encrypted-sync field. Every property the direction (Spirit `dun9`) asks for has a shipped or near-shipped reference implementation, and the field has already paid for the sharp edges. The strongest prior art for our exact stack — typed hash-linked event log + materialized view + signed checkpoints + zero-knowledge server + crypto-shred erasure + capability-gated multi-instance query — is the **Willow protocol family** (data model + Meadowcap + Private Interest Overlap + Confidential Sync) and **Ink & Switch's Keyhive/Beelay/BeeKEM**. Willow gives us the *store/area/reconciliation* model and the *deletion* model; Keyhive/Beelay gives us the *zero-knowledge-server sync of ciphertext the relay cannot read* and *coordination-free revocation*. p2panda gives us the cleanest *Pull/Read/Write/Manage* access tiering and group-encryption CRDT. Tahoe-LAFS and Cryptree give us the rigorous *capability-diminishing* and *lazy-revocation* vocabulary. The cargo-cult traps are real and named below.

## 1. The store/area model: Willow is our event-log federation, already a CRDT

Willow's core object is precisely our store. A Willow **Store** is defined as "a set of `AuthorisedEntries`" sharing one namespace, and joining two stores (the sync operation) forms a **join semi-lattice / CRDT** — deterministic regardless of order ([data model](https://willowprotocol.org/specs/data-model/index.html)). This is exactly our "blake3-hash-linked payload-bearing event LOG (authoritative) → redb materialized view," restated as the convergence object. The mapping is tight:

- Willow organizes every entry across **three dimensions — `subspace` (per-author universe), `path` (hierarchical), `timestamp`** — inside a **`namespace`** (an independent data collection). Our key-scoped store maps to a (namespace, subspace) pair; our Spirit record `path`/kind maps to Willow `path`; our log ordering maps to `timestamp` + the total-order tiebreak.
- An **Entry** is metadata only: `namespace_id`, `subspace_id`, `path`, `timestamp`, `payload_length`, `payload_digest`. The **payload** is a separate bytestring referenced by digest — i.e. **the log commits over the digest, and the payload can be encrypted and pruned independently of the commitment**. This is the same separation we want: the log commits over ciphertext; the digest stays even if the payload is crypto-shredded.
- The **"newer entry" total order** (greater timestamp, then greater `payload_digest`, then greater `payload_length`) is a deterministic last-writer-wins rule we can adopt verbatim for materialized-view conflict resolution, replacing ad-hoc merge logic.

"View = union of accessible stores" is literally Willow's join: a peer's view is the join of every store it can authorise. Our federation query fanning out over key-held stores **is** Willow's `AreaOfInterest` intersection.

## 2. Deletion: Willow prefix-pruning is the metadata-layer twin of our crypto-shred

Willow has the single feature it claims no other protocol has: **prefix pruning** ([data model](https://willowprotocol.org/specs/data-model/index.html); [comparison](https://willowprotocol.org/more/willow_compared/index.html)). Writing a newer entry whose `path` is a *prefix* of existing entries **deletes** all prefixed entries — "similar to overwriting a directory with an empty file," and it deliberately "avoids any cryptographic proofs of completeness, allowing for **traceless data removal**." This is the metadata-layer complement to our payload-layer crypto-shred:

- **Our crypto-shred** (destroy the per-store key) erases payload *plaintext* but leaves the log commitment and entry skeleton (digest, length, path) intact — needed because the hash chain commits over ciphertext.
- **Willow prefix-pruning** erases the entry *skeleton itself* — the existence record — without breaking a completeness proof, because Willow intentionally has none.

Together these give us **two-tier erasure**: crypto-shred for "the content is gone but the slot remains" and prefix-pruning for "the slot's existence is gone." This is the resolution to "is per-store the right erasure granularity": **erasure is two operations at two layers, not one**, and the answer to "per-store vs per-record" is that *crypto-shred is per-key (per-store), pruning is per-path-prefix (sub-store, down to per-record)* — they compose, you don't pick one.

Caveat Willow itself flags: prefix-pruning is a *local-then-replicated* mutation; it does not and cannot retract data a peer already pulled. **Deletion is "stop propagating + overwrite," never "unsend"** — same fundamental limit as our crypto-shred against a recipient who cached plaintext.

## 3. Meadowcap: capability = key possession, communal vs owned = our two store-governance modes

Meadowcap ([spec](https://willowprotocol.org/specs/meadowcap/index.html); [intro](https://gwil.garden/posts/meadowcap-intro.html)) makes "access = key possession" rigorous and gives us two governance modes we should adopt as named store types:

- **Communal namespace**: each subspace is owned by an author keypair; the author's public key *is* the `SubspaceId`; peers reject any write not signed by it. This is our **author-scoped store** (a Spirit instance owns its subspace).
- **Owned namespace**: one keypair (the `NamespaceId`) controls everything and **delegates** read/write via signed capability chains. This is our **federation-scoped store** with a custodian (criome) minting delegations.

A capability is "an unforgeable token bestowing read or write access… issued by the owner," restrictable to a 3D **Area** (subspace × path × time range), and delegable by signing `(area, prior-signature, recipient-pubkey)` into a chain verified recursively. This is exactly criome's BLS-signed delegation, with one upgrade we should steal: **capabilities carry a 3D area restriction**, so a delegation can be "read store X, only path `Health/*`, only entries timestamped before T." That is finer than per-store and answers "per-store vs per-record granularity" on the *access* side: **Meadowcap already gates sub-store by path+time inside one key**, so the access boundary and the crypto-shred (key) boundary need not coincide — a deliberate, useful asymmetry.

**Revocation vs erasure — the field's hardest-won lesson, and it confirms the psyche's framing exactly.** Meadowcap "revocation is not a feature in the traditional sense." Owen instead (a) issues short-lived caps (one week) and stops renewing, or (b) uses owner write-authority to overwrite a misbehaving author's future entries. So:

> **Revocation (stop future access) and erasure (destroy past content) are different operations with different mechanisms, and removing a recipient does NOT erase what they already hold.** The psyche stated this in the direction; it is the consensus position across Meadowcap, Keyhive, Cryptree, and Matrix.

## 4. Recipient-change = re-wrap the data key, not re-encrypt content — confirmed by p2panda, Cryptree, KBFS

The GoPass mental model ("re-wrap the data key for the new recipient set") is exactly how the rigorous systems do it:

- **p2panda group encryption** ([2025 post](https://p2panda.org/2025/02/24/group-encryption.html)): adding a member **distributes existing key material** ("a list of all previously used secrets… so they can decrypt all previously created data"); removal **rotates the symmetric key and HPKE-wraps the new key for each remaining member** — never re-encrypting content. Two schemes: **Data Encryption** (2SM, stable key across messages, late-joiners get history — our log case) and **Message Encryption** (Double Ratchet, per-message forward secrecy — for the genuinely ephemeral).
- **Cryptree** ([paper](https://tik-db.ee.ethz.ch/file/146566189b90f952b8ab1dcf98010781/)): the canonical **lazy revocation** — defer re-encryption until the next write, because re-wrapping is cheap and re-encrypting is not. Our log is append-only so "next write" = "next entry"; old ciphertext stays readable to old recipients (which is *correct* — revocation ≠ erasure).
- This makes **the per-store data key the unit of both access AND crypto-shred**, exactly as the direction proposes — but with the explicit understanding (Cryptree, p2panda) that **rotation on recipient-removal protects only data written after the rotation**.

## 5. Zero-knowledge-server sync: Keyhive/Beelay is the direct precedent for ouranos

This is the single most on-point piece of prior art for our ouranos mirror, and it is 2024-2026 and Rust ([Keyhive notebook](https://www.inkandswitch.com/keyhive/notebook/); [syncing Keyhive](https://www.inkandswitch.com/keyhive/notebook/05/)):

- **Beelay** "synchronizes end-to-end encrypted payloads that a server cannot decrypt" and "provides defense-in-depth by only syncing with replicas granted access in Keyhive." That is ouranos verbatim: a zero-knowledge append-only relay that holds only ciphertext and gates replication by capability.
- It reconciles **without comparing ciphertext**: peers first sync the **Keyhive membership graph** to learn which documents the other should see, then reconcile state *hashes* via **RIBLT (Rateless Invertible Bloom Lookup Tables)** — symbols are `(document_id, hash(heads, cgka_ops))`, and traffic is ≈1.35–1.7× the *set difference*. This is a concrete, better alternative to naive checkpoint-diffing for our redb-view reconciliation, and it composes with Willow's 3d range-based set reconciliation.
- **Sedimentree** solves a problem we will hit: you cannot compact an encrypted log on the server (it can't read plaintext). Sedimentree compacts deterministically by **trailing-zero-bits of the commit hash as chunk boundaries**, so divergent replicas pick the same boundaries and the server compresses ciphertext ranges blindly. **We should adopt this for ouranos-side compaction of our event log.**
- **Coordination-free revocation** (Keyhive group-management CRDT): removing a member "blanks" their leaf and path in the key tree; **blanking causally dominates concurrent updates from the revoked member**, so a removed member's concurrent write does not survive the merge — without any central coordinator. This is the answer to "how do you revoke in a multi-instance federation with no quorum."
- **BeeKEM** (concurrency-friendly CGKA): handles *concurrent* key rotations by keeping "conflict keys" until a causally-later op collapses them — relevant because our branch/fork/merge over the log means concurrent recipient-set changes are normal, not exceptional.

## 6. Store-existence-as-metadata: Willow's PIO is the rigorous answer, with an honest threat model

The direction's hardest new problem — "is knowing a store EXISTS a privacy leak" — is exactly what Willow's **Private Interest Overlap (PIO)** and **Private Area Intersection (PAI)** solve, and the threat model is precise ([read access & confidentiality](https://willowprotocol.org/specs/pio/index.html); [confidential sync](https://willowprotocol.org/specs/sync/index.html)):

- Two peers discover the namespaces/subspaces/paths they **share** "without leaking any `NamespaceIds`, `SubspaceIds` or `Paths` that are not covered by the other peer's read capabilities." Mechanism: a **Diffie-Hellman / salted-hash private set intersection** (the spec's colour-mixing analogy) over hashed `PrivateInterest`s, plus per-session random salts (initiator uses `rnd`, responder uses bit-flipped `rnd`) to **block mirror/echo attacks**.
- **The honest limit, stated by the spec and which we must inherit:** PIO hides existence against *blind* probing but **cannot prevent confirmation of a correct guess**. "Such solutions cannot prevent peers from confirming guesses about data they shouldn't know about." Security therefore rests on **store identifiers (namespace/subspace/path) being long, random, and per-subspace-encrypted** so they are unguessable; **timestamps are conceded as guessable and out of scope.**

This directly resolves our "tier the store registry/discovery by privacy" question: **the store identifier IS the discovery capability**, exactly like Earthstar's share address ([how it works](https://earthstar-project.org/docs/how-it-works)) where "the share's *address* grants discovery and replication; the share's *secret* confers write." So there is no separate public registry to tier — **discoverability is a property of who you hand the (high-entropy) store id to**, and PIO ensures handing your store-id list to a peer reveals only the ids they already independently hold. The Privacy magnitude should therefore **route a record into a store whose id-entropy and recipient-set match its sensitivity**, rather than gate a per-record flag — high-privacy records land in stores whose mere id is a secret.

## 7. Multi-reader / multi-writer on shared ciphertext + partial/scoped sync — solved, three ways

- **Willow Confidential Sync**: **3d range-based set reconciliation (3dRBSR)** over (namespace, subspace, time) — exchange **fingerprints** of 3D ranges, recursively subdivide on mismatch, transmit only differing entries. Peers act as client+server simultaneously over multiplexed logical channels; sync is scoped to the **non-empty intersection of both peers' `AreasOfInterest`** (true partial/sparse replication). Tolerates concurrent insertion *during* sync ("peers neither preannounce how many entries nor payload bytes they will send"). **Verified streaming** lets interrupted encrypted-payload transfer resume against a Merkle digest.
- **Keyhive/Beelay**: RIBLT set reconciliation + sedimentree, above — the encrypted-blob-aware variant.
- **Automerge / Automerge-repo** ([automerge.org](https://automerge.org/); [Automerge 3](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=44777086)): the mature multi-device CRDT — one document, multiple devices/cursors, transport-agnostic sync protocol sending only missing changes; Automerge 3 (2025) cut memory ~10×. **Ditto** ([docs](https://docs.ditto.live/home/about-ditto); [delta CRDTs](https://ditto.live/blog/dittos-delta-state-crdts)) is the production multi-writer mesh (delta-state CRDTs, Bluetooth/WiFi/LAN transports, MongoDB connector) — proof the multi-writer-on-shared-store model runs at commercial scale.

Multi-writer is the *default*, not a feature: every system here is a CRDT precisely so concurrent writers on a shared (cipher)store converge. Our branch/fork/merge/rebase-over-the-log design is the same bet; Willow's join-semilattice and p2panda's causal-DAG membership are the two proven shapes.

## 8. The genuinely new problems and their best-known resolutions

**(a) Cross-store references / referential integrity across encryption boundaries.** This is the one problem the field has *not* cleanly solved and which our cross-store Spirit references will hit hard. A reference from store A into store B is a **capability-as-pointer** (Tahoe-LAFS read-caps embed the target's decryption key — [Tahoe caps](https://tahoe-lafs.org/trac/tahoe-lafs/wiki/Capabilities); Cryptree links are cryptographic edges). The hazards:
  - **Dangling references after crypto-shred**: shredding store B's key leaves A's reference pointing at undecryptable ciphertext — a cryptographic dangling pointer. The database-world answer is **relaxed/eventual referential integrity** (tolerate dangling refs, repair lazily) — see [Shapiro et al., "Ensuring referential integrity under causal consistency," PaPoC 2018](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1803.03482). Under our model this is *correct behavior*, not corruption: a shredded target *should* dangle. We must design the view to render a dangling cross-store ref as "redacted/erased," never as an error.
  - **Visibility asymmetry**: because view = union of *decryptable* stores, the same reference resolves for an instance holding B's key and dangles for one that doesn't — **the same query returns different results by design** (the psyche's explicit goal), but it means referential integrity is **per-instance and partial**, never global. No prior system offers global referential integrity across encryption boundaries; **don't promise it.**

**(b) Atomicity across stores.** Each store is its own CRDT/log with its own checkpoint; a write spanning two stores is **two independent appends**, not one transaction. The field's position (Willow, automerge, p2panda) is **no cross-namespace atomicity** — you get per-store causal consistency only. Resolution: make cross-store operations **idempotent + causally referenced** (the dependent write names the hash it depends on) and let the materialized view treat a half-applied cross-store op as a dangling ref (per (a)) until both arrive. Do not design a two-phase commit across stores; it contradicts the offline-first, partial-replication premise.

**(c) Per-store guardian / schema / branch / migration.** Each Willow namespace can have its own write-capability policy; p2panda's **Pull/Read/Write/Manage** tiers ([access control 2025](https://p2panda.org/2025/07/28/access-control.html)) are per-group. So **per-store guardian (IntakePolicy), schema, and branch policy is the prior-art norm** — our existing per-component policy generalizes correctly. Migration is the open edge: migrating a schema *inside* a store the migrator can't decrypt is impossible, so **migrations must run inside each instance that holds the key** (client-side, like Tahoe's "all crypto on the client"), and the server/relay only ever moves ciphertext. The Privacy magnitude becomes a **routing function** (which store/recipient-set an entry lands in), and changing an entry's privacy = **re-routing it to a different store = re-wrapping under a different key**, i.e. a write to a new store + prefix-prune of the old slot, not an in-place flag flip.

**(d) Per-store vs per-record granularity — resolved as a layered answer, not a choice.** Synthesizing the above: **key/crypto-shred granularity = per-store** (the recipient-set boundary); **access granularity = sub-store** (Meadowcap 3D area: path+time inside one key); **existence-erasure granularity = per-path-prefix** (Willow pruning, down to one record). These three boundaries are *deliberately distinct*. Forcing them to coincide (one key per record) would explode key-management cost (the problem Cryptree/BeeKEM key-trees exist to avoid); forcing per-store-only would lose path-scoped delegation and record-level pruning. **The right design uses all three layers.**

## Bottom line for our design

Adopt Willow's store-as-join-semilattice + 3dRBSR + prefix-pruning as the log/view/erasure-metadata model; adopt Keyhive/Beelay's membership-graph-gated, RIBLT+sedimentree, zero-knowledge-server sync as the ouranos protocol; adopt Meadowcap's 3D-area delegated read/write capabilities (criome already has the BLS signing) with the explicit **revocation ≠ erasure** discipline; adopt p2panda's Pull/Read/Write/Manage tiers and re-wrap-not-re-encrypt key rotation; adopt PIO/PAI (DH-PSI + salted hashes) for store discovery, with the honest threat model that **existence is hidden only as well as the store-id is unguessable**. Treat cross-store referential integrity as **per-instance, partial, dangling-tolerant** — that is the frontier and the field has no better answer; do not promise global cross-store atomicity or integrity.
