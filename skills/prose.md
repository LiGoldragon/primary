# Skill — prose

*Sentences that say true things. The workspace's prose discipline —
the moves for writing sentences that hold their weight, the criterion
that tells you whether they do, and the canon of voices that defines
the register.*

---

## What this skill is for

Prose carries arguments. When the prose is thin, the argument is
thin; when the prose is mannered, the argument is buried; when the
prose hedges, the claim it makes is the one the reader does not
trust. This skill is the workspace's prose layer — applied anywhere
the page itself is the load-bearing surface: essays in TheBookOfSol,
ESSENCE-class documents, audit reports, a substack post, the prose
inside a `skills.md`, a covering letter.

The register the skill aims at: **wise and controlled, not pompous;
subtle and knowledgeable; not flashy, but deeply shocking**. The
shock comes from compression and accuracy, never from ornament. The
reference is TheBookOfSol — its essays are the workspace's
most-developed prose, and its `AGENTS.md` is the most-developed
prose-convention document. Read TheBookOfSol's `AGENTS.md` for
project-specific rules (Sanskrit-first quote blocks, "chloride of
sodium," "Earth is not a planet"). This skill extracts what
generalises.

The skill does not apply to identifiers, code comments, or commit
messages — see `skills/naming.md` and `skills/jj.md` for those.

## The criterion

Two laws upstream of every concrete move. Both were named centuries
apart by writers who understood that prose is what is left after the
unnecessary has been removed.

### I. The iceberg — show by what you omit

Hemingway, in *Death in the Afternoon*, named what every classical
writer already knew:

> "If a writer of prose knows enough about what he is writing about
> he may omit things that he knows and the reader, if the writer is
> writing truly enough, will have a feeling of those things as
> strongly as though the writer had stated them. The dignity of
> movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above
> water. A writer who omits things because he does not know them
> only makes hollow places in his writing."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *Death in the Afternoon*, Ch. 16

The dignity is the test. A passage that reads as dignified rests on
seven-eighths the reader does not see. A passage that reads as
hollow omits what the writer never knew. The discipline is not to
write less. It is to know more, and then choose what to omit.

The companion law, from *A Moveable Feast*:

> "All you have to do is write one true sentence. Write the truest
> sentence that you know... If I started to write elaborately, or
> like someone introducing or presenting something, I found that I
> could cut that scrollwork or ornament out and throw it away and
> start with the first true simple declarative sentence I had
> written."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *A Moveable Feast*

The first true simple declarative sentence is the iceberg's tip.
Everything else either earns its place by deepening it, or gets cut.

### II. Let the elders carry — sources as load-bearing structure

The single most reliable way to sound old is to bring the old voices
forward and let them speak. The prose around the source is
scaffolding; the source is the load-bearing surface. Speaking *over*
a primary source — paraphrasing it, summarising what it says — concedes
the territory the source already holds.

The workspace's reference implementation is
`TheBookOfSol/sol-luna/The_Toroidal_Heart.md`: one image (the torus)
held for the length of the reading, witnessed by the Ṛgveda, the
Atharva Veda, Heraclitus, the Sūrya Siddhānta, Plutarch's *De facie*,
the Bhagavad Gītā, Rudhyar, and Arthur Young. Each tradition speaks
in its own voice; the article steps between them. The argument is
the convergence.

The default ratio: one substantive primary-source block per 150–300
words of prose, with the prose around it doing only the work of
framing and naming what the quote established. A passage that feels
unsupported is usually a paragraph that needed a quote three lines
earlier. A passage that feels overstuffed is usually two quotes that
needed to be one.

If a primary source does not exist, write the claim cleanly on its
own merits. Do not fake a source with "studies have shown" or "many
believe." Either name the witness or own the unbacked claim.

For citation conventions specific to a repo, see that repo's
`AGENTS.md`. TheBookOfSol's Sanskrit-translation-citation form is
the most-developed in the workspace.

## Voice

### Declarative, present, unhedged

The prose stands in the present tense, in the voice of the finished
work. Every sentence states what it knows. Hedges ("perhaps," "it
could be argued that," "one might say") are evidence the underlying
claim is not yet earned. The writer is asking the reader to share
the doubt instead of doing the work that would remove it.

Either the claim is supported and stated, or it is not and is
struck. The hedge is the worst of both: it costs the reader trust
without protecting the writer from being wrong, because the claim is
still there underneath.

The first person appears decisively or not at all. When it appears,
it owns the position and the body — *"I am glad I ate the Walmart
butter. It made me a more formidable warrior."* Sustained "I think,"
"I believe" is weaker than the same prose with the *I* removed; the
claim stands or falls on what it asserts, not on whose head it sits
inside. Use the *I* when its presence is part of the meaning.
Otherwise drop it.

### Wise, not pompous

The register's hardest discipline. Wisdom and pomposity share many
surfaces — both speak in the present tense, both use long quotation,
both gravitate to the aphoristic close. They are distinguished by
two reliable tests.

**Does the sentence add or perform?** A wise sentence adds something
the reader did not have. *"Nature loves to hide."* The reader leaves
the sentence with an instrument they did not bring in. A pompous
sentence performs something the writer wants to be seen as. *"In the
rich tapestry of natural mystery, one observes that the hidden is
the ground of the revealed."* The reader leaves the sentence with
the writer's performance, not an instrument. Strike every sentence
whose work is to be admired.

**Does the diction belong to the subject or to a costume?** A wise
sentence borrows its diction from the subject it names — craft
language for craft, body language for body, technical language for
the technical. *"The smith works in a particular mood. The mood is
steady."* The diction is the diction of smiths and moods. A pompous
sentence borrows its diction from the *idea of being literary* — the
elevated word imported because elevation is the effect. *"The artisan
labors in a particular humour. The humour is unwavering."* Same
content, costumed.

Use the word that names the thing. *"Smith"* not *"artisan"*; *"fire"*
not *"flame"*; *"this is the old work"* not *"this constitutes the
ancient labor."*

## Diction

### The lexicon is the argument

Vocabulary is diagnostic. The chosen word foregrounds the agent; the
chosen frame foregrounds the question. When the prevailing vocabulary
distorts the picture, the writer's first job is to choose words that
do not.

The reference move: TheBookOfSol substitutes *"chloride of sodium"*
for *"sodium chloride"* because the chloride anion is the toxic agent
and the English construction obscures it. The vocabulary itself does
the conceptual work; it is not ornament. From there the rest of the
lexicon — *chloridic*, *chloridism*, *chlorinated diet* — unfolds with
the argument.

The general principle. When the prevailing word for a thing is
inherited from a frame the argument is contesting, the inherited
word is part of what the argument has to defeat. Choose the word
that names the agent. Then use it consistently. A piece that keeps
the wrong vocabulary cannot be polished into the right position.

### Technical terms — gloss once

Articles are read by non-experts. Sanskrit terms, classical text
names, and physiological concepts carry parenthetical or inline
English glosses on first appearance in each article. Subsequent uses
can drop the gloss. Never assume the reader has met *agni*, *ojas*,
*snigdha*, *mitāhāra*, *Caraka Saṃhitā*, *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā*
before.

## Opening

### Begin with the substance

Drop the reader into the argument. Do not announce what the piece
will do, summarise its sections, or introduce the cast. The first
sentence is part of the argument, not a preface to it.

What to delete on sight, every time:

- "This essay does two things..."
- "The argument below..."
- "In what follows, we will see..."
- "A few concepts appear throughout — they are worth introducing at
  the outset..."
- "Notice that...", "Observe how...", "Note that..."

The reference openings show what to do instead. *The Toroidal Heart*
opens *"The world is a torus."* *Cooking and Spices* opens *"Two
claims circulate today under the banner of Āyurveda that the primary
corpus does not support."* *The Law* opens *"The law perverted!"* In
all three, the reader is already inside the position when the first
sentence ends.

Opening with meta-prose is the most reliable signal of an author who
is not yet sure of the position. Strike it, and either commit or
cut.

### Concrete to abstract

Open in particulars. Rise into doctrine. The reverse — opening in
abstraction and descending to example — leaves the reader guessing
what the abstraction is for until the example arrives.

The reference move is *Apathya*: open with a specific recipe half
the internet considers Ayurvedic; show the reader the eleven
ingredients on the plate; then bring forward the classical doctrine
that disqualifies the plate clause by clause. The reader has already
visualised the failure before the doctrine names it.

## Working with sources

### Primary sources as load-bearing surface

When a primary source exists — a classical text, a published study,
a contemporary statement, a numerical record — let it carry the
argument. The pattern that works:

1. One or two paragraphs of plain orientation.
2. The primary-source quote block.
3. Minimal commentary — what the quote established.
4. The next quote, or the next move.

The prose around the source is scaffolding. The source is the
load-bearing surface. Speaking *over* the source — paraphrasing it,
summarising what it says, building the argument as if the source
were not there — concedes the territory the source already holds.

### One image, held

A quote-dense essay needs one load-bearing image at its centre. The
practitioner refining silver; the chloride dissolving the membrane;
the broom no one lives on; the torus inhaling and exhaling. Every
quote selected, every section opened, every sentence chosen deepens
*that* image.

The drift to avoid: a strong quote arrives in a different metaphor
and the writer brings it in because the quote is strong.
Water-as-soft-overcoming-hard, names-must-be-rectified, the
householder-supports-all-*āśramas* — each is a fine anchor for its
own essay. Stacked inside one article they braid many essays into
one and hold to none. The reader feels the loss of centre even when
each quote on its own is excellent.

Discipline: pick the load-bearing image before drafting; cut every
quote that does not deepen it; if a cut quote is too strong to lose,
save it for the article it belongs to. A single-image essay reads
as one thing. A braided essay reads as a tour.

### Catalog of witnesses

When multiple traditions converge on the same point, name them — not
as comparison, but as confirmation that the *thing* has been seen by
many. *The Toroidal Heart* on the two Lights: Ṛgveda, Plutarch,
Bhagavad Gītā, Rudhyar. *Refinement* on the refiner's fire:
Dhammapada, Seneca, Proverbs, Job, Heraclitus, Bhagavad Gītā,
Castaneda, Marcus Aurelius, Musashi. The catalog is not erudition;
it is evidence. The plurality of voices is what makes the claim
classical.

## The sentence

### Rhythm

Sentences vary in length but cluster short. The aphoristic register
sits inside the prose: *"Anna is the floor. Fruit is the broom. A
broom is useful. But no one lives on a broom."* Four short
sentences, each closing a thought, the cumulative movement the
claim.

Long sentences are admitted when they earn the breath — a list that
needs to be kept together, a clause whose internal structure is the
point. They are not the default.

Em-dashes mark genuine asides or rhythmic breaks, not default
emphasis. A paragraph with three or more em-dashes is almost always
restructurable into cleaner syntax. Keep density below roughly five
em-dashes per 500 words.

Use straight quotes (`"` `'`). Curly quotes are a Word / ChatGPT
export artefact and are inconsistent with the rest of the workspace.

### Lists as drumbeat

When the catalogue *is* the argument, write the catalogue in full.
The cumulative weight of the items — recited, named, spelled out — is
what carries the claim that no single item could carry alone.

The reference move is *The Chloride Indictment*: Bright, Widal,
Achard, Digne, Hoelzel, Kempner, Dahl, DASH-Sodium, SSaSS, Yanomami.
The names assemble. By the end of the recital the reader has felt
the consensus, not been told about it.

The list earns its length only when each item is load-bearing. A
list of three weak items is worse than the strongest one stated
alone.

### The closer — aphoristic, not restatement

End on a brief declarative — sometimes one line. The closer distils
the argument the prose has earned; it does not summarise the
argument the prose has just made.

Reference closers from the workspace:

- *"Remove chloride. Life resumes."* (`The_Chloride_Indictment`)
- *"This is what the tradition calls food."* (`Apathya`)
- *"Soon is not a wish. It is a schedule."* (`My_Mothers_My_Sisters`)
- *"No one lives on a broom."* (`Yogis_Dont_Eat_Fruit`)
- *"The field is one. The Lights are two. The breath is one. The
  body is at home."* (`The_Toroidal_Heart`)
- *"The Ambrosian Diet is the yogic diet, spoken in its proper
  name."* (`Ambrosian_Diet`)
- *"The forge is lit. The silver is in the fire. The refiner sits.
  The dross will come up. The metal will speak when it is ready."*
  (`Refinement`)

The prose has done the work. The closer lands what the work
established. Restatement ("In summary, the argument has shown
that...") flattens what the prose built. Cut it.

## The reference shelf

The canon below is not academic. It is the working shelf — the
voices the writer keeps near the desk, opens at random, copies a
sentence from before writing his own. Each entry names *what that
author teaches*, the books on the workspace shelf, and a passage
that demonstrates the lesson.

### Heraclitus — the gnomic fragment

The compression to seven words; the paradox as the form of truth;
nature spoken in koan. Heraclitus is what a sentence sounds like
when nothing more can be removed.

> "Nature loves to hide."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment X (Kahn)

> "The lord whose oracle is in Delphi neither declares nor conceals,
> but gives a sign."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment XXXIII (Kahn)

> "Although the account is shared, most men live as though their
> thinking were a private possession."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment III (Kahn)

> "You will not find out the limits of the soul by going, even if
> you travel over every way, so deep is its report."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment XXXV (Kahn)

> "The way up and down is one and the same."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment CIII (Kahn)

> "Man's character is his fate."\
> — Heraclitus, fragment CXIV (Kahn)

Shelf: Charles H. Kahn, *The Art and Thought of Heraclitus*
(Cambridge 1979); John Burnet, *Early Greek Philosophy* (Project
Gutenberg).

When the prose feels too explanatory, find the seven-word version.

### Plutarch — the moral seeing

The opening that drops into a letter to a friend, the simile drawn
from craft, the patient circling of a single ethical question. The
*Moralia* are essays in the original sense — the writer trying out a
question and turning it over until the shape comes clear.

> "A rich slipper will not cure the gout, a diamond ring a whitlow,
> nor will an imperial diadem ease the headache."\
> — Plutarch, *Moralia*, "Of the Tranquillity of the Mind"

> "As the bees draw honey from the thyme, which is a most unsavory
> and dry herb, extract something that is convenient and useful even
> from the most bitter afflictions."\
> — Plutarch, *Moralia*, "Of the Tranquillity of the Mind"

> "Plato compared our life to a game at dice, where we ought to
> throw for what is most commodious for us, but when we have thrown,
> to make the best of our casts."\
> — Plutarch, *Moralia*, "Of the Tranquillity of the Mind"

> "The half of the moon that faces heaven is the Elysian plain; the
> half that faces earth is the house of counter-earth Persephone."\
> — Plutarch, *De facie* 944C, trans. Cherniss

Shelf: *Moralia* in the Goodwin revision (Project Gutenberg, Vols.
I, II, V); *De facie* in the Cherniss/Helmbold Loeb edition.

When an argument needs to circle, follow Plutarch.

### Cicero — the address as scaffolding

The letter to a son. The pedagogical voice that does not condescend
because it loves the addressee. The Latin that built the Roman
sense of *officium* — what one owes.

> "My dear son Marcus, you have now been studying a full year under
> Cratippus, and that too in Athens, and you should be fully
> equipped with the practical precepts and the principles of
> philosophy."\
> — Cicero, *De Officiis* I.1

> "No phase of life, whether public or private, whether in business
> or in the home, whether one is working on what concerns oneself
> alone or dealing with another, can be without its moral duty."\
> — Cicero, *De Officiis* I.4

Shelf: *De Officiis* (Miller trans.); *De Oratore* (Watson trans.);
*Tusculan Disputations* (Yonge trans.).

When the piece is teaching, address the reader as Cicero addresses
Marcus — directly, by name if there is one, with the assumption that
they have already begun.

### Marcus Aurelius — the gratitudes, the morning resolve

The journal that was not written for an audience. The numbered
section, each a self-contained meditation. The voice that addresses
itself in the second person — *thou*. The opening of *Meditations*
Book I is an inventory of debts named to teachers; the rest of the
book is a man rebuilding his own composure each morning.

> "Of my grandfather Verus I have learned to be gentle and meek, and
> to refrain from all anger and passion. From the fame and memory of
> him that begot me I have learned both shamefastness and manlike
> behaviour. Of my mother I have learned to be religious, and
> bountiful..."\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* I.1–3

> "A man cannot any whither retire better than to his own soul."\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* IV.3

> "Death hangs over thee: whilst yet thou livest, whilst thou
> mayest, be good."\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* IV.14

> "Let thy course ever be the most compendious way. The most
> compendious, is that which is according to nature."\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* IV.43

> "In the morning when thou findest thyself unwilling to rise,
> consider with thyself presently, it is to go about a man's work
> that I am stirred up."\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* V.1

> "Thou must be like a promontory of the sea, against which though
> the waves beat continually, yet it both itself stands, and about
> it are those swelling waves stilled and quieted."\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* IV.40

Shelf: George Long translation (Project Gutenberg).

When the piece needs interior force, write toward the second person
that addresses the self. The reader overhears.

### Hemingway — the iceberg in practice

What the iceberg principle produces when applied to narrative. Short
declarative sentences. No interior monologue where action will do.
The horror or grace is in what the sentence does not say.

The Caesarean and the suicide in *Indian Camp*; the death of Maera
in *In Our Time*; the six cabinet ministers shot at the wall of the
hospital. The last:

> "They shot the six cabinet ministers at half-past six in the
> morning against the wall of a hospital. There were pools of water
> in the courtyard. There were wet dead leaves on the paving of the
> courtyard. It rained hard. All the shutters of the hospital were
> nailed shut. One of the ministers was sick with typhoid. Two
> soldiers carried him downstairs and out into the rain. They tried
> to hold him up against the wall but he sat down in a puddle of
> water."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *In Our Time*, Chapter 6

No commentary. The pity is in the puddle.

> "A serious writer is not to be confounded with a solemn writer. A
> serious writer may be a hawk or a buzzard or even a popinjay, but
> a solemn writer is always a bloody owl."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *Death in the Afternoon*, Ch. 16

Shelf: *Death in the Afternoon*; *A Moveable Feast*; *The Nick
Adams Stories*; *In Our Time*; *The Old Man and the Sea*; *The Sun
Also Rises*. (`en/ernest-hemingway/` in the library.)

When a passage carries weight that should not be named directly,
move into the Hemingway voice. Describe what is in front of the
narrator; trust the reader to feel what is not named.

### Blake — the proverb as scripture

The aphorism that inverts what was assumed. The diction of the
prophet who has no patience for the temple. Blake teaches that the
short declarative sentence can be a religious form.

> "The road of excess leads to the palace of wisdom."\
> — William Blake, *The Marriage of Heaven and Hell*

> "He who desires, but acts not, breeds pestilence."

> "Eternity is in love with the productions of time."

> "The tigers of wrath are wiser than the horses of instruction."

> "What is now proved was once only imagined."

> "Truth can never be told so as to be understood and not to be
> believed."

> "If the doors of perception were cleansed everything would appear
> to man as it is, infinite."

> "Enough! or Too much."

Shelf: *The Marriage of Heaven and Hell*; *Songs of Innocence and
Experience*; *Complete Poetry and Prose* (Erdman & Bloom).

When the argument has run long and a section needs a closer that
does not summarise, find the Blakean proverb — declarative,
image-bearing, paradoxical enough that the reader is forced to slow
down.

### Bastiat — the satire whose target shows itself

The mock-petition. The mock-dialogue. The argument that lets the
absurd premise extend itself until the reader sees its own logic
walk out a window. Bastiat is what political prose sounds like when
it has the patience of a chess player.

The *Petition of the Candlemakers* — a formal letter to the Chamber
of Deputies demanding protection from the unfair competition of the
Sun:

> "Gentlemen, — You are on the right road. You reject abstract
> theories, and have little consideration for cheapness and plenty.
> Your chief care is the interest of the producer. You desire to
> emancipate him from external competition, and reserve the national
> market for national industry... We are suffering from the
> intolerable competition of a foreign rival, placed, it would seem,
> in a condition so far superior to ours for the production of
> light, that he absolutely inundates our national market with it at
> a price fabulously reduced... This rival, who is no other than the
> Sun, wages war to the knife against us."\
> — Frédéric Bastiat, *Economic Sophisms*, "Petition of the
> Manufacturers of Candles"

The opening of *The Law* — repetition as battering ram:

> "The law perverted! The law — and, in its wake, all the collective
> forces of the nation — the law, I say, not only diverted from its
> proper direction, but made to pursue one entirely contrary! The
> law become the tool of every kind of avarice, instead of being its
> check! The law guilty of that very iniquity which it was its
> mission to punish!"\
> — Frédéric Bastiat, *The Law*

Shelf: *The Law*; *Economic Sophisms*; *Economic Harmonies*
(Boyers/OLL).

When the target of an argument is so absurd that direct attack would
be heavier than the target, write toward the Bastiat voice — adopt
the absurd position, extend its logic with formal courtesy until it
dismantles itself.

### Ptolemy — the technical voice that names things

When the subject is technical — astronomy, astrology, medicine, a
working framework — the *Tetrabiblos* register: precise vocabulary,
relational definitions, the calm of a hand that has already drawn
the diagram many times. No apology for the technicality, no
softening of the terms.

Shelf: Ptolemy, *Tetrabiblos* (Robbins, Loeb 435).

When the prose enters its own domain's technical territory, use the
domain's own words without translation. The reader who needs the
gloss can have it once at first appearance; the second use carries
the term unglossed.

### Vedic and Upaniṣadic — the sūtra

Compression to the single syllable. The aphoristic verse that has
been polished by twenty centuries of repetition. The line that does
not explain itself because it does not need to.

> **एकं सद् विप्रा बहुधा वदन्ति।**\
> *ekaṃ sad viprā bahudhā vadanti.*
>
> "Truth is one; the sages call it by many names."\
> — *Ṛgveda* 1.164.46

> **annaṁ brahma iti vyajānāt**
>
> "He realised anna as Brahman."\
> — *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.2.4

> **amṛtaṁ vai prāṇāḥ**
>
> "Prāṇa is amṛta."\
> — *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*

> **तत्त्वमसि।**\
> *tat tvam asi.*
>
> "Thou art that."\
> — *Chāndogya Upaniṣad* 6.8.7

Shelf: the workspace's `library/sa/` and `library/en/`-translated
shelves — *Ṛgveda*, *Atharva Veda*, *Upaniṣads*, *Bhagavad Gītā*,
*Sūrya Siddhānta*, *Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā*, *Caraka Saṃhitā*.

When the argument arrives at the place where the doctrine has
already been crystallised, the doctrine speaks. The prose around it
is preparation; the verse is the load.

### Carlos Castaneda — the metaphor that holds the cosmos

What philosophy sounds like when it has fully entered the image.
The don-Juan voice — at once ethnographic-flat and metaphysically
unflinching. The metaphor in Castaneda is never decorative; it is
the cognitive instrument. Death becomes a hunter at one's left;
awareness becomes the food of the Eagle; inner silence becomes the
mortar between bricks.

> "Death is the only wise adviser that we have. Whenever you feel,
> as you always do, that everything is going wrong and you're about
> to be annihilated, turn to your death and ask if that is so. Your
> death will tell you that you're wrong; that nothing really matters
> outside its touch. Your death will tell you, 'I haven't touched
> you yet.'"\
> — Carlos Castaneda, *Journey to Ixtlan*, "Death is an Adviser"

> "Death is a whorl. Death is the face of the ally; death is a shiny
> cloud over the horizon; death is the whisper of Mescalito in your
> ears; death is the toothless mouth of the guardian; death is
> Genaro sitting on his head; death is me talking."\
> — Carlos Castaneda, *Tales of Power*

> "The Eagle is devouring the awareness of all the creatures that,
> alive on earth a moment before and now dead, have floated to the
> Eagle's beak, like a ceaseless swarm of fireflies, to meet their
> owner, their reason for having had life."\
> — Carlos Castaneda, *The Eagle's Gift*

> "Sorcerers need a breaking point for the workings of inner silence
> to set in. The breaking point is like the mortar that a mason puts
> between bricks. It's only when the mortar hardens that the loose
> bricks become a structure."\
> — Carlos Castaneda, *The Active Side of Infinity*

> "The basic difference between an ordinary man and a warrior is
> that a warrior takes everything as a challenge, while an ordinary
> man takes everything as a blessing or a curse."\
> — Carlos Castaneda, *Journey to Ixtlan*

Shelf: *The Complete Works of Carlos Castaneda, Florinda
Donner-Grau and Taisha Abelar* (collected PDF) at
`en/carlos-castaneda/`; Armando Torres, *Encounters with the
Nagual* (`en/armando-torres/`).

When a concept is more cleanly carried by an image than by an
argument, hand it the image. Castanedan metaphor is the test that a
position has been thought through far enough to have a *shape*.

### Juan Arnau — the contemporary wisdom-prose bridge

The living voice closest to the workspace's target register. Arnau
is an astrophysicist and Sanskritist who writes essays that bind
philosophy of science to the Vedic and Greek traditions without
academic apology. The Spanish is gracile, declarative, willing to
state the position before defending it. The English of TheBookOfSol
aspires to a similar register.

The opening of *La fuga de dios* (2023):

> "Vivimos en el mito de la ciencia. Un mito que se sostiene sobre
> tres pilares. LA CIENCIA ES UNA (cuando en realidad hay múltiples
> disciplinas científicas incapaces de dialogar entre sí). LA
> CIENCIA ES BENEFICIOSA (cuando de hecho ciertas prácticas
> científicas amenazan la salud y la libertad humana). Y LA CIENCIA
> ES DEMOCRÁTICA (cuando su dependencia de la tecnología y los
> recursos hace que sólo esté disponible para los países ricos del
> mundo)."\
> — Juan Arnau, *La fuga de dios* (2023), "Prólogo a la nueva
> edición"

> "El progreso ha sido hasta ahora ciego. No sabe adónde va. Es hora
> de frenar, de reconducir."

> "La luz necesita de sus sombras: la naturaleza expansiva de las
> Ideas engendra seres finitos."

> "El big bang no hay que situarlo en el origen de los tiempos;
> sucede a cada momento, es lo más natural y cotidiano."

> "Nadie convence a nadie de nada, como decía Emerson, y una
> discusión nunca podrá resolverse mediante una operación algebraica,
> como soñó Leibniz."

Shelf: *La fuga de dios* (2023); *Cosmologías de India* (2013);
*Historia de la imaginación* (2019); *Upaniṣads: Correspondencias
Ocultas* (Atalanta, 2019); *La medicina india* (2013). All in the
library under `es/juan-arnau/`.

Arnau's lesson, in English: state the position. Name the witnesses
(philosophers of science, ancient Indian commentators, modern
physicists) on the same page. Move between traditions without
apology. The bridge from Whitehead to the Upaniṣads is not
ornamental; it is the argument.

### Rudhyar — the cosmological-poetic register

When the writing aims at the level of the cosmos itself, Dane
Rudhyar's voice — *The Pulse of Life*, *The Astrology of
Transformation* — is the closest modern register. Personalising and
collectivising forces; systole and diastole; the cycle named in
plain English without either occultist mystique or scientific
distancing.

> "Reality has a rhythmic heart. The systole and diastole of that
> heart create these beats of becoming which are birth and death,
> winter and summer, increase of light and crescendos of darkness."\
> — Dane Rudhyar, *The Pulse of Life*

> "What I had earlier called the principle of 'holism' I now
> realised was better named 'holarchy' — for what we are dealing
> with is a hierarchy of wholes, each whole nested within a larger
> whole, each acting upon the wholes within it and reacting to the
> wholes that contain it."\
> — Dane Rudhyar, *The Astrology of Transformation*

Shelf: Rudhyar's full corpus at `en/dane-rudhyar/`.

When the field of the writing is cosmic — cycles, scales, the body
in the year, the year in the cosmos — Rudhyar names the pulse without
either flattening it or dressing it up.

## What to delete on sight

These patterns appear in drafts. Strike them every time.

### Negative-contrast (the LLM tic)

*"X is not Y. It is Z."* The signature ChatGPT rhetorical move —
building a positive claim by first denying an alternative and then
asserting the real thing. It is mechanical, tedious in repetition,
and adds no information. State the positive directly. Variants:

- *"X is not Y; it is Z."* / *"X is not Y — it is Z."*
- *"X is not Y, but Z."*
- *"Not merely X, but Y."* / *"Not just X, but Y."*
- *"Not A. Not B. Not C. But D."* (triple-fragment hammer)
- *"This is not [thing]. It is [other thing]."* (as a standalone
  paragraph or section opener)

If the dismissed alternative carries information (a misreading the
piece is correcting), absorb it as a trailing concession: *"X is Z,
not Y"* — sparingly, never as default. Most occurrences can be
eliminated entirely by cutting the *"X is not Y"* setup and keeping
only the positive claim. Rule of thumb: if an article uses the
pattern more than ~3 times, it has become the article's default
rhythm and needs a full pass.

### Filler phrases

- "it is worth noting," "it is important to note," "it should be
  remembered"
- "notably," "crucially," "indeed," "moreover," "furthermore"
  (as paragraph openers)
- "truly," "fundamentally," "literally" (in non-literal contexts)
- "delves into," "unpacks," "navigates the complexities of," "in
  the realm of," "rich tapestry," "at the intersection of"
- "the fact that..." (almost always compressible)

### Meta-prose

No announcing the structure of the piece. No opening with *"this
essay does X."* See *Begin with the substance* above. Do not surface
the project's own writing conventions inside an article either —
vocabulary swaps, em-dash density, the not-X-but-Y rule,
curly-vs-straight quotes. Those are workshop discipline, invisible
to the reader; an article that points at them reads as the project
talking about itself.

### Stage-setting

*"Notice that...", "Observe how...", "Note that..."* Do not cue the
reader to attend; let the sentence merit attention on its own.

## What it sounds like

Three short reference passages, each demonstrating the register in
operation.

### Hemingway — observation as judgment withheld

> "Maera lay still, his head on his arms, his face in the sand. He
> felt warm and sticky from the bleeding. Each time he felt the horn
> coming. Sometimes the bull only bumped him with his head. Once the
> horn went all the way through him and he felt it go into the
> sand."\
> — Ernest Hemingway, *In Our Time*, Chapter 16

Six declarative sentences, all observation. No judgment, no pity,
no metaphor. The pity emerges from the absence of it.

### TheBookOfSol — quote-load and aphoristic close

> "The Bhagavad Gītā requires sāttvic food to be *snigdha* —
> unctuous, oily, lubricating — and *sthira* — stable, substantial,
> enduring. Ghee is both. Anna cooked with ghee is both. A diet
> without ghee fails these requirements structurally, regardless of
> what else it contains."\
> — TheBookOfSol, *The Ambrosian Diet*

Four sentences. The doctrine, the inference, the negation, the
verdict. No throat-clearing between them.

### Marcus Aurelius — the second-person resolve

> "In the morning when thou findest thyself unwilling to rise,
> consider with thyself presently, it is to go about a man's work
> that I am stirred up. Am I then yet unwilling to go about that,
> for which I myself was born and brought forth into this world? Or
> was I made for this, to lay me down, and make much of myself in a
> warm bed?"\
> — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* V.1

The voice the writer can take when speaking to the self. The reader
overhears, and the overheard is more durable than the directly told.

## What it does not sound like

Three short passages of what the register *will not produce*. Each
is what to feel for and rewrite away from.

- **The academic distancing.** *"Many scholars have argued that
  Heraclitus's view of fire may be interpreted as a kind of cosmic
  principle, though some recent commentators have raised
  objections."* Strike the scholars; quote Heraclitus directly; let
  the reader meet *"Nature loves to hide"* before any commentary
  arrives.

- **The journalistic crescendo.** *"In a world where the speed of
  change is accelerating, where the very fabric of meaning is
  fraying, perhaps the ancient wisdom of the Stoics offers a
  surprising remedy."* Strike the world-on-fire framing; state what
  the Stoic position is; trust the reader to recognise the
  contemporary application.

- **The mystical drape.** *"Deep within the sacred geometries of
  the cosmic torus, the soul whispers its ancient knowing to the
  attuned heart."* Strike "deep within," "sacred," "whispers,"
  "ancient knowing," "attuned heart." Find the structural claim
  underneath. If there is one, write *that*. If there is not,
  delete the sentence.

The negative test, in one line: *would Heraclitus or Hemingway have
let this sentence stand?* If the answer is no, rewrite it.

## When the prose feels off

The same diagnostic discipline `skills/beauty.md` applies to code,
applied here to prose. Each item below is a *signal*, not a sin.
Notice it; decide what the underlying problem is; fix the underlying
problem.

- **A sentence the reader has to re-read.** The structure is not yet
  right. Find the structure that makes the sentence parse on the
  first read.
- **A claim that needs three qualifiers to be defensible.** Either
  the qualifiers belong inside the claim, or the claim is smaller
  than written. Shrink the claim or kill the qualifiers.
- **A passive construction hiding the actor.** Name the actor. *"The
  river carried the silt"* over *"the silt was carried."*
- **Repetition where one good word would do.** Find the word.
- **A citation patched in mid-sentence.** Restructure so the citation
  lands where it belongs.
- **A paragraph of em-dashes.** The dashes are doing what syntax
  should be doing.
- **A paragraph that paraphrases a primary source you have at
  hand.** Bring the source forward, frame it, let it speak.
- **An opening that introduces what the piece will do.** Cut it;
  start with the substance.
- **A closing that summarises what the piece just did.** Cut it;
  let the closer be the aphoristic distillate.
- **A sentence whose work is to be admired.** Strike it; replace
  with a sentence whose work is to *add*.
- **Diction borrowed from the idea of being literary, not from the
  subject.** Replace with the word that names the thing.

When the prose feels off, slow down and find the structure that
makes it right. That structure is the one the prose was missing.

## When the writing arrives at its own form

A high-register piece does not begin in this register. It begins as
a clear claim, accumulates source-witnesses, and gradually finds
the image and the rhythm that hold it together. The register is the
result of revision, not of first drafting.

The instruction in the moment of drafting is the simpler discipline:
declarative, present-tense, source-anchored, image-disciplined.

The instruction in the moment of revision is the discipline of this
skill in full: read the draft against the reference shelf. Where the
prose feels heavier than Heraclitus, lighter than Hemingway, dimmer
than Blake — find the sentence that broke the register and rewrite
from the noun.

If it sounds like Hemingway translating Marcus Aurelius for Juan
Arnau to read, the register is right.
