# Skill — prose

*Sentences that say true things. Concrete craft moves derived
from the workspace's most-developed prose surface, generalized
for any place prose carries the argument.*

---

## What this skill is for

Prose carries arguments. When the prose is thin, the argument is
thin; when the prose is mannered, the argument is buried; when
the prose hedges, the claim it makes is the one the reader does
not trust. This skill is the craft layer between
`skills/poet.md` (the role) and `skills/beauty.md` (the
criterion). It names concrete moves — voice, opening, closing,
diction, rhythm, deletions — that show up across the workspace's
reference prose.

The reference is TheBookOfSol. Its essays are the workspace's
most-developed prose, and its `AGENTS.md` is the most-developed
prose-convention document. Read TheBookOfSol's `AGENTS.md` for
project-specific rules — Sanskrit-first quote blocks, "chloride
of sodium," "Earth is not a planet." This skill extracts what
generalizes: the moves that hold across any register, any
subject, any repo where prose is the load-bearing surface.

The skill applies anywhere prose carries the weight — essays,
reports, `ESSENCE.md`, the prose layer of a `skills.md`, a
covering letter, a substack post. It does not apply to
identifiers, code comments, or commit messages, which have
their own conventions in `skills/naming.md` and `skills/jj.md`.

## Voice — declarative, present, unhedged

The prose stands in the present tense, in the voice of the
finished work. Every sentence states what it knows. Hedges
("perhaps," "it could be argued that," "one might say") are
evidence the underlying claim is not yet earned. The writer is
asking the reader to share the doubt instead of doing the work
that would remove it.

Either the claim is supported and stated, or it is not and is
struck. The hedge is the worst of both: it costs the reader
trust without protecting the writer from being wrong, because
the claim is still there underneath.

The first person appears decisively or not at all. When it
appears, it owns the position and the body — *"I am glad I
ate the Walmart butter. It made me a more formidable warrior."*
Sustained "I think," "I believe" is weaker than the same prose
with the *I* removed; the claim stands or falls on what it
asserts, not on whose head it sits inside. Use the *I* when
its presence is part of the meaning. Otherwise drop it.

## Begin with the substance

Drop the reader into the argument. Do not announce what the
piece will do, summarise its sections, or introduce the cast.
The first sentence is part of the argument, not a preface to
it.

What to delete on sight, every time:

- "This essay does two things..."
- "The argument below..."
- "In what follows, we will see..."
- "A few concepts appear throughout — they are worth
  introducing at the outset..."
- "Notice that...", "Observe how...", "Note that..."

The reference openings show what to do instead. *Cooking and
Spices* opens inside the disagreement: *"Two claims circulate
today under the banner of Āyurveda that the primary corpus
does not support."* The reader is already inside the argument
before the first paragraph closes.

## The lexicon is the argument

Vocabulary is diagnostic. The chosen word foregrounds the
agent; the chosen frame foregrounds the question. When the
prevailing vocabulary distorts the picture, the writer's first
job is to choose words that do not.

The reference move: TheBookOfSol substitutes *"chloride of
sodium"* for *"sodium chloride"* because the chloride anion is
the toxic agent and the English construction obscures it. The
vocabulary itself does the conceptual work; it is not ornament.
From there the rest of the lexicon — *chloridic*, *chloridism*,
*chlorinated diet* — unfolds with the argument.

The general principle. When the prevailing word for a thing is
inherited from a frame the argument is contesting, the
inherited word is part of what the argument has to defeat.
Choose the word that names the agent. Then use it
consistently. A piece that keeps the wrong vocabulary cannot
be polished into the right position.

## Primary sources as load-bearing surface

When a primary source exists — a classical text, a published
study, a contemporary statement, a numerical record — let it
carry the argument. The pattern that works:

1. One or two paragraphs of plain orientation.
2. The primary-source quote block.
3. Minimal commentary — what the quote established.
4. The next quote, or the next move.

The prose around the source is scaffolding. The source is the
load-bearing surface. Speaking *over* the source — paraphrasing
it, summarising what it says, building the argument as if the
source were not there — concedes the territory the source
already holds.

If a primary source does not exist, write the claim cleanly on
its own merits. Do not fake a source with "studies have shown"
or "many believe." Either name the witness or own the unbacked
claim.

For citation conventions specific to a repo, see that repo's
`AGENTS.md`. TheBookOfSol's Sanskrit-translation-citation form
is the most-developed in the workspace.

## Movement — concrete to abstract

Open in particulars. Rise into doctrine. The reverse — opening
in abstraction and descending to example — leaves the reader
guessing what the abstraction is for until the example arrives.

The reference move is *Apathya*: open with a specific recipe
half the internet considers Ayurvedic; show the reader the
eleven ingredients on the plate; then bring forward the
classical doctrine that disqualifies the plate clause by
clause. The reader has already visualised the failure before
the doctrine names it.

---

## Lists as drumbeat

When the catalogue *is* the argument, write the catalogue in
full. The cumulative weight of the items — recited, named,
spelled out — is what carries the claim that no single item
could carry alone.

The reference move is *The Chloride Indictment*: Bright,
Widal, Achard, Digne, Hoelzel, Kempner, Dahl, DASH-Sodium,
SSaSS, Yanomami. The names assemble. By the end of the recital
the reader has felt the consensus, not been told about it.

The list earns its length only when each item is load-bearing.
A list of three weak items is worse than the strongest one
stated alone.

## Rhythm — cadence, em-dash discipline, straight quotes

Sentences vary in length but cluster short. The aphoristic
register sits inside the prose: *"Anna is the floor. Fruit is
the broom. A broom is useful. But no one lives on a broom."*
Four short sentences, each closing a thought, the cumulative
movement the claim.

Long sentences are admitted when they earn the breath — a list
that needs to be kept together, a clause whose internal
structure is the point. They are not the default.

Em-dashes mark genuine asides or rhythmic breaks, not default
emphasis. A paragraph with three or more em-dashes is almost
always restructurable into cleaner syntax. Keep density below
roughly five em-dashes per 500 words.

Use straight quotes (`"` `'`). Curly quotes are a Word /
ChatGPT export artifact and are inconsistent with the rest of
the workspace.

## The closer — aphoristic, not restatement

End on a brief declarative — sometimes one line. The closer
distils the argument the prose has earned; it does not
summarise the argument the prose has just made.

Reference closers:

- *"Remove chloride. Life resumes."* (`The_Chloride_Indictment`)
- *"This is what the tradition calls food."* (`Apathya`)
- *"Soon is not a wish. It is a schedule."* (`My_Mothers_My_Sisters`)
- *"No one lives on a broom."* (`Yogis_Dont_Eat_Fruit`)

The prose has done the work. The closer lands what the work
established. Restatement ("In summary, the argument has shown
that...") flattens what the prose built. Cut it.

## What to delete on sight

These patterns appear in drafts. Strike them every time.

**Negative-contrast.** *"X is not Y. It is Z."* The signature
LLM rhetorical move — building a positive claim by first
denying an alternative and then asserting the real thing. State
the positive directly. Variants:

- "X is not Y; it is Z." / "X is not Y — it is Z."
- "X is not Y, but Z."
- "Not merely X, but Y." / "Not just X, but Y."
- "Not A. Not B. Not C. But D." (triple-fragment hammer)

If the dismissed alternative carries information (a misreading
the piece is correcting), absorb it as a trailing concession:
*"X is Z, not Y"* — sparingly, never as default.

**Filler phrases.**

- "it is worth noting," "it is important to note,"
  "it should be remembered"
- "notably," "crucially," "indeed," "moreover," "furthermore"
  (as paragraph openers)
- "truly," "fundamentally," "literally" (in non-literal
  contexts)
- "delves into," "unpacks," "navigates the complexities of,"
  "in the realm of," "rich tapestry,"
  "at the intersection of"
- "the fact that..." (almost always compressible)

**Meta-prose.** No announcing the structure of the piece. No
opening with "this essay does X." See *Begin with the substance*
above.

**Stage-setting.** "Notice that...", "Observe how...",
"Note that..." Do not cue the reader to attend; let the
sentence merit attention on its own.

## When the prose feels off

The same diagnostic discipline `skills/beauty.md` applies to
code, applied here to prose. The catalogue:

- A sentence the reader has to re-read — the structure is not
  yet right.
- A claim that needs three qualifiers to be defensible — either
  the qualifiers belong inside the claim, or the claim is
  smaller than written.
- A passive construction hiding the actor — name the actor.
- Repetition where one good word would do — find the word.
- A citation patched in mid-sentence — restructure so the
  citation lands where it belongs.
- A paragraph of em-dashes — the dashes are doing what syntax
  should be doing.
- A paragraph that paraphrases a primary source you have at
  hand — bring the source forward, frame it, let it speak.
- An opening that introduces what the piece will do — cut it;
  start with the substance.
- A closing that summarises what the piece just did — cut it;
  let the closer be the aphoristic distillate.

When the prose feels off, slow down and find the structure
that makes it right. That structure is the one the prose was
missing.
