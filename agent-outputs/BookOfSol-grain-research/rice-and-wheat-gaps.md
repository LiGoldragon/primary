## Conventions (identical to the parent document)

- **[E1] Strong** — multiple independent human RCTs, a systematic review/
  meta-analysis of human trials, or a structural/genetic result replicated
  across labs.
- **[E2] Moderate** — a single well-designed human trial, a large prospective
  cohort, or a well-replicated in-planta molecular result.
- **[E3] Weak** — animal or in vitro only, or human data with small n, poor
  blinding, or industry sponsorship.
- **[E4] Thin/contested** — mechanistic plausibility with little or no direct
  evidence at realistic exposure, or evidence that fails to replicate.

**(v)** = fetched and read in this session. **(r)** = recalled, not re-fetched.
Where a publisher blocked full text, the citation is explicitly marked
**record-level** and no abstract is paraphrased as though it were the paper.

### DISCONFIRMING / DEFLATING

Payne's original design was **correlational across varieties of related
pedigree**, not experimental. Varieties differing at Glu-D1 also differ at
thousands of other loci, in protein content, and in agronomic background. The
causal claim rests entirely on the deletion, near-isogenic and transgenic work
below — not on Payne. Payne's own framing (a *score*, i.e. a breeding selection
index) is more modest than the mechanistic claim it is now routinely used to
support.

### OBSERVATION — the decisive reciprocal experiment [E2, in planta, single lab]

This is the strongest single piece of evidence for the Dx5 cysteine hypothesis
and it is very recent.

**Wei B, Zhang L, Fan R, Zhang Q, Sun J, Yin C, Liu D, Liu X, Liu A, Jiao Y, Gao
C, Jia X, Wang X, Zhang A, Cao X, Wang D, Zhang X.** "Improving End-Use Quality
Through Introducing Cysteines Into the Central Repeat Domain of High-
Molecular-Weight Glutenin Subunit 1Dx2 in Bread Wheat." *Plant Biotechnol J*
2026;24(3):1792–1803. doi:10.1111/pbi.70451, PMC12946485 (v — full text read)

Design: site-directed mutagenesis of *1Dx2*, then transgenic complementation of
a **1Dx2 knockout line** (KN1Dx2⁻, in cv. KN199, HMW-GS composition 1Ax1,
1Bx7+1By9, 1Dy12) under a 1Bx endosperm promoter — i.e. all lines share one
genetic background and one expression cassette.

Reported results (all farinograph/baking, three replicates, one-way ANOVA with
Dunnett's test, p < 0.05):

| Line | Manipulation | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| TL1Dx2m1 | WT 1Dx2 **plus** a Cys at the position corresponding to 1Dx5 Cys118 | development time, stability time, farinograph quality number each **increased 1–2 fold** vs TL1Dx2; degree of softening down; GMP, SDS-sedimentation value and swelling index of glutenin all up |
| TL1Dx5m | WT 1Dx5 with **Cys118 removed** | all the same parameters "dramatically decreased" vs TL1Dx5; degree of softening up; GMP down |
| TL1Dx2m2 | Cys added near the **C-terminus** of the central repeat domain | larger effect than m1 |
| TL1Dx2m1/2 | both | additive |

Loaf volume (100 g flour, three replicates):
KN1Dx2⁻ 688.75 ± 30.10 cm³ < TL1Dx2 790.00 ± 18.26 < TL1Dx2m1 897.50 ± 9.57 <
TL1Dx2m2 932.50 ± 17.08 < TL1Dx2m1/2 995.00 ± 10.00 cm³.

Total sulfur and **disulfide bond content** were significantly higher in the
Cys-added lines, with free thiol unchanged — i.e. the added cysteines went into
crosslinks, not into free SH. Field trials found no penalty on key agronomic
traits.

The paper also states the sequence fact directly: **1Dx2 and 1Dx5 share 98.93%
protein sequence identity**, the crucial difference being the Cys residue at
**position 118** near the N-terminal end of the central repeat domain of 1Dx5.
The primary sequence references it cites are **Anderson OD, Greene FC, Yip RE,
Halford NG, Shewry PR, Malpica-Romero JM.** "Nucleotide sequences of the two
high-molecular-weight glutenin genes from the D-genome of a hexaploid bread
wheat, *Triticum aestivum* L. cv Cheyenne." *Nucleic Acids Res*
1989;17(1):461–462. doi:10.1093/nar/17.1.461 (v — record; full text not
retrieved) and Lawrence et al. 1987 above.

### Structural background [E1 for the general architecture]

Li et al. 2020 (PMC7795185, v) states, with citations to Anderson & Greene 1989
(*Theor Appl Genet* 77:689–700, doi:10.1007/BF00261246) and Anderson et al. 1989
(*NAR* 17:461–462):

- "most x-type subunits contain **four cysteines, three in the N-terminal domain
  and one in the C-terminal domain**"
- "The **1Dx5 subunit contains an additional cysteine at the beginning of the
  repetitive domain**"
- "A typical y-type subunit contains **seven cysteines: five in N-terminal
  domains, one in the central repetitive domain, and one in the C-terminal
  domain**"

Shewry PR, Halford NG, Belton PS, Tatham AS. "The structure and properties of
gluten: an elastic protein from wheat grain." *Phil Trans R Soc Lond B*
2002;357(1418):133–142. doi:10.1098/rstb.2001.1024, PMC1692935 — **abstract-level
only**; Europe PMC has no full text and the PMC landing page returned only
abstract and references. From the abstract (v): HMW subunits "are present in HMM
polymers that are stabilized by disulphide bonds and are considered to form the
'elastic backbone' of gluten. However, the glutamine-rich repetitive sequences
that comprise the central parts of the HMM subunits also form extensive arrays of
interchain hydrogen bonds that may contribute to the elastic properties via a
'loop and train' mechanism." **Note that this is two mechanisms, not one** — the
disulphide/polymer-size account and the hydrogen-bond loop-and-train account are
complementary and their relative weight is not settled.

### DISCONFIRMING — an extra cysteine is not automatically good [E2]

Two independent results cut against a naive "more cysteine → stronger dough"
rule:

1. **Gao X, Zhang Q, Newberry MP, Chalmers KJ, Mather DE.** "A cysteine in the
   repetitive domain of a high-molecular-weight glutenin subunit interferes with
   the mixing properties of wheat dough." *Amino Acids* 2013;44(4):1061–1071.
   doi:10.1007/s00726-012-1441-5 (v — abstract read). Subunit **Bx7.1** carries a
   cysteine in its repetitive domain. Site-directed mutagenesis replaced it with
   tyrosine; both wild-type and mutant subunits were expressed in *E. coli*,
   purified, and incorporated into flour. The extra cysteine **worsened** mixing
   properties, apparently by forming an intra-subunit crosslink so that Bx7.1
   "act[s] as a chain terminator."
2. Consistent with this, Wei et al. 2026 explicitly report that their own C-
   terminal-side insertion (m2) outperformed the N-terminal-side Cys118-analogue
   insertion (m1), and that they had *expected* a repetitive-domain cysteine to
   be harmful on the basis of Gao et al.

**The correct generalisation is therefore positional, not quantitative: it is
where the cysteine sits, and whether it can reach a partner on another chain,
that determines whether it extends or terminates the polymer.**

Supporting the same positional logic from the other direction:

- **Lindsay MP, Tamás L, Appels R, Skerritt JH.** "Direct evidence that the
  number and location of cysteine residues affect glutenin polymer structure."
  *J Cereal Sci* 2000;31(3):321–333. doi:10.1006/jcrs.2000.0309 (v — record; no
  abstract in Europe PMC)
- **Pirozi MR, Margiotta B, Lafiandra D, MacRitchie F.** "Composition of
  polymeric proteins and bread-making quality of wheat lines with allelic HMW-GS
  differing in number of cysteines." *J Cereal Sci* 2008;48(1):117–122.
  doi:10.1016/j.jcs.2007.08.011 (v — abstract read). Avocet, Halberd and Fiorello
  near-isogenic/biotype sets: Bx7 (four cysteines) vs Bx20 (two cysteines).
  Bx7-carrying lines had **higher unextractable polymeric protein, higher dough
  strength and larger loaves**; Bx20 lines had more total polymeric protein but a
  marked decrease in UPP, greater extensibility, shorter mixing development time
  and smaller loaves. This is a *Glu-B1* result, not *Glu-D1*, but it isolates
  cysteine number with the same logic and in the same direction as Dx5.

### VERDICT for §1.2

- That Glu-D1 allelic variation causes large differences in dough strength:
  **[E1]**, replicated across deletion lines, biotypes, NILs, transgenics and
  breeding populations on four continents.
- That **the specific Cys118 residue of 1Dx5** is a major part of the
  cause: **[E2]** — one reciprocal gain-and-loss-of-function transgenic study in
  a single background from a single group (Wei et al. 2026), consistent with
  30 years of correlational cysteine-counting. It has not yet been independently
  replicated.
- That the effect works through **larger disulphide-linked polymers**: **[E2]**;
  measured as GMP/UPP, with the hydrogen-bonded loop-and-train contribution
  unquantified.
- That cysteine number alone predicts dough quality: **FALSE [E2 negative]**
  (Gao et al. 2013).

## 1.3 What *Aegilops tauschii* actually contributed — and a correction

### OBSERVATION [E2, single large study, well designed]

**Delorean E, Gao L, Cervantes Lopez JF, Open Wild Wheat Consortium, Wulff BBH,
Ibba MI, Poland J.** "High molecular weight glutenin gene diversity in *Aegilops
tauschii* demonstrates unique origin of superior wheat quality." *Commun Biol*
2021;4:1242. doi:10.1038/s42003-021-02563-7, PMC8560932, PMID 34725451
(v — full text read)

Design: Illumina 150 bp paired-end short reads, >7× coverage, for **273
*Ae. tauschii* accessions (234 unique)**, aligned to the AL8/78 reference;
variants extracted at annotated *Glu-D1x* (chr1D:419,306,988–419,309,556) and
*Glu-D1y* (chr1D:419,364,015–419,365,995). Bread wheat comparators: CDC Stanley
and CDC Landmark (5+10), Chinese Spring and LongReach Lancer (2+12). SDS-PAGE
performed on 72 unique accessions.

Results:

- **308 variants** at *Glu-D1*; **32 x-subunit and 33 y-subunit haplotypes**;
  **45 complete-locus haplotypes** — against **two** alleles in the great
  majority of world bread wheat.
- x and y haplotypes were "almost exclusively associated with each other,"
  confirming very limited recombination between the two tightly linked genes.
- Including 2.5 kb flanking sequence added **no** further haplotypes.
- Gene-level phylogeny recovered three major clades and 15 subclades: eight
  exclusively Lineage 2, five Lineage 1, one Lineage 3.
- **The wheat 2+12 allele matched an Ae. tauschii Lineage 2 haplotype exactly
  (Dx1a+Dy1a, same SDS-PAGE mobility).** Lineage 2 (*Ae. tauschii* ssp.
  *strangulata*) is the recognised D-subgenome donor.
- **The wheat 5+10 allele clustered inside the Lineage 3 subclade.** Lineage-3-
  specific *k*-mer bins from Gaurav et al. co-localised with the 5+10 allele
  across 11 sequenced bread wheat genomes. Authors' words: "the superior quality
  wheat allele *5 + 10* allele originated in **Lineage 3**, a recently
  characterized lineage of *Ae. tauschii*, showing a unique origin of this
  important allele."
- **Honest negative reported by the authors:** "the exact wheat *5 + 10*
  haplotype was **not detected** in this panel," and **no 5+10 SDS-PAGE
  mobilities were observed at all**. The closest was Lineage 3 haplotype
  Dx7a+Dy7a, differing by **eight variants**. They note the panel contains only
  **five** Lineage 3 accessions and that earlier SDS-PAGE reports of 5+10-mobility
  *Ae. tauschii* (Williams et al.) did not report accession identities.
- Counterintuitive finding the authors flag against their own expectation: 2+12
  and 5+10 "clustered relatively closely within major-clade III" — the two alleles
  that divide world bread-making quality are **molecularly similar**, and
  *Ae. tauschii* Lineage 1 carries *Glu-D1* alleles "very unlike anything seen in
  bread wheat."

### CORRECTION to the parent document

The parent report says the D genome came from *Ae. tauschii* and that "*Glu-D1*
alleles (notably the Dx5+Dy10 subunit pair) are the strongest single determinant
of dough strength." Both halves survive. But the compressed picture — one
hybridisation, one donor, one package — does not. The primary genomics says:

- The **D subgenome** came predominantly from **Lineage 2**, which carries 2+12.
- The **5+10 allele that makes bread wheat superior** traces to **Lineage 3**, a
  lineage only recently recognised, i.e. it entered the hexaploid gene pool by a
  **separate contribution**, not as part of the single founding *Ae. tauschii*
  genome.
- So the elegant "one polyploidisation event gave wheat both its best and its
  worst property" framing is **too tidy on the quality side**. The 33-mer arrived
  with the D genome; the elite glutenin allele appears to have arrived by a
  distinct route.

## 1.4 The 33-mer: who measured it, in how many accessions, and the exception

### The peptide itself [E1]

The 33-mer — sequence **LQLQPFPQPQLPYPQPQLPYPQPQLPYPQPQPF**, α2-gliadin
**positions 56–88** — was identified as protease-resistant and immunodominant by
Shan L, Molberg Ø, Parrot I, Hausch F, Filiz F, Gray GM, Sollid LM, Khosla C,
*Science* 2002;297(5590):2275–2279 (r — not re-fetched here; the exact sequence
and residue numbering above are quoted from Schalk et al. 2017 (v), which I did
read in full).

**Note a numbering discrepancy with the parent document**, which gives the
33-mer as α2-gliadin **p57–89**. Schalk et al. give **56–88**. Both conventions
appear in the literature depending on whether the signal peptide is counted.
This is a citation-hygiene point, not a substantive one.

### Sequence-level evidence: genome-of-origin of α-gliadin epitopes [E1]

**van Herpen TWJM, Goryunova SV, van der Schoot J, Mitreva M, Salentijn E, Vorst
O, Schenk MF, van Veelen PA, Koning F, van Soest LJM, Vosman B, Bosch D, Hamer
RJ, Gilissen LJWJ, Smulders MJM.** "Alpha-gliadin genes from the A, B, and D
genomes of wheat contain different sets of celiac disease epitopes." *BMC
Genomics* 2006;7:1. doi:10.1186/1471-2164-7-1, PMC1368968 (v — full text read)

- **230 distinct α-gliadin gene sequences** from diploid wheat species
  representing the ancestral A, B and D genomes.
- **87% carried an internal stop codon** — a fact routinely omitted when
  α-gliadin gene counts are quoted.
- Sequences separated cleanly by genome of origin on sequence similarity,
  polyglutamine repeat length, and epitope content.
- ***T. monococcum* (A) and chromosome 6A of bread wheat: "almost invariably
  contained epitope glia-α9 and glia-α20, but never the intact epitopes glia-α
  and glia-α2."**
- ***T. speltoides* (B) and chromosome 6B: a number of sequences contained none
  of the four screened T-cell epitopes.**
- ***T. tauschii* (D) and chromosome 6D: "found to contain all of these T cell
  epitopes in variable combinations per gene."**
- The differences arose mainly from **point mutations**, and the substitutions
  were genome-specific.

### T-cell-level evidence in the ancestors [E2, record-level citation]

**Molberg Ø, Uhlen AK, Jensen T, Flaete NS, Fleckenstein B, Arentz-Hansen H,
Raki M, Lundin KEA, Sollid LM.** "Mapping of gluten T-cell epitopes in the bread
wheat ancestors: implications for celiac disease." *Gastroenterology*
2005;128(2):393–401. doi:10.1053/j.gastro.2004.11.003, PMID 15685550 —
**record-level only**; paywalled, not read here. As quoted inside Schalk et al.
2017 (v): a minority of einkorn cultivars and **three of ten durum wheat
cultivars activated DQ2.5-glia-α1b- and DQ2.5-glia-α2-specific T-cell clones.**
That is: *absence of the intact 33-mer does not mean absence of DQ2.5-restricted
reactivity.*

### Quantitative measurement — the strongest single dataset [E2]

**Schalk K, Lang C, Wieser H, Koehler P, Scherf KA.** "Quantitation of the
immunodominant 33-mer peptide from α-gliadin in wheat flours by liquid
chromatography tandem mass spectrometry." *Sci Rep* 2017;7:45092.
doi:10.1038/srep45092, PMC5361186, PMID 28327674 (v — full text read)

Method: stable isotope dilution assay (¹³C/¹⁵N-labelled 33-mer internal
standard) with targeted LC-MS/MS. **57 samples** from around the world.

| Group | n | 33-mer |
|---|---|---|
| Hexaploid common wheat, modern | 23 | detected in **all** |
| Hexaploid common wheat, old | 15 | detected in **all** |
| Spelt (hexaploid) | 2 | detected in both |
| **Range across all hexaploids** | | **91–603 µg/g flour** |
| Durum (AABB) | 2 | **< LOD** |
| Emmer (AABB) | 2 | **< LOD** |
| Einkorn (AA) | 2 | **< LOD** |

Method controls reported: reduction/alkylation (TCEP + iodoacetamide) did not
change the measured 33-mer (6.3 ± 0.4 vs 6.2 ± 0.4 mg/g gliadin, p = 0.705), so
disulphide entrapment is not hiding peptide. Harvest year significantly affected
33-mer content within the same cultivar (all pairwise year comparisons
p ≤ 0.034), i.e. **environment moves the number substantially**.

Concordant prior reports cited by Schalk: Prandi B et al. *Anal Bioanal Chem*
2012;403:2909–2914 (33-mer absent from durum; used as a marker for common-wheat
adulteration of durum) (v — record); van den Broeck HC et al. *J Chromatogr A*
2015;1391:60–71 (33-mer not detected in the one durum cultivar tested)
(v — record).

**Critical limitation of Schalk et al.:** the negative species are represented by
**two cultivars each**. n = 2 is not a survey.

### THE EXCEPTION — a direct, recent contradiction [E2 vs E2, unresolved]

**Norwig MC, Geisslitz S, Scherf KA.** "Comparative Label-Free Proteomics Study
on Celiac Disease-Active Epitopes in Common Wheat, Spelt, Durum Wheat, Emmer, and
Einkorn." *J Agric Food Chem* 2024;72(26):15040–15052.
doi:10.1021/acs.jafc.4c02657, PMC11228976 (v — full text read)

Design: untargeted LC-MS/MS peptidomics, trypsin + chymotrypsin hydrolysis,
**eight cultivars of each of the five species (40 samples)**, same senior author
(Scherf) as Schalk 2017. Data in PRIDE, PXD050917.

Headline compositional results:
- **494** distinct peptides carrying a CD-active epitope identified in total.
- Spelt carried the **most** different epitope-bearing peptides (193 ± 12, mean ±
  SD across its eight cultivars).
- Einkorn carried the **fewest** (63 ± 4) — but **higher amounts of certain
  peptides** than the other species.

The exception, in the authors' own words:

> "EPep 4 of row cluster E (**33-mer**) is of particular interest as it contains
> all three epitopes (PYY, PFY, and PLQ) and was present **not only in hexaploid
> samples but also in di- and tetraploid ones**."

and

> "The identification of EPeps with the α-gliadin epitopes PYY, PLQ, or QGS
> including the 33-mer in diploid or tetraploid samples **is not in concordance
> with the genetic analyses** because these species lack the D subgenome."

Their own quality assessment: identification confidence for the 33-mer was
"somewhat less certain" in einkorn K02 and TER, in **all five emmer samples**
(EM1, EM5, EM9, OSI, TEU) and durum WIN; but "comparable to that in hexaploid
samples" in einkorn K06 and TIF and durum ELS and LOG. After manual verification
they "judged the identification of the 33-mer to be confident" and called for
combined genomic + proteomic work on larger di-/tetraploid panels.

Limitations they state, which are load-bearing here:
- Trypsin alone was insufficient (few R/K next to epitopes); trypsin +
  chymotrypsin covered 91% of epitope-bearing proteins but with more missed
  cleavages and incomplete coverage.
- **The UniProtKB *Triticum* database (2021) was unbalanced**: >50% of entries
  from *T. aestivum*, <5% each from emmer and einkorn. A more balanced database
  "may lead to more peptide identifications, especially in emmer or einkorn, and
  could shift the ratio of EPeps between the species."
- Digestion was **not** intended to mimic human gastrointestinal proteolysis.

An additional discordant data point from the same discussion: the epitope PYY has
been detected in **tritordeum** (AABBH^chH^ch, durum × wild barley — no D genome)
by Vaquero L et al., *J Sci Food Agric* 2018;98:2201–2209.
doi:10.1002/jsfa.8705 (v — record via Norwig reference list); and PLQ has been
reported in durum by Pilolli R et al., *Nutrients* 2019;11:2321.
doi:10.3390/nu11102321 (v — record via same).

### HYPOTHESES (not observations)

1. The Norwig detections are real low-abundance 33-mer arising from
   non-D-genome α-gliadin loci by convergent point mutation. Testable by paired
   genome + proteome on the same accessions; **not yet done**.
2. The Norwig detections are cross-contamination during milling/handling or
   carryover in the LC-MS system. The authors do not report a contamination
   control experiment; **untested**.
3. Database-driven false assignment. Partly argued against by manual spectral
   verification, but the unbalanced database is a stated weakness.

### UNKNOWN

- Whether einkorn/emmer/durum contain the intact 33-mer at any level, and if so
  at what concentration relative to the 91–603 µg/g in hexaploids. Targeted SIDA
  (Schalk's own method) applied to a *large* di-/tetraploid panel would settle
  it; that experiment does not exist.
- Whether any of this has clinical consequence. **No** human challenge trial has
  compared einkorn/emmer/durum with common wheat in celiac patients with the
  33-mer as the declared variable.

# GAP 2 — Rice Aerenchyma, the ROL Barrier, and Arsenic

## 2.1 Constitutive versus inducible aerenchyma — two different regulatory systems

### OBSERVATION [E2, replicated within one strong group]

Rice forms lysigenous aerenchyma (gas space created by programmed death of
cortical cells) **both** constitutively under aerobic conditions **and**
inducibly under oxygen deficiency, and the two are under **different hormonal
control**.

**Yamauchi T, Tanaka A, Inahashi H, Nishizawa NK, Tsutsumi N, Inukai Y,
Nakazono M.** "Fine control of aerenchyma and lateral root development through
AUX/IAA- and ARF-dependent auxin signaling." *Proc Natl Acad Sci USA*
2019;116(41):20770–20775. doi:10.1073/pnas.1907181116, PMC6789968 (v — full text
read)

- Statement of the dichotomy, verbatim: "In rice, root aerenchyma constitutively
  forms under aerobic conditions and is further induced under oxygen deficiency.
  Although **ethylene** is involved in inducible aerenchyma formation, the
  factors involved in **constitutive** aerenchyma formation remain unclear."
- Constitutive aerenchyma (CA) is **reduced in the *iaa13* mutant** (dominant-
  negative mutated AUX/IAA), which also has fewer lateral roots.
- **ARF19** identified as an IAA13 interactor; **LBD1-8** (lateral organ boundary
  domain protein) identified as an ARF19 target.
- *IAA13*, *ARF19* and *LBD1-8* were all highly expressed in root **cortex** and
  lateral root primordia.
- Restoring *LBD1-8* expression in the *iaa13* background **recovered aerenchyma
  formation** and partly recovered lateral roots.
- An auxin transport inhibitor suppressed CA and lateral roots; natural auxin
  restored CA in the presence of the inhibitor.
- CA initiation **lagged** lateral root initiation — the two share the pathway but
  are separated in developmental time.

Inducible aerenchyma has a separate, also well-mapped route:

- Ethylene-dependence and its species differences: Yamauchi T, Tanaka A, Mori H,
  Takamure I, Kato K, Nakazono M. "Ethylene-dependent aerenchyma formation in
  adventitious roots is regulated differently in rice and maize." *Plant Cell
  Environ* 2016;39(10):2145–2157. doi:10.1111/pce.12766, PMID 27169562
  (v — record)
- ROS via RBOH: Li J, Ishii T, Yoshioka M, Hino Y, Nomoto M, Tada Y, Yoshioka H,
  Takahashi H, Yamauchi T, Nakazono M. "CDPK5 and CDPK13 play key roles in
  acclimation to low oxygen through the control of RBOH-mediated ROS production
  in rice." *Plant Physiol* 2024;197(1):kiae293. doi:10.1093/plphys/kiae293,
  PMC11663579 (v — record)
- Calcium: Jingxia L, Nakazono M, Yamauchi T. "Candidates for regulating cytosolic
  Ca²⁺ influx during inducible aerenchyma formation under low-oxygen conditions."
  *Plant Cell Rep* 2025;44:116. doi:10.1007/s00299-025-03497-8, PMC12062121
  (v — record)
- Auxin also participates in the *inducible* route: Yamauchi T, Tanaka A,
  Tsutsumi N, Inukai Y, Nakazono M. *Plants* 2020;9(5):610.
  doi:10.3390/plants9050610, PMC7284992 (v — record)

### Quantitative anatomy [E2]

From Colmer 2003 (below), root porosity (gas volume as % of tissue volume) in
adventitious roots across **12 rice genotypes** (7 upland, 3 paddy, 2 deep-water):

| Growth condition | Root porosity |
|---|---|
| Aerated nutrient solution | **19.7–25.9%** |
| Stagnant deoxygenated solution (0.1% agar) | **28.9–40.8%** |

Porosity rose in **all 12** genotypes (P < 0.001), by 1.2–1.8-fold. So the
constitutive component is roughly 20–26% porosity and stagnation adds a further
~10–15 percentage points.

## 2.2 The ROL barrier — inducible, suberin-based, and now genetically dissected

### OBSERVATION — the barrier is INDUCED, not constitutive [E1]

**Colmer TD.** "Aerenchyma and an inducible barrier to radial oxygen loss
facilitate root aeration in upland, paddy and deep-water rice (*Oryza sativa*
L.)." *Ann Bot* 2003;91(2):301–309. doi:10.1093/aob/mcf114, PMC4795684 (v — full
text read)

Design: 12 genotypes; 10 d in aerated solution, then 25 d in either aerated or
stagnant deoxygenated solution; one intact root on each of three plants per
treatment per genotype.

- **Aerated-grown roots:** ROL was *lowest near the apex* and *increased* toward
  the base — in 11 of 12 genotypes. There is no barrier.
- **Stagnant-grown roots:** ROL was *highest just behind the apex* and fell to
  very low or zero at basal positions — in 10 of 12 genotypes. The barrier is
  present.
- ROL at 5 mm behind the apex, stagnant vs aerated: upland types **6–85-fold**
  higher; paddy types **3–39-fold**; deep-water types **258–266-fold**.
- Distance from the apex at which ROL became very low, in stagnant-grown roots:
  ≥50 mm (one deep-water type), ≥40 mm (five upland), ≥30 mm (three paddy/deep-
  water), ≥20 mm (one paddy).
- Adventitious root extension rate did not differ between aerated and stagnant
  treatments in nine of 12 genotypes.

Replication and time-course: **Shiono K, Ogawa S, Yamazaki S, Isoda H, Fujimura
T, Nakazono M, Colmer TD.** "Contrasting dynamics of radial O₂-loss barrier
induction and aerenchyma formation in rice roots of two lengths." *Ann Bot*
2011;107(1):89–99. doi:10.1093/aob/mcq221, PMC3002478 (v — record).

### OBSERVATION — the anatomical and chemical basis is suberin in the exodermis [E1]

**Kotula L, Ranathunge K, Schreiber L, Steudle E.** "Functional and chemical
comparison of apoplastic barriers to radial oxygen loss in roots of rice (*Oryza
sativa* L.) grown in aerated or deoxygenated solution." *J Exp Bot*
2009;60(7):2155–2167. doi:10.1093/jxb/erp089, PMID 19443620 (v — abstract read)

- Stagnant growth raised adventitious root porosity **~20%** and drove ROL to
  **zero at 40 mm** from the apex; aerated-grown roots showed *maximum* ROL at
  30 mm.
- Histochemistry: **early development of Casparian bands and suberin lamellae in
  the exodermis, plus lignified sclerenchyma**, in deoxygenated-grown roots.
- Biochemistry: absolute suberin and lignin contents in the outer part of the
  root (OPR) were higher under deoxygenation, and both increased basipetally.
- **Authors' explicit reservation:** "the relative contribution of suberin and
  lignin in the formation of a tight barrier is unclear."

**Shiono K, Yamauchi T, Yamazaki S, Mohanty B, Malik AI, Nagamura Y, Nishizawa
NK, Tsutsumi N, Colmer TD, Nakazono M.** "Microarray analysis of laser-
microdissected tissues indicates the biosynthesis of suberin in the outer part of
roots during formation of a barrier to radial oxygen loss in rice (*Oryza
sativa*)." *J Exp Bot* 2014;65(17):4795–4806. doi:10.1093/jxb/eru235, PMID
24913626 (v — abstract read)

- Laser microdissection of OPR tissue + microarray: **128 genes** significantly
  regulated during barrier formation.
- **Suberin biosynthesis genes strongly upregulated; lignin biosynthesis genes
  were not.**
- Promoter analysis implicated WRKY, AP2/ERF, NAC, bZIP, MYB, CBT/DREB and MADS
  cis-elements; WRKY, AP2 and MYB transcription factor genes were themselves
  upregulated.
- Conclusion: **suberin is the major constituent** of the ROL barrier. This
  resolves, in one direction, the ambiguity Kotula et al. left open.

### OBSERVATION — a gene that controls barrier tightness [E2, new]

**Jiménez JC, Noorrohmah S, Suresh K, Zeisler-Diehl VV, Peralta Ogorek LL, Herzog
M, Pellegrini E, Nagai K, Ashikari M, Takahashi H, Pedersen O, Schreiber L,
Nakazono M.** "Leaf Gas Film 1 promotes glycerol ester accumulation and formation
of a tight root barrier to radial O₂ loss in rice." *Plant Physiol*
2024;196(4):2437–2449. doi:10.1093/plphys/kiae458, PMC11637764 (v — abstract and
introduction read)

- Compared the rice mutant ***drp7* (dripping wet leaf 7)**, which lacks
  functional *LGF1*, its wild type, and an *LGF1* overexpression line.
- ***drp7* had a weak ROL barrier**; reintroducing functional *LGF1* **fully
  restored** tight barrier function.
- The tight barrier correlated with **increased glycerol ester levels in
  exodermal cells**, and **not** with differences in total root suberisation or
  lignification.

This is a genuinely surprising refinement: the *amount* of suberin is not the
control variable for barrier tightness — a specific monomer class is.

Other induction signals now mapped:
- Nitrate: Shiono K, Ejiri M, Sawazaki Y, Egishi Y, Tsunoda T. "Low nitrate under
  waterlogging triggers exodermal suberization to form a barrier to radial oxygen
  loss in rice roots." *Plant Physiol* 2024;196(1):551–563.
  doi:10.1093/plphys/kiae278, PMID 38761404 (v — record)
- Sulfide: Armstrong J, Armstrong W. "Rice: sulfide-induced barriers to root
  radial oxygen loss, Fe²⁺ and water uptake, and lateral root emergence." *Ann
  Bot* 2005;96(4):625–638. doi:10.1093/aob/mci215, PMC4247030 (v — record)
- ABA (in barley, cross-species): Shiono K, Matsuura H. *Ann Bot*
  2024;133(7):931–940. doi:10.1093/aob/mcae010, PMC11089260 (v — record)
- **Silicon feeds back into the barrier**: Fleck AT, Nye T, Repenning C, Stahl F,
  Zahn M, Schenk MK. "Silicon enhances suberization and lignification in roots of
  rice (*Oryza sativa*)." *J Exp Bot* 2011;62(6):2001–2011.
  doi:10.1093/jxb/erq392, PMC3060683 (v — record); and Fleck AT et al., *PLoS One*
  2015;10(9):e0138555. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0138555, PMC4575055 (v — record).
  This closes a loop with Part I §9 of the parent document that the parent did
  not draw.

## 2.3 How this physiology governs arsenic — and the direction reverses twice

### Step 1: flooding creates arsenite [E1]

**Takahashi Y, Minamikawa R, Hattori KH, Kurishima K, Kihou N, Yuita K.**
"Arsenic Behavior in Paddy Fields during the Cycle of Flooded and Non-flooded
Periods." *Environ Sci Technol* 2004;38(4):1038–1044. doi:10.1021/es034383n
(v — abstract read). Field paddy in Japan, soil/soil-water/groundwater to 2 m
depth, **in situ XANES speciation** plus sequential extraction. Fe (hydr)oxide
hosts soil As during the non-flooded period; on flooding, As is "quickly released
from soil to water because of **reductive dissolution of the Fe (hydr)oxide phase
and reduction of As from As(V) to As(III)**." High As/Fe ratios in soil water
during flooding show the As reduction itself enhances dissolution, over and above
Fe dissolution.

**Xu XY, McGrath SP, Meharg AA, Zhao FJ.** "Growing Rice Aerobically Markedly
Decreases Arsenic Accumulation." *Environ Sci Technol* 2008;42(15):5574–5579.
doi:10.1021/es800324u (v — abstract read). Greenhouse, ± 10 mg As/kg as arsenite
or arsenate:

- Soil-solution As **7–16× higher** flooded vs aerobic in the unamended control,
  **4–13×** higher in the +As treatments.
- Arsenite dominated flooded soil solution; **arsenate** dominated aerobic soil.
- **Grain As 10–15-fold higher** in flooded than aerobically grown rice.
- **Inorganic As specifically 2.6–2.9-fold higher** in flooded-treatment grain.
- As grain total As rose, the **proportion** of inorganic As fell and DMA rose.

**DISCONFIRMING NOTE — published critique:** van Geen A, Duxbury JM. "Comment on
'Growing Rice Aerobically Markedly Decreases Arsenic Accumulation.'" *Environ Sci
Technol* 2009;43(10):3971. doi:10.1021/es9002982 (v — record; full text not
retrieved). The aerobic-cultivation recommendation drew a formal comment; the
underlying redox chemistry is not in dispute, but the agronomic
recommendation was contested at the time. This is the same tension the parent
document records under the As/Cd tradeoff.

### Step 2: the silicon transporters carry it [E1]

**Ma JF, Yamaji N, Mitani N, Xu X-Y, Su Y-H, McGrath SP, Zhao F-J.** "Transporters
of arsenite in rice and their role in arsenic accumulation in rice grain." *PNAS*
2008;105(29):9931–9935. doi:10.1073/pnas.0802361105, PMC2481375 (v — full text
read this session; the parent document cited it but not the field numbers)

Mutants: *lsi1* in cv. Oochikara; **two independent *lsi2* alleles** — *lsi2-1*
in cv. T-65 and *lsi2-2* in cv. Koshihikari (gamma-ray mutagenesis). Also
Nipponbare and Dongjin.

Short-term hydroponics, 30 min at 2 µM arsenite:
- *lsi1*: As uptake **57% lower** than wild type (P < 0.01) — Lsi1 is the influx
  route.
- *lsi2*: **no significant difference** in 30-min uptake — consistent with Lsi2
  being an efflux transporter toward the stele, not an influx channel.

**THE FIELD EXPERIMENT** (the item the parent document lacked): Experimental Farm
of Okayama University, 2007; background soil total As **5.3 mg/kg** (i.e. an
ordinary, not contaminated, soil); plots 0.7 × 0.7 m, 36 seedlings per plot,
three replicates per line.

| Tissue | *lsi2-1* as % of WT | *lsi2-2* as % of WT |
|---|---|---|
| Straw | **13%** (P < 0.01) | **19%** (P < 0.01) |
| **Grain** | **63%** (P < 0.01) | **51%** (P < 0.01) |

*lsi1* showed **no significant difference** in grain As from its wild type in the
field.

Silicon competition: adding silicic acid significantly decreased shoot As in wild
type but **not** in *lsi2* mutants — direct evidence of a shared pathway.

Authors' conclusion, verbatim: "High expression of *Lsi1* and *Lsi2* in rice
leads to a large accumulation of silicon, which benefits yield production but
unfortunately also enhances As accumulation in rice shoots and grain."

**Two things the parent document should have carried and did not:**

1. **The grain effect is much smaller than the straw effect.** Knocking out Lsi2
   cut straw As by 81–87% but grain As by only 37–49%. Node-level and phloem
   processes, not root influx, dominate the last step into the grain. (Consistent
   with the parent's own §10.4 on OsABCC1 and node biology.)
2. **A stated methodological limitation:** the authors "failed to detect transport
   activity" for Lsi2 in oocytes, yeast and *E. coli*, attributing this to
   arsenite toxicity or thiol binding, and relied on mutant phenotypes instead.
   The Lsi2-as-arsenite-efflux-transporter claim is therefore **genetic, not
   biochemical**.
3. The authors also recommend "ensuring sufficient silicon availability in the
   soil is likely to suppress As accumulation" — which reads awkwardly beside
   their central finding, and is a competition argument (Si outcompeting As for
   the same carriers), not a contradiction.

### Step 3: the counter-current — ROL *reduces* arsenic uptake [E2]

This is the part that makes the "aerenchyma → arsenic" story non-linear, and it
was absent from the parent document.

**Wu C, Ye Z, Li H, Wu S, Deng D, Zhu Y, Wong M.** "Do radial oxygen loss and
external aeration affect iron plaque formation and arsenic accumulation and
speciation in rice?" *J Exp Bot* 2012;63(8):2961–2970. doi:10.1093/jxb/ers017,
PMC3350917 (v — full text read)

Hydroponics, multiple rice genotypes, three aeration regimes (aerated, normal,
stagnant):

- **Significant correlations between ROL and Fe concentration in root iron
  plaque.**
- Fe plaque formation was greatest at the root tip and decreased basipetally —
  tracking the spatial ROL pattern.
- **Genotypes with higher ROL formed more iron plaque.**
- Iron plaque **sequestered As on the root surface** (arsenate roughly double
  arsenite), **decreasing As accumulation in both roots and shoots**.
- Arsenite was the dominant species in roots and shoots, 34–78% of total As
  depending on treatment and genotype.

Converging genotype-panel evidence:
- Mei XQ, Ye ZH, Wong MH. "The relationship of root porosity and radial oxygen
  loss on arsenic tolerance and uptake in rice grains and straw." *Environ Pollut*
  2009;157(8–9):2550–2557. doi:10.1016/j.envpol.2009.02.037 (v — record)
- Mei XQ, Wong MH, Yang Y, Dong HY, Qiu RL, Ye ZH. "The effects of radial oxygen
  loss on arsenic tolerance and uptake in rice and on its rhizosphere." *Environ
  Pollut* 2012;165:109–117. doi:10.1016/j.envpol.2012.02.018 (v — record)
- Li H, Ye ZH, Wei ZJ, Wong MH. "Root porosity and radial oxygen loss related to
  arsenic tolerance and uptake in wetland plants." *Environ Pollut*
  2011;159(1):30–37. doi:10.1016/j.envpol.2010.09.031 (v — record)

### SYNTHESIS — the honest causal chain, with its sign changes

**OBSERVED:**
1. Flooding → reducing soil → reductive dissolution of Fe (hydr)oxides and
   As(V)→As(III) → soil-solution As up **4–16×**, dominated by uncharged As(OH)₃
   [E1, field + greenhouse].
2. Constitutive aerenchyma (auxin/IAA13-ARF19-LBD1-8) plus stagnation-induced
   aerenchyma (ethylene, ROS, Ca²⁺) supplies O₂ down the root [E2].
3. An **inducible**, suberin-based exodermal barrier restricts *radial* O₂ loss,
   directing O₂ to the apex [E1]. Barrier tightness is controlled at least partly
   by glycerol esters via *LGF1* [E2].
4. Whatever O₂ does escape oxidises Fe²⁺ to **iron plaque** on the root surface,
   which **sorbs As and lowers uptake** [E2].
5. As(OH)₃ that gets past the plaque enters via **Lsi1** and is pumped stelewards
   by **Lsi2**; *lsi2* knockouts cut field grain As to 51–63% of wild type [E1].

**The sign of the aerenchyma/ROL effect is therefore opposite at two steps:**
aerenchyma is what lets the plant thrive in the reducing conditions that mobilise
As, but ROL *itself* forms iron plaque that *reduces* As uptake, and the ROL
*barrier* — the trait that makes flooded rooting efficient — suppresses that
protective plaque. Reporting "aerenchyma causes rice's arsenic problem" without
this is too simple.

**HYPOTHESIS (not established):** that breeding for high ROL / weaker ROL barrier
could lower grain As agronomically. The genotype correlations (Mei, Wu, Li) are
hydroponic and correlational; **no field trial manipulating the ROL barrier for
grain As exists**, and the *LGF1* line that would enable it was only published in
2024.

### UNKNOWN

- Relative contribution of suberin vs lignin to barrier *function* — Shiono 2014
  says suberin transcriptionally; Kotula 2009 declines to apportion; Jiménez 2024
  says total suberin is not the control variable at all.
- Whether constitutive aerenchyma has an arsenic phenotype in its own right. The
  *iaa13* / *lbd1-8* materials exist; nobody has measured their grain As.
- The quantitative split, in a real paddy, between root-surface plaque exclusion,
  Lsi1/Lsi2 influx, and node-level (OsABCC1) retention in setting final grain As.
  Ma 2008's straw-vs-grain discrepancy (87% vs 49% reduction) shows the split is
  large but does not resolve it.

# GAP 3 — Non-Celiac Gluten/Wheat Sensitivity

## 3.1 The diagnostic standard: what the Salerno Criteria actually specify

**Catassi C, Elli L, Bonaz B, Bouma G, Carroccio A, Castillejo G, Cellier C,
Cristofori F, de Magistris L, Dolinsek J, Dieterich W, Francavilla R,
Hadjivassiliou M, Holtmeier W, Körner U, Leffler DA, Lundin KE, Mazzarella G,
Mulder CJ, Pellegrini N, Rostami K, Sanders D, Skodje GI, Schuppan D, Ullrich R,
Volta U, Williams M, Zevallos VF, Zopf Y, Fasano A.** "Diagnosis of Non-Celiac
Gluten Sensitivity (NCGS): The Salerno Experts' Criteria." *Nutrients*
2015;7(6):4966–4977. doi:10.3390/nu7064966, PMC4488826 (v — full text read)

This is an **expert consensus document**, not a trial. What it specifies:

**Step 1 — response to gluten-free diet.** Six weeks on a GFD while the patient
records a modified Gastrointestinal Symptom Rating Scale weekly. The patient
nominates **one to three main symptoms**, scored 1–10 on a Numerical Rating Scale.
A "responder" shows **>30% reduction** in one to three main symptoms (or at least
one symptom improved with none worsened) for **at least 50% of the observation
time** (≥3 of 6 weekly evaluations). Failure to improve over six weeks
**excludes** NCGS.

**Step 2 — double-blind placebo-controlled crossover challenge.**
- Baseline diet strictly gluten-free, at celiac standard, for **≥4 weeks** before
  challenge, dietitian-assessed.
- **Gluten dose: 8 g/day.** Rationale given: "close to the average daily intake of
  gluten in Western countries (10–15 g) and easy to mix with the vehicle."
- **Structure: one-week challenge → one-week washout on strict GFD → crossover to
  the second one-week challenge.**
- Vehicle: **not gelatin capsules**; muesli bar, bread or muffin, with **cooked,
  homogeneously distributed** gluten; **FODMAP-free**; placebo indistinguishable
  in look, texture and taste and matched for fibre, carbohydrate, fat and if
  possible protein.
- **ATI specification:** "gluten preparations should be prepared/tested for ATI
  bioactivity to contain at least **0.3 g of ATIs / 8 g of gluten**, or gluten
  should be used with defined ATI content."
- **Positivity threshold: ≥30% variation** in the main symptoms between gluten and
  placebo.

**Two candid admissions in the document itself, which matter:**
- "The threshold of 30% increment in symptoms is **somewhat arbitrary and needs
  scientific validation**."
- "the gluten challenges **may need to be repeated to offset the strong nocebo
  effect** often seen in these patients."

Catassi's framing reviews: Catassi C, Bai JC, Bonaz B, et al. "Non-Celiac Gluten
sensitivity: the new frontier of gluten related disorders." *Nutrients*
2013;5(10):3839–3853. doi:10.3390/nu5103839, PMC3820047 (v — record); Catassi C,
Alaedini A, Bojarski C, et al. "The Overlapping Area of Non-Celiac Gluten
Sensitivity (NCGS) and Wheat-Sensitive Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): An
Update." *Nutrients* 2017;9(11):1268. doi:10.3390/nu9111268, PMC5707740
(v — record).

## 3.2 The trials, with designs, n, doses and washouts

All abstracts below were read in this session via Europe PMC (v); full texts were
not retrieved except where noted.

### Biesiekierski 2011 — the founding positive [E2, superseded by its own group]

Biesiekierski JR, Newnham ED, Irving PM, Barrett JS, Haines M, Doecke JD,
Shepherd SJ, Muir JG, Gibson PR. *Am J Gastroenterol* 2011;106(3):508–514.
doi:10.1038/ajg.2010.487, PMID 21224837 (v — abstract read)

- Design: DBPC rechallenge in IBS patients, celiac excluded, already
  symptomatically controlled on a GFD.
- **n = 34** completed per protocol (aged 29–59, 4 men). 56% carried HLA-DQ2
  and/or DQ8.
- **Dose: two bread slices + one muffin per day**, gluten or placebo, on a GFD
  background, **for up to 6 weeks**. (Commonly quoted as ~16 g/day.)
- **Result:** 13 of 19 (68%) on gluten reported symptoms not adequately
  controlled vs 6 of 15 (40%) on placebo, P = 0.0001 (GEE). VAS worse on gluten
  within 1 week for overall symptoms (P = 0.047), pain (0.016), bloating (0.031),
  stool consistency satisfaction (0.024), tiredness (0.001).
- **Every mechanistic marker was negative:** no anti-gliadin antibody induction,
  no change in faecal lactoferrin, celiac antibodies, hs-CRP, or intestinal
  permeability. No difference by DQ2/DQ8 status.
- Authors' own conclusion: "'Non-celiac gluten intolerance' **may** exist, but
  **no clues to the mechanism were elucidated**."
- **Design weakness that the 2013 study was built to fix: no control of background
  FODMAP intake.**

### Biesiekierski 2013 — the same group's reversal [E2]

Biesiekierski JR, Peters SL, Newnham ED, Rosella O, Muir JG, Gibson PR.
*Gastroenterology* 2013;145(2):320–328.e3. doi:10.1053/j.gastro.2013.04.051,
PMID 23648697 — **the parent document tagged this (r); it is now (v)**, abstract
read.

- Design: double-blind crossover, **n = 37** (aged 24–61, 6 men) with self-
  reported NCGS + Rome III IBS, celiac excluded.
- **Run-in: 2-week reduced-FODMAP diet for everyone.**
- Then randomised to **high gluten (16 g/day)**, **low gluten (2 g/day + 14 g whey
  protein/day)**, or **control (16 g whey protein/day)** for **1 week**, with
  **washout ≥2 weeks**.
- A second phase: 22 participants crossed over to gluten 16 g/d, whey 16 g/d, or
  no added protein for **3 days**.
- **Results:** symptoms improved consistently and significantly on the reduced-
  FODMAP diet in *all* participants, then **worsened to a similar degree whether
  the added protein was gluten or whey**. **Gluten-specific effects were observed
  in only 8% of participants.** No diet-specific change in any biomarker. In the
  3-day rechallenge, symptoms rose similarly across arms and **gluten-specific GI
  effects were not reproduced**. **An order effect was observed.**
- Authors: "we found **no evidence of specific or dose-dependent effects of
  gluten** in patients with NCGS placed on diets low in FODMAPs."

### Di Sabatino 2015 — a positive at a low, capsule-delivered dose [E2]

Di Sabatino A, Volta U, Salvatore C, Biancheri P, Caio G, De Giorgio R, Di
Stefano M, Corazza GR. *Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol* 2015;13(9):1604–1612.e3.
doi:10.1016/j.cgh.2015.01.029, PMID 25701700 (v — abstract read)

- Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover. **n = 61 enrolled, 59
  completed** (per-protocol analysis).
- **Dose: 4.375 g/day gluten vs rice starch placebo, in gastrosoluble capsules,
  1 week each, with a 1-week gluten-free washout between.**
- **Result:** gluten significantly increased overall symptoms vs placebo
  (P = 0.034); significant for bloating (0.040), abdominal pain (0.047), foggy
  mind (0.019), depression (0.020), aphthous stomatitis (0.025).
- **Note the design conflict with Salerno:** Salerno explicitly *discourages*
  gelatin capsules and requires a cooked, food-matrix vehicle. Di Sabatino used
  capsules. Capsules eliminate FODMAP and ATI-matrix confounding but also
  eliminate the oro-sensory realism that makes blinding meaningful — and change
  where in the gut the protein is released. The field has not resolved which
  vehicle is correct.

### Elli 2016 — a large multicentre trial with a small yield [E2]

Elli L, Tomba C, Branchi F, Roncoroni L, Lombardo V, Bardella MT, et al.
*Nutrients* 2016;8(2):84. doi:10.3390/nu8020084, PMID 26867199, PMC4772047
(v — abstract read; the parent document cited this only as "PMC4772047")

- Multicentre DBPC crossover in patients with functional GI symptoms.
- **140 enrolled; 134 completed the first period; 101 (mean age 39.3, BMI 22.3)
  improved on a 3-week GFD** (VAS 6.5 ± 2.2 → 2.3 ± 1.2, P = 0.001).
- **98 underwent challenge: gluten 5.6 g/day vs placebo for 7 days, then
  crossover.** Primary endpoint: VAS increase ≥3 cm.
- **28 relapsed on gluten — but 14 also relapsed on placebo.** Net: "about **14%**
  of patients responding to gluten withdrawal showed a symptomatic relapse during
  the gluten challenge."
- **No parameter — iron indices, transaminases, CRP — was statistically associated
  with challenge positivity.**

### Carroccio 2012 — the wheat-sensitivity (not gluten) case [E2, retrospective]

Carroccio A, Mansueto P, Iacono G, Soresi M, D'Alcamo A, Cavataio F, Brusca I,
Florena AM, Ambrosiano G, Seidita A, Pirrone G, Rini GB. *Am J Gastroenterol*
2012;107(12):1898–1906. doi:10.1038/ajg.2012.236, PMID 22825366 (v — abstract
read)

- **Retrospective chart review** of all IBS-like patients diagnosed with wheat
  sensitivity by DBPC challenge, 2001–2011. **276 patients**; controls 100 celiac,
  50 IBS.
- Two groups distinguished: wheat sensitivity alone, and wheat sensitivity with
  **multiple food hypersensitivity**.
- Correlates reported vs IBS controls: higher frequency of anaemia, weight loss,
  self-reported wheat intolerance, atopy, infant food allergy; more positive
  IgG/IgA anti-gliadin; more positive **cytometric basophil activation**; and
  **eosinophil infiltration of duodenal and colonic mucosa** as the main histology
  finding.
- **Design caveat that is decisive for weighting:** this is a retrospective series
  from a single group's own referral population, with challenge protocols spanning
  a decade. The basophil-activation and eosinophil findings have not been
  independently replicated at this scale. It is the strongest published case that
  *something* immunological is happening in a subgroup, and it is not a
  prospectively designed trial.

### Skodje 2018 — fructans beat gluten

Covered in the parent document §6 (v). n = 59, 7-day arms, ≥7-day washouts,
gluten 5.7 g vs fructan 2.1 g vs placebo in muesli bars; GSRS-IBS overall gluten
33.1 ± 13.3, **fructan 38.6 ± 12.3**, placebo 34.3 ± 13.9, P = 0.04; bloating
P = 0.003. Not re-derived here.

### Crawley 2022 — a population-based negative [E2]

Crawley C, Savino N, Halby C, Sander SD, Andersen AN, Arumugam M, Murray J,
Christensen R, Husby S. *Aliment Pharmacol Ther* 2022;55(9):1116–1127.
doi:10.1111/apt.16914, PMC9313792 (v — abstract read)

- **Population-based** recruitment (a cohort of 1266 adolescents), not a
  self-selected clinic population — the crucial difference from every trial above.
- 273 eligible with ≥4 GI symptoms → 54 entered a 2-week GFD run-in → 35 eligible
  → **33 randomised, 32 completed**. Median age 20.3; 32 of 33 female.
- **Dose: two granola bars containing 10 g gluten/day vs placebo bars, 7 days
  each, 7-day washout, blinded crossover.**
- **Result: no effect.** Difference in average VAS **−0.01 (95% CI −2.07 to
  2.05)**. No effect on mental health outcomes either.

### de Graaf 2024 — the expectancy dissection [E1 for the nocebo component]

**de Graaf MCG, Lawton CL, Croden F, Smolinska A, Winkens B, Hesselink MAM, van
Rooy G, Weegels PL, Shewry PR, Houghton LA, Witteman BJM, Keszthelyi D, Brouns
FJPH, Dye L, Jonkers DMAE.** "The effect of expectancy versus actual gluten
intake on gastrointestinal and extra-intestinal symptoms in non-coeliac gluten
sensitivity: a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, international,
multicentre study." *Lancet Gastroenterol Hepatol* 2024;9(2):110–123.
doi:10.1016/S2468-1253(23)00317-5, PMID 38040019 — **abstract-level**: Lancet
returned HTTP 403 for the full text; the complete structured abstract was
retrieved from Europe PMC (v).

Design: **2 × 2 factorial** — participants randomised 1:1:1:1 (blocks of 8,
stratified by site and gender) to be **told** they were getting gluten-containing
(E⁺) or gluten-free (E⁻) oat bread, crossed with **actually** receiving
gluten-containing (G⁺) or gluten-free (G⁻) oat bread. Two slices at breakfast and
two at lunch, on a single 8-hour test day; symptoms on a 100 mm VAS hourly,
corrected for baseline; per-protocol analysis. Sites: Leeds, Maastricht,
Wageningen. Participants 18–70 with self-reported NCGS (symptoms within 8 h of
gluten), celiac and wheat allergy excluded, on a GFD/gluten-restricted diet ≥1
week and asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic (≤30 mm) at baseline.
NCT05779358.

Recruitment Oct 2018 – Feb 2022: 165 screened, **84 randomised** (E⁺G⁺ 21, E⁺G⁻
21, E⁻G⁺ 20, E⁻G⁻ 22), **83 in the per-protocol analysis**. Median age 27.0
(IQR 21.0–45.0); **86% women**.

Mean overall GI symptom score (mm, 95% CI):

| Arm | Score |
|---|---|
| **E⁺G⁺** (told gluten, given gluten) | **16.6 (13.1–20.0)** |
| E⁺G⁻ (told gluten, given placebo) | 11.7 (8.3–15.1) |
| E⁻G⁺ (told gluten-free, given gluten) | 6.9 (3.5–10.4) |
| E⁻G⁻ (told gluten-free, given placebo) | 7.4 (4.2–10.7) |

Pairwise:
- E⁺G⁺ vs E⁻G⁺: **+9.6 mm (3.0 to 16.2), p = 0.0010**
- E⁺G⁺ vs E⁻G⁻: **+9.1 mm (2.7 to 15.6), p = 0.0016**
- E⁺G⁺ vs E⁺G⁻: +4.9 mm (−1.7 to 11.5), **p = 0.28 (ns)**
- E⁺G⁻ vs E⁻G⁺: +4.7 mm (−1.8 to 11.3), p = 0.33 (ns)
- **E⁻G⁺ vs E⁻G⁻: −0.5 mm (−7.0 to 5.9), p = 1.0** — gluten with no expectancy did
  literally nothing.

Authors: "The combination of expectancy and actual gluten intake had the largest
effect on gastrointestinal symptoms, **reflecting a nocebo effect, although an
additional effect of gluten cannot be ruled out**."

**Limitations that must travel with this result:** it is a **single 8-hour acute
test day**, not a multi-day challenge; the vehicle was oat bread; the population
was young and 86% female; and the **funding list is dominated by the baking and
wheat industry** — Government of the Netherlands Topsector Agri & Food, AB Mauri
Global Bakery Ingredients, Baking Industry Research Trust, Borgesius-Albert
Heijn, CSM Innovation Centre, CIMMYT, DSM Food Specialties, Fazer, Healthgrain
Forum, ICC, International Wheat Gluten Association, Lantmännen, Mondelez
International, Nederlands Bakkerij Centrum, Nutrition & Santé, Puratos,
Rademaker, Sonneveld Group, Zeelandia HJ Doeleman. The design is strong and the
result is internally coherent, but the sponsorship is exactly the sponsorship a
sceptic would predict for a null-gluten finding, and should be stated whenever
the result is cited.

### Iven 2025 — Biesiekierski's group again, 14 years on [E3]

Iven J, Geeraerts A, Vanuytsel T, Tack J, Van Oudenhove L, **Biesiekierski JR**.
"Impact of Acute and Sub-Acute Gluten Exposure on Gastrointestinal Symptoms and
Psychological Responses in Non-Coeliac Gluten Sensitivity: A Randomised Crossover
Study." *United European Gastroenterol J* 2025;13(8):1295–1306.
doi:10.1002/ueg2.70014, PMC12463691. NCT03798262 / NCT03798249 (v — abstract read)

- **Single-blind** (not double-blind) randomised crossover. **20 healthy controls
  + 16 NCGS**.
- **Acute:** 16 g gluten or whey in yoghurt. **Sub-acute:** gluten-containing
  (16 g/day) or gluten-free muffins for **5 days**.
- Measured: intestinal permeability, hs-CRP, cortisol awakening response,
  (extra)intestinal symptoms, affect.
- **No significant group-by-nutrient interactions.** NCGS participants had higher
  negative affect (p = 0.01) and lower positive affect (p = 0.04) **at baseline**;
  higher acute fatigue (p = 0.03) and higher sub-acute abdominal pain (p < 0.001)
  and bloating (p = 0.001) than controls — **regardless of what they were given**.
  Tension rose after gluten vs placebo in both groups (p = 0.01 acute, p = 0.05
  sub-acute). **No differences in any biological marker.**
- Authors: findings "may be explained by nocebo effects, warranting research into
  novel mechanisms and **re-evaluating the NCGS definition**."

### Goyal 2025 — a large recent positive using Salerno [E2]

Goyal O, Goyal MK, Gupta A, Bharadwaj A, Mehta A, Kumar P, Goyal P, Sood A.
"Prevalence and predictors of nonceliac wheat sensitivity in refractory irritable
bowel syndrome and functional dyspepsia: results from a randomized double-blind
placebo-controlled study." *Eur J Gastroenterol Hepatol* 2025;37(10):1238–1248.
doi:10.1097/MEG.0000000000003046, PMID 41025571. CTRI/2021/10/037323
(v — abstract read)

- Prospective, multicentre (India), Rome IV IBS or functional dyspepsia
  **refractory to standard therapy**, aged 18–65.
- 252 screened → 177 enrolled for a **6-week GFD** → 154 completed (mean age 41.9,
  53.2% male — note, unusually, a **male-majority** NCGS population).
- **82 (52.3%) responded to the GFD**; 77 entered DBPC gluten challenge with
  crossover, per **Salerno criteria**.
- **31 (20.1% of the 154) had significant symptom worsening on blinded gluten.**
- Independent predictors of NCWS: female sex, FD–IBS overlap, headache, fatigue,
  **anxiety**.

## 3.3 Where the field disagrees with itself — stated plainly

### Meta-analytic position [E1 for the synthesis, of E2/E3 inputs]

**Lionetti E, Pulvirenti A, Vallorani M, Catassi G, Verma AK, Gatti S, Catassi
C.** "Re-challenge Studies in Non-celiac Gluten Sensitivity: A Systematic Review
and Meta-Analysis." *Front Physiol* 2017;8:621. doi:10.3389/fphys.2017.00621,
PMC5591881 (v — full text read)

- **11 studies** included. "Considerable heterogeneity related to different sample
  size, type, and amount of gluten administered, duration of challenge and
  different type of placebo."
- **Overall pooled relapse on gluten challenge: 30%, range 7–77%.**
- **Overall relative risk of relapse on gluten vs placebo: RR = 0.4 (95% CI
  −0.15–0.9), p = 0.16 — NOT SIGNIFICANT.** (The CI as printed spans a negative
  value, which is not a coherent CI for a risk ratio; treat the point estimate as
  unreliable and the *non-significance* as the reportable finding.)
- **Restricted to studies using Salerno criteria: 40% relapse on gluten vs 24% on
  placebo, p = 0.003; RR = 2.8 (1.5–5.5), p = 0.002 — SIGNIFICANT.**
- Authors: "The prevalence of NCGS after gluten re-challenge is low, and the
  percentage of relapse after a gluten or a placebo challenge is similar. However,
  a higher number of patients will be **correctly classified** with NCGS if
  applying the recent Salerno criteria."

**This is the single most important structural fact about the field: whether
gluten beats placebo depends on the protocol used.** That can be read two ways —
Salerno protocols are more sensitive and the older null results were
under-powered/mis-specified; or Salerno protocols (which require a prior GFD
response as an entry criterion and use a 30% threshold) enrich for expectancy and
regression artefacts. Both readings are live.

Also relevant: **Molina-Infante J, Santolaria S, Sanders DS, Fernández-Bañares
F.** "Systematic review: noncoeliac gluten sensitivity." *Aliment Pharmacol Ther*
2015;41(9):807–820. doi:10.1111/apt.13155, PMID 25753138 (v — abstract read).
17 studies, 1561 patients. Reported prevalence **0.5–13%**. HLA-DQ2/DQ8 present
in 44% of NCGS patients. **Among 189 NCGS patients re-examined with advanced
techniques combining lymphocytic enteritis and HLA-DQ2/DQ8, 39 (20%) were
reclassified as celiac disease.** That is a large diagnostic-leakage rate and
directly relevant to interpreting positive challenge trials.

And the parent document's existing citation: Molina-Infante J, Carroccio A.
*Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol* 2017;15(3):339–348. doi:10.1016/j.cgh.2016.08.007.

### The live disagreements, itemised

1. **Vehicle.** Salerno forbids capsules and requires a cooked, FODMAP-free food
   matrix with specified ATI content. Di Sabatino 2015 (positive) used
   gastrosoluble capsules. Skodje 2018 and Biesiekierski 2013 used bars/food.
   de Graaf 2024 used oat bread. **No head-to-head comparison of vehicles
   exists.**
2. **Dose.** 4.375 g (Di Sabatino, positive), 5.6 g (Elli, 14% net positive),
   5.7 g (Skodje, gluten scored *below* placebo), 8 g (Salerno recommendation),
   10 g (Crawley, null), 16 g (Biesiekierski 2013, null; Iven 2025, null).
   **There is no dose–response relationship across the literature.** If anything
   the higher-dose trials are the more consistently negative ones, which is the
   opposite of what a direct pharmacological mechanism would predict.
3. **Population.** Every positive trial recruits from tertiary GI clinics or from
   people already self-identifying as gluten-sensitive. The one **population-
   based** trial (Crawley 2022) found **nothing** (VAS difference −0.01, CI −2.07
   to 2.05). Selection is doing heavy lifting.
4. **What "gluten" is being tested.** No trial has separated gluten from
   co-delivered ATIs. Salerno *requires* ≥0.3 g ATI per 8 g gluten — i.e. the
   consensus challenge protocol **deliberately includes** the other main candidate
   agent. A positive Salerno challenge therefore cannot discriminate gluten from
   ATIs by construction.
5. **Nocebo vs signal.** de Graaf 2024 shows that with expectancy removed, gluten
   produced a **−0.5 mm** difference from placebo (p = 1.0). Iven 2025 finds NCGS
   status, not gluten, explains the symptoms. Against this, Goyal 2025 (n = 154
   completing GFD, 77 challenged) found 20.1% blinded-gluten responders under
   Salerno, and Lionetti's Salerno-restricted pooling gives RR 2.8. The field has
   **not** converged.
6. **Diagnostic leakage.** 20% of carefully re-examined NCGS patients were
   reclassified as celiac (Molina-Infante 2015). Some of the historical "NCGS
   responder subgroup" is probably seronegative or early celiac disease.
7. **Biomarkers.** Every trial that measured them — Biesiekierski 2011 (anti-
   gliadin, faecal lactoferrin, hs-CRP, permeability), Biesiekierski 2013, Elli
   2016 (iron indices, transaminases, CRP), Iven 2025 (permeability, hs-CRP,
   cortisol awakening response) — found **nothing**. Carroccio 2012's basophil
   activation and mucosal eosinophilia are the only positive biomarker signals,
   from a retrospective single-group series.

### VERDICT for GAP 3

- **That the Salerno protocol exists and specifies 8 g/day, 1 week on / 1 week
  washout / 1 week crossover, ≥4 weeks prior strict GFD, food-matrix vehicle,
  30% symptom threshold: [E1] as a description of the consensus** — but the
  document itself calls its own 30% threshold "somewhat arbitrary and needs
  scientific validation."
- **That some non-celiac people have reproducible wheat-triggered symptoms:
  [E2].** Best supported by Goyal 2025 and by the Salerno-restricted arm of
  Lionetti 2017.
- **That gluten *specifically* is the trigger at population level: [E4].**
  Biesiekierski's own 2013 reversal (8% gluten-specific), Crawley's population
  null, and de Graaf's expectancy-free null are three independent failures.
- **That nocebo/expectancy is a large measured component: [E1].** de Graaf 2024
  is now the reference experiment for this, with the industry-funding caveat
  attached.
- **That fructans contribute more than gluten in self-reported NCGS: [E2]**
  (Skodje 2018 and its 2024 microbiota follow-up, per the parent document).
- **That ATIs are the trigger: [E4], and now structurally untestable under the
  consensus protocol**, which mandates their presence.
- **That a biomarker exists: no. [E1 negative.]**
