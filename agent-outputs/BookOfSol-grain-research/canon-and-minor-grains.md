# Canon and Minor Grains

Two separate enquiries, kept separate on purpose.

**Part 1** records what the Āyurvedic and yogic texts *say*, in their own vocabulary
(rasa, guṇa, vīrya, vipāka, doṣa), with the Sanskrit where it could be obtained and the
botanical identifications and their disputes.

**Part 2** records what modern biology and biochemistry have *measured*, with study
design, dose, and an evidence-strength verdict for each claim.

No reconciliation between the two is attempted anywhere in this document. Part 1 is not
evidence for Part 2 and Part 2 is not a validation of Part 1.

Compiled 2026-07-22.

### 1.2 The classification frame, and the fact that it moves

The grain vocabulary is not stable across the canon. This matters more than any single
grain's properties, because the *category* a grain sits in carries much of its meaning.

**Caraka** (Sū. 27.5) opens the food catalogue with twelve *vargas*, of which
*śūkadhānya* (awned grain) is first and *śamīdhānya* (legume) second. Caraka himself
gives no sub-classification of the awned grains and never uses the word *kudhānya*
("bad grain").

**Cakrapāṇidatta** (11th c.), commenting, splits *śūkadhānya* into three classes —
*śāli*, *ṣaṣṭika*, *vrīhi* — and it is Cakrapāṇi, not Caraka, who introduces *kudhānya*
as a label for the millet block at 27.16–18.

**Gaṅgādhara** groups differently again: *śūkadhānya* vs *kudhānya*, with *śāli*,
*ṣaṣṭika* and *vrīhi* falling under *kudhānya*.

**Suśruta** has no *śūkadhānya* varga at all. The same grains are distributed across a
*śāli* varga, a *kudhānya* varga, and — significantly — *godhūma* (wheat) is placed in
the *Mudgādi* (mung-and-others) varga rather than with the awned grains.

**Vāgbhaṭa** (*Aṣṭāṅga Saṅgraha* and *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*) names the millet block
*tṛṇa-dhānya*, "grass grain", not *kudhānya*.

**Bhāvaprakāśa** splits *dhānya-varga* into five: *śāli*, *vrīhi*, *śūka*, *śimbī*,
*kṣudra-dhānya*. By this stage *śūka-dhānya* has contracted to **exactly two members —
yava and godhūma** — because rice has been given its own two vargas and the millets
their own.

> Source for the comparative frame: Kisan P, Yadav SS. "Comparative Study on Suka Dhanya
> Varga Mentioned in Brihat-Trayi." *Int J Health Sci Res* 2020;10(4):65–70.
> https://www.ijhsr.org/IJHSR_Vol.10_Issue.4_April2020/10.pdf — this paper cites the
> Trikamji editions with Cakrapāṇi and Dalhaṇa; the commentarial positions attributed to
> Cakrapāṇi, Gaṅgādhara and Yogendranāth Sen below come from it.

**Observation worth stating plainly.** In the earliest layer (Caraka) barley and wheat
sit in the *same* category as rice and the millets and are minority members of it. In the
latest layer surveyed (Bhāvaprakāśa) barley and wheat are the *only* members of the
"awned grain" category. The category narrowed around them.

### 1.3 Caraka's śūkadhānya list, with Sanskrit

Sanskrit as given at carakasamhitaonline.com (Annapānavidhi Adhyāya, Sū. 27.8–22);
English following the Sharma 2014 rendering held locally.

#### Śāli — the winter rices (27.8–12)

> रक्तशालिर्महाशालिः कलमः शकुनाहृतः । तूर्णको दीर्घशूकश्च गौरः पाण्डुकलाङ्गुलौ ॥८॥
> सुगन्धको लोहवालः सारिवाख्यः प्रमोदकः । पतङ्गस्तपनीयश्च ये चान्ये शालयः शुभाः ॥९॥
> शीता रसे विपाके च मधुराश्चाल्पमारुताः । बद्धाल्पवर्चसः स्निग्धा बृंहणाः शुक्रमूत्रलाः ॥१०॥
> रक्तशालिर्वरस्तेषां तृष्णाघ्नस्त्रिमलापहः । महांस्तस्यानु कलमस्तस्याप्यनु ततः परे ॥११॥

Named cultivars: **rakta-śāli, mahāśāli, kalama, śakunāhṛta, tūrṇaka, dīrghaśūka, gaura,
pāṇḍuka, lāṅgula, sugandhaka, lohavāla, sārivākhya, pramodaka, pataṅga, tapanīya**,
"and other good śālis".

Properties of the group (27.10): *śīta* (cold) in vīrya; *madhura* in both rasa and
vipāka; *alpa-māruta* (produces little vāta); binding, producing scanty formed stool;
*snigdha*; *bṛṃhaṇa* (bulk-building); *śukrala* and *mūtrala* (semen- and urine-promoting).

**Rakta-śāli is declared best** (*varas teṣām*), *tṛṣṇāghna* (thirst-destroying) and
*trimalāpaha* (removing all three malas/doṣas). Then mahāśāli, then kalama, then the rest.

Inferior śālis (27.12): **yavaka, hāyana, pāṃsuvāpya, naiṣadhaka** — said to "imitate the
śālis in merits and demerits" (*śālīnāṃ ... anukāraṃ guṇāguṇaiḥ*).

#### Ṣaṣṭika — the sixty-day rices (27.13–14)

> शीतः स्निग्धोऽगुरुः स्वादुस्त्रिदोषघ्नः स्थिरात्मकः । षष्टिकः प्रवरो गौरः कृष्णगौरस्ततोऽनु च ॥१३॥

*Śīta*, *snigdha*, ***aguru*** (not heavy), *svādu*, ***tridoṣaghna*** (destroying all
three doṣas), *sthirātmaka* (stability-giving). Best variety **gaura** (white), then
**kṛṣṇagaura**. Slightly inferior (27.14): **varaka, uddālaka, cīna, śārada, ujjvala,
dardura, gandhana, kuruvinda**.

#### Vrīhi — the rains/autumn rices (27.15)

> मधुरश्चाम्लपाकश्च व्रीहिः पित्तकरो गुरुः । बहुमूत्रपुरीषोष्मा त्रिदोषस्त्वेव पाटलः ॥१५॥

*Madhura* in rasa but ***amla* in vipāka** — sour post-digestion — hence *pittakara* and
*guru*. The **pāṭala** variety produces copious urine, faeces and heat and disturbs all
three doṣas.

This is the sharpest intra-rice distinction in the text: the same species, sorted by
season and cultivar, is assigned opposite vipāka and opposite doṣa effect.

#### Śyāmāka and the millet block (27.16–18)

> सकोरदूषः श्यामाकः कषायमधुरो लघुः । वातलः कफपित्तघ्नः शीतः सङ्ग्राहिशोषणः ॥१६॥

**Śyāmāka with koradūṣa**: *kaṣāya-madhura* (astringent-sweet), *laghu* (light),
*vātala* (vāta-aggravating), *kapha-pitta-ghna*, ***śīta***, *saṅgrāhi* (binding),
*śoṣaṇa* (absorbing/drying).

Seventeen further names said to be *śyāmāka-sadṛśā guṇaiḥ* (27.17–18):
**hasti-śyāmāka, nīvāra, toyaparṇī, gavedhukā, praśāntikā, ambhaḥ-śyāmāka, lauhitya,
aṇu, priyaṅgu, mukunda, jhiṇṭī, garmūṭī, varuka, varaka, śibira, utkaṭa, jūrṇa**.

#### Yava — barley (27.19)

> रूक्षः शीतोऽगुरुः स्वादुर्बहुवातशकृद्यवः । स्थैर्यकृत्सकषायश्च बल्यः श्लेष्मविकारनुत् ॥१९॥

*Rūkṣa* (rough/dry), *śīta*, ***aguru*** (light), *svādu*; ***bahu-vāta-śakṛt*** —
producing abundant flatus *and* abundant faeces; *sthairyakṛt* (stabilising);
*sa-kaṣāya* (with astringent secondary taste); *balya* (strength-giving);
*śleṣma-vikāra-nut* (removing kapha disorders).

Note the exact contrast with the śāli verse: śāli is *alpa-māruta* and produces
*alpa-varcas*; yava is *bahu-vāta-śakṛt*. The text is explicitly using stool and gas
volume as a discriminating property, and barley is its high-volume grain.

#### Veṇuyava — bamboo seed (27.20)

> रूक्षः कषायानुरसो मधुरः कफपित्तहा । मेदःक्रिमिविषघ्नश्च बल्यो वेणुयवो मतः ॥२०॥

*Rūkṣa*, astringent after-taste, *madhura*, *kapha-pitta-hā*; destroys **medas** (fat),
**krimi** (worms) and **viṣa** (poison); *balya*. **This is not barley** — see §1.5.

#### Godhūma — wheat (27.21–22)

> सन्धानकृद्वातहरो गोधूमः स्वादुशीतलः । जीवनो बृंहणो वृष्यः स्निग्धः स्थैर्यकरो गुरुः ॥२१॥
> नान्दीमुखी मधूली च मधुरस्निग्धशीतले । इत्ययं शूकधान्यानां पूर्वो वर्गः समाप्यते ॥२२॥

***Sandhāna-kṛt*** — knitting/uniting (used of fracture healing) — *vāta-hara*, *svādu*,
*śītala*, *jīvana* (vitalising), *bṛṃhaṇa*, *vṛṣya* (aphrodisiac), *snigdha*,
*sthairyakara*, ***guru*** (heavy). **Nāndīmukhī** and **madhūlī** (wheat types):
*madhura*, *snigdha*, *śītala*.

Barley is *aguru*; wheat is *guru*. The two are opposed on exactly that axis.

### 1.4 Caraka's superlatives and scattered grain rulings

These are the operationally strongest statements in the text, because they are rankings,
not descriptions.

- **Sū. 25.38** — *rakta-śāli* is the single most wholesome (*pathyatama*) substance among
  the *śūkadhānya*; *mudga* (green gram) is best among legumes.
- **Sū. 25.39** — ***yavaka* is the single most unwholesome (*apathyatama*) among the
  *śūkadhānya***; *māṣa* (black gram) worst among legumes.
- **Sū. 25.40** (the "best of" list, 152 items) —
  - *gavedhuka*-diet: best among **karśanīya** (emaciating/reducing) foods;
  - *kodrava*-diet: best among **rūkṣaṇa** (roughening, fat-reducing) foods;
  - *yava*: best among **purīṣa-janana** (faecal-bulk producing) substances;
  - *yavakṣāra* (barley alkali): best among laxative/digestive agents and in piles.
- **Sū. 27.4** — the rule "sweet taste is mostly kapha-aggravating" is given **four grain
  exceptions plus honey**: *old śāli*, *ṣaṣṭika*, *barley*, *wheat*. These four are the
  only sweet grains the text exempts from its own kapha rule.
- **Sū. 27.305** — *yāvaśūka* (alkali from barley awns) for heart disease, anaemia,
  grahaṇī, splenomegaly, throat disease, kaphaja cough, piles.
- **Sū. 27.309–310** — cereals and legumes are recommended **one year old**; new grain is
  *guru* and kapha-producing, old grain mostly *rūkṣa*.
- **Sū. 27.339** — processing overrides intrinsic quality: *vrīhi*, heavy, becomes light
  when parched into *lājā*.
- **Sū. 27, kṛtānna section** — *apūpa*, *yāvaka* and *vāṭya* made of barley remove
  *udāvarta*, coryza, cough, ***prameha*** (the polyuric/diabetic syndrome) and throat
  disorders. Barley is the text's named grain for prameha.
- **Sū. 5.12** — the daily-routine verse lists *ṣaṣṭika*, *śāli*, *mudga*, *āmalaka*,
  *yava*, milk, ghee, honey (plus two disputed items). **Caveat:** the received Sanskrit of
  this verse has three independent anomalies (a number/gender disagreement at
  *saindhavān lavaṇaṃ*; *dhānyaṃ* rendered "rain water" by two translators without
  warrant; *ghṛtam* appearing twice), and one lineage adds *jāṅgala-māṃsa*. These are
  documented in `repos/caraka-samhita/notes/philology/sutrasthana-5-12.md`. The *grain*
  items (*ṣaṣṭika*, *śāli*, *mudga*, *yava*) are not among the disputed readings.

**Grains conspicuously absent from every list above:** oats (*Avena sativa*), rye
(*Secale cereale*), maize (*Zea mays*), and buckwheat. None of the śūkadhānya,
kudhānya, tṛṇa-dhānya or kṣudra-dhānya lists surveyed here — Caraka Sū. 27, Suśruta Sū.
46, AH Sū. 6, Bhāvaprakāśa Dhānya-varga — contains a name that has been identified with
*Avena*. **The canon has nothing to say about oats.** Any Āyurvedic property assignment
for oats is a modern extrapolation from taste and texture, not a textual transmission.
This is stated here because Part 2 is heavily about oats; the two parts do not meet.

### 1.6 The *yavaka* problem

This is the single sharpest internal contradiction in Caraka's grain material and it is
worth isolating.

- Sū. **27.12** places *yavaka* first among the inferior śālis and says they "imitate the
  śālis in merits and demerits" — i.e. roughly śāli-like.
- Sū. **25.39** names *yavaka* the ***most unwholesome substance in the entire
  śūkadhānya class***.

The commentators split on it:

- **Cakrapāṇidatta** and **Gaṅgādhara** hold that yavaka is *trimalakara* (deranging all
  three malas) and possesses qualities *opposite* to rakta-śāli. Cakrapāṇi reads the
  "*guṇāguṇaiḥ*" of 27.12 as *guṇa-viparīta-doṣa* — the resemblance is inverted.
- **Yogendranāth Sen** holds the plain reading: yavaka and the rest have the *same*
  guṇas as śāli.

(Attributions per Kisan & Yadav 2020, citing the Trikamji editions.)

So the text names a grain, ranks it worst of its whole class, and its commentators cannot
agree whether it is barley-like, rice-like, or the inverse of rice. Anyone building on
Caraka's grain rankings has to decide this and should say that they are deciding it.

### 1.7 Where the saṃhitās disagree with each other

Kept as a list because these are the load-bearing disagreements.

1. **Millets: cold or hot.** Caraka 27.16 assigns śyāmāka-with-koradūṣa ***śīta*** vīrya
   and *kapha-pitta-ghna*. Suśruta's general kudhānya statement (Sū. 46.21–25) makes the
   group ***uṣṇa*** ("generate heat"), *rūkṣa*, *kaṭu*-vipāka, kapha-subduing but
   ***vāta- and pitta-aggravating***, and urine-retaining. Suśruta then partially walks it
   back for kodrava, nīvāra, śyāmāka and śāntanu specifically, calling them
   kaṣāya-madhura and curative in *śītapitta*. Vāgbhaṭa's *tṛṇa-dhānya* verse sides with
   Caraka: *śīta*, *laghu*, vāta-increasing, ***lekhana*** (scraping), kapha-pitta-pacifying.
   Bhāvaprakāśa splits kodrava (śīta) from vana-kodrava (uṣṇa). **There is no canonical
   consensus on millet vīrya.**
2. **Barley's vipāka.** Caraka 27.19 states no vipāka for yava. Suśruta Sū. 46.40–42 gives
   *kaṭu* vipāka. Bhāvaprakāśa gives *kaṭu* vipāka. So the earliest text is silent and the
   later two agree against silence.
3. **Barley's doṣa target.** Caraka: *śleṣma-vikāra-nut* — kapha only. Suśruta: subdues
   *pitta and kapha*. Bhāvaprakāśa: kapha-pitta-śāmaka, ***vāta-vardhaka***. Caraka's
   *bahu-vāta* wording implies the same vāta effect without saying "vardhaka".
4. **Barley's weight.** Caraka 27.19: ***aguru*** (light). Bhāvaprakāśa: ***īṣat guru***
   (slightly heavy). A secondary digest of AH Sū. 6.13–14 renders Vāgbhaṭa's yava as
   "heavy" — **this reading is unverified against the Sanskrit here and should not be
   relied on** until checked.
5. **Barley's *lekhana* status.** Suśruta explicitly says yava "scrapes fat"; Bhāvaprakāśa
   lists *lekhanīya* among its guṇas. Caraka does not use *lekhana* of yava — Caraka's
   fat-reducing superlatives go to *gavedhukā* (karśanīya) and *kodrava* (rūkṣaṇa)
   instead (Sū. 25.40). The popular "barley is the Āyurvedic fat-scraper" claim rests on
   Suśruta and Bhāvaprakāśa, **not** on Caraka.
6. **Wheat's placement.** Caraka: śūkadhānya. Suśruta: *Mudgādi varga* — filed with the
   pulses. Bhāvaprakāśa: śūka-dhānya (one of only two).
7. **Wheat's temperature.** Caraka: *śītala*. Suśruta: "extremely cooling". Both agree,
   unusually, and both agree it is *guru* and *sandhāna-kṛt* (fracture-knitting) — the
   most stable grain description in the canon.
8. **Rakta-śāli's doṣa action.** Caraka 27.11: *trimalāpaha*, removes all three. Suśruta
   Sū. 46.4–6: the śāli group *subdues pitta* but *slightly generates vāyu and kapha*, and
   lohitaka is diuretic, spermatopoietic, refrigerant, eye-invigorating. Caraka's
   tridoṣa-clearing claim is stronger than Suśruta's.

### 1.8 The asymmetry nobody comments on

Counting names in Caraka Sū. 27.8–22:

- **Rice**: ~15 śāli cultivars + 4 inferior śālis + 2 ṣaṣṭika grades + 8 ṣaṣṭika varieties
  + vrīhi + pāṭala ≈ **30 named entities**.
- **Millets and small grains**: śyāmāka, koradūṣa + 17 more ≈ **19 named entities**.
- **Barley**: **one word** — *yava*. (*Veṇuyava* is bamboo.)
- **Wheat**: **one word** — *godhūma* — plus two unidentified types, nāndīmukhī and madhūlī.

The text's descriptive resolution is overwhelmingly on rice. Barley and wheat each get a
single śloka. This is a fact about the text's agricultural horizon, not about the grains.

### 1.9 The yogic manuals

#### Haṭha Yoga Pradīpikā — the pathya verse

Verified Sanskrit (edition numbering varies: **1.62–63** in the numbering used by the
IRJAY review; **1.65** in the Brahmānanda-based numbering at ashtangayoga.info):

> गोधूमशालियवषाष्टिकशोभनान्नं क्षीराज्यखण्डनवनीतसिद्धामधूनि ।
> शुण्ठीपटोलकफलादिकपञ्चशाकं मुद्गादिदिव्यम् उदकं च यमीन्द्रपथ्यम् ॥

> *godhūma-śāli-yava-ṣāṣṭika-śobhanānnaṃ kṣīrājya-khaṇḍa-navanīta-siddhā-madhūni |*
> *śuṇṭhī-paṭola-kaphalādika-pañca-śākaṃ mudgādi-divyam udakaṃ ca yamīndra-pathyam ||*

Source: https://www.ashtangayoga.info/philosophy/source-texts-and-mantra/hatha-pradipika/chapter-1/

The grain terms, in order: ***godhūma*** (wheat), ***śāli*** (rice), ***yava***
(barley), ***ṣāṣṭika*** (sixty-day rice) — collectively *śobhanānna*, "excellent food";
then milk, ghee, *khaṇḍa* sugar, butter, *sitā*/candied sugar, honey; dry ginger,
*paṭola*, five leaf-vegetables; ***mudga*** (green gram) and the like; and "divine water"
(rainwater). *Yamīndra-pathyam* — "wholesome for the lord of ascetics".

**Points of substance.**
- The yogic list is *four grains*, in that fixed compound order, and it is a **strict
  subset of Caraka's praised grains**: śāli, ṣaṣṭika, yava, godhūma are precisely the four
  that Caraka Sū. 27.4 exempts from the sweet-taste kapha rule (with "old śāli"), and
  *mudga* is Caraka's *pathyatama* legume (Sū. 25.38).
- **No millet appears in the HYP pathya list.** Not śyāmāka, not kodrava, not priyaṅgu,
  not nīvāra. The entire Caraka millet block is simply absent — neither praised nor
  forbidden.
- HYP 1.59–60 (apathya) forbids, among others, sour, bitter, pungent, salty and heating
  things, green leafy vegetables other than those ordained, oil, sesame, mustard,
  alcohol, fish, goat meat, curd, buttermilk, *piper longum*, asafoetida and garlic. **No
  grain is forbidden.** The apathya list operates on tastes, oils, ferments and flesh, not
  on cereals.

#### Gheraṇḍa Saṃhitā

Seventeen verses on diet: **5.2** and **5.16–31**. (Verse-range per Chhetri et al. 2024,
*Int Res J Ayurveda Yoga* 7(5):17–21, DOI 10.48165/IRJAY.2024.70504 —
https://irjay.com/index.php/irjay/article/download/1050/1214. **Sanskrit not obtained in
this pass**; the content below is from that review's rendering of the Vasu translation.)

- **5.21–22** — mitāhāra: half the stomach food, a quarter water, a quarter empty.
- **5.17–20, 5.28–29** — pathya. The grain items: **rice, barley *sattu* (roasted barley
  flour), wheat flour**, plus **mung dal, *urad* dal and chickpea** — clean and free from
  chaff — then a long list of gourds, plantain parts, roots and five leaf-vegetables.
- **5.23–27, 5.29–31** — apathya: bitter, acid, salt, pungent and roasted things, curd,
  whey, heavy vegetables, wine, *kulattha* and *masūr*, many fruits — **and also fresh
  butter, ghee, thickened milk, sugar and date-sugar**.

**Two things follow.**
1. Gheraṇḍa's grain list is *broader* than HYP's on the legume side: it admits **māṣa
   (urad)** and **caṇaka (chickpea)**, where Caraka Sū. 25.39 ranks **māṣa the single most
   unwholesome legume**. The yogic and the medical canon diverge here.
2. Gheraṇḍa's apathya includes **ghee, butter, sugar and thickened milk — the very items
   HYP's pathya verse names as wholesome.** This is a flat contradiction between the two
   principal haṭha manuals. It may be softened by the Vasu translation's framing ("in the
   beginning of yoga-practice, one should discard…"), i.e. a staged rather than absolute
   prohibition. **Flagged as translation-dependent and not resolved here.**

#### Haṭha Ratnāvalī

Śrīnivāsa's pathya and apathya lists are reported by secondary sources to reproduce the
HYP lists essentially unchanged (wheat, rice, barley, milk, ghee, honey, dry ginger,
paṭola, five vegetables, mudga, pure water; forbidden: sour, bitter, salty, heating,
unordained greens, oil, sesame, mustard, alcohol, fish, flesh, curd, buttermilk, oil
cakes, garlic). **No primary text was obtained. Treat as unverified.**

#### Śiva Saṃhitā

**Not verified.** The Mallinson translation's index places "dietary restrictions" and the
vāta/pitta/kapha discussion on the same page, and its introduction locates a list of
things "to be avoided by the yogi" at **3.35–37**, with a second, only-partly-overlapping
list at 5.3–8 (Mallinson notes the two lists' inconsistency as evidence the text is a
compilation). **The verse text could not be retrieved in this pass and no grain list from
Śiva Saṃhitā is asserted here.** It should be checked directly against Mallinson or Vasu
before use.

### 1.10 Part 1 summary — the canonical grain roster

**Praised, unambiguously, across Āyurveda and the yogic manuals:**
*rakta-śāli* (best of all awned grains, Caraka), *ṣaṣṭika* (tridoṣaghna and light),
*yava* (barley), *godhūma* (wheat), and — Caraka only — old śāli.

**Praised in Āyurveda, silent in the yogic manuals:**
the entire millet block — *śyāmāka*, *kodrava*, *nīvāra*, *priyaṅgu*, *gavedhukā*,
*veṇuyava* and the rest. Āyurveda gives them precise therapeutic roles (*karśanīya*,
*rūkṣaṇa*, *lekhana*, śītapitta) and disagrees internally about their vīrya. The haṭha
texts do not mention them at all.

**Restricted or downgraded:**
*vrīhi* (amla-vipāka, pitta-producing, guru; the *pāṭala* variety tridoṣa-deranging);
*yavaka* (worst of the class, identity unresolved); new grain of any kind (Sū. 27.309);
millets by Suśruta's reckoning (uṣṇa, vāta- and pitta-aggravating, urine-retaining).

**Absent from the canon entirely:** oats, rye, maize, buckwheat.

### 2.1 Oats — avenanthramides

**What is observed.** Avenanthramides (AVA) are N-cinnamoylanthranilic acid alkaloids.
Two independent primary sources state them to be restricted to oats: Chen et al. describe
them as "alkaloids occurring only in oats" (*J Nutr* 2007), and Armeni et al. open with
"Avenanthramides (AVAs) and Avenacosides (AVEs) are unique to oats (*Avena sativa*)"
(*Nutr J* 2025).

**Human pharmacokinetics — the number that matters.**
Chen CY, Milbury PE, Collins FW, Blumberg JB. "Avenanthramides are bioavailable and have
antioxidant activity in humans after acute consumption of an enriched mixture from oats."
*J Nutr* 2007;137(6):1375–82. DOI 10.1093/jn/137.6.1375 (PMID 17513394).
Design: randomised, placebo-controlled, 3-way crossover, **n = 6** healthy older adults
(60.8 ± 3.6 y), 1-week washouts. Dose: 0.5 or 1 g avenanthramide-enriched mixture in
360 mL skim milk. Result: **maximum plasma concentrations (free + conjugated) were
112.9 and 374.6 nmol/L for AVA-A, 13.2 and 96.0 nmol/L for AVA-B, and 41.4 and
89.0 nmol/L for AVA-C** at the two doses. T-max ≈ 1.75–2.30 h.

Read that again: peak plasma avenanthramide is in the **tens-to-hundreds of nanomolar**
range, from a *concentrate* dose far above what a bowl of oats delivers.

Armeni M, Cardilin T, Fristedt R, et al. "Avenanthramides and avenacosides as biomarkers
of oat intake: a pharmacokinetic study of solid and liquid oat consumption under single
and repeated dose conditions." *Nutr J* 2025;24. DOI 10.1186/s12937-025-01204-7
(PMID 40926245). Design: **n = 21**, non-blinded randomised crossover, two oat products
(solid and liquid), single-dose postprandial phase plus a 4-day repeated-dose phase,
compartmental PK modelling of AVA 2p/2c/2f/2fd/2pd and avenacosides A and B. Framing is
**biomarker validation**, not health outcome.

**Human intervention data.**
Koenig R, Dickman JR, Kang C, Zhang T, Chu YF, Ji LL. "Avenanthramide supplementation
attenuates exercise-induced inflammation in postmenopausal women." *Nutr J* 2014;13:21.
DOI 10.1186/1475-2891-13-21 (PMID 24645793). Design: double-blind, **n = 16 total**
(two arms of 8), 8 weeks, oat-flour cookies delivering **9.2 mg AVA/d vs 0.4 mg/d**,
downhill-walking challenge. Results: AVA arm showed lower neutrophil respiratory burst at
24 h, lower CRP at 48 h, suppressed plasma IL-1β and mononuclear-cell NF-κB binding, and
higher plasma total antioxidant capacity and erythrocyte SOD (all P < 0.05). Companion
trial: Koenig RT et al., *Eur J Appl Physiol* 2016;116:67–76.
DOI 10.1007/s00421-015-3244-3 (PMID 26289619).

**Mechanistic work is cell-culture and animal.**
Liu L, Zubik L, Collins FW, Marko M, Meydani M. "The antiatherogenic potential of oat
phenolic compounds." *Atherosclerosis* 2004;175(1):39–49.
DOI 10.1016/j.atherosclerosis.2004.01.044 (PMID 15186945).
Sur R, Nigam A, Grote D, Liebel F, Southall MD. "Avenanthramides, polyphenols from oats,
exhibit anti-inflammatory and anti-itch activity." *Arch Dermatol Res* 2008;300(10):569–74.
DOI 10.1007/s00403-008-0858-x (PMID 18461339).

**Evidence-strength verdict: MODERATE for "avenanthramides exist, are oat-restricted, and
are absorbed"; WEAK for any specific human health outcome.** The absorption result is
solid but rests on n = 6. The two human inflammation trials are n = 16, single lab, same
group, industry-adjacent (a co-author affiliation to an oat company appears on both), with
surrogate endpoints. There is a real and unquantified gap between the nanomolar plasma
concentrations Chen measured and the concentrations at which cell-culture papers
demonstrate NF-κB and adhesion-molecule effects — **I did not verify the assay
concentrations in Liu 2004 or Sur 2008 in this pass**, and that comparison is the single
most important check anyone building on this literature should do before claiming a
mechanism operates in vivo.

**Open item, flagged not asserted:** the "found in no other food" claim is repeated in the
primary oat literature but is a negative claim about all other plants. Structurally
analogous N-cinnamoylanthranilates are reported as phytoalexins in other genera. **Not
verified here.** Treat "unique to oats" as "unique among common foods, as asserted by
oat researchers", not as an established botanical fact.

### 2.2 Oats and barley — mixed-linkage β-glucan

The best-evidenced grain claim in this entire document.

**The dose-response and the physics behind it.**
Wolever TMS, Tosh SM, Gibbs AL, et al. "Physicochemical properties of oat β-glucan
influence its ability to reduce serum LDL cholesterol in humans: a randomized clinical
trial." *Am J Clin Nutr* 2010;92(4):723–32. DOI 10.3945/ajcn.2010.29174 (PMID 20660224).
Design: double-blind, parallel, multicentre; 367 enrolled, **345 completed**; LDL 3.0–5.0
mmol/L; 4 weeks; arms = wheat-fibre control, 3 g high-MW (2,210,000 g/mol), 4 g medium-MW
(850,000), 3 g medium-MW (530,000), 4 g low-MW (210,000) oat β-glucan/d.
Results: LDL fell vs control by **0.21 mmol/L (5.5%)**, **0.26 (6.5%)** and **0.19 (4.7%)**
in the first three arms. **The 4 g low-MW arm produced no significant effect
(2.3%; 95% CI 0.02, −0.20).** log(MW × C) was a significant determinant of LDL
(P = 0.003). Authors' conclusion: efficacy "reduced by 50% when MW was reduced to
210,000 g/mol."

This is the strongest single result in the field, and it is a *disconfirming* one for the
naive reading of the health claim: **3 g of β-glucan is not 3 g of β-glucan.** Depolymerise
it — by processing, by β-glucanase, by extrusion — and the effect goes away at the same
stated dose.

**Meta-analytic effect sizes.**
Whitehead A, Beck EJ, Tosh S, Wolever TM. "Cholesterol-lowering effects of oat β-glucan:
a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials." *Am J Clin Nutr* 2014;100(6):1413–21.
DOI 10.3945/ajcn.114.086108 (PMID 25411276). **28 RCTs**, ≥3 g oat β-glucan/d vs control,
random-effects. Result: **LDL −0.25 mmol/L (95% CI 0.20, 0.30)**; total cholesterol
−0.30 mmol/L; **no change in HDL or triglycerides**. Note the search and funding context:
the searches included "in-house study reports at CreaNutrition AG", and the author list is
the same group as the Wolever trial.

Ho HV, Sievenpiper JL, Zurbau A, et al. "A systematic review and meta-analysis of
randomized controlled trials of the effect of barley β-glucan on LDL-C, non-HDL-C and apoB
for cardiovascular disease risk reduction." *Eur J Clin Nutr* 2016;70(11):1239–45.
DOI 10.1038/ejcn.2016.89 (PMID 27273067). **14 trials, N = 615**, median dose
**6.5–6.9 g/d**, median 4 weeks. Result: **LDL-C −0.25 mmol/L (95% CI −0.30, −0.20)**;
non-HDL-C −0.31 mmol/L; **no significant change in apoB**; **I² = 98%** for non-HDL-C.

Talati R, Baker WL, Pabilonia MS, White CM, Coleman CI. "The effects of barley-derived
soluble fiber on serum lipids." *Ann Fam Med* 2009;7(2):157–63. DOI 10.1370/afm.917
(PMID 19273871). **8 trials, n = 391**, 4–12 weeks. Total cholesterol
−13.38 mg/dL (95% CI −18.46, −8.31); LDL −10.02 mg/dL (−14.03, −6.00); triglycerides
−11.83 mg/dL (−20.12, −3.55); **HDL not significantly changed (P = 0.07)**.

**Barley needs roughly twice the dose for the same LDL drop.** Oat meta-analysis:
−0.25 mmol/L at ≥3 g/d. Barley meta-analysis: −0.25 mmol/L at a median of 6.5–6.9 g/d.
That is a genuine, citable asymmetry between the two grains, and it is not usually stated.
The best-supported explanation is **not** the DP3:DP4 difference — see §2.2b, where the
leading candidate is that **oats are routinely kilned and barley is not**, so oat β-glucan
reaches the gut with its endogenous β-glucanase already destroyed.

**Regulatory position (verified primary text).**
21 CFR 101.81, retrieved from govinfo (CFR-2023-title21-vol2-sec101-81; amendments through
88 FR 17718, 24 March 2023). Eligible sources and minimum β-glucan on a dry-weight basis:
oat bran ≥ 5.5%; rolled oats ≥ 4%; whole oat flour ≥ 4%; oatrim up to 10%; whole grain and
dry-milled barley ≥ 4% (5.5% for bran/sieved meal); barley betafiber ≥ 70%. Threshold:
"**3 g or more per day of β-glucan soluble fiber from either whole oats or barley, or a
combination**", with **≥ 0.75 g soluble fiber per RACC**.
**EFSA:** the corresponding opinion is EFSA Journal 2010;8(12):1885
(DOI 10.2903/j.efsa.2010.1885). **The full text was paywalled at fetch time and its exact
wording is not quoted here** — verify before citing.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for a small LDL reduction; STRONG for
viscosity/MW-dependence; WEAK-to-ABSENT for hard cardiovascular endpoints.** Every
number above is a lipid surrogate over 3–12 weeks. **No trial cited here measured
myocardial infarction, stroke or mortality.** Heterogeneity in the barley pooling
(I² = 98%) is severe. And the oat literature is dominated by one author group with
industry links, which does not make it wrong but does mean independent replication carries
extra weight.

### 2.2b β-glucan structure and genetics — what the fine-structure story actually supports

All citations in this subsection are **[delegated]**.

**Content: barley's distinction is its ceiling, not its median.** The cleanest ranked
comparison avoids hull dilution by using 29 **hulless** genotypes — Izydorczyk MS, Storsley J,
Labossiere D, MacGregor AW, Rossnagel BG, *J Agric Food Chem* 2000;48(4):982–9.
DOI 10.1021/jf991102f (PMID 10775338):

| Hulless starch type | Mean total β-glucan | Extractability |
|---|---|---|
| High-amylose | **7.49%** | 20.6–29.7% (**lowest**) |
| Waxy | 6.86% | 36.7–52.7% (**highest**) |
| Zero-amylose waxy | 6.30% | 34.0–52.5% |
| Normal | 4.38% | 29.8–44.3% |

**Note the inversion that reviews collapse: high-amylose barley has the MOST β-glucan and the
LEAST soluble β-glucan.** For a viscosity-mediated clinical effect that is the wrong trade.

Elite germplasm spans 2.2–8.4% (Houston K et al., *BMC Genomics* 2014;15:907, PMID 25326272,
603 cultivars); a Glacier AC38 × CDC Fibar RIL population spans **4.3–18.4%** with
transgressive segregation both ways (Gianinetti A et al., *Int J Mol Sci* 2024;25(12):6296,
PMID 38928003); oat runs **3.0–7.0%** (Peterson DM, Wesenberg DM, Burrup DE, *Crop Sci*
1995;35:965–70). **Corrected framing: barley and oat overlap almost entirely in ordinary food
germplasm (~3–7%); no oat approaches barley's 10–18% ceiling.**

**DP3:DP4 — my §2.10 hypothesis, tested and found weak.** The structural fingerprint is real
(Cui W, Wood PJ, Blackwell B, Nikiforuk J, *Carbohydr Polym* 2000;41:249–58: **wheat 4.5,
barley 3.3, oat 2.2**), with compiled ranges barley 1.8–3.4, oat 1.5–2.3, wheat 3.0–4.5 and
**rye 1.9–3.0 — rye is at or below barley, not above it** (Lazaridou A, Biliaderis CG,
*J Cereal Sci* 2007;46:101–18).

But three results undercut DP3:DP4 as the explanatory variable. (i) Cory AT, Gangola MP,
Anyia A, Båga M, Chibbar RN, *J Sci Food Agric* 2017;97(3):743–52. DOI 10.1002/jsfa.7789
(PMID 27145288), 89 RILs across two environments: β-glucan, DP3 and DP4 are all **positively
correlated** — raising total content does not shift the ratio — **only DP3:DP4 shows
significant G × E**, and content and ratio map to **separate chromosomal regions**. (ii) A
barley CRISPR *CslF6* dosage series left **DP3:DP4 unchanged at 2.6–3.1 across all genotypes**
while content fell from 5.00% to 0.11%. (iii) There are two competing conformational
regularities, not one — consecutive DP3 blocks *and* long cellulosic DP≥5 runs (~10% of the
chain) — so **DP3:DP4 alone is an inadequate solubility descriptor.**

**The mechanism, solved at atomic resolution.** Purushotham P, Ho R, Yu L, Fincher GB,
Bulone V, Zimmer J. *Sci Adv* 2022;8(45):eadd1596. DOI 10.1126/sciadv.add1596 (PMID 36367939).
CslF6 carries a **switch motif MTASCSAY** where cellulose synthases carry VIGGVSAH — critically
**His→Tyr at 787**. Y787 contacts the C6-OH of the penultimate glucosyl unit and repositions
the acceptor so transfer goes to **C3 instead of C4**. Whether the enzyme engages the second or
third residue from the non-reducing terminus decides whether the (1→3) bond lands after two or
three (1→4) bonds — **DP3:DP4 is set by a register-dependent step in one active site, not by a
separate (1→3)-transferase.**

**Molecular weight × concentration is the operative variable, and the scaling is brutal.**
Wang Q, Ellis PR, *Br J Nutr* 2014;112:S4–S13. DOI 10.1017/S0007114514002256: intrinsic
viscosity follows Mark–Houwink [η] = k·Mw^α with critical overlap at c[η] ≈ 1. At
Mw 1×10⁶ Da, **c\* ≈ 0.1%**; at 1×10⁵ Da, **c\* ≈ 0.5%**. Below c\* specific viscosity scales
as c^1.1; above it, **c^4.4**. **Because the exponent jumps fourfold at c\*, and c\* itself
rises as Mw falls, halving Mw does far more than halve viscosity — it can push a solution out
of the entangled regime altogether.** This is the physics behind Wolever 2010.

**And solubility is non-monotonic.** In an oat-bran muffin series, cutting Mw from 2,200,000 to
400,000 *raised* solubility 44% → 57%, but further reduction to 120,000 **dropped it to 26%** —
heavily depolymerised chains self-associate into insoluble aggregates. Depolymerisation is
doubly damaging at the extreme: less viscosity *and* less soluble material.

**The likely answer to the oat-vs-barley clinical asymmetry.** Enzymatic degradation, not fine
structure. Barley carries **HvGlbI**, a β-glucanase whose transcript peaks around 15 days post
anthesis — the grain degrades its own β-glucan while making it (Garcia-Gimenez et al.,
*Sci Rep* 2019;9:17250) — and β-glucanases act during **mixing, fermentation and proofing, so
the damage is done before the oven** (Rieder A, Ballance S, Løvaas A, Knutsen SH, *LWT*
2015;64:767–74). **Oats are routinely kilned, which inactivates that enzyme; barley usually is
not.** With well-designed extraction, native oat β-glucan is 2–3 × 10⁶ Da and barley clusters
near ~1.5 × 10⁶. **Status: HYPOTHESIS, well-supported, and better supported than the DP3:DP4
story it should replace.**

Processing can be far more destructive than kilning: high-energy extrusion (148 Wh/kg, 237 °C
die) cut oat-bran β-glucan Mw **2,484,000 → 251,000** while *raising* extractability
(Tosh SM et al., *J Agric Food Chem* 2010;58:7723–30. DOI 10.1021/jf904553u, PMID 20527967) —
solubilising material while shortening it, which is exactly the combination Wolever 2010 shows
does not work.

**Genetics, briefly, with two useful negatives.** Complete-loss *betaglucanless* mutants
(Taketa S et al., *J Exp Bot* 2012;63(1):381–92, PMID 21940720) have **no β-glucan anywhere**,
are **viable and fertile**, show chilling injury, and compensate with **1.7–2.0× more
arabinoxylan** — so the polymer is dispensable in barley. Critically, **27 of 30 natural
polymorphisms across 29 accessions lie in introns**, and *HvCslF6* shows **no significant
association in 603 elite cultivars** (all carried the Morex allele regardless of content).
**HYPOTHESIS: CslF6 is the causal biosynthetic gene but is near-fixed in elite germplasm, so
natural β-glucan variation is regulatory rather than structural** — with HvMYB61 and
HvMYB46/83 nominated as activators (Garcia-Gimenez G et al., *Front Plant Sci* 2022;13:883139,
PMID 36160970; **no stable mutant yet**). Practical implication: marker-assisted selection on
CslF6 coding SNPs in adapted material is likely wasted effort.

**Why waxy and high-amylose barleys have more β-glucan: probably carbon partitioning, not cell
wall genetics.** *amo1* sits on **1H**, unlinked to both *Wx* (7HS) and *CslF6* (7HL,
pericentromeric) — so linkage to the biosynthetic gene is ruled out by map geometry. The
unifying evidence is reciprocal: the ***lys5*** mutant of the **plastidial ADP-glucose
transporter HvBT1** — a gene with no connection to cell walls — gives low starch and
**"exceptionally high β-glucan"**; RNAi of all three starch branching enzymes redirects carbon
to protein and β-glucan; and forcing MLG synthesis up **depletes starch and blocks sugar flow
into the endosperm** (Lim WL et al., *J Exp Bot* 2020;71(1):138–53, PMID 31536111), producing
shrunken grain with a central cavity. **Status: HYPOTHESIS, well-supported by reciprocity, not
proven for *Wx* or *amo1* specifically.** It also predicts the Prowashonupana phenotype without
invoking any cell-wall gene.

**Environment.** Direction is consistent across at least five independent studies: **water
deficit and warm, dry grain filling raise grain β-glucan; irrigation and wet, cool grain
filling lower it.** The most functionally important result is Anker-Nilssen K et al.,
*J Cereal Sci* 2008;48(3):670–7. DOI 10.1016/j.jcs.2008.02.009 — under controlled growth
temperature, **water-soluble β-glucan, its viscosity AND its molecular weight all increased
with rising growth temperature.** The dominant environmental effect may be on solubility and
Mw rather than total content, which matters more for function than the content figure printed
on a label.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for content ranges, the DP3:DP4 fingerprint, and the
CslF6 catalytic mechanism; STRONG for the Mw/viscosity scaling law; NEGATIVE for DP3:DP4 as
the explanation of clinical differences; HYPOTHESIS (well-supported) for both the
kilning/β-glucanase explanation of the oat-barley asymmetry and the shared-carbon-pool
explanation of the waxy effect.**

### 2.3 Oats — avenacins, and the mycorrhiza question

**What is established.**
Papadopoulou K, Melton RE, Leggett M, Daniels MJ, Osbourn AE. "Compromised disease
resistance in saponin-deficient plants." *PNAS* 1999;96(22):12923–8.
DOI 10.1073/pnas.96.22.12923 (PMID 10536024). Avenacin A-1 is a triterpene glycoside of
oat **roots**, uniquely UV-fluorescent among saponins; that fluorescence was used to
isolate *saponin-deficient* (*sad*) mutants of diploid *Avena strigosa*, and those mutants
are **compromised in resistance to several fungal pathogens**. This is a clean
genetic-causal demonstration that a plant secondary metabolite is a defence compound.

Mylona P, Owatworakit A, Papadopoulou K, et al. "Sad3 and sad4 are required for saponin
biosynthesis and root development in oat." *Plant Cell* 2008;20(1):201–12.
DOI 10.1105/tpc.107.056531 (PMID 18203919). Nine loci are required for avenacin synthesis,
**eight of them clustered**. *sad3* and *sad4* mutants accumulate monodeglucosyl
avenacin A-1 and show **stunted roots, root-epidermal membrane-trafficking defects and
root-hair deficiency** — i.e. the pathway is entangled with root development, not merely
bolted on.

Later cluster work: Mugford ST et al., *Plant Cell* 2009, DOI 10.1105/tpc.109.065870;
Li Y et al., "Subtelomeric assembly of a multi-gene pathway for antimicrobial defense
compounds in cereals," *Nat Commun* 2021;12, DOI 10.1038/s41467-021-22920-8; Qiao X et al.,
*PNAS* 2025, DOI 10.1073/pnas.2417588122.

**Three corrections to the usual gene list** **[delegated]**. *Sad3* is not an oxidase — it is
a **vacuolar GH1 transglucosidase** adding the terminal glucose (Orme A et al., *PNAS*
2019;116:27105–14, PMID 31806756), which is noncanonical since plant glycosylation is normally
UGT-mediated. *Sad4* is **not in the cluster** at all (chromosome 4; Qiao X et al., *PNAS*
2025;122(12):e2417588122, PMID 40106352). And *Sad2* (AsCYP51H10) was shown by Qi X et al.,
*PNAS* 2006;103:18848–53 (PMID 17124172) to be **"dispensable for synthesis of essential
sterols and recruited for the production of antimicrobial compounds"** — the evolutionary link
to CYP51 is real, the functional one is not. That closes the sterol route to any mycorrhizal
inference. Pathway completion and heterologous reconstitution in *N. benthamiana*: Li Y et
al., *Nat Commun* 2021;12:2563 (PMID 33963185) — the 12-gene cluster "formed de novo in a
subtelomeric region of chromosome 1 that lacks homology with other grasses," with gene order
approximating pathway order.

**Two internal controls in Papadopoulou 1999 that are usually omitted** **[delegated]**:
1,289 M2 families were screened, yielding 10 *sad* mutants; **leaf inoculation with
*Stagonospora* showed no wild-type/mutant difference** (root compound, root phenotype); and
mutant 791, retaining ~15% of wild-type avenacin, developed "only limited lesions"
(dose-dependence). Confound to disclose: 2 of 10 mutants were stunted.

**Host-range demonstration.** Bowyer P, Clarke BR, Lunness P, Daniels MJ, Osbourn AE. "Host
range of a plant pathogenic fungus determined by a saponin detoxifying enzyme." *Science*
1995;267(5196):371–4. DOI 10.1126/science.7824933 (PMID 7824933) **[delegated]**: avenacinase
knockouts in the wheat take-all fungus "were no longer able to infect the saponin-containing
host oats but retained full pathogenicity to wheat." Note the logic — this shows avenacinase
is *necessary* for oat pathogenicity; no gain-of-function transfer was done, so sufficiency is
untested.

**The mycorrhizal claim: resolved, and it is an over-reading of one real paper.** An
exhaustive search — the full Europe PMC "avenacin" corpus (**297 records**, none concerning
mycorrhiza or symbiosis), `saponin AND "arbuscular mycorrhizal"` (217 hits, **none** testing
saponins as AM modulators), and full-text greps of the reviews that would host such a claim
including **the Osbourn lab's own review** of below-ground terpene interactions (Huang AC &
Osbourn A, *Pest Manag Sci* 2019;75:2368–77, PMID 30884099 — zero occurrences of "symbiosis")
— returned nothing **[delegated]**.

What exists is this: **Turner TR, Ramakrishnan K, Walshaw J, …Osbourn A, Grant A, Poole PS.
"Comparative metatranscriptomics reveals kingdom level changes in the rhizosphere microbiome
of plants." *ISME J* 2013;7(12):2248–58. DOI 10.1038/ismej.2013.119 (PMID 23864127)**
**[delegated]**. RNA-based metatranscriptomics of bulk soil and the rhizospheres of wheat,
pea, wild-type oat and the avenacin-deficient ***sad1*** mutant. Verbatim: **"Avenacins had
little effect on the prokaryotic community of oat, but the eukaryotic community was strongly
altered in the *sad1* mutant"** — the affected taxa being **Amoebozoa and Alveolata**.
**Glomeromycota and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi are neither mentioned nor measured.**

That is almost certainly the origin: a genuine finding that avenacins reshape the rhizosphere
*eukaryotic* community, paraphrased forward into an AM-symbiosis claim the paper does not
make. **No study has ever inoculated *sad* mutants with an AM fungus and scored
colonisation.** Two arguments cut against a strong inhibitory role anyway: *A. strigosa* is
a widely used mycorrhizal cover crop that colonises readily, and avenacins are confined to
root-tip epidermis and lateral root initials whereas AM entry and arbuscule formation occur
in more mature root regions — **spatially largely disjunct**.

**Correction to an earlier draft of this document.** I previously wrote that "avenacins are
root compounds and avenacosides are leaf/shoot compounds." **The second half is wrong.**
Avenacosides are abundant in **grain**: 49.6–443.0 mg/kg across 15 commercial oat products
(Yang J, Wang P, Wu W, Zhao Y, Idehen E, Sang S, *J Agric Food Chem* 2016;64(7):1549–56,
PMID 26852819 **[delegated]**), and Hu C & Sang S, *J Agric Food Chem* 2020;68(23):6381–9
(PMID 32436699) **[delegated]** purified and NMR-characterised **13 triterpenoid saponins from
oat bran** at 1.77–18.20 µg/g. So the flat claim "avenacins are not in grain" is a
pre-2016-sensitivity statement that now needs qualifying — though **biosynthesis** remains
strictly confined to root-tip epidermis, and grain triterpenoids run 50–400× below the
steroidal avenacosides.

**And the one properly designed test of oat saponins in nutrition came out null.** Önning G &
Asp N-G, *Br J Nutr* 1995;73(2):275–86 (PMID 7718546) **[delegated]**: cholesterol-containing
diets at **three saponin levels** — zero (ethanol-extracted oats), normal, and double — plus a
cellulose control, **in two species**. Oat diets raised HDL and lowered liver cholesterol
versus cellulose, **but there were no differences among the three saponin levels.** "Oat
saponins had only minor effects on lipid metabolism." This is a real dose-ranging
de-confounding of saponin from oat matrix, and it is negative.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for avenacins as root antifungal defence compounds, for
the gene-cluster architecture, and for the avenacinase host-range result; STRONG that
avenacins alter the rhizosphere eukaryotic community; UNSUPPORTED and never formally proposed
in the primary literature for any AM-symbiosis effect; NULL for oat saponins as the agent of
oat's lipid effects.** The best-established human role for avenacosides is as an **oat-intake
exposure biomarker** (Armeni 2025), and their best-established food role is as **bitter and
astringent principles** of oat flour (Günther-Jordanland K, Dawid C, Dietz M, Hofmann T,
*J Agric Food Chem* 2016;64:9639–52, PMID 27983806 **[delegated]**) — a flavour liability, not
a health property.

### 2.4 Oats — lipid content and why oats are kilned

**What is observed.**
Ekelund S, Gladkauskas E, Krause S, Sirijovski N, Adlercreutz P. "Oat Kilning and Its
Effects on Liquid Oat-Base Production." *Foods* 2024;13(24):4083.
DOI 10.3390/foods13244083 (PMID 39767025). Storing **non-kilned** dehulled oat kernels at
room temperature or 37 °C for up to 64 days produced **increasing lipase activity over
time, despite falling moisture content and water activity** — the authors' point being
that drying alone does not control the enzyme. **Steaming with saturated steam at 90 °C
for 30 min followed by 100 °C drying for 30 min inactivated all lipase** while retaining
42% of total soluble protein. Kilning also drove sensory acceptability.

Corroborating process work: Blontrock E et al., *Food Chem X* 2025,
DOI 10.1016/j.fochx.2025.102834 (enzyme activity and extractability, kilned vs non-kilned
oats); Lambrechts E et al., *Curr Res Food Sci* 2026, DOI 10.1016/j.crfs.2026.101349
(kilning and fermentability); Doehlert DC, Angelikousis S, Vick B, "Accumulation of
Oxygenated Fatty Acids in Oat Lipids During Storage," *Cereal Chem* 2010,
DOI 10.1094/cchem-05-10-0074; Doehlert DC, Zhang D, Moore WR, *J Sci Food Agric* 1997,
DOI 10.1002/(sici)1097-0010(199705)74:1<125::aid-jsfa782>3.0.co;2-s (heat pretreatment and
flour-slurry viscosity).

**"Highest lipid of common cereals" — resolved, and it should not be stated flat.**
The cross-cereal comparison everyone cites is Price PB & Parsons JG, "Lipids of seven cereal
grains," *JAOCS* 1975;52(12):490–3 **[delegated]**: wheat 2.3%, triticale 2.4%, barley 3.2%,
sorghum 3.3%, rye 3.6%, **corn 5.8%, oats 6.6%.** That is **one cultivar per species**, and
oats beat corn by 0.8 percentage points with no replication and no statistics. Modern surveys
give oat 2.4–10.0% (mean 5.8%, 95 Irish cultivars) and 2.0–11.0% across 445 *Avena*
accessions — **a floor below ordinary dent corn** — while high-oil maize breeding has pushed
maize well past 20%. The most careful primary source declines the ranking: Leonova S et al.,
*J Exp Bot* 2010;61(11):3089–99 (PMC2892156) **[delegated]** writes that oat ranges 2–18%
against 2–3% for wheat and barley, "**Maize represents the only comparable exception.**"
**Defensible framing: oat's distinguishing feature is its RANGE, not a ranking.**

**The real lipid singularity is where the oil sits.** Banaś A, Dębski H, …Marttila S, Stymne S,
Carlsson AS. "Lipids in grain tissues of oat: differences in content, time of deposition, and
fatty acid composition." *J Exp Bot* 2007;58(10):2463–70. DOI 10.1093/jxb/erm125
**[delegated]**. Two cultivars, four developmental stages, TLC + GC + light and electron
microscopy: **the endosperm contains 86–90% of total grain lipid; embryo plus scutellum only
10–14%.** In the endosperm the oil bodies **fuse on maturation into a continuous smear**
through the starch and protein matrix, with oleosins at much lower levels than in the embryo.

**This directly causes the rancidity problem: you cannot degerm the oil out of oats, because
the oil is not in the germ.** Caveat: the 86–90% figure comes from one research group and has
**not been independently replicated** despite being very widely repeated.

**Correction — lipoxygenase should not be named as a driver of oat rancidity.**
Tang Y, Zhou C, Yu Z, et al. "Formation of lipid-derived volatile products through
lipoxygenase (LOX)- and hydroperoxide lyase-mediated pathway in oat, barley and soy bean."
*Food Chem X* 2024;22:101514. DOI 10.1016/j.fochx.2024.101514 (PMID 38883919) **[delegated]**:
**"LOX activity was observed only in barley and soy bean, but not in oat"** — soy
553.5–715.9, barley 1.17–2.59, oat **not detected** (µmol min⁻¹ g⁻¹). Consistent with
Lehtinen P & Laakso S, *Agric Food Sci* 2004;13:88–99 **[delegated]**, who attribute oat rancid
flavour to **non-enzymatic** autoxidation. One 2025 paper reporting heat "inactivating
lipoxygenase" measured LOX **protein abundance by proteomics, not activity** — protein present
without measurable activity would reconcile both, and nobody has tested that.

**Two surprises about lipase that undercut the obvious breeding strategy** **[delegated]**.
Dahlgren L et al., *Food Chem* 2026;435:148160 (PMID 41619683) tested lines **bred for reduced
lipase**: "**initial lipase activity did not correlate with FFA formation or lipid oxidation
in the non-kilned material**." And Doehlert 2010 found "oxidized fatty acids… in both
esterified lipids and free fatty acids, indicating that **lipase action was not necessary for
lipid oxidation**." Free fatty acids nonetheless reach ~80% of total lipid within 12 months in
non-kilned oats.

**Peroxidase is the industrial indicator enzyme, and it is a conservative one.**
Pynket I, Janssen F, Van Gils J, Courtin CM, Wouters AGB, *Curr Res Food Sci* 2024;9:100932
**[delegated]**: at 80 °C/30 min/16.5% moisture, **peroxidase fell 33% while lipase fell 89%**;
peroxidase never fully inactivated at 80 °C under any condition tested. Because peroxidase is
strictly harder to kill than lipase, "peroxidase-negative" **guarantees** lipase-negative — at
the cost of protein extractability falling from 85.7% (untreated) to **20.6% (industrially
heat-treated)**. The industry accepts that price for a simple QC test. Note also that **wet
heat works and dry heat does not**: roasting at 106 °C did **not** inactivate lipase while
steaming did, so steaming results must never be generalised to roasting or extrusion.

**No D-values or z-values exist for oat lipase or peroxidase**, and Ekelund 2024 found lipase
inactivation "did not perfectly follow first-order kinetics" — a real gap in a mature
industrial process.

**Correction on kilning and β-glucan — this revises what §2.2 implies.** I framed kilning as a
threat to β-glucan molecular weight. The primary data say otherwise, and are more interesting.
Zhang D, Doehlert DC, Moore WR, *Cereal Chem* 1998;75(4):433–8. DOI 10.1094/cchem.1998.75.4.433
**[delegated]**: **"the average Mr values among raw, roasted and steamed oat samples were
equivalent, but extracts from steamed oat grain had significantly higher intrinsic
viscosity."** Åman P, Rimsten L, Andersson R, *Cereal Chem* 2004;81(3):356–60.
DOI 10.1094/cchem.2004.81.3.356 **[delegated]**: oats, rolled oats, oat bran and bran
concentrate all at 206–230 × 10⁴ g/mol, and **"boiling porridge or frying pancakes did not
result in any β-glucan degradation."** Where degradation did appear (some breads, fresh pasta,
pasteurised juice) the polymodal MW distributions indicated it was **enzymatic**.

**But MW preservation does not guarantee delivered dose.** Grundy MML, Quint J, Rieder A, et
al., *Carbohydr Polym* 2017;175:20–7 (PMC5388193) **[delegated]**: cooking **halved
extractability** (56.3% → 28.8% for flour; 50.5% → 25.1% for flakes) **with molecular weight
essentially unchanged**, attributed to starch gelatinisation physically trapping the β-glucan.

**So there are three separable mechanisms, and the popular one is the weakest:**
β-glucanase inactivation (real, and Zhang 1997 suggests a substantial share of that hydrolase
activity may be **microbial, on the groat surface**); direct thermal depolymerisation
(**small to negligible** under kilning and normal cooking); and physical/conformational
effects (steaming *lowers* extractable quantity while *raising* the viscosity of what is
extracted; cooking halves extractability with no MW change).

**The consequence sharpens the §2.2 critique rather than softening it.** A product can be
perfectly kilned, retain 200 × 10⁴ g/mol β-glucan, and still under-deliver the
physiologically effective dose — because the regulation keys on **grams**, the biology keys on
**MW × solubilised concentration**, and solubilised concentration is decoupled from MW by
cooking and freezing. Dose reality check: a standard 40 g oat portion delivers ~1 g β-glucan,
so meeting the 3 g threshold takes **three standard portions daily** unless a high-β-glucan
cultivar is used **[delegated]**.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for the lipase/rancidity problem being the operative
reason oats are steamed and kilned; STRONG for the endosperm lipid distribution (with a
replication caveat); STRONG that kilning trades enzyme inactivation against protein
solubility; NEGATIVE for lipoxygenase as an oat rancidity driver; and CORRECTED — kilning
does not meaningfully depolymerise β-glucan, but cooking does halve its extractability.**

### 2.4b Oats — avenalin, and why oat protein behaves unlike wheat

All citations in this subsection are **[delegated]**.

**The structural fact.** Shewry PR & Halford NG, *J Exp Bot* 2002;53(370):947–58.
DOI 10.1093/jxb/53.370.947: in oats and rice the **globulins** form the major endosperm
storage protein, "about **70–80%** of the total protein," and are "related to the widely
distributed 'legumin' type globulins which occur in most dicotyledonous species." In wheat,
barley and rye the prolamins are 60–80% of grain protein; **oat prolamins (avenins) are only
about 10%**.

**Characterisation.** Peterson DM, *Plant Physiol* 1978;62(4):506–9 (PMID 16660548): **12.1 S,
MW 322,000**, α-subunit 21,700 and β-subunit 31,700 Da in equimolar ratio, a 6α + 6β hexameric
model. Walburg G & Larkins BA, *Plant Physiol* 1983;72(1):161–5 (PMID 16662952) is decisive
for the legumin relationship: large subunits acidic, small basic, disulfide-linked, and **"the
NH₂-terminal sequence of the small subunits was homologous to a small subunit of soybean
glycinin"**, synthesised as a single Mr 60,000–68,000 precursor and then cleaved.

**Caveat on the percentage.** Reported globulin fractions span **50–90%** across four modern
sources, and this is a methodological artefact: Sagu SR, Wiedemann L, Nichani K, Henze A,
Rawel H, *Separations* 2024;11(9):271. DOI 10.3390/separations11090271 used LC-MS/MS across 24
oat samples and found **avenin-E at 32.2% of the avenin fraction, 58.0% of the "avenalin"
fraction and 59.8% of the albumin/globulin fraction**. Osborne solubility fractions in oat are
not clean protein classes. What rescues the qualitative claim is sequencing, which is
independent of extraction chemistry: **21 avenin genes vs 75 globulin genes** by PacBio
(Leišová-Svobodová L et al., *Sci Rep* 2022;12:8660, PMID 35606450).

**The mechanism that matters nutritionally.** Peterson DM, *Crop Sci* 1976;16(5):663–6: across
nitrogen-rate experiments in three states, "an increase in the total protein level was
primarily related to an increase in the level of the **globulin** fraction… As protein
increased, **the amino acid balance was unchanged.**" In wheat, added nitrogen goes into
lysine-poor prolamins and protein *quality degrades as quantity rises*; in oat it goes into
globulin, so **quality is buffered**. Quantitatively, avenins are 39.4–47.6% Pro+Gln while oat
globulins are 16.7–19.1%.

**Amino acids, oat vs wheat, same laboratory and methods** — L'Hocine L et al., *Nutrients*
2023;15(6):1347 (PMID 36986077), g/100 g protein: **lysine 4.34 vs 2.19**; threonine 3.27 vs
2.33; Met+Cys 2.40 vs 1.53; tryptophan 1.50 vs 1.11. USDA FDC 169705 gives oat lysine
4.15 g/100 g protein — agreement within ~5%.

**Disconfirming, and this is where the marketing fails.** Abelilla JJ, Liu Y, Stein HH.
"Digestible indispensable amino acid score (DIAAS) and PDCAAS in oat protein concentrate
measured in 20- to 30-kilogram pigs." *J Sci Food Agric* 2018;98(1):410–4.
DOI 10.1002/jsfa.8457 (PMID 28573795) — standardised ileal digestibility in the FAO-recommended
model. **DIAAS 41 (infants, aromatic AA limiting), 56 (6 mo–3 y, lysine limiting), 67 (>3 y,
lysine limiting).** FAO thresholds are ≥100 for "excellent" and ≥75 for "good source." **Oat
clears neither.** The authors state oat protein concentrate "needs to be complemented by other
proteins with greater lysine concentrations."

Three further constraints. Oat protein is **less digestible than wheat** (L'Hocine 2023:
IVPD-T 75.0% vs 82.5%) — its quality edge comes entirely from amino acid profile and is
partly given back in digestibility. Higher protein is not better protein: wild *A. sterilis*
at 25.9% protein has **less** lysine than cultivated oat at 17.1% (Briggle LW et al., *Crop
Sci* 1975;15(4):547–9). And **isolating oat protein makes it worse** — in vitro digestibility
26.3% (water-extracted isolate) and 45.3% (acid-precipitated isolate) against **66.2% for
plain oat flour** (Tagle-Freire D et al., *Curr Res Food Sci* 2026, PMID 42359050).

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG that oat storage protein is globulin-dominated and
legumin-like, and STRONG for the quality-buffering mechanism; the specific "70–80%" figure is
SOFT because Osborne fractionation cross-contaminates; and "oat is a complete protein" is
FALSE by the standard scoring method.** "Contains all nine essential amino acids" is
technically true and nutritionally misleading — presence is not sufficiency, which is exactly
what DIAAS exists to measure. No human ileal-digestibility DIAAS for oat protein exists; all
values are pig-model or in vitro, and the two disagree by roughly twofold.

### 2.5 Oats — avenin and coeliac disease

**What is observed.**
Arentz-Hansen H, Fleckenstein B, Molberg Ø, Scott H, Koning F, et al. "The molecular basis
for oat intolerance in patients with celiac disease." *PLoS Med* 2004;1(1):e1.
DOI 10.1371/journal.pmed.0010001 (PMID 15526039). Design: **nine adults** with coeliac
disease and a history of oat exposure. Four had clinical symptoms on an oats-containing
diet; three of those four had coeliac-typical intestinal inflammation at the time. The
authors established **avenin-specific and avenin-reactive intestinal T-cell lines** from
those three patients — and also from two patients who appeared to tolerate oats.

Comino I, Real A, de Lorenzo L, et al. "Diversity in oat potential immunogenicity: basis
for the selection of oat varieties with no toxicity in coeliac disease." *Gut*
2011;60(7):915–22. DOI 10.1136/gut.2010.225268 (PMID 21317420). Oat cultivars fell into
**three groups by monoclonal antibody G12 reactivity** — considerable, slight, and no
detectable reactivity — and G12 reactivity **correlated directly** with T-cell
proliferation and interferon-γ release from coeliac patients' PBMCs.

Real A, Comino I, de Lorenzo L, et al. "Molecular and immunological characterization of
gluten proteins isolated from oat cultivars that differ in toxicity for celiac disease."
*PLoS One* 2012;7(12):e48365. DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0048365 (PMID 23284616). Avenin
sequences fall into three groups homologous to S-rich Triticeae prolamins; **avenins have
lower proline content than wheat gliadin**, which the authors propose contributes to lower
toxicity; oats carry both monomeric and polymeric avenins.

**Disconfirming / complicating.** Dvořáček V et al., "Specific Avenin Cross-Reactivity
with G12 Antibody in a Wide Range of Current Oat Cultivars," *Foods* 2022;11(4):567.
DOI 10.3390/foods11040567 (PMID 35206043). Two-year study of **132 oat cultivars**. Its
stated framing is that some published cultivar differences may reflect **contamination
with gluten-rich species or random cross-reactivity of homologous oat epitopes with
anti-gliadin antibodies** rather than true immunogenicity — i.e. the G12 assay that
underpins the cultivar-toxicity literature may partly be measuring an artefact.

Current clinical position: ESsCD 2025 updated guidelines, Al-Toma A et al.,
*United European Gastroenterol J* 2026;14. DOI 10.1002/ueg2.70195 (PMID 41831197).
**The specific oat recommendation was not extracted in this pass** — go to the document
itself for the graded statement.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG that avenin-reactive T cells exist in a minority of
coeliac patients; MODERATE that oat cultivars differ in immunogenicity; CONTESTED whether
the cultivar differences are real or partly assay artefact.** The honest summary is: oats
are tolerated by most coeliac patients but not by all, the mechanism of intolerance in the
minority is demonstrated at cell level, and the tool used to rank cultivars is itself under
question.

### 2.6 Barley — hulless, tocotrienols, and the compounds that do not deliver

**Hulless barley — established genetics.**
Taketa S, Amano S, Tsujino Y, et al. "Barley grain with adhering hulls is controlled by an
ERF family transcription factor gene regulating a lipid biosynthesis pathway." *PNAS*
2008;105(10):4062–7. DOI 10.1073/pnas.0711034105 (PMID 18316719). The covered/naked
caryopsis is controlled by a single locus (*nud*, 7HL) encoding an **ERF-family
transcription factor** homologous to Arabidopsis WIN1/SHN1, expressed **strictly in the
testa**. A 17-kb deletion removing the gene is **fixed in all 100 naked cultivars
studied** — evidence for a **monophyletic origin of naked barley**. Mechanism: the gene
controls a lipid-biosynthesis pathway, i.e. the hull adheres via a testa lipid layer.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG.** This is a clean positional-cloning result, and the
single-origin conclusion is well supported.

**Tocotrienols and HMG-CoA reductase — where the story splits.**
Parker RA, Pearce BC, Clark RW, Gordon DA, Wright JJ. "Tocotrienols regulate cholesterol
production in mammalian cells by post-transcriptional suppression of
3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A reductase." *J Biol Chem* 1993;268(15):11230–8.
DOI 10.1016/s0021-9258(18)82115-9 (PMID 8388388). γ-Tocotrienol inhibited ¹⁴C-acetate (but
not ³H-mevalonate) incorporation into cholesterol; **~50% inhibition at ≈2 µM**, maximum
~80% within 6 h in HepG2 cells. HMG-CoA reductase protein fell; **synthesis rate dropped
to 57% of control while degradation rate rose 2.4-fold**. Related: Pearce BC et al.,
*J Med Chem* 1992;35:3595–606, DOI 10.1021/jm00098a002.

**The mechanism paper is real and is cell-culture at micromolar concentrations.** The
human clinical claims attached to barley tocotrienols are a separate matter: I searched for
Qureshi's barley-tocotrienol human trials and **the 1991 human trial did not surface in
this retrieval**; what did surface under that author name were later, low-visibility
outlets (e.g. *Ann Clin Case Rep* 2022; *J Clin Exp Cardiolog* 2016,
DOI 10.4172/2155-9880.1000434). A recent meta-analysis of tocotrienol-rich fraction is
**animal-model only**: Abdah HW et al., *Sci Rep* 2025, DOI 10.1038/s41598-025-11160-1;
the human systematic review that did surface is for **palm**, not barley: Looi AD et al.,
*Nutr Rev* 2025, DOI 10.1093/nutrit/nuae061.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for the in vitro post-transcriptional HMGCR mechanism
at ~2 µM; UNSUPPORTED (here) for the claim that eating barley delivers a
tocotrienol-mediated cholesterol effect in humans.** The two should never be stated in the
same breath. Note also that §2.2 already gives barley a demonstrated LDL effect — via
β-glucan, at 6.5 g/d — so the tocotrienol story is not needed to explain barley's lipid
effect and should not be smuggled in as a second mechanism.

**Hordenine — the popular claim inverts the literature.** Searching for hordenine returned
essentially no support for the nootropic/MAO-B/dopaminergic supplement claims. What it did
return: Wu Y, Li T, Zhou NY, "Microbial degradation of two major **phytotoxic** alkaloids
from barley," *J Hazard Mater* 2025, DOI 10.1016/j.jhazmat.2025.139299 — i.e. hordenine is
studied as a **phytotoxin**; and Xu Z et al., *LWT*, on hordenine as a **quorum-sensing
inhibitor** against *Bacillus cereus*. A single conference abstract on scopolamine-induced
models (Singhal & Agrawal, *Alzheimers Dement* 2025, no DOI/PMID) is not evidence.
**Evidence-strength verdict: UNSUPPORTED for any human nootropic or metabolic claim.**

**Proanthocyanidin-free barley — real breeding genetics.** Molobekova CA et al.,
"Breeding for the absence of proanthocyanidins in grain of barley," *Vavilov J Genet Breed*
2025, DOI 10.18699/vjgb-25-142; Takahashi A et al., *Breed Sci* 2025,
DOI 10.1270/jsbbs.24080 (a six-rowed waxy hull-less high-β-glucan proanthocyanidin-free
cultivar). **Verdict: established as plant breeding; no human health claim attached.**

### 2.7 Barley — stress and salinity tolerance

**The quantitative fact.** FAO Irrigation and Drainage Paper 29 rev. 1 (Ayers & Westcot),
Table 4, after Maas & Hoffman: https://www.fao.org/4/t0234e/T0234E03.htm

| Crop | ECe threshold (dS/m) | % yield loss per dS/m above threshold | Rating |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Barley (grain)** | **8.0** | ~5.2 | **Tolerant** |
| Sorghum | 6.8 | ~3.4 | Moderately tolerant |
| Wheat (grain) | 6.0 | ~5.4 | Moderately tolerant |
| Rice (paddy) | 3.0 | ~2.6 | Moderately sensitive |
| Maize | 1.7 | ~4.1 | Moderately sensitive |

Oats are not in Table 4; they appear in Table 5 rated moderately tolerant. **Important
footnote carried in the source:** barley is "less tolerant during germination and seedling
stage; ECe should not exceed 4–5 dS/m". So the headline tolerance is a *mature-plant*
figure, and the crop's real-world limit is often set by establishment.

**Mechanism — and a genuine surprise.**
Kuang L, Zhou H, Zhang T, et al. "MicroRNA164d suppresses the HvNAC92-HvHKT1;5 module to
enhance salinity tolerance in barley." *PNAS* 2025;122. DOI 10.1073/pnas.2514555122
(PMID 41433060). In salt-*sensitive* cereals, HKT1;5 confers tolerance by mediating
shoot-to-root Na⁺ **exclusion**. In salt-tolerant barley, **HvHKT1;5 paradoxically promotes
root-to-shoot Na⁺ translocation**. The paper shows a miR164d–HvNAC92–HvHKT1;5 module in
which miR164d downregulates HvHKT1;5, reducing shoot Na⁺ and **increasing K⁺ retention**.

This is worth stating plainly because it contradicts the textbook story: **barley's
tolerance is not simply "better sodium exclusion".** It involves shoot tissue tolerance and
potassium retention, and its HKT1;5 works in the opposite direction to rice's and wheat's.

Context/reviews retrieved: Shabala S, Chen X, Yun P, Zhou M, "Salinity tolerance in wheat:
rethinking the targets," *J Exp Bot* 2026, DOI 10.1093/jxb/eraf152; Raza A, Zaman QU,
Shabala S, Tester M, Munns R, et al., *Plant Biotechnol J* 2025, DOI 10.1111/pbi.70104;
Wegner LH, Pottosin I, Dreyer I, Shabala S, "Potassium homeostasis and signalling,"
*Quant Plant Biol* 2025, DOI 10.1017/qpb.2025.10.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for barley being the most salt-tolerant common cereal
by agronomic threshold; MODERATE and actively revising for the physiological basis.** The
K⁺-retention framework has strong support across the Shabala programme; the specific
miR164d module is a single 2025 paper and should be treated as new, not settled.

### 2.8 Millets, wild rice, and the minor grains

**Finger millet calcium — the claim survives on content and is unproven on absorption.**
Teklu D, Gashu D, Joy EJM, Bailey EH, Wilson L, Amede T, Broadley MR, et al. "Differences
in the nutritional quality of improved finger millet genotypes in Ethiopia." *Sci Rep*
2024;14. DOI 10.1038/s41598-023-48749-3 (PMID 38172143). Design: **15 genotypes**,
research-station grown 2019–2020, RCBD, three replicates. Measured:
**Ca 3762 ± 332 to 5893 ± 353 mg/kg** (= **376–589 mg/100 g**) — an order of magnitude
above ordinary cereals; Zn 19.9–26.2 mg/kg; Fe 36.3–52.9 mg/kg; Se 36.6–60.9 µg/kg.
Antinutrients: phytate 308–360 µg/g; tannin 0.15–0.51 mg/g; oxalate 1.26–4.41 mg/g.

**Read the bioavailability sentence carefully.** The authors state that
"antinutritional factors to minerals molar ratio method was used to **estimate** mineral
bioavailability" and that ratios were "below the threshold for low mineral bioavailability".
That is a **calculated proxy, not a measurement**. No absorption was measured. I searched
specifically for stable-isotope or human/rat absorption studies of finger millet calcium
and **found none in this pass**.

Two further cautions. (i) The reported phytate (308–360 µg/g ≈ 0.031–0.036 g/100 g) is
roughly an order of magnitude **below** commonly reported finger-millet phytate values;
either these genotypes are unusual or the assay differs from the norm. Do not propagate
this number without checking. (ii) Oxalate at up to 4.41 mg/g is present and is a
calcium-binding species the molar-ratio method used here does not fully account for.

**Independent confirmation of the content claim.** Amalraj A, Pius A. "Influence of Oxalate,
Phytate, Tannin, Dietary Fiber, and Cooking on Calcium Bioavailability of Commonly Consumed
Cereals and Millets in India." *Cereal Chem* 2015;92:389–394. DOI 10.1094/CCHEM-11-14-0225-R
**[delegated]** — total Ca across Indian cereals 10.2–324.6 mg/100 g, **finger millet
highest**. Compiled genotype values in Puranik S et al., "Harnessing Finger Millet to Combat
Calcium Deficiency in Humans," *Front Plant Sci* 2017;8:1311. DOI 10.3389/fpls.2017.01311
**[delegated]**: 344 mg/100 g typical, wild *E. coracana africana* 515, high-Ca genotypes
CO 11 at 487.

**Now the mechanism that makes the nutrition claim doubtful.** Sharma D, Jamra G, Singh UM,
Sood S, Kumar A. *Front Plant Sci* 2017;7:2028. DOI 10.3389/fpls.2016.02028 **[delegated]**:
the seed coat is the major Ca²⁺ storage tissue, and the high grain calcium is attributed to
**"prominent, insoluble calcium oxalate crystals (especially in seed coat) and calcium
phytates in the embryo."** Onipe OO, Ramashia SE, *Molecules* 2022;27(22):7837.
DOI 10.3390/molecules27227837 **[delegated]**: the seed coat holds ~90% of the grain's
polyphenols and fibre and its Ca and P are "6- to 25-fold that of other cereals."

**Calcium oxalate is essentially non-absorbable.** And the decortication result is the
internal tell: Puranik 2017 reports that **decortication raises Ca bioaccessibility by 37.5%
while cutting total Ca by 40%** (citing Krishnan et al. 2012). Removing 40% of the calcium
makes the remainder *more* available — exactly what you expect if the removed fraction is
oxalate-bound seed-coat calcium.

**The bioavailability evidence base, in full.** Anitha S, Givens DI, Botha R, Kane-Potaka J,
et al. "Calcium from Finger Millet — A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis on Calcium
Retention, Bone Resorption, and In Vitro Bioavailability." *Sustainability* 2021;13(16):8677.
DOI 10.3390/su13168677 **[delegated]** reports "significant retention of 23.4 ± 2.9%" —
**from two eligible studies, balance studies published in 1955 and 1959.** A dedicated
Europe PMC query for stable-isotope or fractional-absorption calcium studies in millet
returned **zero**. The only stable-isotope human absorption study using finger millet is for
**iron**: Reddy BHR et al., *Front Nutr* 2021, DOI 10.3389/fnut.2021.791392 (PMID 35402470)
**[delegated]** — dual-isotope erythrocyte incorporation in Indian women, fractional iron
absorption **3.7% (low-phytate accession) vs 1.3% (high-phytate), p<0.05**. Note what that
demonstrates: finger millet's own antinutrients measurably suppress mineral absorption in
humans.

**The one human bone trial is confounded by design.** Sahaya Rani G, Swaminathan A,
Vijayaraghavan R. *Evid Based Complement Alternat Med* 2021;2021:4757991.
DOI 10.1155/2021/4757991 **[delegated]**. 150 premenopausal women with low BMD; the
experimental arm received **physical activity 5 days/week *plus* finger millet 3 days/week**.
The millet effect is not identifiable.

**A framing correction worth making explicitly.** The stock phrase "three times the calcium
of milk" compares **dry grain to fluid milk**. Milk is ~87% water at ~120 mg Ca/100 mL;
finger millet is eaten as hydrated porridge or balls. Per serving as eaten, and before any
bioavailability correction, the multiple largely disappears. (Arithmetic, not a citation.)

**A question nobody has asked.** Cercamondi CI et al., *Int J Vitam Nutr Res*
2017;87(1-2):75–84, DOI 10.1024/0300-9831/a000422 **[delegated]** found that **86–97% of the
iron in Ethiopian teff injera batter is extrinsic soil contamination** from threshing and
grinding, not intrinsic grain iron. Whether any part of finger millet's exceptional calcium
is likewise extraneous has, as far as this retrieval found, **never been investigated**.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG that finger millet is exceptionally calcium-dense;
UNKNOWN whether that calcium is absorbed at a nutritionally meaningful rate, with the
oxalate localisation and the decortication result both pointing the wrong way.** This is the
single largest unfilled hole in the millet literature. Anyone asserting "finger millet fixes
calcium deficiency" is resting on two balance studies from the Eisenhower administration and
one confounded trial.

**Seed-coat polyphenols — real, and located in the same tissue as the problem.**
Shobana S, Sreerama YN, Malleshi NG. *Food Chem* 2009;115(4):1268–73.
DOI 10.1016/j.foodchem.2009.01.042 **[delegated]**: finger millet seed-coat phenolics inhibit
α-glucosidase (IC₅₀ 16.9 µg) and pancreatic amylase (IC₅₀ 23.5 µg), non-competitively.
Rat: Shobana S et al., *Br J Nutr* 2010;104(12):1787–95. DOI 10.1017/S0007114510002977
**[delegated]** — 20% seed-coat matter, 6 weeks, STZ diabetic rats: less hyperglycaemia,
nephro- and lens-protection. **The catch: the enzyme-inhibiting seed coat is the same
fraction carrying the oxalate that blocks the calcium.** They cannot be separated by
processing — decortication removes both.

**Kodo millet poisoning — genuinely distinctive, and real.**
Deepika C, Hariprasanna K, Das IK, Jacob J, Ronanki S, Ratnavathi CV, et al.
"'Kodo poisoning': cause, science and management." *J Food Sci Technol* 2022;59.
DOI 10.1007/s13197-021-05141-1 (PMID 35734115). Kodo millet (*Paspalum scrobiculatum*)
consumption is "often hindered by the condition called 'kodo poisoning' resulting from
**fungal contamination producing cyclopiazonic acid**". The authors describe the available
information as **limited** and call for work on host resistance and pre-/post-harvest
management.
**The index toxicology paper.** Lalitha Rao B, Husain A. "Presence of cyclopiazonic acid in
kodo millet (*Paspalum scrobiculatum*) causing 'kodua poisoning' in man and its production by
associated fungi." *Mycopathologia* 1985;89(3):177–80. DOI 10.1007/BF00447028 (PMID 3921845)
**[delegated]**. CPA was isolated from a grain sample that had caused human illness; the
grain carried ***Aspergillus flavus* and *A. tamarii***, both of which produced CPA in
culture; the toxic extract injected into mice caused "depression and complete loss of
mobility." This is a **single-sample case-linked isolation, not epidemiology** — n is not
stated for grain samples or mice.

**The molecular target is known and specific.** Seidler NW, Jona I, Vegh M, Martonosi A.
"Cyclopiazonic acid is a specific inhibitor of the Ca²⁺-ATPase of sarcoplasmic reticulum."
*J Biol Chem* 1989;264(30):17816–23 (PMID 2530215) **[delegated]** — stoichiometric SERCA
inhibition, IC₅₀ ≈ 0.6 µM, with **no** comparable action on Na⁺/K⁺-, H⁺/K⁺-, F1- or
erythrocyte Ca²⁺-ATPases.

**Dose.** Nishie K, Cole RJ, Dorner JW. *Food Chem Toxicol* 1985;23(9):831–9.
DOI 10.1016/0278-6915(85)90284-4 (PMID 4043883) **[delegated]**: mouse i.p. LD50
**13 ± 0.05 mg/kg**; syndrome of hypokinesia, hypothermia, catalepsy, ptosis, action tremor,
convulsions. Oral LD50 in mice **64 ± 4.4 mg/kg** (Nishie et al., *Res Commun Chem Pathol
Pharmacol* 1987;55(3):303–15, PMID 3575873). Safety assessment: Burdock GA, Flamm WG,
*Int J Toxicol* 2000;19(3):195–218. DOI 10.1080/10915810050074964 **[delegated]** — **not a
potent acute toxin**, oral LD50 30–70 mg/kg, target organs muscle, liver and spleen, proposed
ADI ≈ 10 µg/kg/day.

**A modern clinical case consistent with the mechanism.** Surendar R et al., *Asia Pac J Med
Toxicol* 2021;10(2):65–68. DOI 10.22038/apjmt.2021.18231 **[delegated]**: 48-year-old woman,
vomiting, disorientation, tremors, **pulse 50/min, sinus bradycardia, ST depression V3–V6**,
troponin negative; resolved in 24 h. **No toxin assay was performed** — the CPA attribution
is inferential. Historical descriptions of the syndrome (Chevers 1870; Swarup 1922, four
cases with unconsciousness lasting 1–3 days; Bhide 1962, "delirium with violent tremors")
are catalogued in Deepika 2022; the local names are *Matawna Kodoo* / *Malona*.

**Evidence-strength verdict: ESTABLISHED as a phenomenon with a coherent molecular
mechanism, THIN as a literature, and unmonitored.** Deepika 2022 gives **no ppm figures and
no percentage of positive samples**; there is **no published contamination survey, no
regulatory CPA limit for kodo, and no surveillance programme** — at exactly the moment kodo
is being scaled up under India's millet promotion. Note also that the toxicity is **not
intrinsic to the grain** but to a mycotoxigenic contaminant, which is why handling and
post-harvest practice matter. The claim that ergot is an alternative cause has **no primary
source** — see §2.10.

This is a rare case where the Āyurvedic record and the modern record concern the same plant.
Part 1 records that Caraka ranks *kodrava* the best *rūkṣaṇa* food and says nothing about
poisoning. The two are not describing the same thing and must not be merged.

**Pearl millet goitrogens — genuinely distinctive, and well established.**
Gaitan E, Lindsay RH, Reichert RD, Ingbar SH, et al. "Antithyroid and goitrogenic effects of
millet: role of C-glycosylflavones." *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 1989;68:707–14.
DOI 10.1210/jcem-68-4-707 (PMID 2921306) **[delegated]**, confirmed in vivo by Gaitan E et
al., *J Clin Endocrinol Metab* 1995;80:1144–7. DOI 10.1210/jcem.80.4.7714083 (PMID 7714083)
**[delegated]**: the C-glycosylflavones **glucosylvitexin, glycosylorientin and vitexin**
inhibit thyroid peroxidase concentration-dependently, and vitexin inhibits the coupling
mechanism without suppressing iodine uptake. The rebuttal — Anitha S et al., *Front Nutr*
2024;11:1323336 (PMID 38515523) — comes from the same millet-promotion programme discussed
below, and should be read alongside the Gaitan primary work rather than instead of it.

**Proso millet's one genuinely species-specific molecule: miliacin.** Olean-18-en-3β-ol
methyl ether, uniquely abundant in proso and used by archaeologists as a millet biomarker in
pottery residues and dental calculus — Heron C et al., *Sci Rep* 2016;6:38767.
DOI 10.1038/srep38767 (PMID 28004742) **[delegated]**. **No metabolic or pharmacological
bioactivity data exist for isolated miliacin.** The separate proso-millet-protein HDL and
adiponectin literature is **nine papers from one laboratory (Nishizawa/Nagasawa, Iwate
University, 1990–2009), n = 6–8 per group, unadjusted t-tests, protein concentrates, no human
data, no independent replication in over three decades** — and the lab's own fractionation
experiment (Nishizawa et al., *Biosci Biotechnol Biochem* 2009;73(2):351–60, PMID 19202295
**[delegated]**) failed to reproduce the HDL and adiponectin endpoints with the nominated
active prolamin fraction.

**Millets and glycaemia — advocacy-adjacent meta-analyses.**
Anitha S, Kane-Potaka J, Tsusaka TW, Botha R, Rajendran A, Givens DI, et al.
"A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of the Potential of Millets for Managing and
Reducing the Risk of Developing Diabetes Mellitus." *Front Nutr* 2021;8:687428.
DOI 10.3389/fnut.2021.687428 (PMID 34395493). 65 studies collected; **39 studies /
111 observations** for GI; 56 studies for fasting/post-prandial glucose, insulin index,
HbA1c. Headline: **mean GI of millets 52.7 ± 10.3, ~36% lower than milled rice
(71.7 ± 14.4) and refined wheat (74.2 ± 14.9)**; minimally processed millets 30% more
effective at lowering meal GI than milled rice/refined wheat.
Companion: Anitha S, Botha R, Kane-Potaka J, Givens DI, et al., "Can Millet Consumption
Help Manage Hyperlipidemia and Obesity?" *Front Nutr* 2021;8:700778.
DOI 10.3389/fnut.2021.700778 (PMID 34485362) — **19 studies**, 21 days to 4 months,
reductions in TC, TG, LDL-C, VLDL-C.

**Disconfirming context.** These are not independent assessments. The author group is
ICRISAT-affiliated and the wider programme is explicitly promotional — the same group
authored "How the Smart Food Concept Can Lead to the Transformation of Food Systems"
(*World Rev Nutr Diet* 2020, DOI 10.1159/000507494) and a systematic review defending pearl
millet against the goitre claim (*Front Nutr* 2024, DOI 10.3389/fnut.2024.1323336). A
comparison of GI across "millets" as a category also aggregates botanically unrelated
species (sorghum, foxtail, barnyard, kodo, proso, fonio, teff, Job's tears) whose starch
and polyphenol chemistry differ, and mixes processing states. **I did not find an
independent meta-analysis of millet GI to weigh against these.**

**The direct contradiction.** Malavika M, Shobana S, Vijayalakshmi P, …Mohan V, Sudha V.
"Assessment of quality of minor millets available in the south Indian market & glycaemic
index of cooked unpolished little & foxtail millet." *Indian J Med Res* 2020;152(4):401–9.
DOI 10.4103/ijmr.ijmr_2309_18 (PMID 33380705) **[delegated]**. **12 healthy volunteers**,
standard international GI methodology: **unpolished foxtail millet GI 88.6 ± 8.7; unpolished
little millet GI 88.6 ± 5.7; white rice GI 82.5 ± 8.8.** The millets scored **higher than
white rice.** The same paper surveyed 48 brands across 100 retailers and found **~90% of
market millet samples heavily polished** despite label claims, market fibre only 1–2%, and
60% of consumers unaware millets are polished like rice — "product labels were misleading."
Conclusion: substituting millets for white rice is "of limited benefit considering the
glycaemic property."

Anitha 2021 pools foxtail at GI 54.5. Malavika measures 88.6. **These cannot both describe
the same food.** One is a pooled estimate over heterogeneous published values from a
millet-promotion programme; the other is a direct measurement by a diabetes research group
with no promotional mandate.

**The best-powered millet RCT missed its primary endpoint.** Duraiswamy M, Jayaseelan V,
Ramakrishnan J, et al. "Effect of Millets Once a Day on Glycaemic Control among Women with
Gestational Diabetes Mellitus — A Randomized Controlled Trial." *Indian J Endocrinol Metab*
2024;28(6):581–8. DOI 10.4103/ijem.ijem_314_24 (PMID 39881761) **[delegated]**. Parallel-arm,
**n = 224 randomised, 219 completed (97.8%)**, 200 g millets/day vs enhanced medical
nutrition therapy, 1 month. **Primary outcome: an additional 11.6% achieved glycaemic
control, 95% CI −1.5% to 24.7%, p = 0.083 — not significant.** Secondary postprandial glucose
−4.55 mg/dL (95% CI −8.55 to −0.56, p = 0.025). Authors: "Both interventions effectively
controlled glycaemic levels."

**Independent syntheses are far more cautious.** Naik NS, Nadiger N, Mukhopadhyay A.
*Diabetes Metab Syndr* 2026;20(1):103359. DOI 10.1016/j.dsx.2025.103359 (PMID 41447873)
**[delegated]** — an independent systematic review finding **six studies total** (one RCT,
three randomised-crossover, two non-randomised), against Anitha's 65, and concluding that
robust evidence does not yet exist. Editorial: Raizada N, Madhu SV. "Can millets mitigate
diabetes?" *Indian J Endocrinol Metab* 2024;28(6):551–3. DOI 10.4103/ijem.ijem_514_24
(PMID 39881758) **[delegated]** — most studies showed only "mild reduction in post-prandial
glucose"; the HbA1c intervention periods "was not long enough to comment on HbA1c"; "clinical
data on millets is still sparse."

**Anitha 2021's own numbers undercut the grain-centric story.** Its regression found cooking
method shifting GI by **+18.4 units (steaming), +16.4 (baking/roasting), +11.3 (boiling)** —
comparable in magnitude to the between-grain differences. And its companion child-growth
paper (Anitha S et al., *Nutrients* 2022;14(1):225, DOI 10.3390/nu14010225, PMID 35011100)
contains its own refutation in the text: when "an enhanced and diverse diet was served,
replacing rice with millet had only minimal growth improvement." Against an adequate diet,
the millet effect largely vanishes — it is a diet-quality effect measured against poor
rice-based diets. Note also that the meta-analysis handled funnel asymmetry by **trim-and-fill
applied until the plot became symmetrical**, which is a strong intervention that should be
reported with pre- and post-adjustment estimates.

**Evidence-strength verdict: WEAK, and the headline number is contradicted by direct
measurement.** The most defensible statement — the one their own regression supports and that
requires trusting nobody — is that **processing state (polishing, cooking method, cooling and
retrogradation, structural integrity) dominates species identity**. Since ~90% of marketed
millets are heavily polished, the "millets are low-GI" claim describes a preparation almost
nobody actually eats.

**Wild rice — a taxonomic warning first.** North American wild rice is *Zizania palustris* /
*Z. aquatica*; the genus differs from *Oryza* at base chromosome number (x = 15 vs x = 12),
which is genus-level separation, not a naming quibble. *Z. palustris* is native to North
America (McGilp L et al., *Ecol Evol* 2025;15(3):e71033. DOI 10.1002/ece3.71033,
PMID 40092897 **[delegated]**). **Zizania has no native Indian distribution, so Vedic and
Āyurvedic *nīvāra* cannot refer to it.** See §1.5 for the *Hygroryza* vs wild-*Oryza*
dispute. A further caution: *Oryza nivara* was erected by **Sharma & Shastry in 1965**, so
using that modern binomial to prove what a Vedic word meant is circular.

**Composition (USDA FoodData Central, SR Legacy, per 100 g raw)** **[delegated]**: wild rice
protein 14.73 g vs brown rice 7.54; fibre 6.20 vs 3.60; magnesium 177 vs 116 mg; phosphorus
433 vs 311 mg; zinc 5.96 vs 2.13 mg. **But it is not uniformly superior: selenium 2.8 vs
17.1 µg (≈6× lower), manganese 1.33 vs 2.85 mg, calcium lower than white rice.** And the
lysine claim needs splitting: absolute lysine is 2.08× brown rice, but **per gram of protein
it is only ~6% higher** (42.7 vs 40.2 mg/g), with threonine and tryptophan *lower*. The
protein advantage is a quantity effect; wild rice protein remains a lysine-limited cereal
protein.

**The cardiovascular literature is mouse-only, one laboratory, at implausible doses.** All
*Z. palustris* cardiometabolic work traces to the University of Manitoba / St Boniface group
(Moghadasian, Shen, Beta, Aliani). Surendiran G et al., *Atherosclerosis* 2013;230(2):284–92.
DOI 10.1016/j.atherosclerosis.2013.07.042 (PMID 24075758) **[delegated]**: LDLr-knockout
mice, 24 weeks, aortic lesion −71% (M) / −61% (F) — but **"the antiatherogenic effect of wild
rice was not associated with increased superoxide dismutase and catalase activities"**, and
males had the *smaller* cholesterol reduction with the *larger* lesion reduction, inverting
the proposed dose-response. Moghadasian MH et al., *J Agric Food Chem* 2017;65(41):9054–60.
DOI 10.1021/acs.jafc.7b03216 (PMID 28942638) **[delegated]** then reported that "white or wild
rice did not significantly alter the levels of cholesterol, triglycerides, or antioxidant
enzymes in plasma" — contradicting the 2013 mechanism from overlapping authors in the same
model. Moghadasian MH et al., *Nutrients* 2019;11(12):2894. DOI 10.3390/nu11122894
(PMID 31795092) **[delegated]**, n = 8/group: lesion area −75%, but **plasma glucose increased
61%**, which the authors concede "begs more investigation."

**Dose and human data.** The atherosclerosis studies fed **60% of diet by weight**; the
insulin-resistance work 26 wt%. **No published human trial of wild rice grain on any
cardiometabolic outcome exists.** The one registered trial, NCT04119791, is **single-group,
open-label, n = 48, ~30 g/day**, run by the same lab that produced the mouse work, with final
completion 2028 and no results — a design that cannot establish causal lipid effects.
A published human RCT does exist but is a **different species and a different product**:
Ahn YJ et al., *Foods* 2024;13(11):1725. DOI 10.3390/foods13111725 (PMID 38890953)
**[delegated]** — enzyme-treated ***Zizania latifolia*** ethanol extract, 400 mg/day, 8 weeks,
100 Korean males; ALT and hepatic steatosis index significant between groups; **HDL-C change
was within-group only and not significant between groups**; two authors employed by the
extract supplier; 23 of 100 randomised subjects excluded post hoc.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for the composition table; UNSUPPORTED for any human
cardiovascular benefit; and the *nīvāra* connection is a category error in both directions.**

### 2.9 Phytate, phytase, fermentation, resistant starch, microbiome

**Intrinsic phytase differs enormously between cereals — measured.**
Greiner R, Egli I. "Determination of the activity of acidic phytate-degrading enzymes in
cereal seeds." *J Agric Food Chem* 2003;51(3):847–50. DOI 10.1021/jf0204405 (PMID 12568536).
Direct incubation of dry-milled grain at pH 5.0: **rye 6752 mU/g, wheat 2931 mU/g, barley
2093 mU/g**. Aqueous extraction recovered only 30–50% of activity (rye 3443, wheat 1026,
barley 1032 mU/g), and activity was partly membrane-associated. **Rye ≫ wheat > barley.**

Note precisely what this does and does not show: **it measured rye, wheat and barley. It
did not measure oats, maize or the millets.** The widely-repeated statement that oats and
maize have negligible intrinsic phytase is consistent with the literature but is **not
established by this paper**, and I did not retrieve a primary source for it here.

Corroborating applied result: Koréissi-Dembélé Y, Fanou-Fogny N, Moretti D, et al.
"Dephytinisation with intrinsic wheat phytase and iron fortification significantly increase
iron absorption from fonio meals." *PLoS One* 2013;8(10):e70613.
DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0070613 (PMID 24124445) — a **human iron-absorption** study showing
that removing phytate raises iron absorption from a minor-cereal meal.

**The oat question is now answered, and the usual phrasing is wrong.** Blontrock E,
Lambrechts E, Janssen F, De Bondt Y, et al. "Enzyme activity and constituent extractability of
kilned and non-kilned oats at pH values relevant for acidic food fermentations." *Food Chem X*
2025;29:102834. DOI 10.1016/j.fochx.2025.102834 (PMID 40799189) **[delegated]**: non-kilned
oats showed higher activity across protease, xylanase, β-glucanase, amylase, **phytase** and
lipase, and **incubated non-kilned oats achieved 43–49% phytate hydrolysis versus
substantially less in kilned oats**. So: **raw oats have appreciable phytase; commercial
kilned oats do not.** The correct statement is about the *product*, not the *species* — and
it connects directly to §2.4: the same heat treatment applied to kill lipase also kills
phytase.

**How much phytate actually matters — the human dose-response.** This is the best-quantified
part of the whole topic and the numbers are startlingly small.
Hallberg L, Rossander L, Skånberg AB. *Am J Clin Nutr* 1987;45:988–96.
DOI 10.1093/ajcn/45.5.988 (PMID 3034044) **[delegated]**: **"as little as 5–10 mg phytate
phosphorus added to a wheat roll containing 3 mg iron inhibited iron absorption by 50 per
cent."** Hallberg L, Brune M, Rossander L. *Am J Clin Nutr* 1989;49:140–4.
DOI 10.1093/ajcn/49.1.140 (PMID 2911999) **[delegated]**: **2 mg phytate inhibited absorption
by 18%, 25 mg by 64%, 250 mg by 82%** — a semilogarithmic relationship.

**And removing it restores absorption dramatically.** Hurrell RF, Reddy MB, Juillerat MA,
Cook JD. "Degradation of phytic acid in cereal porridges improves iron absorption by human
subjects." *Am J Clin Nutr* 2003;77(5):1213–9. DOI 10.1093/ajcn/77.5.1213 (PMID 12716674)
**[delegated]**. Native → dephytinised absorption: **wheat 0.99 → 11.54% (~11.6×); maize
1.80 → 8.92%; rice 1.73 → 5.34%; oat 0.33 → 2.79%.** Low-tannin sorghum ~2×. **High-tannin
sorghum: no improvement at all** — for tannin-rich grain, removing phytate is not enough.
Reconstituting with milk "markedly decreased or completely removed" the benefit.

**Important counterweight on generalisation.** Hurrell R, Egli I. *Am J Clin Nutr*
2010;91:1461S–7S. DOI 10.3945/ajcn.2010.28674f (PMID 20200263) **[delegated]** caution that
these are **single-meal isotope studies**, and multi-meal studies show more modest effects.
The Hallberg dose-response probably overstates the chronic dietary consequence.

**Fermentation and germination — measured, and variable.** Best documented: Makokha AO et al.,
*Food Nutr Bull* 2002;23(3 Suppl):241–5. DOI 10.1177/15648265020233s147 (PMID 12362804)
**[delegated]** — Kenyan sorghum and finger millet, natural lactic fermentation: **−39.0% /
−64.8% (sorghum, 72/96 h)** and **−54.3% / −72.3% (finger millet)**; malting managed only
−23.9% / −45.3%. Ben-saalga (Burkina Faso pearl millet): **−75% IP6** at final pH 4.0
(Tou EH et al., *Int J Food Microbiol* 2006;106(1):52–60. DOI 10.1016/j.ijfoodmicro.2005.05.010,
PMID 16225950 **[delegated]**). **But traditional teff injera fermentation removes only ~33%**
(Cercamondi 2017), and Songré-Ouattara LT et al., *J Food Sci* 2010, DOI
10.1111/j.1750-3841.2010.01640.x (PMID 20629882) **[delegated]** found **no phytate decrease at
all** in pearl millet–soybean slurries.

**Disconfirming, and it matters more than the phytate numbers.** Two of the four long-term
human trials in the only systematic review of this question (Nikolaou A et al., *Front Nutr*
2026;13:1778997. DOI 10.3389/fnut.2026.1778997, PMID 42163962 **[delegated]** — **eight human
intervention studies total**) found iron status getting **worse** on the low-phytate arm:
Hoppe 2019, low-phytate sourdough rye over 3 months, **ferritin 33 → 27 µg/L (p = 0.018)**;
Bach Kristensen 2005, phytase-treated fibre-rich wheat bread over 4 months, **ferritin
45 → 33 µg/L**. Fractional absorption and iron status are not the same endpoint.

**A structural point that may dominate all of it.** Ben-saalga delivers ~30 kcal/100 g with
protein, lipid and mineral content well below complementary-feeding recommendations
(Mouquet-Rivier C et al., *Int J Food Sci Nutr* 2008;59(7-8):716–29.
DOI 10.1080/09637480802206389, PMID 18991104 **[delegated]**), and the same processing causes
"considerable lipid, protein, iron and zinc losses." Raising the *fractional* absorption of a
mineral present in trace absolute amounts, in a food of inadequate energy density, is a small
win.

**Evidence-strength verdict: STRONG for the phytase ranking among rye/wheat/barley; STRONG
for the phytate dose-response and for dephytinisation raising single-meal iron absorption;
STRONG for the raw-vs-kilned oat distinction; MIXED-to-NEGATIVE for whether traditional
fermentation and germination actually improve iron status over months.**

**Whole grain, microbiome and glucose — the two studies people cite, read properly.**
Kovatcheva-Datchary P, Nilsson A, Akrami R, et al. "Dietary Fiber-Induced Improvement in
Glucose Metabolism Is Associated with Increased Abundance of *Prevotella*." *Cell Metab*
2015;22(6):971–82. DOI 10.1016/j.cmet.2015.10.001 (PMID 26552345). Design: healthy subjects,
**3-day** barley-kernel-bread intervention, comparing those whose glucose metabolism
improved against those who responded least. The **Prevotella/Bacteroides ratio was higher
in responders**; responder microbiota was enriched in *Prevotella copri*; and — the part
that carries the causal weight — **germ-free mice transplanted with responder microbiota
showed improved glucose metabolism and higher liver glycogen** than mice receiving
non-responder microbiota.

Martínez I, Lattimer JM, Hubach KL, et al. "Gut microbiome composition is linked to whole
grain-induced immunological improvements." *ISME J* 2013;7(2):269–80.
DOI 10.1038/ismej.2012.104 (PMID 23038174). Design: randomised crossover, **n = 28**
healthy adults, 4-week arms, 60 g/d of whole-grain barley, brown rice, or a 50:50 mixture.
All treatments raised microbial diversity, the Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio, and
*Blautia*. Whole-grain barley specifically enriched *Roseburia*, *Bifidobacterium*,
*Dialister*, *Eubacterium rectale*, *Roseburia faecis*, *R. intestinalis*. Whole grains —
**especially the barley + brown rice combination** — reduced plasma IL-6 and peak
postprandial glucose.

**Correction — the *Prevotella* responder hypothesis failed prospective testing, by the same
group.** Sandberg J, Kovatcheva-Datchary P, Björck I, Bäckhed F, Nilsson A. "Abundance of gut
*Prevotella* at baseline and metabolic response to barley prebiotics." *Eur J Nutr*
2019;58:2365–76. DOI 10.1007/s00394-018-1788-9 (PMID 30046942) **[delegated]**. Randomised
crossover, n = 33 **pre-stratified** by Prevotella/Bacteroides ratio: the barley kernel bread
**lowered glucose responses independently of that ratio** (p < 0.01), and the authors state
their results **do not support** using the baseline ratio to stratify metabolic responders.
An earlier draft of this document described the effect as "conditional on baseline
microbiota" — that is the 2015 hypothesis, and the 2019 test refuted it.

Compounding the problem: Pedersen HK et al., *Nature* 2016;535:376–81.
DOI 10.1038/nature18646 (PMID 27409811) **[delegated]** report that **"*P. copri* can induce
insulin resistance"** in mice — the opposite direction to the 2015 transplant result. And
Tett A et al., *Cell Host Microbe* 2019;26(5):666–79.
DOI 10.1016/j.chom.2019.08.018 (PMID 31607556) **[delegated]** show *P. copri* comprises
**four clades with >10% genetic divergence and notably different carbohydrate metabolism** —
so a 16S-level "*P. copri* abundance" is not a functional measurement, which is the most
parsimonious explanation for the replication failure.

**Note also the internal null in Martínez 2013:** the paper reports "no significant
differences were detected in the amounts of short-chain fatty acids for any of the
treatments," and its hs-CRP halving was **not** statistically significant. The proposed
mediating variable did not move while the microbiota did.

**Evidence-strength verdict: WEAK, downgraded.** These are the strongest whole-grain
microbiome results available and they are small (n = 28–33), short (3 days to 4 weeks), on
surrogate endpoints, with the causal step only in gnotobiotic mice, the SCFA mediator null in
one and the stratification hypothesis refuted in the other. The defensible statement is
"barley fibre shifts the faecal microbiota, and short-term glucose measures sometimes
improve" — with no demonstrated causal link between the two.

**Resistant starch — the hard-endpoint trial, and it was negative.**
Burn J, Bishop DT, Mecklin JP, et al., CAPP2 Investigators. *N Engl J Med*
2008;359(24):2567–78. DOI 10.1056/NEJMoa0801297 (PMID 19073976) **[delegated]** and
Mathers JC, Movahedi M, Macrae F, et al. *Lancet Oncol* 2012;13(12):1242–9.
DOI 10.1016/S1470-2045(12)70475-8 (PMID 23140761) **[delegated]**. 2×2 factorial,
**resistant starch 30 g/day** in Lynch syndrome carriers across 43 centres. At mean 29 months
(n = 727): neoplasia 18.7% vs 18.4%, **RR 1.0 (95% CI 0.7–1.4)**. At median 52.7 months
(**463 RS vs 455 placebo**): ITT **HR 1.40 (95% CI 0.78–2.56; p = 0.26)** — "resistant starch
had no detectable effect on cancer development." Twenty-year follow-up (Mathers JC et al.,
*Cancer Prev Res* 2022;15(9):623–34, PMID 35878732 **[delegated]**) leaves colorectal cancer
**still null (HR 0.92; 95% CI 0.62–1.34; p = 0.63)**; the widely-cited reduction in
*non-colorectal* Lynch cancers is **post-hoc on a secondary endpoint in a trial whose primary
endpoint failed** and is hypothesis-generating only.

**30 g/day of resistant starch, for up to four years, in 918 randomised high-risk humans, did
nothing to the colon — the organ the butyrate hypothesis is about.** This trial is routinely
absent from resistant-starch reviews.

**And the mechanism itself has been measured flat at high whole-grain intake.**
Roager HM, Vogt JK, Kristensen M, et al. "Whole grain-rich diet reduces body weight and
systemic low-grade inflammation without inducing major changes of the gut microbiome: a
randomised cross-over trial." *Gut* 2019;68(1):83–93. DOI 10.1136/gutjnl-2017-314786
(PMID 29097438) **[delegated]**. Randomised crossover, two **8-week** periods, 60 Danish
adults at metabolic-syndrome risk (50 completing both), achieved intakes **179 ± 50 g/day
whole grain vs 13 ± 10 g/day** — roughly double the increment used in the observational
literature. Result: **no major microbiome change, and null on breath hydrogen, plasma
short-chain fatty acids, intestinal integrity and transit time** — while body weight
(p < 0.0001), IL-6 (p = 0.009) and CRP (p = 0.003) improved anyway. The entire proposed causal
chain was measured and was flat while the downstream outcome moved.

**Two further constraints on the butyrate story.** Boets E, Gomand SV, Deroover L, Preston T,
et al. *J Physiol* 2017. DOI 10.1113/JP272613 (PMID 27510655) **[delegated]**, using
stable-isotope colonic administration in humans: **systemic availability of colonic acetate,
propionate and butyrate was 36%, 9% and 2% respectively** — butyrate, the molecule the
hypothesis rests on, is ~98% consumed locally. And faecal SCFA is an **excretion balance, not
a production measure**: Müller M et al., *Sci Rep* 2019;9. DOI 10.1038/s41598-019-48775-0
(PMID 31467327) **[delegated]**, n = 160, found **circulating rather than faecal SCFA**
associated with GLP-1, lipolysis and peripheral insulin sensitivity.

**Meta-analytic verdict on RS.** Snelson M et al., *Nutrients* 2019;11(8):1833.
DOI 10.3390/nu11081833 (PMID 31398841) **[delegated]** — 22 RCTs, 670 participants, RS2
≥8 g/d: **null on fasting glucose, HbA1c, HOMA-IR, total cholesterol, LDL-C and HDL-C**;
conclusion "short-term RS2 supplementation in humans is of limited cardiometabolic benefit."
Xiong K et al., *Br J Nutr* 2021. DOI 10.1017/S0007114520003700 (PMID 32959735)
**[delegated]** — 19 RCTs, fasting glucose −0.09 mmol/L (≈1.6 mg/dL, clinically negligible),
null on fasting insulin and every insulin-sensitivity index. **The two meta-analyses disagree
with each other** on HOMA-IR and fasting glucose, and neither reports GRADE.

**Evidence-strength verdict: WEAK-to-NEGATIVE.** Resistant starch reliably changes
fermentation substrate and some short-term postprandial measures. On clinical outcomes, the
only large long-duration RCT was null, the mediating mechanism was null at double the
observational whole-grain dose, and the meta-analyses are null on almost everything and
mutually inconsistent on the rest.

**One more piece of context worth stating.** The 20–25% mortality reduction popularly
attributed to whole grains is **entirely observational**. Aune D et al., *BMJ* 2016.
DOI 10.1136/bmj.i2716 (PMID 27301975) **[delegated]** pooled 45 cohorts (per 90 g/d: CHD
RR 0.81, all-cause mortality 0.83, **I² = 83%**; stroke not significant). Against that,
Kelly SA, Hartley L, Loveman E, et al. *Cochrane Database Syst Rev* 2017;8:CD005051.
DOI 10.1002/14651858.CD005051.pub3 (PMID 28836672) **[delegated]** found, across nine RCTs and
1,414 participants: **"no studies that reported the effect of whole grain diets on total
cardiovascular mortality or cardiovascular events"**, and no difference in total cholesterol
(MD 0.07; 95% CI −0.07 to 0.21), evidence quality **low**.

**No millet has ever been tested in a completed human RCT with a gut microbiome outcome.**
The delegated search found only in vitro faecal-inoculum work, millet RCTs without microbiome
endpoints, and one unpublished protocol preprint.

### 2.10 Popular claims I could NOT trace to primary evidence in this pass

Listed as leads for further checking, not as refutations.

1. **Avenacins suppress or modulate arbuscular mycorrhizal symbiosis in oats.** **Resolved
   in §2.3 and it is an over-reading**: the real result is Turner et al. 2013 *ISME J*, in
   which *sad1* mutants show a strongly altered rhizosphere **eukaryotic** community
   (Amoebozoa, Alveolata) — Glomeromycota were never measured. No study has inoculated a
   *sad* mutant with an AM fungus. The claim is not merely untraced; it is a paraphrase drift
   from a paper that says something else.
2. **Barley tocotrienols lower cholesterol in humans via HMG-CoA reductase.** The
   mechanism paper (Parker 1993) is cell-culture at ~2 µM. The human barley-tocotrienol
   trials did not surface; the tocotrienol meta-analysis that did is **animal-model only**,
   and the human systematic review that did is for **palm**.
3. **Hordenine as a nootropic / MAO-B inhibitor / dopaminergic agent at dietary or
   supplement doses.** Nothing supporting it. The retrievable literature treats hordenine
   as a **barley phytotoxin** and a quorum-sensing inhibitor.
4. **Barley as a notable dietary betaine source with a homocysteine effect.** No
   grain-composition primary source and no barley-specific betaine trial retrieved.
5. **Oats have the highest lipid content of common cereals.** **Resolved in §2.4: true on
   average for commercial cultivars, false as an absolute.** The canonical comparison used
   one cultivar per species and put oats 0.8 points above maize; oat's floor is below dent
   corn and high-oil maize exceeds 20%. Cite oat's *range* (2–18% vs 2–3% in wheat/barley),
   not a ranking. Also untraced: the frequently quoted "up to 18%" ceiling does not match the
   source population's own mean of 15.85%.
6. **Oats have negligible intrinsic phytase.** **Resolved in §2.9, and the usual phrasing is
   wrong**: raw oats have appreciable phytase (43–49% phytate hydrolysis on incubation),
   commercial **kilned** oats do not. The claim is about the product, not the species. The
   maize half remains unverified here.
7. **Avenanthramides occur in no other food.** Asserted in two primary oat papers, but it
   is an unbounded negative claim; structurally analogous N-cinnamoylanthranilates in other
   genera were not checked.
8. **The DP3:DP4 ratio explains the oat-vs-barley clinical difference.** **Resolved in §2.2b
   and it does not hold.** Content and DP3:DP4 map to separate chromosomal regions, are
   positively correlated rather than trading off, and a CslF6 dosage series changed content
   45-fold with **no change in DP3:DP4**. The better-supported explanation is enzymatic:
   **oats are kilned (β-glucanase destroyed), barley usually is not.** Also corrected: the
   commonly quoted rye DP3:DP4 of 3.0–3.5 is wrong — rye is 1.9–3.0, at or below barley.
9. **Prowashonupana barley contains 15–18% β-glucan.** The most-repeated untraceable number
    in the barley field. The "17%" traces to the body text of one 2005 clinical paper and
    thence to corporate documentation; the cultivar appears **never to have been formally
    registered in *Crop Science***, and the original-mutant attribution has no indexed
    primary publication. Treat as industry-sourced.
10. **Betzes / Waxy Oderbrucker / Glacier per-cultivar β-glucan figures.** The canonical
    sources are paywalled and not open access; Waxy Oderbrucker returns essentially nothing
    in indexed literature.
11. **"Burton 2011 raised grain β-glucan 80-fold."** The paper says **"more than 80%"** —
    about 1.8-fold. The associated "grain shrivelling" phenotype is also routinely mis-cited
    to it; it belongs to Lim et al. 2020.
12. **A narrow-sense heritability estimate for barley β-glucan.** None verifiable. Broad-sense
    estimates range H² 0.42–0.97 and the variance partition is strongly scale-dependent on
    germplasm breadth (wide panels give G ≳ 78%; narrow elite panels give environment
    dominating) — so "how heritable is β-glucan" has no single answer.
13. **Proso millet raises HDL / increases adiponectin.** **Resolved in §2.8 and it does not
   survive**: nine papers from one laboratory over 1990–2009, n = 6–8, unadjusted t-tests,
   protein concentrates, **no human data at any dose**, no independent replication in three
   decades, and the lab's own fractionation experiment failed to reproduce the endpoints.
14. **Finger millet calcium is well absorbed.** **Actively contradicted in §2.8.** Content is
    exceptional (376–589 mg/100 g) but the calcium sits in the seed coat substantially as
    **insoluble calcium oxalate**; decortication *raises* bioaccessibility 37.5% while
    *removing* 40% of total calcium; **zero stable-isotope absorption studies exist**; and the
    only meta-analysis rests on two balance studies from 1955 and 1959.
15. **Wild-rice (*Zizania*) cardiovascular findings apply to Indian *nīvāra*.** A botanical
    category error — different genera, different continents (see §1.5).
16. **EFSA's exact oat β-glucan claim wording and conditions of use.** Opinion identified
    (EFSA Journal 2010;8(12):1885, DOI 10.2903/j.efsa.2010.1885) but full text paywalled at
    fetch time; not quoted here.
17. **Avenacoside haemolytic activity.** No primary measurement exists anywhere retrievable —
    no haemolytic index, no HD50, and no head-to-head comparison with digitonin, quillaja or
    alfalfa saponins. The claim traces to an unreadable 1977 *Chem Ber* paper and rests
    otherwise on analogy to the bidesmoside rule, which runs the *opposite* way for digitonin
    and avenacin A-1. **Do not assert it.**
18. **"Avenacosides are stored in the vacuole."** The three source papers have no abstract in
    any index. What *is* verified is the negative — they are **not** in etioplasts — and the
    activating enzyme's own localisation is contested (plastid stroma vs tonoplast).
19. **"Oomycetes are saponin-resistant because they lack sterols."** The usual citation could
    not be verified to exist in any electronic index.
20. **"Oat lipolytic enzymes are 10–15× more active than wheat."** Secondary summaries only.
21. **Avenanthramides are heat-stable under commercial processing.** Asserted in a 2019 review
    citing a "Dimberg et al. 2001" paper that **does not appear to exist**, and contradicted by
    the primary kilning data.
22. **No D-values or z-values exist for oat lipase or peroxidase** — a real gap in a mature
    industrial process, compounded by lipase inactivation not following first-order kinetics.
23. ***Zizania* wild rice prevents atherosclerosis in humans.** Mouse-only, one laboratory, at
    26–60% of diet by weight, with the proposed antioxidant mechanism explicitly null in the
    proponents' own experiments and plasma glucose rising 61% in one of them. The single
    registered human trial is single-arm, open-label, run by the same lab, and unreported.
24. **Ergot as an alternative cause of kodo poisoning.** No primary source; the only link is a
    pharmacological analogy between cyclopiazonic acid and ergotamine.

### 2.11 Part 2 summary — what actually holds up

**Strongest findings, in order of evidential weight:**

1. **β-glucan LDL lowering is dose- *and physics*-dependent.** 345 completers, 4 weeks:
   3 g high-MW works, 4 g low-MW does not. log(MW × C) predicts the effect. (Wolever 2010)
2. **Effect size is small and consistent: about −0.25 mmol/L LDL**, no HDL or triglyceride
   change in the oat pooling. Oats reach it at ≥3 g/d; barley at a median 6.5–6.9 g/d.
   (Whitehead 2014; Ho 2016; Talati 2009)
3. **Avenacins are genuinely causal plant-defence compounds**, demonstrated by mutant
   genetics, encoded in a mostly-clustered nine-locus pathway that is entangled with root
   development. (Papadopoulou 1999; Mylona 2008)
4. **Naked barley has a single genetic origin**: a fixed 17-kb deletion of a testa-expressed
   ERF transcription factor controlling a lipid pathway. (Taketa 2008)
5. **Barley is the most salt-tolerant common cereal** (ECe 8.0 dS/m vs wheat 6.0, rice 3.0,
   maize 1.7) — and its HKT1;5 works in the *opposite* direction to that of salt-sensitive
   cereals. (FAO-29/Maas & Hoffman; Kuang 2025)
6. **Kilning exists because oat lipase survives drying.** Lipase activity rises during
   storage of non-kilned groats even as water activity falls; 90 °C steam for 30 min
   inactivates it at a measurable cost to protein solubility. (Ekelund 2024)
7. **Avenanthramide plasma concentrations are nanomolar**, from concentrate doses.
   (Chen 2007)
8. **Avenin-reactive intestinal T cells exist in some coeliac patients**, and the cultivar
   ranking tool used to manage that risk is itself contested. (Arentz-Hansen 2004;
   Comino 2011; Dvořáček 2022)
9. **Oat protein quality is buffered against nitrogen fertilisation** because the added
   nitrogen goes into lysine-rich globulin rather than lysine-poor prolamin — the opposite of
   wheat — but oat protein still scores DIAAS 56–67 and is **not a complete protein**.
   (Peterson 1976; Abelilla 2018)
10. **Finger millet is calcium-dense at 376–589 mg/100 g, and that calcium is largely
    insoluble oxalate in the seed coat.** Decortication raises bioaccessibility while removing
    calcium; no stable-isotope absorption study has ever been done. (Teklu 2024; Sharma 2017;
    Puranik 2017)
11. **Millet glycaemic index is contested at the level of direct measurement**: unpolished
    foxtail and little millet measured GI 88.6 against white rice 82.5, and ~90% of marketed
    millet is heavily polished. The best-powered millet RCT missed its primary endpoint.
    (Malavika 2020; Duraiswamy 2024)
12. **Kodo millet's toxicity is fungal cyclopiazonic acid acting on SERCA**, with a coherent
    mechanism, primary LD50 data, a matching clinical case — and **no surveillance, no
    regulatory limit and no contamination survey**. (Lalitha Rao 1985; Seidler 1989;
    Deepika 2022)
13. **The resistant-starch/microbiome causal chain has been tested and failed twice.**
    30 g/day RS for up to four years in 918 randomised humans did nothing to the colon
    (CAPP2); 179 g/day whole grain for 8 weeks left microbiome, breath hydrogen and plasma
    SCFA flat while weight and inflammatory markers improved anyway (Roager 2019).

14. **Barley's β-glucan distinction is its ceiling, not its median.** Barley and oat overlap
    almost entirely in ordinary food germplasm (~3–7%); barley alone reaches 10–18%. And
    high-amylose barley inverts the useful trade — most β-glucan, least soluble.
    (Izydorczyk 2000; Houston 2014; Gianinetti 2024; Peterson 1995)
15. **The physics is Mw × concentration, not grams.** Specific viscosity scales as c^1.1
    below the critical overlap concentration and **c^4.4 above it**, and c\* itself rises as
    Mw falls — so halving molecular weight can drop a solution out of the entangled regime
    entirely. Solubility is also non-monotonic: extreme depolymerisation gives *less*
    viscosity **and** *less* soluble material. (Wang & Ellis 2014)
16. **Mixed-linkage glucan is dispensable in barley.** Complete-loss *bgl* mutants have no
    β-glucan in any tissue, are viable and fertile, and compensate with 1.7–2.0× more
    arabinoxylan. (Taketa 2012)

**Corrections this document makes to claims it earlier contained.** Five things were revised
during compilation and are flagged in place rather than silently fixed: avenacosides are a
**grain** compound, not a leaf compound; kilning does **not** meaningfully depolymerise
β-glucan (cooking halves its *extractability* instead, at unchanged molecular weight);
lipoxygenase is **not detectable** in oat and should not be named as a rancidity driver; the
**DP3:DP4 ratio does not explain** the oat-vs-barley clinical asymmetry (kilning-driven
β-glucanase inactivation is the better candidate); and the *Prevotella* responder hypothesis
was **refuted** by its own authors' prospective test, not merely qualified by it.

**The general shape of the evidence.** Where these grains have been studied with large,
randomised, mechanistically-informed designs — essentially only β-glucan — the effect is
real, small, and conditional on physical properties that ordinary food processing degrades.
Where the mechanism has been directly measured rather than assumed, it has repeatedly come
out flat while the outcome moved. Everywhere else, the strongest results are in plant biology
(defence chemistry, salt physiology, domestication genetics, storage-protein architecture),
where the experiments are clean, rather than in human nutrition, where they are small, short,
surrogate-endpoint, and frequently produced by parties with an interest in the answer.

**One asymmetry worth naming explicitly.** Three of the attractive claims in this document —
proso millet's HDL effect, wild rice's antiatherogenic effect, and barley tocotrienols'
cholesterol effect — each rest on a single laboratory, in rodents, at doses with no dietary
analogue, unreplicated for decades. Meanwhile the one genuinely dangerous finding here, kodo
poisoning, has a known molecular target, primary toxicology, and documented human cases — and
is omitted from the current promotional literature at exactly the moment the crop is being
scaled up.
