# What Is Genuinely Unique About Rice and Wheat

A literature review of the biology, biochemistry, agronomy and nutrition of
*Oryza sativa* and *Triticum* spp., written to separate what has been measured
from what has been hypothesised from what remains unknown.

Compiled 2026-07-22.

## How To Read This Document

**Evidence tiers used throughout.**

- **[E1] Strong** — multiple independent human RCTs, or a systematic
  review/meta-analysis of human trials, or a structural/genetic result
  replicated across labs.
- **[E2] Moderate** — a single well-designed human trial, a large prospective
  cohort, or a well-replicated in-planta molecular result.
- **[E3] Weak** — animal or in vitro only, or human data with small n, poor
  blinding, or industry sponsorship.
- **[E4] Thin/contested** — mechanistic plausibility with little or no direct
  evidence at realistic dietary exposure, or evidence that fails to replicate.

**Source verification.** Citations marked **(v)** were fetched and read during
this session. Citations marked **(r)** are recalled from the literature and were
not re-fetched here — treat their exact volume/page details as provisional even
where the finding itself is well established.

**Study-type tags.** `in vitro` / `animal` / `human` are stated for every
biological claim, with dose where dose matters.

## Executive Summary Of The Genuinely Unusual

**Rice.**

1. Rice is the only major cereal whose dominant seed storage protein is a
   *glutelin* (oryzenin, ~80% of storage protein), not a prolamin. Every other
   major cereal — wheat, barley, rye, maize, sorghum — stores nitrogen mainly as
   prolamin. **[E1, well replicated]**
2. Rice starch granules are the smallest of any cereal (2–7 µm, angular/
   polygonal, packed as compound granules). **[E1]**
3. Rice accumulates silicon to ~10% of shoot dry weight — one to two orders of
   magnitude above most crops — via a dedicated two-transporter polar
   influx/efflux system (Lsi1/Lsi2) that has no equivalent in wheat. **[E1]**
4. That same silicon pathway is the arsenite pathway. Rice's arsenic problem is
   a direct, molecularly demonstrated consequence of its silicon adaptation plus
   flooded, reducing soil. **[E1]**
5. Momilactones A and B — diterpenoid phytoalexins/allelochemicals — have been
   found in only two organisms on earth: rice and the moss *Hypnum plumaeforme*.
   **[E2]**
6. γ-Oryzanol, a mixture of >20 ferulic-acid esters of phytosterols and
   triterpene alcohols, is essentially a rice-bran-specific class of molecule.
   **[E1 for chemistry; E2 for lipid effects]**

**Wheat.**

1. Wheat gluten is the only cereal protein system that forms a covalently
   crosslinked, gas-retaining viscoelastic polymer network — the basis of
   leavened bread. Rye and barley prolamins are homologous but do not do it.
   **[E1]**
2. Hexaploidy (the D genome from *Aegilops tauschii*, ~8–10 kya) both created
   the HMW glutenin alleles that make bread wheat superior to tetraploid wheat
   *and* created the 33-mer α-gliadin peptide — six overlapping celiac epitopes
   in one protease-resistant fragment. The same evolutionary event gave wheat its
   best technological property and its most immunogenic one. **[E1]**
3. Wheat α-amylase/trypsin inhibitors activate TLR4 — the first demonstration
   that a dietary protein family engages an innate pattern-recognition receptor.
   **[E1 for mechanism; E4 for human dietary relevance]**
4. Wheat aleurone is the richest known plant food source of betaine. **[E2]**
5. Wheat germ is among the richest dietary sources of spermidine, the polyamine
   with the strongest inverse nutrient–mortality association in the Bruneck
   cohort. **[E2 for the epidemiology; E3 for causality]**

**Popular claims that do not survive the evidence** are listed in their own
section near the end.

# PART I — RICE (*Oryza sativa*)

## 1. Oryzenin / Glutelin: The Cereal That Broke the Prolamin Rule

### Observation

Rice is the outlier among cereals in seed storage protein class. In a
genome-wide association study across 527 rice accessions grown in two
environments, the measured fractions were:

| Fraction | Content (mg/g grain) | % of total storage protein |
|---|---|---|
| Albumin | 2.9–3.3 | ~5% |
| Globulin | 5.3–5.8 | ~10% |
| Prolamin | 2.1–2.8 | ~5% |
| **Glutelin (oryzenin)** | **43.5–59.4** | **~80%** |
| Total | 53.7–70.9 | |

Chen P, et al. *Frontiers in Plant Science* 2018;9:612.
doi:10.3389/fpls.2018.00612 (v)

A narrative review reports glutelin at 75–81% of brown rice protein and 79–83%
of milled rice protein, with prolamin at 3–6% and 2–7% respectively.

Jayaprakash G, et al. "A Narrative Review on Rice Proteins." *Polymers*
2022;14(15):3003. doi:10.3390/polym14153003 (v)

In wheat, barley, rye and maize the prolamin fraction is the *majority* storage
protein (gluten is ~80% of grain protein in European wheats — Shewry PR, "Wheat",
*J Exp Bot* 2009;60(6):1537–1553, doi:10.1093/jxb/erp058 (v)). Rice inverts this
ratio.

### Why it matters biochemically

The two fractions are sequestered in structurally distinct organelles:

- **PB-I** (protein body type I): ER-derived, spherical, prolamin-filled,
  surrounded by a persistent membrane.
- **PB-II**: protein-storage-vacuole-derived, irregular, glutelin- and
  globulin-filled.

PB-I is remarkably resistant to digestion. Intact rice prolamin/PB-I particles
have been recovered by electron microscopy from fresh human faeces, having
survived pepsin proteolysis; PB-II glutelin is readily digested.

- Tanaka K, et al. "Properties of Rice Faecal Protein Particles: Light and
  Electron Microscopic Observations." *J Cereal Sci* (v — title/journal
  confirmed via ScienceDirect listing S0733521097901491)
- "In Vivo Digestibility of Rice Prolamin/Protein Body-I Particle Is Decreased by
  Cooking." *J Nutr Sci Vitaminol* 2014;60(4):300–. PMID 25297621 (v — title and
  bibliographic record confirmed)

**[E2]** The prolamin/glutelin split into differently digestible protein bodies
is well documented; the exact quantitative contribution of PB-I escape to whole-
diet protein losses in humans is **unknown**.

### Disconfirming / deflating notes

The widely repeated claim that "rice protein has the highest quality of any
cereal" needs qualifying. On the FAO-preferred **DIAAS** metric (pig ileal
digestibility, 0.5–3 y reference pattern), pooled values are:

| Protein | DIAAS | Limiting AA |
|---|---|---|
| Corn | 36 ± 14.9 | lysine |
| **Rice** | **47 ± 2.3** | **lysine** |
| **Wheat** | **48 ± 10.6** | **lysine** |
| Oat | 57 ± 5.8 | lysine |
| Whey | 85 ± 10.8 | histidine |
| Egg | 101 ± 11.7 | none |
| Casein / pork | 117 | none |

Herreman L, Nommensen P, Pennings B, Laus MC. *Food Sci Nutr*
2020;8(10):5379–5391. doi:10.1002/fsn3.1809 (v)

Rice and wheat are statistically indistinguishable, and both sit **below oat**.
Rice's advantage is real but narrow: it is *lysine-limited like every other
cereal*, just less severely than maize, and its variance is much smaller
(±2.3 vs ±10.6 for wheat). The older claims of rice superiority rest largely on
rat NPU/PER assays, which the FAO has explicitly moved away from. **[E1 for
the DIAAS numbers; the "highest quality cereal" framing is E4.]**

Separately, a human indicator-amino-acid-oxidation study in healthy young men
reported that lysine from cooked white rice is *highly metabolically available*
— relevant because it tests the limiting amino acid directly in humans rather
than by pig proxy ("Lysine from Cooked White Rice Consumed by Healthy Young Men
Is Highly Metabolically Available When Assessed Using the Indicator Amino Acid
Oxidation Technique", *J Nutr*; ScienceDirect S0022316622011294 (v — title/
journal confirmed, volume/year not verified)). **[E2]**

### Unknown

Why rice made the evolutionary switch to glutelin dominance is not established.
The functional consequences for the plant (nitrogen remobilisation kinetics,
germination) are not well characterised in the literature reviewed here.

## 2. Absence of Gluten and the Hypoallergenicity Question

### Observation — the gluten part is solid

Rice contains no gliadin/glutenin-equivalent proteins and forms no viscoelastic
network. Rice prolamins carry none of the DQ2.5/DQ8-restricted epitope families
found in wheat, barley and rye. Rice is therefore the default base for
gluten-free product technology worldwide — chosen for blandness, white colour,
and hypoallergenicity — and its inability to form a network is precisely the
central technological *problem* of gluten-free baking.

"Rice-Based Gluten-Free Foods and Technologies: A Review." PMC10670158 (v)

**[E1]** Rice's celiac safety is settled clinical practice.

### Observation — the hypoallergenicity part is real but overstated

Rice is genuinely a low-frequency allergen, but not a non-allergen.

- A 16 kDa rice protein is an established major rice allergen and mediates
  cross-reactivity among Poaceae grains. Izumi H, et al. *FEBS Lett*
  1992 (PubMed 1725171) (v — record confirmed)
- Identified rice allergens include non-specific lipid transfer protein 1,
  **α-amylase/trypsin inhibitor family proteins** (the same family implicated in
  wheat — see Part II §4), α-globulin, and β-expansin.
- Prevalence is geographically skewed: IgE-mediated rice allergy is reported
  mainly from Japan and Korea; in Western countries it is rare (reported ranges
  of roughly 0.7–2% in selected atopic series, with wide methodological
  variation).

**[E2]** The direction of the finding (rice is a comparatively weak allergen) is
consistent across sources; the prevalence numbers are heterogeneous and
population-selected, so treat specific percentages as soft.

**Genuinely interesting**: hydrolysed rice protein infant formulas are in active
clinical use for cow's-milk-allergic infants, and are under RCT evaluation
(e.g. the RIGHT-HY protocol, *Front Nutr* 2026, doi:10.3389/fnut.2026.1798624
(v — record confirmed)). This is a real, clinically deployed consequence of rice
protein biology, not a marketing claim. **[E2]**

### Unknown

Whether rice's low allergenicity is *causal* — i.e. an intrinsic property of
glutelin conformation and epitope structure — or partly an artefact of very
early and universal introduction in rice-eating populations (oral tolerance), is
not resolved.

## 3. Starch: The Smallest Granules in the Cereal World

### Observation

Rice starch granules are 2–7 µm, angular and polygonal, the smallest among
cereals (compare wheat's bimodal ~2–3 µm B-granules and ~15–35 µm A-granules,
maize ~5–20 µm, potato ~15–100 µm). They are laid down as *compound* granules
within a single amyloplast, then separate.

Shoukat R, Cappai M, Pilia L, Pia G. "Rice Starch Chemistry, Functional
Properties, and Industrial Applications: A Review." *Polymers* 2025;17(1):110.
doi:10.3390/polym17010110 (v)

Amylose content spans essentially the entire possible range across cultivars:
waxy (<1.3–2%), very low (5–12%), low (12–20%), intermediate (20–25%), high
(>25%), reaching ~35–37%. The genetic basis is allelic variation at *Waxy*
(GBSSI). **[E1]**

Practical consequence: small granule size gives rice starch a distinctive smooth
mouthfeel, high acid resistance, good freeze–thaw paste stability — and makes it
the fat-mimetic starch of choice industrially.

### Disconfirming — resistant starch in rice is small

The popular framing of rice as a resistant-starch food is not supported by direct
measurement of native rice starch. In four Thai commercial cultivars:

| Cultivar | Amylose (%) | Resistant starch (%) |
|---|---|---|
| RD57 | 23.60 ± 0.77 | 0.150 ± 0.01 |
| RD29 | 22.80 ± 1.17 | 0.153 ± 0.01 |
| KDML105 | 13.74 ± 0.69 | 0.027 ± 0.00 |
| RD6 (waxy) | 1.65 ± 0.04 | 0.025 ± 0.00 |

Sangwongchai W, Tananuwong K, Krusong K, Natee S, Thitisaksakul M. *Polymers*
2023;15(3):574. doi:10.3390/polym15030574 (v)

Those are **tenths of a percent**. Even high-amylose rice is not, natively, a
high-RS food. Amylose correlates with RS, and RS can be raised substantially by
processing (parboiling, retrogradation, lipid complexing) — but the baseline is
low, and cooked-then-cooled rice does not become a resistant-starch supplement.
**[E1 for the measured values.]**

### Unknown

The mechanistic weight of granule *size* versus amylopectin chain-length
distribution in setting digestibility is still debated; a mutant study with
matched amylose found both contributed (Springer, *J Food Sci Technol* 2018,
doi:10.1007/s13197-018-3500-8 (v — record confirmed)), but the relative
contributions are not settled.

## 4. Parboiling: The Best-Documented Traditional Grain Process

Parboiling — soak, steam/gelatinise in the husk, dry, then mill — is one of the
few traditional food processes whose biochemistry is well characterised.

### Measured effects

1. **Vitamin translocation.** Thiamine, niacin and vitamin B6 in the milled
   endosperm rise roughly **three-fold** versus non-parboiled milled rice,
   because water-soluble vitamins migrate inward from the bran/aleurone during
   soaking and steaming and are then fixed by gelatinisation. This is a genuine
   nutrient-rescue mechanism unique to hydrothermally processed cereals in the
   husk. **[E1]**
2. **Starch restructuring.** Gelatinisation followed by retrogradation on drying
   converts rapidly digestible starch into slowly digestible and resistant
   starch; total starch and RDS fall, SDS and RS rise. **[E1 in vitro]**
   - "Impact of parboiling on nutritionally important starch fractions, pasting
     properties, and in vitro starch digestibility of rice genotypes." *Cereal
     Res Commun* 2024. doi:10.1007/s42976-024-00547-x (v — record confirmed)
3. **Phytate reduction with mineral inward diffusion.** Parboiling reduces phytic
   acid and improves Fe and Zn bioavailability in milled rice; iron content
   itself rises by inward diffusion.
   - "Effect of parboiling on starch digestibility and mineral bioavailability in
     rice." *LWT* 2022. ScienceDirect S0023643821021794 (v — record confirmed)
   **[E2, in vitro bioaccessibility]**

### Human evidence

A randomised crossover pilot compared parboiled rice (PBR) and white rice (WR)
at 50 g available carbohydrate:

- Healthy (n=9): glucose AUC 177.86 ± 50.02 (PBR) vs 245.56 ± 87.78
  mmol·min/L (WR), **p = 0.047**
- Type 2 diabetes (n=8 PBR / 6 WR): 187.38 ± 75.87 vs 278.83 ± 84.28,
  **p = 0.051**
- Insulin: no significant within-group differences.
- GLP-1 AUC roughly two-fold higher in healthy vs diabetic participants;
  ~500 pM·min increment after PBR in healthy, ~300 in T2D.

Alkandari S, Zafar TA, Al-Sabah S, Abu Farha M, Abubaker J, Al-Mulla F. *Foods*
2025;14(11):1905. doi:10.3390/foods14111905 (v)

**[E3]** Real human data, correct direction, but n=9/8, acute only, COVID-
disrupted recruitment, and the T2D result missed significance. Treat as
supportive, not conclusive.

### Bonus: parboiling as an arsenic intervention

Modifying parboiling — parboiling **wholegrain (already-husked) rice** rather
than rough paddy — cut inorganic arsenic in final polished grain by ~25% in
village-scale trials in rural Bangladesh, and a modified protocol using
calcium-rich water simultaneously fortifies and decontaminates.

- Menon M, et al. "Modifying the Parboiling of Rice to Remove Inorganic Arsenic,
  While Fortifying with Calcium." *Environ Sci Technol* 2019. PMID 30993982 (v —
  record confirmed) **[E2]**

## 5. Ageing: A Grain That Improves in Storage, Measurably

Rice is unusual among staples in that ageing is a *deliberate, priced* quality
step (aged basmati commands a premium). The biochemistry is characterised:

**Observations.** During 3–12 months of storage:

- Lipase hydrolyses triglycerides → **free fatty acids rise**; FFA is the
  standard ageing index.
- Freed fatty acids form **amylose–lipid (V-type) inclusion complexes**, reducing
  water-soluble starch and raising pasting temperature.
- Peak viscosity, breakdown, adhesiveness and solid loss **fall**; final
  viscosity, setback, pasting temperature, water uptake, volume expansion,
  minimum cooking time, head rice yield and hardness **rise**.
- Cooked aged rice is harder, less sticky, more separate-grained — exactly the
  property valued in pilaf/biryani styles.
- Higher storage temperature accelerates all of this (and raises the b* yellowness
  index); chilled storage retards it.

Sources: "Aging kinetics of low amylose rice during storage at ambient and
chilled temperatures", *Int J Food Prop* 2017, doi:10.1080/10942912.2016.1223127
(v — record confirmed); "Changes in pasting properties and free fatty acids of
different brown rice cultivar during storage", *Korean J Food Preserv* 2017 (v —
record confirmed); "Natural Aging of a Local Indonesian Rice Variety", PMC12714122
(v — record confirmed).

**[E2]** Direction and mechanism are consistent across multiple independent
groups. **Unknown**: whether the protein fraction (disulfide crosslinking of
glutelin during ageing) or the lipid fraction dominates the texture change is
still argued.

## 6. Germination and GABA

### Observation

Brown rice germination activates **glutamate decarboxylase (GAD)**, which
decarboxylates L-glutamate to γ-aminobutyric acid. GABA accumulation correlates
positively with both substrate (glutamate) availability and GAD activity, and is
maximal at intermediate germination/aeration parameters then declines.

Measured contents (highly method-dependent):

| Material | GABA |
|---|---|
| Ungerminated brown rice | ~7–27 mg/100 g |
| Germinated 36 h | ~+330% (≈3.3-fold) |
| Njavara, 72 h soak+germinate | 28.63 → 130.29 mg/kg (≈4.5-fold) |
| CaCl₂/Glu + ultrasound | 102–111 mg/100 g |
| Optimised submerged cultivation | 243.97 ± 7.18 mg/100 g DW |
| Milled white rice | negligible |

Sources: "Effects of Different Pretreatments on the GABA Content of Germinated
Brown Rice", *Appl Sci* 2024;14(13):5771 (v — record confirmed); "Optimized
submerged cultivation for GABA-enriched sprouts from brown rice", ScienceDirect
S2772502225002513 (v — record confirmed); *Int J Agric Biol Eng* germination/
aeration study (v — record confirmed).

**[E1]** The chemistry is not in doubt. Germination genuinely creates a compound
that is essentially absent from the ungerminated milled grain.

### The human-effects evidence is much weaker

The most-cited human trial: 80 Vietnamese adults (mean 65.1 ± 3.8 y), 200 g/day
pre-germinated brown rice vs white rice, 3 months. Reported: significant
reduction in metabolic syndrome prevalence, HDL 1.11 → 1.44 mmol/L, reduced
fasting glucose and HOMA-IR (all p < 0.05).

Mai TT, Trang TT, Hai TT. *AIMS Public Health* 2020;7(1):33–43.
doi:10.3934/publichealth.2020005 (v)

**[E3]** No blinding described; product supplied by a commercial rice company;
government-institute funding; 6 dropouts. Critically, **the comparator is white
rice**, so any effect could be attributable to bran (fibre, oryzanol,
magnesium, tocotrienols) rather than to GABA. No trial reviewed here isolates
GABA as the active principle.

### Unknown / cautionary

Whether orally ingested GABA crosses the human blood–brain barrier in
physiologically meaningful amounts is **unresolved and contested**; the
peripheral (enteric nervous system, vagal) route is the more defensible
hypothesis. Attributing the metabolic effects of germinated brown rice to
central GABAergic action is an unsupported leap. **[E4 for any CNS claim.]**

## 7. Rice Bran: γ-Oryzanol, Ferulate Esters, Phytosterols, Tocotrienols

### γ-Oryzanol — chemistry [E1]

Not a single compound: a mixture of **>23 steryl and triterpenyl ferulates**.
Typical proportions (French Camargue rice):

- 24-methylenecycloartanyl ferulate 27–38%
- cycloartenyl ferulate 23–41%
- campesteryl ferulate 19–31%
- β-sitosteryl ferulate 7–20%

Content:

- Crude rice bran oil: ~1599–1666 mg/100 g γ-oryzanol, ~1362–1376 mg/100 g
  phytosterols
- **Refining destroys much of it**: 933–960 mg/100 g γ-oryzanol, 820–895 mg/100 g
  phytosterols

"Biochemical, Biological, and Clinical Properties of γ-Oryzanol." PMC12466495
(2025) (v)

This is a genuinely rice-specific molecular class. Ferulic acid is ubiquitous in
cereals; ferulic acid *esterified to cycloartenol and 24-methylenecycloartanol
at gram-per-100 g concentrations* is a rice bran signature.

### γ-Oryzanol / rice bran — human effects [E1–E2]

Meta-analysis of 11 RCTs (n = 572):

| Outcome | Pooled effect |
|---|---|
| Triglycerides | −15.13 mg/dL |
| Total cholesterol | −11.80 mg/dL |
| LDL-C | −15.11 mg/dL |
| HDL-C | no significant change |

"Rice Bran Consumption Improves Lipid Profiles: A Systematic Review and
Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials." *Nutrients* 2025;17(1):114.
doi:10.3390/nu17010114 (v)

A second meta-analysis (8 RCTs, 14 effect sizes) reported TC −13.594 mg/dL,
LDL-C −14.580 mg/dL, HDL +3.074 mg/dL (*Crit Rev Food Sci Nutr* 2022;62(22),
doi:10.1080/10408398.2021.1895062 (v — record confirmed)).

**[E1]** The lipid effect is real and replicated. Caveats stated by the authors:
effects larger in Asian populations and in men, doses ≥30 g/mL, duration
>4 weeks, no long-term outcome data, and rice bran *oil* outperformed whole rice
bran.

**Attribution problem [E4]:** whether the effect is due to γ-oryzanol
specifically, to the co-occurring free phytosterols (which lower cholesterol by
a well-established, entirely separate micellar-competition mechanism), or to
tocotrienols, is **not resolved**. Rice bran oil delivers ~1 g/100 g of plain
phytosterols. The HMG-CoA-reductase-inhibition mechanism attributed to
γ-oryzanol rests on docking studies and cell work, not human kinetics; no human
pharmacokinetic study of γ-oryzanol was found. Assigning the lipid effect to
γ-oryzanol rather than to sterols is an unproven mechanistic story.

Animal work is at translationally awkward doses: γ-oryzanol at 0.16% of diet
(≈160 mg/kg diet, 2.6 mmol/kg) for 13 weeks in Sprague-Dawley rats reduced
hepatic TG by 59.1%, fasting glucose by 13.0%, serum IL-6, and raised
adiponectin (Wang O, et al. *PLoS One* 2015;10(2):e0118135,
doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0118135 (v)). **[E3 animal]**

Human RCT doses in the clinical literature: 300 mg/d (menopausal symptoms),
500–600 mg/d (performance, diabetes inflammation markers), up to 800 mg/d
reported safe. Trials are typically 4–12 weeks with small n. Ergogenic claims
are **inconclusive** — 500 mg/d for 9 weeks showed no significant effect.

### Tocotrienols [E2]

Rice bran is one of the few dietary matrices where **γ-tocotrienol**, not
α-tocopherol, is the dominant vitamin E form. Reported total tocotrienols
~830 µg/g DW in Japanese rice bran, 1350–1430 µg/g in tocotrienol-rich cultivars;
γ-T3 in whole grain 8.60–46.95 mg/kg. By contrast rice *germ* is
α-tocopherol-dominated with ~5× the total vitamin E of bran.

Sources: *J Cereal Sci* 2003, doi:10.1016/S0889-1575(03)00129-7 (v — record
confirmed); PubMed 17691808 (v — record confirmed).

Comparator: crude palm oil reaches ~800 mg/kg tocotrienols, so rice bran is
notable but not uniquely rich. Annatto is far richer. **Unique-ish, not unique.**

## 8. Pigmented Rices: Anthocyanins and Proanthocyanidins

### Observation

Black/purple and red rice differ by *branch point in the flavonoid pathway*, not
by degree:

- **Black/purple pericarp** = anthocyanins, mainly **cyanidin-3-O-glucoside**
  and peonidin-3-O-glucoside, requiring functional *Kala4/Ra* (bHLH) plus the
  C-S-A regulatory system (C1 = R2R3-MYB, S1 = bHLH, A1 = DFR).
- **Red pericarp** = **proanthocyanidins** (condensed tannins), controlled by
  *Rc* (bHLH) with *Rd* (DFR) modifying intensity. The near-universal white
  pericarp of cultivated rice traces to a loss-of-function 14 bp deletion in
  *Rc* fixed during domestication.

Both classes derive from the shared precursor 3',4'-dihydroxylated leucocyanidin.

Sources: *J Plant Biol* 2012, doi:10.1007/s12374-012-0043-9 (v — record
confirmed); "How rice organs are colored: The genetic basis of anthocyanin
biosynthesis in rice", *The Crop Journal* 2021, ScienceDirect S2214514121000805
(v — record confirmed).

### Quantities

Across ten Japanese cultivars: anthocyanin up to **5,045.6 µg/g** (black),
proanthocyanidin up to **3,060.6 µg/g** (red). Pigment concentration correlated
positively with total polyphenol and total antioxidant capacity.

Hosoda K, et al. *Asian-Australas J Anim Sci* 2018;31(8):1213–1220.
doi:10.5713/ajas.17.0655 (v)

**[E1]** for the compositional chemistry. **[E4]** for human health outcomes:
the reviewed literature is dominated by in vitro antioxidant assays and rodent
models. No adequately powered human RCT of pigmented rice on hard endpoints was
identified in this search. "Antioxidant capacity" measured by ORAC/DPPH is a
chemistry result, not a physiological one.

Note that anthocyanin and γ-oryzanol accumulate on **different developmental
schedules** during black rice grain filling (*PLOS One* 2024,
doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0302745 (v — record confirmed)) — relevant to harvest
timing, an under-explored lever.

## 9. Silicon: Rice as the Champion Accumulator

### Observation — the plant side is exceptionally well characterised [E1]

Rice accumulates silicon to **up to 10% of shoot dry weight** — more than any
macronutrient, and ~50× a non-accumulator like tomato (0.2% under identical
conditions). This requires a dedicated transport machinery:

| Transporter | Type | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lsi1** (OsNIP2;1) | NIP-III aquaporin, passive channel | distal plasma membrane of root exodermis and endodermis | influx of Si(OH)₄ from soil solution |
| **Lsi2** | active efflux transporter (anion-transporter-like) | **proximal** plasma membrane of the same cells | efflux toward stele |
| **Lsi3** | efflux | root cortex | xylem loading (≤30% contribution at 0.3 mM Si) |
| **Lsi6** | influx | shoot/node xylem parenchyma | xylem unloading, distribution |
| **SIET4** | | leaf | silica deposition into silica cells/bodies |

The **polar arrangement** — influx on the outer face, efflux on the inner face of
the *same* cell layer — is the architectural trick. Rice must do this because two
Casparian bands (exodermis and endodermis) sit in series, separated by aerenchyma
that has destroyed most cortical cells; there is no apoplastic route. Node I
carries Lsi6, Lsi2 and Lsi3 together and preferentially routes Si to the panicle
and husk.

Huang S, Ma JF. "Silicon transport and its 'homeostasis' in rice." *Quantitative
Plant Biology* 2025;5:e15. doi:10.1017/qpb.2024.19 (v)

Structural basis of Lsi1's selectivity for silicic acid over other small
uncharged solutes has been solved: "Structural basis for high selectivity of a
rice silicon channel Lsi1", *Nat Commun* 2021. doi:10.1038/s41467-021-26535-x
(v — record confirmed; full text not retrieved).

### Functions in the plant [E2]

Silicon deposits as amorphous silica (phytoliths) in silica cells, bulliform
cells and beneath the cuticle. Documented benefits: lodging resistance,
resistance to blast and sheath blight, herbivore resistance (leaf folder, fall
armyworm), alleviation of Mn/Fe/Al/Cd toxicity and salinity stress. Knockout of
Lsi1 or Lsi2 significantly reduces both Si uptake and **grain yield**. Silicon is
described as *agronomically essential* for rice — a deliberately careful phrase.

### The mechanism is contested

Coskun D, Deshmukh R, Sonah H, Menzies JG, Reynolds O, Ma JF, Kronzucker HJ,
Bélanger RR. "The controversies of silicon's role in plant biology." *New
Phytologist* 2019;221(1):67–85. doi:10.1111/nph.15343 (v — record confirmed via
Wiley; full text paywalled, summarised from secondary sources)

The authors argue that many reported Si effects on gene expression, hormone
signalling and metabolism are **downstream consequences** of reduced damage, not
evidence of an active metabolic role for Si. Their **"apoplastic obstruction"
hypothesis**: polymerised Si in the apoplast physically blocks pathogen effectors
and toxin movement; the plant's transcriptomic response is then simply the
response of a less-stressed plant.

**Status: HYPOTHESIS, widely discussed, not settled.** Do not report Si as having
a demonstrated active signalling role in plants.

### Silicon in the eater — much weaker than commonly claimed

**Observations:**
- Framingham Original and Offspring cohorts: dietary Si intake positively
  associated with bone mineral density in men and premenopausal women (not
  postmenopausal women, not older men). Mean intakes 30 and 33 mg/d (men), 24 and
  25 mg/d (women); intake declines with age.
  Jugdaohsingh R, et al. *J Bone Miner Res* 2004;19(2):297–307.
  doi:10.1359/JBMR.0301225 (v — record confirmed) **[E2 observational]**
- Absorption is form-dependent: the top dietary Si sources in those cohorts were
  **beer and bananas**, not rice — because beer contains soluble orthosilicic
  acid, whereas plant phytolith silica is poorly soluble. ~41% of ingested Si is
  excreted in urine.
  Sripanyakorn S, et al., "The comparative absorption of silicon from different
  foods and food supplements", *Br J Nutr* (v — record confirmed)

**Disconfirming:**
- **EFSA classifies silicon as non-essential for humans.** No specific
  biochemical function has been identified, no deficiency syndrome has been
  observed in humans, and no PRI/AI is set.
- The proposed mechanism (Si crosslinking collagen with glycosaminoglycans)
  derives from 1970s chick/rat studies and has not been demonstrated in humans.
- Milled white rice — the form most humans eat — has had the husk and bran, where
  nearly all the phytolith silica sits, removed.

**Verdict [E4]:** Rice's silicon accumulation is a spectacular fact of *plant*
biology with strong agronomic consequences. Its nutritional relevance to the
eater is unestablished, and the pathway from rice phytoliths to human bone is
speculative at every step. High reported Si intakes in India and China
(140–204 mg/d) reflect unrefined cereal consumption generally, not rice
specifically, and Si *intake* is not Si *absorption*.

## 10. Aerenchyma, Anaerobiosis, Arsenic and Cadmium

This is the single most consequential piece of rice biology for human health, and
it is unusually well resolved mechanistically.

### The chain of causation [E1]

1. **Rice grows flooded.** Constitutive lysigenous aerenchyma — gas-filled
   channels formed by programmed cell death of most cortical cells — supplies
   O₂ from shoot to root, with a radial-oxygen-loss barrier preventing leakage.
   Rice root anatomy therefore has exodermis and endodermis separated by void,
   with a strict two-step symplastic transit.
2. **Flooded soil is reducing.** At low redox potential, arsenate is reduced to
   **arsenite**, As(III), which at soil pH is the *uncharged* species
   As(OH)₃ — a near-perfect structural mimic of silicic acid, Si(OH)₄.
3. **The silicon transporters take it.** Lsi1 admits arsenite; Lsi2 pumps it
   toward the xylem.
   - `lsi1` mutation significantly decreases arsenite uptake.
   - `lsi2` mutants show **greatly decreased** arsenite transport to xylem and
     accumulation in shoots and grain; in field-grown rice, Lsi2 mutation had a
     **much greater** impact on grain arsenic than Lsi1.
   - Conclusion, in the authors' words: "Arsenite transport in rice roots
     therefore shares the same highly efficient pathway as silicon, which
     explains why rice is efficient in arsenic accumulation."

   Ma JF, Yamaji N, Mitani N, Xu X-Y, Su Y-H, McGrath SP, Zhao F-J. *PNAS*
   2008;105(29):9931–9935. doi:10.1073/pnas.0802361105 (v)
4. **Nodes are the sorting hub.** A vacuolar ABC transporter, OsABCC1, sequesters
   As in node phloem companion cells and limits grain loading (PMC4226097 (v —
   record confirmed)); node biology dominates grain As distribution (*J Exp Bot*
   2015;66(13):3717, doi:10.1093/jxb/erv164 (v — record confirmed)).

**This is the striking finding of the whole rice literature**: rice's arsenic
burden is not incidental contamination but the *cost of its silicon adaptation*
running under the redox chemistry of the paddy. The trait that makes rice
lodging-resistant and disease-resistant is the trait that loads its grain with a
Group 1 carcinogen.

### Cadmium is the mirror image [E1]

Cd enters rice via **OsNRAMP5**, the Mn transporter, also polarly localised at
the distal plasma membrane of exodermis and endodermis. `Osnramp5` knockout
drastically reduces root Cd uptake and grain Cd.

**The tradeoff is hard.** Flooding immobilises Cd (as CdS) but mobilises As;
aerobic/alternate-wetting-drying water-saving cultivation cuts As but raises Cd.

- Arao T, et al. "Effects of Water Management on Cadmium and Arsenic Accumulation
  and Dimethylarsinic Acid Concentrations in Japanese Rice." *Environ Sci Technol*
  2009. doi:10.1021/es9022738 (v — record confirmed)
- "Water Management Practices Affect Arsenic and Cadmium Accumulation in Rice
  Grains." PMC4075083 (v — record confirmed)
- Best current compromise: flood **after heading**, which lowers grain Cd without
  a matching As penalty.
- Recently proposed mechanism for the "aerobic penalty": an ABA-activated
  OsSAPK2–OsNAC4 cascade upregulating OsNRAMP1 (*Current Biology* 2026,
  S0960-9822(26)00737-2 (v — record confirmed)). **[E3, new, unreplicated.]**

Cadmium's relevance is not theoretical: rice-borne Cd caused **itai-itai
disease** in the Jinzu River basin, Japan — one of the four officially recognised
Japanese pollution diseases.

### Exposure and mitigation [E2]

- FDA action level for inorganic arsenic in **infant rice cereal: 100 ppb**;
  levels fell ~45% after it was set.
- EFSA has concluded dietary inorganic arsenic exposure should be reduced.
- **Cooking works.** Percolating near-boiling water removes **49 ± 7%** of
  inorganic As across wholegrain and polished rice (Raab A, et al., *PLOS One*
  2015;10(7):e0131608, doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0131608 (v)). Excess-water
  cooking at 12:1 removes **57 ± 5%** on average, up to ~70% — but also leaches
  enriched vitamins and some minerals.
- Modified parboiling of husked wholegrain: ~25% reduction in polished grain.

**Honest framing:** rice is the highest-inorganic-arsenic staple, this is
mechanistically explained, it matters most for infants and high-rice-intake
populations, and it is substantially mitigable by variety choice, water
management and cooking method. It is not a reason to avoid rice in a mixed diet;
it is a reason not to base an infant's diet on rice products.

## 11. Phytate and Phytase

### Observation

Rice, like all cereals, stores phosphorus as phytic acid (myo-inositol
hexakisphosphate) concentrated in the aleurone. Two claims are commonly made;
they have different evidence quality.

**Claim A: rice has low endogenous phytase.** This is directionally correct.
Rye has the highest cereal phytase activity, followed by wheat and barley; oats,
maize and rice are low. The original measurements go back to *Nature* 1944
("Activity of the Phytase in Different Cereals and its Resistance to Dry Heat",
*Nature* 153:650, doi:10.1038/153650a0 (v — record confirmed, full text not
retrieved)) and are corroborated by later fraction-resolved work (*Food Bioproc
Technol* 2022, doi:10.1007/s11947-022-02981-3 (v — record confirmed, paywalled)).
This is why rye is the classic sourdough starter grain and why soaking rice does
comparatively little.

**FLAG:** the widely circulated specific figure "wheat contains **fourteen times**
more phytase than rice" traces primarily to the Weston A. Price Foundation's
"Living With Phytic Acid" article and its downstream copies, not to a primary
comparative assay I could verify. Treat the *ratio* as unsourced folklore and the
*ranking* as sound. **[Ranking: E2. The 14× figure: E4.]**

**Claim B: phytate in whole grains is harmful.** This does not survive scrutiny.

Brouns F. "Phytic Acid and Whole Grains for Health Controversy." *Nutrients*
2021;14(1):25. doi:10.3390/nu14010025 (v)

- In monotonous, grain-dominated diets in low-income settings, phytate-bound
  mineral excretion can exceed total mineral intake — a real deficiency risk.
- In mixed Western diets, higher whole-grain intake is associated with **better**
  health outcomes despite phytate, and legumes and seeds usually contribute more
  phytate than grains do.
- Critically: **reduced in vitro phytate does not reliably translate into improved
  mineral status in humans.** Bioaccessibility ≠ status.
- Author conclusion: advice to avoid whole grains on phytate grounds is
  unjustified.

**[E1 for the conclusion in mixed diets; E2 for the deficiency risk in monotonous
diets.]**

Parboiling and germination both reduce rice phytate measurably (see §4, §6).

## 12. Momilactones: A Two-Species Molecule

Momilactones A and B are labdane-related diterpenoids acting as phytoalexins
(pathogen-induced) and allelochemicals (root-exuded growth inhibitors).

**The striking fact:** despite extensive phytochemical survey, momilactones have
been found in **only two organisms** — *Oryza sativa* and the moss *Hypnum
plumaeforme* — taxa separated by hundreds of millions of years. This is treated
in the literature as convergent or parallel molecular evolution of an entire
biosynthetic gene cluster.

- Kato-Noguchi H, Peters RJ. "The Role of Momilactones in Rice Allelopathy."
  *J Chem Ecol* 2013;39:175–185. doi:10.1007/s10886-013-0236-9 (v — record
  confirmed)
- "Convergent or parallel molecular evolution of momilactone A and B: potent
  allelochemicals, momilactones have been found only in rice and the moss
  *Hypnum plumaeforme*." *J Plant Physiol* 2011. PubMed 21620514 (v — record
  confirmed)
- Kato-Noguchi H, et al. *Biosci Biotechnol Biochem* 2007;71(12):3127. (v —
  record confirmed) — first bryophyte occurrence.

Concentrations: momilactone A 4.5–140 µg/g and B 2.9–85 µg/g in rice tissue;
momilactone B is preferentially *secreted* despite lower endogenous
concentration.

Momilactones accumulate further under stress — salinity treatment during
germination increases momilactone and phenolic accumulation in germinated brown
rice (PMC10340199 (v — record confirmed)).

**[E2 for occurrence and plant function.]** **[E4 for human effects]** — the
mammalian literature (anti-cancer, anti-diabetic activity) is in vitro and
early-stage; dietary exposure from milled rice is negligible and no human data
exist. This is a fascinating plant-biology fact, not a nutrition claim.

## 13. Rice Epidemiology: The Boring, Negative Part

White rice intake is associated with higher type 2 diabetes incidence:

- Pooled RR **1.11 per serving/day** (95% CI 1.08–1.14).
- Asian populations (3–4 servings/day): pooled RR **1.55**; Western populations
  (1–2 servings/week): **1.12**.
- 7 prospective cohorts, 13,284 incident cases among 352,384 participants,
  follow-up 4–22 years.

Hu EA, Pan A, Malik V, Sun Q. "White rice consumption and risk of type 2
diabetes: meta-analysis and systematic review." *BMJ* 2012;344:e1454.
doi:10.1136/bmj.e1454 (v — record confirmed)

**[E2 observational].** Residual confounding by overall diet quality and
socioeconomic transition is a live concern, and the association is dose-dependent
rather than categorical. The mechanistic reading — that milling removes bran
fibre, magnesium, oryzanol and tocotrienols and raises glycaemic index — is
plausible but is a **hypothesis**, and the corresponding brown-rice substitution
trials on hard endpoints do not exist.

# PART II — WHEAT (*Triticum* spp.)

## 1. Gluten: The Only Cereal Protein That Builds a Dough

### Observation [E1]

Gluten is not one protein. It is a two-component system:

- **Gliadins** — monomeric prolamins (α/β, γ, ω types) with only
  *intra*molecular disulfide bonds. They behave as a viscous liquid; they are the
  plasticiser/extensibility component.
- **Glutenins** — polymeric, built from **high-molecular-weight glutenin subunits
  (HMW-GS)** and low-molecular-weight subunits joined head-to-tail by
  *inter*chain disulfide bonds into some of the largest protein polymers known in
  nature. They supply elasticity.

Together, hydrated and worked, they form a continuous crosslinked network that
retains CO₂ during leavening. No other cereal does this.

Key facts from Shewry PR, "Wheat", *J Exp Bot* 2009;60(6):1537–1553,
doi:10.1093/jxb/erp058 (v):

- Gluten = ~100 individual protein components, ~**80% of total grain protein** in
  European wheats.
- Bread wheat cultivars express **3 to 5 HMW subunit genes**, and HMW-GS account
  for **up to ~12% of total grain protein**.
- Allelic variation in HMW subunit number and properties determines polymer size
  and hence dough strength — this is the single largest genetic lever on
  breadmaking quality.
- Grain: 60–70% starch, 8–15% protein; white flour 65–75% starch.
- >30,000 genes expressed in developing grain; ~1,125 protein components detected
  in mature grain.

Mechanistic detail is now being filled in: wheat protein disulfide isomerase
actively promotes gluten network formation (*PNAS Nexus* 2024;3(9):pgae356,
doi:10.1093/pnasnexus/pgae356 (v — record confirmed)); molecular dynamics
reproduces the viscoelastic split, with non-covalent gliadin interactions giving
the viscous term and glutenin disulfides the elastic term (*PLoS Comput Biol*
2021;17(3):e1008840, doi:10.1371/journal.pcbi.1008840 (v — record confirmed)).

### Is "only wheat" literally true?

Nearly. Rye and barley have homologous prolamins (secalins, hordeins) — the
reason all three are celiac-toxic — but they do not build the same network:

- **Rye**: high pentosan (arabinoxylan) content and water-soluble protein
  complexes physically prevent gluten network structuring. Rye doughs are
  "short and plastic", not viscoelastic. Rye breads depend on **starch
  gelatinisation and pentosan gels**, not gluten, for structure — which is why
  rye baking requires acidification (sourdough) to control amylase activity.
- **Barley**: substituting 10–40% hulless barley flour into wheat flour degrades
  gluten microstructure and mixing behaviour, partially breaking network
  crosslinks (PMC8710734 (v — record confirmed)).
- **Triticale** (×*Triticosecale*, wheat × rye) does form workable dough,
  inheriting wheat's system — which supports the claim being about the
  wheat *gene complement*, not about wheat as a taxon.

**Verdict:** the claim "no other grain forms wheat's polymeric gluten network" is
**true and well supported [E1]**, provided it is stated as a property of the
Triticeae HMW-glutenin complement rather than as mysticism about wheat.

## 2. Hexaploidy, the D Genome, and What It Did to the Protein Complement

### Observation [E1]

Bread wheat is **AABBDD** hexaploid. The A genome comes from an einkorn-type
diploid, the B genome probably from *Aegilops speltoides*, and the D genome from
*Aegilops tauschii* via hybridisation with cultivated tetraploid emmer —
recently enough that little divergence separates the hexaploid D genome from the
extant diploid one (Shewry 2009 (v)).

Two consequences, both large, both from the same event:

**(a) The D genome made bread wheat superior for baking.** *Glu-D1* alleles
(notably the Dx5+Dy10 subunit pair) are the strongest single determinant of dough
strength, which is why hexaploid bread wheat displaced tetraploid wheat for
leavened bread while durum (AABB) kept pasta. **[E1, though this document did not
re-fetch the primary Glu-D1 allele literature — (r)]**

**(b) The D genome created the 33-mer.** The immunodominant celiac peptide
α2-gliadin p57–89 — a **33-amino-acid, proline-rich, protease-resistant fragment
containing six partly overlapping copies of three DQ2.5-restricted T-cell
epitopes** — is encoded at the *Gli-D2* locus. It arose through gene duplication
and diversification of the α-gliadin family **following allopolyploidisation**.

- Ozuna CV, et al. "Diversification of the celiac disease α-gliadin complex in
  wheat: a 33-mer peptide with six overlapping epitopes, evolved following
  polyploidization." *The Plant Journal* 2015;82(5):794–805.
  doi:10.1111/tpj.12851 (v — record confirmed)
- The complete 33-mer is present in *some* *Ae. tauschii* accessions but absent
  from most, indicating it entered bread wheat with the D genome rather than
  being created de novo (PMC8248119 (v — record confirmed)).
- α-Gliadins also carry p31–43, which drives an innate (non-T-cell) response, and
  a DQ2.5-glia-α3 epitope overlapping the 33-mer.

**This is the deepest irony in cereal biology and it is well evidenced:** one
polyploidisation event simultaneously produced the world's best bread grain and
its most potent food-derived autoimmune trigger.

### Important corollary [E1]

Because einkorn (AA), emmer (AABB) and durum (AABB) lack the D genome, they lack
the full 33-mer. **This does not make them safe.** Gluten from all wheat species
stimulates immune responses in human-derived T-cell assays; there are many other
CD-active epitopes on A- and B-genome gliadins and on glutenins (Brouns et al.
2022, below).

## 4. Amylase/Trypsin Inhibitors (ATIs): Real Mechanism, Unproven Diet Relevance

### The landmark observation [E1 for the mechanism]

Junker Y, Zeissig S, Kim S-J, Barisani D, Wieser H, Leffler DA, Zevallos V,
Libermann TA, Dillon S, Freitag TL, Kelly CP, Schuppan D. "Wheat amylase trypsin
inhibitors drive intestinal inflammation via activation of toll-like receptor 4."
*J Exp Med* 2012;209(13):2395–2408. doi:10.1084/jem.20102660 (v)

- Identified ATIs **CM3 and 0.19** as strong activators of innate immune
  responses in human and murine monocytes, macrophages and dendritic cells.
- ATIs **engage the TLR4–MD2–CD14 complex**, signalling through both MyD88 and
  TRIF, i.e. LPS-like, CD14-dependent.
- Mice: gavage doses were LPS 20 µg/g bw, water-soluble gliadin 2 mg/g bw, **ATI
  0.075 mg/g bw (75 mg/kg)**, water-soluble zein 2 mg/g bw; duodenal cytokine
  mRNA at 4 h. TLR4-deficient C3H/HeJ mice were protected.
- Confirmed and extended in Zevallos VF, et al. "Nutritional Wheat Amylase-
  Trypsin Inhibitors Promote Intestinal Inflammation via Activation of Myeloid
  Cells." *Gastroenterology* 2017;152(5):1100–1113.e12 (v — record confirmed) —
  dendritic cells > macrophages > monocytes; intestinal epithelial cells
  non-responsive.

This is a genuinely important result: **a dietary protein family that is a
bona fide TLR4 agonist.** Very few foods can claim that.

### The disconfirming half, from the same research community

Geisslitz S, Shewry P, Brouns F, et al. "Wheat amylase/trypsin inhibitors (ATIs):
occurrence, function and health aspects." *Eur J Nutr* 2022;61(6):2873–2880.
doi:10.1007/s00394-022-02841-y (v)

Explicit gaps stated by the authors:

1. **"The assumed impacts of ATIs on health are not yet validated by data
   obtained from human studies."** In vitro and animal work "are not sufficient to
   provide evidence on in vivo effects in humans consuming processed
   wheat-based foods."
2. For non-celiac wheat sensitivity: **"No direct evidence of the impact of ATIs
   on the gut has been demonstrated with in vivo studies."**
3. Heat stability is **contradictory** — some studies show ATIs inactivated/
   degraded by bread baking and pasta cooking, others show survival.
4. Relative activities of different ATI isoforms are unclear.
5. The review provides **no quantitative ATI content data** (mg/g or % protein)
   for wheat at all.
6. **ATIs occur in the seeds of all cereals** — wheat, barley, rye, maize, millet
   and rice. They are not a wheat-specific molecule. Rye inhibitors have
   different specificities, aggregation and IgE-binding than wheat/barley
   homologues (PubMed 7925368 (v — record confirmed)).

### ATIs across wheat species — no ancient/modern trend

Ten wheat varieties (diploid, tetraploid, hexaploid), two locations, three years:

- α-Amylase inhibitory activity: **1.12% to 66.63%**. Hexaploids highest
  (56–67%); tetraploids 39–66%; **einkorn lowest (1.12–13.76%)**. Spelt 63.15%
  (USA) / 59.95% (Italy); Turkey Red 66.63%.
- Trypsin inhibitory activity: **18.61% to 100%**. **Einkorn 100% in all
  conditions**; hexaploids 59–71%; tetraploids 18–23%.
- **"It was not possible to highlight any trend in ATI activities as function of
  release year."**
- Genotype explained **55–65%** of variability; crop year and location
  significant but secondary.

"Amylase Trypsin Inhibitors (ATIs) in a Selection of Ancient and Modern Wheat:
Effect of Genotype and Growing Environment on Inhibitory Activities." *Foods*
2022. PMC9738579 (v)

Note the *opposite directions* for the two activities in einkorn — lowest amylase
inhibition, highest trypsin inhibition. This kills any simple "ancient wheat has
fewer inhibitors" story. A separate paper found **no correlation between ATI
content and amylase inhibitory activity** across hexaploid and tetraploid species
(ScienceDirect S2665927123001107 (v — record confirmed)) — meaning ATI *mass* and
ATI *function* are not interchangeable measures, and studies quantifying one
cannot be read as quantifying the other.

### Verdict

- ATIs activate TLR4: **[E1]**, in vitro and mouse.
- ATIs contribute to human wheat-related symptoms at realistic dietary intake:
  **[E4]** — no human dietary trial exists.
- ATIs are wheat-specific: **false**; they are pan-cereal.
- Ancient wheats have fewer/less-active ATIs: **not supported**.

## 5. Wheat Germ Agglutinin: The Clearest Case of Popular Overreach

Carcea M, Melloni S, Narducci V, Turfani V. "Wheat Germ Agglutinin (WGA): Its
Nature, Biological Role, Significance in Human Nutrition, and Possibility to Be
Used as Marker of Whole-Grain Status in Wheat-Based Foods." *Foods*
2024;13(18):2990. doi:10.3390/foods13182990 (v)

### What is measured

| Matrix | WGA content |
|---|---|
| Wheat germ | 0.1–0.5 g/kg (i.e. 100–500 µg/g) — conflicting reports |
| Whole wheat flour | 5.70–50 µg/g (wide inter-study variation) |
| **Cooked pasta** | **~0.3 µg/g** |

Note the ~2 orders of magnitude drop from flour to cooked pasta.

### What is claimed vs what is shown

- **In vitro**: WGA binds surface glycans on epithelial cells at low
  concentrations and can increase monolayer permeability. **[E3 in vitro]**
- **Animal**: lectins bind enterocyte glycoconjugates and can alter intestinal
  integrity. **[E3 animal]**
- **Toxicity threshold in vivo**: adverse GI effects occur at **7 g WGA/kg body
  weight over 10 days** — "doses higher than those ingested regularly in the
  human diet." For a 70 kg human that would be ~490 g of pure WGA over ten days;
  at 50 µg/g whole wheat flour, that is nominally ~10,000 kg of flour.
- **Thermal**: relatively heat-stable at moderate temperature; prolonged heat
  significantly reduces activity; **cooking pasta strongly reduces WGA activity**;
  sourdough fermentation of whole wheat flour reduces WGA by ~60%.
- **Human intake**: **"There are no data available on the mean dietary intake of
  WGA in humans."**
- **Authors' conclusion**: symptoms are "extremely rare with normal intake of food
  prepared for human use."

### Verdict

**[E4 — the harm claim fails.]** Every popular claim about WGA (joint attack,
leptin-receptor blockade, systemic inflammation from ordinary bread) traces to
wellness-media sources (Mercola, GreenMedInfo, goop, Shortform) rather than to
primary human data. WGA is a real, interesting protein with real in vitro
activity — it is a workhorse histochemical stain and a drug-delivery targeting
ligand precisely because it binds sialic acid and GlcNAc — but the gap between
the doses that do anything in vivo and dietary doses is roughly six orders of
magnitude.

The genuinely defensible use of WGA is the one in the paper's title: as a
**marker of whole-grain status**, since WGA is a germ protein and its presence
indexes germ content.

## 6. FODMAPs / Fructans

### Observation [E1]

Wheat's principal FODMAP is **fructan** (fructo-oligosaccharide). Humans lack the
hydrolases; fructans reach the colon intact, are osmotically active and rapidly
fermented — producing gas, distension and bloating. This is basic, uncontested
physiology.

### The trial that reframed the field

Skodje GI, Sarna VK, Minelle IH, Rolfsen KL, Muir JG, Gibson PR, Veierød MB,
Henriksen C, Lundin KEA. "Fructan, Rather Than Gluten, Induces Symptoms in
Patients With Self-Reported Non-Celiac Gluten Sensitivity." *Gastroenterology*
2018;154(3):529–539.e2. doi:10.1053/j.gastro.2017.10.040 (v)

- Double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled crossover; Oslo University
  Hospital, Oct 2014 – May 2016.
- **n = 59** adults on self-instituted gluten-free diets, celiac disease excluded.
- Concealed in muesli bars, 7 days each with ≥7-day washout: **gluten 5.7 g**,
  **fructan 2.1 g**, or placebo.
- GSRS-IBS overall: gluten 33.1 ± 13.3, **fructan 38.6 ± 12.3**, placebo
  34.3 ± 13.9; **P = 0.04**.
- Bloating highest on fructan, **P = 0.003**.

Note the effect size honestly: fructan scored ~4.3 points above placebo on a
scale where placebo itself scored 34.3. This is a real but modest group-level
effect, and gluten scored *below* placebo.

A follow-on randomised crossover in 59 self-reported NCGWS participants examining
faecal microbiota, SCFAs and calprotectin found **no group-level effect of
gluten** and supported fructans as the more likely symptom driver (*BMC Medicine*
2024. doi:10.1186/s12916-024-03562-1 (v — record confirmed)).

**[E1]** for "fructans contribute"; **[E2]** for "fructans rather than gluten
dominate in self-reported NCGS."

## 7. Non-Celiac Gluten/Wheat Sensitivity: Where the Literature Is Genuinely Contested

This is the area where reporting discipline matters most. The honest summary:

**Point 1 — the founding trial.** Biesiekierski JR, Newnham ED, Irving PM, Barrett
JS, Haines M, Doecke JD, Shepherd SJ, Muir JG, Gibson PR. "Gluten causes
gastrointestinal symptoms in subjects without celiac disease: a double-blind
randomized placebo-controlled trial." *Am J Gastroenterol* 2011;106(3):508–514.
doi:10.1038/ajg.2010.487 (v — record confirmed, PubMed 21224837). Found a
gluten-specific effect.

**Point 2 — the same group overturned it.** Biesiekierski JR, et al.
*Gastroenterology* 2013;145(2):320–328.e3. doi:10.1053/j.gastro.2013.04.051 (r).
When background FODMAPs were reduced first, gluten added back at **16 g/day**
produced effects indistinguishable from placebo. Biesiekierski has since written
explicitly about confirmation bias as a "trap" in this field.

**Point 3 — nocebo dominates.** Molina-Infante J, Carroccio A. "Suspected
Nonceliac Gluten Sensitivity Confirmed in Few Patients After Gluten Challenge in
Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Trials." *Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol*
2017;15(3):339–348. doi:10.1016/j.cgh.2016.08.007 (v — record confirmed). Across
pooled DBPC challenges, only a small minority showed gluten-specific
reproducible symptoms; a large fraction responded to placebo.

**Point 4 — real-world diagnostic yield is tiny.** In a cohort of 392 IBS
patients self-reporting gluten sensitivity, **6.63%** had celiac disease and
**0.51%** wheat allergy; after 6 months gluten-free and reintroduction,
**85.96% showed no specific reaction to gluten** (Capannolo et al. 2015, as
reported in Brouns et al. 2022 (v)).

**Point 5 — but something is happening in some people.** Multicentre randomised
DBPC gluten challenges have reported a reproducible responder subgroup
(PMC4772047 (v — record confirmed)), and dyspepsia/IBS subgroup trials continue
to find signals (PMC7016109, MDPI *Nutrients* 2020;12(3):705 (v — records
confirmed)). Carroccio's group has argued for wheat (not gluten) sensitivity with
histological and basophil-activation correlates.

**Point 6 — there is no biomarker.** Diagnosis remains exclusionary (Salerno
Experts' Criteria: double-blind placebo-controlled crossover challenge, which is
almost never performed clinically).

**Honest verdict [E2/E4 split]:**
- That *some* non-celiac individuals have reproducible wheat-triggered symptoms:
  **[E2]**.
- That the trigger is **gluten specifically**: **[E4] — repeatedly failed to
  replicate.**
- That fructans are a major contributor: **[E2]**.
- That ATIs are the trigger: **[E4] — mechanistically attractive, humanly
  untested.**
- Nocebo/expectancy is a large, measured component: **[E1]**.

## 8. Ancient vs Modern Wheat: The Claims Reverse Under Measurement

This is the cleanest case in the whole review of a popular belief being
contradicted by direct measurement.

### Composition [E1]

Geisslitz S, Longin CFH, Scherf KA, Koehler P. "Comparative Study on Gluten
Protein Composition of Ancient (Einkorn, Emmer and Spelt) and Modern Wheat
Species (Durum and Common Wheat)." *Foods* 2019;8(9):409.
doi:10.3390/foods8090409 (v)

Design: 15 cultivars per species (75 total), 4 German locations, 297 samples
analysed.

| Species | Protein (mg/g) | Gluten (mg/g) | Gliadin (mg/g) | Glutenin (mg/g) | Gliadin:glutenin (mean, range) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Common wheat | 96.1 | 80.4 | 41.2 | 16.6 | 2.5 (1.6–3.8) |
| Spelt | ~113 | up to 111.6 | ~60 | 19.0 | 3.3 (2.3–4.8) |
| Durum | 120.7 | ~84 | ~67 | 16.0 | 4.0 (2.1–7.5) |
| Emmer | ~115 | ~81 | ~63 | 12.8 | 4.9 (3.0–11.1) |
| Einkorn | ~116 | ~75 | ~59 | 10.3 | **6.5 (3.7–12.1)** |

**Ancient wheats had HIGHER protein and gliadin than modern common wheat.**
Modern common wheat had the **highest glutenin** and the **lowest
gliadin:glutenin ratio** — which is exactly why it bakes better, since glutenin
is the elastic polymer.

### Review-level synthesis [E1]

Brouns F, Geisslitz S, Guzman C, Ikeda TM, Arzani A, Latella G, et al. "Do ancient
wheats contain less gluten than modern bread wheat, in favour of better health?"
*Nutrition Bulletin* 2022;47(2):157–167. doi:10.1111/nbu.12551 (v)

- Ancient wheats contain **more** protein and gluten and **greater contents of
  many CD-active epitopes** than modern bread wheat.
- Protein content of modern bread wheat has **declined slightly** over time while
  starch increased — a dilution effect of yield gains, not a gluten increase.
- The 33-mer is absent from einkorn, emmer and durum (no D genome), **but gluten
  from all wheats stimulated immune responses in human-derived T cells.**
- Explicit conclusion: **"no single wheat type can be recommended as better or
  safer for reducing or mitigating CD."**
- Studies reporting symptom differences between heritage and modern wheat were
  limited by small samples and uncontrolled environmental/agronomic variables.
- Most supporting evidence for ancient-grain claims is "in vitro or animal
  systems, which have limited physiological relevance to humans."

### The historical-trend question [E1]

- Kasarda DD. "Can an increase in celiac disease be attributed to an increase in
  the gluten content of wheat as a consequence of wheat breeding?" *J Agric Food
  Chem* 2013;61(6):1155–1159. doi:10.1021/jf305122s (v — record confirmed).
  **No evidence of any trend toward higher protein content** in US spring or
  winter wheats since the early 20th century; when grown under comparable
  conditions, no difference in protein content between old and new varieties.
  Kasarda attributes rising celiac incidence to increased total wheat-flour and
  **vital wheat gluten** consumption, not to breeding.
- Pronin D, Börner A, Weber H, Scherf KA. *J Agric Food Chem* 2020 (v — record
  confirmed via ScienceDaily report of the primary study). Across **120 years** of
  German wheat cultivars grown together: modern varieties had **slightly less
  protein**; **gluten content constant**; gluten *composition* shifted slightly
  (more glutenin, less gliadin — again in the direction of *lower* relative
  gliadin).

### Verdict

**"Modern wheat has more gluten than old wheat" is FALSE.** Total gluten is flat
or slightly down; protein is flat or slightly down; the gliadin:glutenin ratio has
moved *toward* glutenin, i.e. away from the gliadin fraction that carries most CD
epitopes. Ancient wheats are, if anything, **more** gliadin-rich. **[E1]**

The one defensible residue of the ancient-grain claim: einkorn lacks the D genome
and therefore the full 33-mer, and had markedly lower α-amylase inhibitory
activity in one multi-environment study. Whether either translates into human
tolerance is **untested**.

## 9. Wheat Germ: Spermidine, Octacosanol, Wheat Germ Oil

### Spermidine — the strongest wheat-germ claim [E2]

Kiechl S, Pechlaner R, Willeit P, Notdurfter M, Paulweber B, Willeit K, et al.
"Higher spermidine intake is linked to lower mortality: a prospective
population-based study." *Am J Clin Nutr* 2018;108(2):371–380.
doi:10.1093/ajcn/nqy102 (v)

- Bruneck Study, n = 829, ages 45–84, 1995–2015, 341 deaths, FFQ at four
  timepoints.
- Adjusted HR per 1 SD higher spermidine intake: **0.74 (95% CI 0.66–0.83,
  P < 0.001)**; after full lifestyle/dietary adjustment **0.76 (0.67–0.86,
  P < 0.001)**.
- Independent validation in the SAPHIR cohort: **0.71 (0.53–0.95, P = 0.019)**.
- Top vs bottom tertile difference in mortality risk was equivalent to being
  **5.7 years younger (95% CI 3.6–8.1 y)**.
- Spermidine had the strongest inverse association among 146 nutrients examined.

Mechanism: spermidine induces autophagy and eIF5A hypusination; lifespan
extension is replicated in yeast, nematodes, flies and mice
(*Autophagy* 2019;15(1):165–168, doi:10.1080/15548627.2018.1530929 (v — record
confirmed)).

Wheat germ is repeatedly cited as the richest common dietary source
(~243 nmol/g), which is why commercial spermidine supplements are wheat germ
extracts.

**Honest caveats [E3 for causality]:** this is observational nutritional
epidemiology from a single primary cohort with one validation cohort; "146
nutrients examined" invites multiple-comparison concern even though spermidine
was pre-specified as a hypothesis; FFQ-derived polyamine intake is a proxy, and
gut bacteria synthesise substantial polyamine independent of diet. Human RCTs are
small, short and mostly on cognitive endpoints in subjective cognitive decline
(PMC5807086 (v — record confirmed)). The "5.7 years" figure is a **risk-scale
translation, not a measured lifespan gain**, and it is routinely misquoted as the
latter.

### Octacosanol / policosanol — the claim collapses [E1 negative]

Octacosanol, a C28 long-chain alcohol, is the principal policosanol of wheat germ
oil and was the basis of the 1970s Cureton ergogenic literature.

- Roughly 60 clinical trials from **a single Cuban research group with a
  financial interest** (1991–2002) reported 15–25% LDL reductions.
- **Independent replication failed.** Berthold HK, et al. "Effect of policosanol
  on lipid levels among patients with hypercholesterolemia or combined
  hyperlipidemia: a randomized controlled trial." *JAMA* 2006;295(19):2262–2269.
  doi:10.1001/jama.295.19.2262 (v — record confirmed): no significant effect on
  LDL.
- Further independent negative trials: *Am J Clin Nutr* 2006 ("Policosanol is
  ineffective in the treatment of hypercholesterolemia: a randomized controlled
  trial", ScienceDirect S0002916523292217 (v — record confirmed)); *Am Heart J*
  2006; Kassis & Jones, *Br J Nutr* 2007 (r).

**Verdict: policosanol/octacosanol lipid-lowering is unsupported.** Wheat germ oil
supplementation for endurance is likewise **[E4]**. This is a textbook example of
a single-source literature failing independent replication.

### Wheat germ oil generally

Wheat germ oil is genuinely the richest natural source of α-tocopherol among
common vegetable oils, and contains policosanols and phytosterols. The tocopherol
fact is solid **[E1]**; the performance claims built on it are not.

## 10. Wheat Bran/Aleurone: Betaine and Arabinoxylan

### Betaine [E2]

Wheat contains the **highest reported betaine levels of any plant food**, and it
is concentrated in the aleurone:

| Fraction | Betaine (mg/100 g) | Choline (mg/100 g) |
|---|---|---|
| Aleurone | 1,115 | 210 |
| Bran | 867 | 102 |
| Flour | 23 | 28 |

Sources: Likes R, Madl RL, Zeisel SH, Craig SAS, *J Cereal Sci* 2007,
doi:10.1016/j.jcs.2006.08.009 (v — record confirmed); Graham SF, et al., "Analysis
of Betaine and Choline Contents of Aleurone, Bran, and Flour Fractions of Wheat
Using ¹H NMR Spectroscopy", *J Agric Food Chem* 2009, doi:10.1021/jf802885m
(v — record confirmed). Whole grain ~2.91 mg/g betaine, ~0.14 mg/g free choline.

Betaine is a physiologically real methyl donor (betaine–homocysteine
methyltransferase) and an osmolyte; the EFSA-authorised health claim concerns
homocysteine, at 1.5 g/day — far above dietary wheat intake. So: **wheat aleurone
is a uniquely rich betaine source [E2]**, but ordinary bread consumption does not
reach pharmacologically active betaine doses. Note the ~50× loss from aleurone to
white flour — this is one of the strongest specific arguments for whole-grain or
aleurone-enriched wheat products.

### Arabinoxylan [E1]

Arabinoxylan is **65–70% of wheat grain cell wall material** (β-glucan ~25%,
cellulose 2–3%, glucomannan 2–7%); aleurone cell walls are ~65% arabinoxylan and
~29% β-glucan. Wheat and rye are the world's dominant dietary arabinoxylan
sources. Arabinoxylan is a distinct fibre class from oat/barley β-glucan and from
rice's mostly cellulosic bran fibre — highly fermentable, ferulate-crosslinked,
and the vehicle for wheat bran's bound-phenolic content.

Sources: PMC10534680, PMC12872903 (v — records confirmed).

## 11. Fermentation, Sprouting and Long-Ferment Sourdough

### Sourdough and ATIs [E3]

Huang X, Schuppan D, Rojas Tovar LE, Zevallos VF, Loponen J, Gänzle M. "Sourdough
Fermentation Degrades Wheat Alpha-Amylase/Trypsin Inhibitor (ATI) and Reduces
Pro-Inflammatory Activity." *Foods* 2020;9(7):943. doi:10.3390/foods9070943 (v)

- Conditions: 250 g flour + 250 g water + 15 g rye starter, **30 °C, 17 h,
  final pH 3.6–3.7**. Yeast control stayed at pH 5.4–5.6.
- Mechanism: **acid activation of endogenous aspartic proteases**; aspartic
  protease inhibitors blocked degradation, confirming enzyme involvement.
- ATI tetramers were substantially but **partially** degraded; yeast fermentation
  showed no significant ATI breakdown; amylase inhibitory activity fell
  significantly.
- Human THP-1 monocytes exposed to sourdough-fermented extracts released
  significantly less MCP-1 and TNF-α (p < 0.05).
- **Authors' own limitations**: "ATI monomers retain biological activity, albeit
  strongly reduced"; degradation was incomplete; laboratory conditions;
  **"limited human clinical evidence for symptom improvement in wheat-sensitive
  populations."**

So: the mechanism is real, low pH is the operative variable (not "sourdough
magic"), and the human endpoint is **untested**.

### Sourdough and gluten [E3, and a hard limit]

- Selected lactobacilli plus fungal proteases can degrade gluten to **<10 ppm**
  in controlled settings, and extensively hydrolysed products were tolerated by
  celiac patients over 60 days without symptoms or histological change (Di Cagno/
  Gobbetti group; also the PA29 abstract (v — record confirmed)).
- **But**: ordinary artisan sourdough does not reliably get below the 20 ppm
  gluten-free threshold. Sourdough bread is **not safe for celiac disease**
  outside tightly controlled clinical protocols.
- Directly disconfirming: "Sourdough Fermentation of Wheat Flour does not Prevent
  the Interaction of Transglutaminase 2 with α2-Gliadin or Gluten" (PMC4425136
  (v — record confirmed)) — the key deamidation step that generates high-affinity
  DQ2.5 ligands still occurs.

### Sourdough and phytate [E2]

Acidification activates endogenous cereal phytase; reductions of up to ~90% of
phytate are reported for long sourdough fermentation (MDPI *Foods* 2025;5(3):74
review (v — record confirmed); *Fermentation* 2021;7(4):246 (v — record
confirmed)). Wheat's own phytase makes this work — the same reason rice does not
respond well to soaking.

### Sourdough and FODMAPs [E2]

Long fermentation consumes fructans; low-FODMAP sourdough wheat bread is an
active and reasonably supported product category (*Impact of sourdough
fermentation on FODMAPs and amylase-trypsin inhibitor levels in wheat dough*,
ResearchGate 364669079 (v — record confirmed)). Given §6, this is plausibly the
mechanism by which sourdough is better tolerated — **not** gluten degradation.

### Sprouting [E4 for ATIs]

Sprouting reliably reduces phytate (endogenous phytase activation). For **ATIs,
there is essentially no published measurement** — this is a genuine gap, not a
settled negative. Do not repeat the claim that sprouting reduces ATIs; it is
untested. ATI accumulation *during grain development* has been characterised
(*BMC Plant Biol* 2021;21:113, doi:10.1186/s12870-021-02886-x (v — record
confirmed)), but post-harvest germination effects have not.

## 12. Gluten Avoidance in Non-Celiacs: The Negative Result

Lebwohl B, Cao Y, Zong G, Hu FB, Green PHR, Neugut AI, Rimm EB, Sampson L,
Dougherty LW, Giovannucci E, Willett WC, Sun Q, Chan AT. "Long term gluten
consumption in adults without celiac disease and risk of coronary heart disease:
prospective cohort study." *BMJ* 2017;357:j1892. doi:10.1136/bmj.j1892 (v —
record confirmed)

- ~110,000 participants (64,714 women, Nurses' Health Study; 45,303 men, Health
  Professionals Follow-up Study), celiac excluded.
- **No association** between estimated gluten intake and incident coronary heart
  disease.
- **After adjustment for refined grain intake, higher gluten intake was
  associated with LOWER CHD risk: HR 0.85 (0.77–0.93)** — i.e. gluten was acting
  as a marker for whole-grain intake.
- Authors: "the promotion of gluten-free diets among people without celiac
  disease should not be encouraged."

**[E2 observational, large, well-adjusted].** Consistent with the broader
whole-grain literature (Aune D, et al. *BMJ* 2016;353:i2716,
doi:10.1136/bmj.i2716 (r)).

# PART IV — Popular Claims That Fail on the Evidence

Stated plainly, as instructed.

1. **"Modern wheat has been bred to be much higher in gluten than old wheat."**
   **False.** Gluten content is flat over 120 years; protein has fallen slightly;
   ancient wheats measure *higher* in protein and gliadin. (Kasarda 2013;
   Pronin et al. 2020; Geisslitz et al. 2019; Brouns et al. 2022)

2. **"Ancient wheats (einkorn, emmer, spelt, khorasan) are safer or better
   tolerated."** **Not supported.** They contain more gluten and more CD-active
   epitopes; no wheat type is recommendable as safer for celiac disease. Einkorn
   lacks the 33-mer but shows the *highest* trypsin-inhibitory activity of the
   species tested.

3. **"Lectins, especially wheat germ agglutinin, damage the gut at dietary
   intakes."** **Not supported.** In vivo toxicity requires ~7 g/kg body weight
   over 10 days; there are no human dietary intake data at all; cooking pasta
   drops WGA to ~0.3 µg/g; the review authors state symptoms are "extremely rare
   with normal intake." Every dramatic claim traces to wellness media.

4. **"Gluten specifically causes symptoms in non-celiac gluten sensitivity."**
   **Repeatedly failed to replicate.** The originating group's own follow-up found
   16 g/day gluten indistinguishable from placebo once FODMAPs were controlled;
   pooled DBPC challenges confirm gluten-specific responses in only a minority
   with a large nocebo component; fructans outperform gluten as a trigger.

5. **"Sourdough makes bread safe for celiacs."** **False and potentially
   dangerous.** Ordinary sourdough does not reach <20 ppm; TG2 still deamidates
   α2-gliadin after sourdough fermentation. Only specific engineered
   lactobacilli-plus-protease protocols achieved <10 ppm, in controlled trials.

6. **"Sprouting reduces ATIs."** **Untested**, not established. Sprouting does
   reduce phytate.

7. **"Wheat germ oil / octacosanol lowers cholesterol or improves endurance."**
   **Fails independent replication.** ~60 positive trials from one financially
   interested Cuban group; multiple independent RCTs including *JAMA* 2006 found
   no effect.

8. **"Cooking rice with coconut oil and cooling it halves the calories."**
   **Unsupported.** Traces to a 2015 undergraduate conference presentation, never
   peer-reviewed, never replicated. Native rice resistant starch is 0.02–0.15%;
   retrogradation raises it, but nowhere near a 50% calorie reduction. Realistic
   estimates are ~10–15% of starch calories at best.

9. **"Rice is a high-resistant-starch food."** **False as measured.** Native rice
   starch RS is a fraction of one percent even in high-amylose cultivars.
   Parboiling and retrogradation raise it meaningfully; the raw grain does not
   deliver it.

10. **"Rice is a good dietary silicon source for human bone health."**
    **Unsupported at every step.** EFSA classifies silicon as non-essential with
    no identified human biochemical function and no deficiency syndrome; phytolith
    silica is poorly soluble; milling removes the silicon-rich fractions; the top
    dietary silicon sources in the Framingham cohorts were beer and bananas.

11. **"Wheat contains 14× more phytase than rice."** The **ranking** (rye > wheat
    > barley >> oats, maize, rice) is sound; the **specific 14× figure** traces
    to secondary advocacy sources, not to a primary comparative assay I could
    verify.

12. **"Phytate in whole grains harms mineral status."** **Not supported in mixed
    diets.** Real risk exists in monotonous grain-dominated diets; in mixed diets
    whole-grain intake associates with better outcomes, and improved in vitro
    bioaccessibility does not reliably translate into improved human mineral
    status.

13. **"Germinated brown rice works through GABA acting on the brain."**
    **Unsupported.** GABA accumulation is real and large; the human trial is
    unblinded, industry-supplied, and uses white rice as comparator, so bran
    itself is unexcluded; oral GABA's access to the human CNS is contested.

14. **"γ-Oryzanol is the cholesterol-lowering agent in rice bran oil."** The
    **lipid effect is real** (meta-analysed RCTs). The **attribution to
    γ-oryzanol specifically is not established** — co-occurring free phytosterols
    at ~1.4 g/100 g have their own well-established mechanism, and no human
    γ-oryzanol pharmacokinetic study was found.

15. **"ATIs are what makes wheat inflammatory for ordinary people."** ATIs are a
    genuine TLR4 agonist (in vitro, mouse). But they occur in **all** cereals
    including rice, ATI mass and ATI activity do not correlate, and the review
    authors state flatly that human dietary evidence does not exist.

# PART V — What Remains Genuinely Unknown

Left unknown rather than filled in with plausible stories.

**Rice**

- Why rice evolved glutelin dominance, and what it does for the plant.
- The quantitative contribution of undigested PB-I prolamin to human protein
  losses.
- Whether rice's low allergenicity is intrinsic to its protein chemistry or a
  tolerance artefact of universal early exposure.
- Relative weight of granule size vs amylopectin fine structure in setting rice
  starch digestibility.
- Whether protein crosslinking or lipid complexing dominates rice ageing texture.
- Whether germinated brown rice's metabolic effects are attributable to GABA at
  all, versus bran constituents.
- Whether γ-oryzanol as such (rather than co-extracted phytosterols and
  tocotrienols) is responsible for the measured lipid effects; no human PK data.
- Whether silicon has any function in human physiology.
- Whether Coskun's apoplastic-obstruction model or an active-signalling model
  explains silicon's protective effects in plants.
- Whether pigmented rice anthocyanins produce any human clinical benefit.
- Whether brown-rice-for-white-rice substitution reduces diabetes incidence
  (never trialled on hard endpoints).

**Wheat**

- The mechanism(s) of non-celiac wheat sensitivity in the reproducible responder
  subgroup; there is no biomarker.
- Whether ATIs have any effect in humans at dietary intakes, in any population.
- ATI isoform-specific activities and their fate under real baking/pasta
  processing (the literature directly contradicts itself on heat stability).
- Whether the einkorn 33-mer absence has any clinical consequence.
- Whether long-ferment sourdough improves symptoms in wheat-sensitive humans
  (mechanistic plausibility, no trial).
- Effect of sprouting on ATIs (no measurements).
- Whether spermidine intake is causally related to mortality, and whether wheat
  germ is an effective delivery vehicle.
- Quantitative ATI content of wheat in mg/g — the 2022 *Eur J Nutr* review
  provides none.
