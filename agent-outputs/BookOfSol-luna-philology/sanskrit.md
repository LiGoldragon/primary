# The Moon as Threshold: What the Sanskrit Says

Philological audit of the Sanskrit behind the lunar-threshold material in
*The Book of Sol*. The question under test: does the Sanskrit assert a physical,
bodily destination, a non-physical passage, or is it undetermined?

Method note. Every Sanskrit passage below was retrieved in Devanāgarī from a
primary digital edition and transliterated by hand into IAST. Editions used are
named at each passage. Nothing here is quoted from memory. Where the retrieved
digital text carries an obvious orthographic slip against the printed editions,
the slip is flagged in place.

Discipline applied throughout: **OBSERVATION** = what the grammar and lexicon
say; **HYPOTHESIS** = what it may imply doctrinally; **UNKNOWN** = genuinely
undetermined or where commentators diverge. Where a passage is undetermined
between a physical and non-physical reading, it is left undetermined.

## 1. Chāndogya Upaniṣad 5.3–5.10 — the Pañcāgni-vidyā and the two paths

### 1.1 The frame question (CU 5.3.2)

> वेत्थ यदितोऽधि प्रजाः प्रयन्तीति न भगव इति वेत्थ यथा पुनरावर्तन्त३ इति ।

*vettha yad ito 'dhi prajāḥ prayantīti na bhagava iti, vettha yathā
punar āvartanta3 iti*

Gloss of the clause that carries the weight — *yad ito 'dhi prajāḥ prayanti*:

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *yad* | rel. pron., n. sg., adverbial | "how / whither / that" |
| *itaḥ* | adv., ablatival | "from here" |
| *adhi* | preverb/postposition governing abl. | "up from, away from" |
| *prajāḥ* | f. nom. pl. of *prajā* | "creatures, progeny, people" |
| *prayanti* | 3 pl. pres. act., *pra-√i* | "go forth / depart / pass away" |

**OBSERVATION.** The subject of the whole enquiry is *prajāḥ*, "creatures" —
not *ātman*, not *jīva*, not *prāṇa*, not *śarīra*. The verb is *pra-√i*.
In Vedic and classical Sanskrit *praiti* is the ordinary lexicalised idiom for
*to die* (cf. KsU 1.2 below, *asmāl lokāt prayanti*). Its motion sense is
bleached in exactly the way English "pass away" and "depart" are bleached.
A translation that renders *prayanti* "depart from this world" is neutral;
one that renders it "travel to" adds what the idiom does not supply.

**OBSERVATION.** *itaḥ … adhi* is ablative-of-source, "away from here". The
question asks a *whence*, and only by implication a *whither*. The Sanskrit
question is literally "do you know how creatures go forth away from here?"

### 1.2 The first fire and the birth of Soma (CU 5.4.1–2)

> असौ वाव लोको गौतमाग्निस्तस्यादित्य एव समिद्रश्मयो धूमोऽहरर्चिश्चन्द्रमा अङ्गारा
> नक्षत्राणि विस्फुलिङ्गाः ॥ १ ॥
> तस्मिन्नेतस्मिन्नग्नौ देवाः श्रद्धां जुह्वति तस्या आहुतेः सोमो राजा सम्भवति ॥ २ ॥

*asau vāva loko gautamāgnis, tasyāditya eva samid, raśmayo dhūmo,
'har arciś, candramā aṅgārā, nakṣatrāṇi visphuliṅgāḥ ||1||
tasminn etasminn agnau devāḥ śraddhāṃ juhvati; tasyā āhuteḥ somo rājā
sambhavati ||2||*

(The Wikisource text prints *अहुतेः*; the bhāṣya text and printed editions read
*आहुतेः*, which the syntax requires.)

Gloss of 5.4.2:

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *tasmin etasmin agnau* | loc. sg. ×3 | "in this same fire" |
| *devāḥ* | m. nom. pl. | "the gods" |
| *śraddhām* | f. acc. sg. | "faith / trust" — the oblation |
| *juhvati* | 3 pl. pres., *√hu* | "they pour as offering" |
| *tasyāḥ āhuteḥ* | f. abl. sg. | "from that oblation" |
| *somaḥ rājā* | m. nom. sg. + nom. sg. | "King Soma" |
| *sambhavati* | 3 sg. pres., *sam-√bhū* | "comes into being, arises" |

**OBSERVATION.** The verb is *sam-√bhū*, "come to be". Nothing is transported.
The word *candramas* does **not** occur in 5.4.2. It occurs in 5.4.1, where the
moon is the **embers** (*aṅgārāḥ*) of the cosmic fire — a term in a ritual
analogy, in the nominative, in apposition to *aṅgārāḥ*. In 5.4.1 the moon is
neither a place nor a destination but one of five members of a fire-diagram.

**OBSERVATION.** *śraddhā* is the oblation in CU. Some later restatements
substitute "the waters" (*āpaḥ*); Śaṅkara harmonises by glossing
*śraddhāśabdā āpaḥ*, "the waters denoted by the word *śraddhā*"
(bhāṣya ad 5.10.4). That gloss is Śaṅkara's, not the śruti's.

### 1.3 The corpse (CU 5.9.2) — the only thing explicitly carried

> स जातो यावदायुषं जीवति तं प्रेतं दिष्टमितोऽग्नय एव हरन्ति यत एवेतो यतः सम्भूतो
> भवति ॥ २ ॥

*sa jāto yāvad āyuṣaṃ jīvati; taṃ pretaṃ diṣṭam ito 'gnaya eva haranti,
yata eveto yataḥ sambhūto bhavati ||2||*

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *tam pretam* | m. acc. sg. + m. acc. sg. (pp. *pra-√i*) | "him, the departed one / the corpse" |
| *diṣṭam* | m. acc. sg. (pp. *√diś*) | "whose allotted [span] is spent" |
| *itaḥ* | adv. | "from here" |
| *agnaye* | m. dat. sg. | "to the fire" |
| *eva* | particle | "just, precisely" |
| *haranti* | 3 pl. pres. act., *√hṛ* | "they carry / bear away" |

**OBSERVATION — load-bearing.** This is the one place in the whole
Pañcāgni-vidyā where a plain transitive verb of physical carrying (*haranti*)
occurs. Its object is the *preta*, the corpse; its goal is the cremation
*agni*, in the dative; and its agents are human undertakers on the ground.
Every step *after* this in CU 5.10 uses a different verb. Whatever CU 5.10
describes, the śruti has already disposed of the body one section earlier, by
handing it to the fire.

### 1.4 The devayāna (CU 5.10.1–2)

> तद्य इत्थं विदुः । ये चेमेऽरण्ये श्रद्धा तप इत्युपासते तेऽर्चिषमभिसम्भवन्त्यर्चिषोऽहरह्न
> आपूर्यमाणपक्षमापूर्यमाणपक्षाद्यान्षडुदङ्ङेति मासाँस्तान् ॥ १ ॥
> मासेभ्यः संवत्सरँ संवत्सरादादित्यमादित्याच्चन्द्रमसं चन्द्रमसो विद्युतं तत्पुरुषोऽमानवः स
> एनान्ब्रह्म गमयत्येष देवयानः पन्था इति ॥ २ ॥

*tad ya itthaṃ viduḥ, ye ceme 'raṇye śraddhā tapa ity upāsate,
te 'rciṣam abhisambhavanti; arciṣo 'har; ahna āpūryamāṇapakṣam;
āpūryamāṇapakṣād yān ṣaḍ udaṅṅ eti māsāṃs tān ||1||
māsebhyaḥ saṃvatsaram; saṃvatsarād ādityam; ādityāc candramasam;
candramaso vidyutam; tat puruṣo 'mānavaḥ; sa enān brahma gamayati;
eṣa devayānaḥ panthā iti ||2||*

Gloss of the governing clause *te 'rciṣam abhisambhavanti*:

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *te* | m. nom. pl. | "they" — antecedent *ye … viduḥ / ye … upāsate*, i.e. persons defined by what they know and venerate |
| *arciṣam* | n. acc. sg. of *arcis* | "flame, ray" |
| *abhisambhavanti* | 3 pl. pres. act., *abhi-sam-√bhū* | "they come to be at / merge into / attain by becoming" |

**OBSERVATION — the central verb.** The verb governing the entire devayāna is
*abhi-sam-√bhū*. Its root is *√bhū*, "to be, to become". It is **not** *√gam*,
*√i*, *√yā*, *√car*, *√sṛp* or any other primary verb of locomotion. Monier-
Williams gives for *abhisambhū*: "to become, be changed into; to arrive at,
attain". The "arrive" sense is real and attested, but it is a secondary
extension of a copular-inchoative root, and the root sense of becoming is
never absent. Rendering it "they pass into the flame" is defensible;
rendering it "they go to the flame" or "they travel to" foregrounds one pole
of a two-pole verb and suppresses the other.

**OBSERVATION — the chain has no verb at all.** After *abhisambhavanti* the
text is a bare cascade of ablative → accusative pairs: *arciṣo 'har*, *ahna
āpūryamāṇapakṣam*, *māsebhyaḥ saṃvatsaram*, *saṃvatsarād ādityam*, *ādityāc
candramasam*, *candramaso vidyutam*. **There is no finite verb in any of these
clauses.** Every English translation supplies one — "they go from", "they pass
from", "they proceed to". Which verb is supplied is a translator's decision.
Grammatically the only verb licensed by carry-over is *abhisambhavanti*, i.e.
the becoming-verb. The itinerary reading is produced by the supplied verb, not
by the Sanskrit.

**OBSERVATION — the accusative of goal is not evidence of bodily travel here,
because the same construction in the same sentence takes an abstract goal.**
The one finite verb of going in 5.10.2 is *gamayati*, causative of *√gam*, in
*sa enān brahma gamayati* — double accusative, *enān* ("them") and *brahma*
("brahman", n. acc. sg.). The accusative-of-goal in this very passage governs
*brahman*. *Brahman* is not a location. Therefore the presence of an accusative
after a verb of going in this passage does not by itself establish spatial
destination.

**OBSERVATION — the finite verb of going in 5.10.1 belongs to the sun, not to
the dead.** In *yān ṣaḍ udaṅṅ eti māsāṃs tān*, the verb *eti* (3 sg., *√i*) is
singular; its subject is the implied sun. The clause means "those six months
during which [the sun] goes northward". BU 6.2.15 makes the subject explicit:
*yān ṣaṇmāsān udaṅ ādityaḥ eti*. The dead are plural throughout. The one thing
unambiguously *going* in the devayāna sentence is the sun on its annual course.

**OBSERVATION — the stations are not one ontological kind.** Ranked by what
each word denotes:

| station | form | category |
|---|---|---|
| *arcis* | n., "flame/ray" | luminous phenomenon |
| *ahar* | n., "day" | unit of time |
| *āpūryamāṇapakṣa* | m., "the fortnight that is being filled" (pres. mid. ppl. of *ā-√pṝ* + *pakṣa*) | unit of time named by a process |
| *ṣaṭ māsāḥ* | m. pl., "six months" | unit of time |
| *saṃvatsara* | m., "year" | unit of time |
| *āditya* | m., sun / the deity | luminary or deity |
| *candramas* | m., moon / the deity | luminary or deity |
| *vidyut* | f., "lightning" | luminous phenomenon |
| *brahman* | n. | not a substance, place, or luminary |

Five of the nine stations are **units of time**. One cannot bodily travel to a
fortnight. Any reading that takes the whole sequence as a geographic itinerary
must silently reclassify half of it.

**OBSERVATION.** *tat puruṣo 'mānavaḥ* — "there/then [is] a person, non-human".
*amānava* = *a-* + *mānava* ("descended from Manu, human"). The escort is
defined negatively, by what he is not. The Kāṇva BU reads instead
*puruṣo mānasaḥ*, "a person made of mind" (BU 6.2.15). The two recensions
differ on whether the conductor is *non-human* or *mind-made*.

**Translations side by side, CU 5.10.1–2**

| | rendering of *te 'rciṣam abhisambhavanti* and the chain |
|---|---|
| literal (mine) | "they come to be in the flame; from the flame, the day; from the day, the fortnight that fills; …; from the sun, the moon; from the moon, the lightning. There, a non-human person; he causes them to reach brahman." |
| Olivelle (*Early Upaniṣads*, 1998) | "they pass into the flame, from the flame into the day, … from the sun into the moon, from the moon into the lightning. Then a person who is not human — he leads them to brahman." |
| Hume (*Thirteen Principal Upanishads*, 1921) | "they pass into the flame; from the flame, into the day; … from the moon, into the lightning. There there is a Person who is non-human. He leads them on to Brahma." |
| Radhakrishnan (*Principal Upaniṣads*, 1953) | "they go to light, from light to day, … from the moon to lightning. There is a person, non-human. He leads them to Brahmā." |
| Śaṅkara (bhāṣya ad loc.) | *te sarve 'rciṣam arcir-abhimāninīṃ devatām abhisambhavanti pratipadyante* — "they all attain *arcis*, i.e. **the deity that identifies itself with the flame**." |

**WHERE THE TRANSLATION HARDENS.** Radhakrishnan's "they go to light" converts
a *bhū*-verb into a *gam*-verb outright. Olivelle's and Hume's "pass into" is
the closest of the three to the Sanskrit, because "pass into" preserves the
becoming-sense; but "from … into …" repeated nine times, in English, reads as
an itinerary, and the ablative-accusative pairs in Sanskrit carry no such
force on their own. None of the three flags that no verb is present in the
chain.

### 1.5 The pitṛyāna (CU 5.10.3–4)

> अथ य इमे ग्राम इष्टापूर्ते दत्तमित्युपासते ते धूममभिसम्भवन्ति धूमाद्रात्रिँ रात्रेरपरपक्षमपर-
> पक्षाद्यान्षड्दक्षिणैति मासाँस्तान्नैते संवत्सरमभिप्राप्नुवन्ति ॥ ३ ॥
> मासेभ्यः पितृलोकं पितृलोकादाकाशमाकाशाच्चन्द्रमसमेष सोमो राजा तद्देवानामन्नं तं देवा
> भक्षयन्ति ॥ ४ ॥

*atha ya ime grāma iṣṭāpūrte dattam ity upāsate, te dhūmam abhisambhavanti;
dhūmād rātrim; rātrer aparapakṣam; aparapakṣād yān ṣaḍ dakṣiṇaiti māsāṃs tān;
naite saṃvatsaram abhiprāpnuvanti ||3||
māsebhyaḥ pitṛlokam; pitṛlokād ākāśam; ākāśāc candramasam; eṣa somo rājā;
tad devānām annam; taṃ devā bhakṣayanti ||4||*

Gloss of the load-bearing clauses:

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *na … abhiprāpnuvanti* | 3 pl. pres., *abhi-pra-√āp* + neg. | "they do not reach / attain" |
| *saṃvatsaram* | m. acc. sg. | "the year" |
| *ākāśāt* | m. abl. sg. | "from open space" (*ā-√kāś*, "shine forth, become visible") |
| *candramasam* | m. acc. sg. | "the moon" |
| *eṣaḥ* | m. nom. sg. | "this [one]" |
| *somaḥ rājā* | m. nom. sg. | "King Soma" |
| *tat* | n. nom. sg. | "that" |
| *devānām* | m. gen. pl. | "of the gods" |
| *annam* | n. nom. sg. | "food" |
| *tam* | m. acc. sg. | "him / it" (= *somam / candramasam*) |
| *devāḥ bhakṣayanti* | 3 pl. pres. caus., *√bhakṣ* | "the gods eat / consume" |

**OBSERVATION — load-bearing.** *naite saṃvatsaram abhiprāpnuvanti*, "they do
not reach the year." The verb *abhi-pra-√āp*, "reach, attain", the most
ordinary Sanskrit verb of arriving, here takes **a unit of time** as its
accusative object. This is decisive for the grammatical question posed: in this
very text the accusative of goal after a verb of reaching does **not** entail a
spatial destination. Whatever *candramasam* is in 5.10.4, the construction it
stands in is the same construction that in 5.10.3 governs "the year".

**OBSERVATION — who is said to arrive.** *te*, masculine nominative plural,
antecedent *ye ime grāma iṣṭāpūrte dattam ity upāsate*, "those here in the
village who venerate sacrifice, works of public benefit, and almsgiving."
The subject is **persons designated by their practice**. Across the whole of
CU 5.10 the words *ātman*, *śarīra*, *jīva*, and *prāṇa* do **not occur even
once** (verified by exhaustive search of the section). No body is ever the
subject of any verb in CU 5.10. No soul is ever named as the traveller.

**OBSERVATION — what is called food.** *tad devānām annam; taṃ devā
bhakṣayanti.* The neuter *tad* and the masculine *tam* both resume the
immediately preceding *candramasam / somo rājā*. Grammatically it is **the
moon-Soma** that is the food of the gods and that the gods eat. The proposition
"the dead become the food of the gods" is **not stated in the Chāndogya**. It
is an inference, licensed by the sequence but not written. Śaṅkara draws it
explicitly and knows he is drawing it (see 3.1 below); the Bṛhadāraṇyaka states
it outright (2.2 below). Translations that render CU 5.10.4 "they become the
food of the gods" have supplied the inference into the text.

**OBSERVATION — eat, not drink.** *bhakṣayanti* is from *√bhakṣ*, "to eat,
devour, consume". The noun is *anna*, "food". There is no verb of drinking
(*√pā*) and no noun of drink (*pāna*, *soma-pāna*) anywhere in CU 5.10 or
BU 6.2. Any statement that the dead are *drunk* by the gods has imported the
Ṛgvedic soma-pressing frame into an Upaniṣadic passage that uses eating
vocabulary throughout.

**OBSERVATION — the moon is on both paths in CU.** In CU the devayāna runs
*… āditya → candramas → vidyut → brahman*, and the pitṛyāna runs
*… pitṛloka → ākāśa → candramas*. The moon is a station on **both** routes,
passed straight through on the non-returning one. It is therefore not, in the
Chāndogya, the sorting-gate that keeps some and returns others.

### 1.6 The return (CU 5.10.5–6)

> तस्मिन्यावत्सम्पातमुषित्वाथैतमेवाध्वानं पुनर्निवर्तन्ते यथेतमाकाशमाकाशाद्वायुं वायुर्भूत्वा
> धूमो भवति धूमो भूत्वाभ्रं भवति ॥ ५ ॥
> अभ्रं भूत्वा मेघो भवति मेघो भूत्वा प्रवर्षति त इह व्रीहियवा ओषधिवनस्पतयस्तिलमाषा इति
> जायन्तेऽतो वै खलु दुर्निष्प्रपतरं यो यो ह्यन्नमत्ति यो रेतः सिञ्चति तद्भूय एव भवति ॥ ६ ॥

*tasmin yāvat sampātam uṣitvāthaitam evādhvānaṃ punar nivartante yathetam;
ākāśam; ākāśād vāyum; vāyur bhūtvā dhūmo bhavati; dhūmo bhūtvābhraṃ bhavati
||5||
abhraṃ bhūtvā megho bhavati; megho bhūtvā pravarṣati; ta iha vrīhiyavā
oṣadhivanaspatayas tilamāṣā iti jāyante; 'to vai khalu durniṣprapataram;
yo yo hy annam atti yo retaḥ siñcati tad bhūya eva bhavati ||6||*

(Wikisource prints *तस्मिन्यवात्संपातम्*; the bhāṣya lemma and printed editions
read *तस्मिन् यावत्सम्पातम्*, which Śaṅkara glosses *yāvat karmaṇaḥ kṣayaḥ*.)

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *tasmin* | m./n. loc. sg. | "there / in that" |
| *yāvat sampātam* | adverbial acc. | "for as long as the residue [lasts]" |
| *uṣitvā* | absolutive, *√vas* | "having dwelt / stayed" |
| *etam adhvānam* | m. acc. sg. | "by this road" (acc. of route) |
| *punar nivartante* | 3 pl. pres. mid., *ni-√vṛt* | "they turn back again" |
| *yathetam* | adv. (*yathā* + *ita*, pp. *√i*) | "as they came" |
| *vāyur bhūtvā dhūmo bhavati* | absolutive + 3 **sg.** pres. | "having become wind, [it] becomes smoke" |

**OBSERVATION — the descent is stated as a chain of becomings, in the
singular.** *vāyur bhūtvā dhūmo bhavati; dhūmo bhūtvābhraṃ bhavati; abhraṃ
bhūtvā megho bhavati.* Every verb is *√bhū*. The subject has silently gone
**singular** (*bhavati*), although the sentence began plural (*nivartante*),
and only re-pluralises when they are born as plants (*ta iha … jāyante*, 3 pl.).
Śaṅkara notices the number-shift and explains it: *meghādiṣu pūrveṣv
ekarūpatvād ekavacana-nirdeśaḥ* — "the singular is used because of the
uniformity of form in the earlier stages, cloud and so on" — and the plural
returns *kṣīṇakarmaṇām anekatvāt*, "because those whose karma is spent are
many". The grammatical person of the returning agent dissolves during transit
and is reconstituted at rebirth. This is a fact about the Sanskrit, not a
doctrine imposed on it.

**OBSERVATION — the "road" does not reverse.** *yathetam*, "as they came", is
contradicted by the text itself: the ascent ran months → *pitṛloka* → *ākāśa*
→ moon, and the descent runs *ākāśa* → wind → smoke → mist → cloud → rain →
plants. Śaṅkara raises exactly this objection against himself —
*nanu māsebhyaḥ pitṛlokaṃ … iti gamanakrama uktaḥ, na tathā nivṛttiḥ …
kathaṃ yathetam ity ucyate?* — and answers that *yathetam* is
*upalakṣaṇārtham*, "merely indicative", not a rule: *na cātra yathetam eveti
niyamaḥ*. **The commentator who most insists the ascent is real also concedes
the route is not a route.**

**OBSERVATION.** *durniṣprapataram*, comparative of *dus-* + *niṣprapata*,
"very hard to get out of / hard to fall clear of". Śaṅkara supplies a missing
*t* (*durniṣprapatataram*) and reads it as "harder to emerge from". Olivelle
renders "it is far more difficult to get out of that". No spatial claim is at
stake here beyond the metaphor of egress.

### 1.7 The third state (CU 5.10.8)

> अथैतयोः पथोर्न कतरेण च न तानीमानि क्षुद्राण्यसकृदावर्तीनि भूतानि भवन्ति जायस्व
> म्रियस्वेत्येतत्तृतीयँ स्थानं तेनासौ लोको न सम्पूर्यते ।

*athaitayoḥ pathor na katareṇa cana tānīmāni kṣudrāṇy asakṛd āvartīni bhūtāni
bhavanti — "jāyasva mriyasva" ity — etat tṛtīyaṃ sthānam; tenāsau loko na
sampūryate.*

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *etayoḥ pathoḥ* | m. gen./loc. dual of *pathin* | "of these two paths" |
| *na katareṇa cana* | instr. sg. + neg. | "by neither of the two" |
| *asakṛd-āvartīni* | n. nom. pl. adj. | "returning again and again" |
| *bhūtāni bhavanti* | n. nom. pl. + 3 pl. *√bhū* | "become creatures" |
| *jāyasva mriyasva* | 2 sg. impv. ×2 | "be born! die!" |
| *tṛtīyaṃ sthānam* | n. nom. sg. | "the third *sthāna*" |
| *asau lokaḥ* | m. nom. sg. | "yonder world" |
| *na sampūryate* | 3 sg. pres. pass., *sam-√pṝ* | "does not become full" |

**OBSERVATION.** *sthāna* is from *√sthā*, "to stand": a standing-place, and
by ordinary extension a condition, station, or status. It is genuinely
ambiguous between "place" and "state", and the Upaniṣad supplies no
disambiguator except the appositional gloss *jāyasva mriyasva*, "be born, die"
— two imperatives, that is, **a mode of existence**, not a locale. Translations
that render *tṛtīyaṃ sthānam* "the third place" pick one horn; "the third
state" picks the other. **UNDETERMINED.**

**OBSERVATION.** *loka* in Vedic prose retains its old sense of "free space,
room" (cf. *uru loka*, "wide room"). *asau loko na sampūryate*, "yonder world
does not get full", trades on the container sense. It is a real container
image; it is also the answer to a question posed in those terms in 5.3.3.

## 2. Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 6.2.9–16 (Kāṇva)

### 2.1 The two paths (BU 6.2.15)

> ते य एवमेतद्विदुर्ये चामी अरण्ये श्रद्धां सत्यमुपासते तेऽर्चिरभिसंभवन्ति । अर्चिषोऽहः ।
> अह्न आपूर्यमाणपक्षम् । आपूर्यमाणपक्षाद्यान् षण्मासानुदङ्ङादित्य एति । मासेभ्यो देवलोकम् ।
> देवलोकादादित्यम् । आदित्याद्वैद्युतम् । तान् वैद्युतान् पुरुषो मानस एत्य ब्रह्मलोकान् गमयति ।
> ते तेषु ब्रह्मलोकेषु पराः परावतो वसन्ति । तेषां न पुनरावृत्तिः ॥

*te ya evam etad vidur ye cāmī araṇye śraddhāṃ satyam upāsate,
te 'rcir abhisaṃbhavanti; arciṣo 'haḥ; ahna āpūryamāṇapakṣam;
āpūryamāṇapakṣād yān ṣaṇmāsān udaṅ ādityaḥ eti; māsebhyo devalokam;
devalokād ādityam; ādityād vaidyutam. tān vaidyutān puruṣo mānasa etya
brahmalokān gamayati. te teṣu brahmalokeṣu parāḥ parāvato vasanti.
teṣāṃ na punarāvṛttiḥ.*

**OBSERVATION.** BU's devayāna contains **no moon**. The sequence is
flame → day → waxing fortnight → six northern months → world of the gods →
sun → the lightning-realm → brahma-worlds. Chāndogya's devayāna does include
the moon. The two recensions of the "same" doctrine differ on whether the moon
is on the upward path at all.

**OBSERVATION.** *etya* is the absolutive of *ā-√i*, "having come"; *gamayati*
is the causative of *√gam*. So BU **does** use motion verbs at the top of the
devayāna, of the escorting *puruṣa*, and takes *brahmalokān* (acc. pl.) as
goal. The plural *brahmalokān / brahmalokeṣu* is itself the pivot of a
classical dispute (see 3.2).

**UNKNOWN.** *parāḥ parāvataḥ vasanti* — *parāvat* f. "distance, far region";
*parāḥ* "further, supreme". The phrase is idiomatically obscure. Olivelle:
"they live there for the longest time"; Hume: "they live for very long
periods"; Radhakrishnan: "they dwell for the longest periods". Some read
*parāvataḥ* as an accusative-of-extent of **space** ("far distances"), some as
extent of **time** ("remote ages"). The published translations silently pick
time. The Sanskrit is undetermined between spatial remoteness and temporal
duration.

### 2.2 The moon in BU 6.2.16

> अथ ये यज्ञेन दानेन तपसा लोकाञ्जयन्ति ते धूममभिसंभवन्ति । धूमाद्रात्रिम् ।
> रात्रेरपक्षीयमाणपक्षम् । अपक्षीयमाणपक्षाद्यान् षण्मासान् दक्षिणादित्य एति ।
> मासेभ्यः पितृलोकम् । पितृलोकाच्चन्द्रम् । ते चन्द्रं प्राप्यान्नं भवन्ति । तांस्तत्र देवा यथा
> सोमं राजानमाप्यायस्वापक्षीयस्वेत्येवमेनांस्तत्र भक्षयन्ति । तेषां यदा तत्पर्यवैत्यथेममेवाकाश-
> मभिनिष्पद्यन्ते । आकाशाद्वायुम् । वायोर्वृष्टिम् । वृष्टेः पृथिवीम् । ते पृथिवीं प्राप्यान्नं भवन्ति ।
> ते पुनः पुरुषाग्नौ हूयन्ते ततो योषाग्नौ जायन्ते ।

*atha ye yajñena dānena tapasā lokāñ jayanti te dhūmam abhisaṃbhavanti;
dhūmād rātrim; rātrer apakṣīyamāṇapakṣam; apakṣīyamāṇapakṣād yān ṣaṇmāsān
dakṣiṇādityaḥ eti; māsebhyaḥ pitṛlokam; pitṛlokāc candram.
te candraṃ prāpyānnaṃ bhavanti. tāṃs tatra devā yathā somaṃ rājānam
"āpyāyasvāpakṣīyasva" ity evam enāṃs tatra bhakṣayanti.
teṣāṃ yadā tat paryavaity athemam evākāśam abhiniṣpadyante; ākāśād vāyum;
vāyor vṛṣṭim; vṛṣṭeḥ pṛthivīm. te pṛthivīṃ prāpyānnaṃ bhavanti.
te punaḥ puruṣāgnau hūyante; tato yoṣāgnau jāyante.*

Gloss of the load-bearing clause *te candraṃ prāpyānnaṃ bhavanti*:

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *te* | m. nom. pl. | "they" — antecedent *ye … lokāñ jayanti*, "those who conquer worlds by sacrifice, gift, and austerity" |
| *candram* | m. acc. sg. (*candra*, short form of *candramas*) | "the moon" |
| *prāpya* | absolutive, *pra-√āp* | "having reached / obtained" |
| *annam* | n. acc./nom. sg. | "food" |
| *bhavanti* | 3 pl. pres., *√bhū* | "they become" |

**OBSERVATION — load-bearing.** Here, unlike CU, the arrival IS predicated of
the dead in a verb of reaching (*prāpya*), and the becoming-food IS predicated
of them (*annaṃ bhavanti*), and the eating IS predicated of them (*enāṃs …
bhakṣayanti*, "they eat **them**"). BU is materially stronger than CU on all
three points. If the book wants the proposition "the dead become the food of
the gods", **BU 6.2.16 is the passage that says it; CU 5.10.4 is not.**

**OBSERVATION — the decisive symmetry.** The identical construction
*X prāpya annaṃ bhavanti* occurs **twice** in this one section: once of the
moon (*te candraṃ prāpyānnaṃ bhavanti*) and once of the earth (*te pṛthivīṃ
prāpyānnaṃ bhavanti*). Whatever kind of "reaching" and "becoming food" is
meant of the moon is grammatically the same kind that is meant of the earth,
where it plainly means becoming rice and barley that someone eats. The
construction therefore does not, on its own, mark the moon-arrival as a
different order of event from the earth-arrival.

**OBSERVATION.** *yathā somaṃ rājānam "āpyāyasvāpakṣīyasva" iti* — "as [they
consume] King Soma [saying] 'wax! wane!'". *āpyāyasva* and *apakṣīyasva* are
2 sg. middle imperatives of *ā-√pyai* ("swell, be filled") and *apa-√kṣi*
("waste away"). This is an explicit ritual quotation, i.e. the eating of the
dead is stated **as a simile drawn from the sacrificial handling of soma**
(*yathā …*). The simile marker *yathā* is in the text. Translations that drop
or weaken *yathā* convert a comparison into an identity.

**OBSERVATION.** *abhiniṣpadyante* (*abhi-nis-√pad*), "they issue forth into,
are produced into" — again an inchoative/productive verb, not a travel verb,
for the passage into *ākāśa*.

**Translations side by side, BU 6.2.16**

| | *pitṛlokāc candram / te candraṃ prāpyānnaṃ bhavanti* |
|---|---|
| literal (mine) | "from the world of the fathers, the moon. Having reached the moon they become food." |
| Olivelle | "from the months to the world of the fathers; and from the world of the fathers to the moon. Reaching the moon they become food." |
| Hume | "from the months to the world of the fathers, from the world of the fathers to the moon. Reaching the moon, they become food." |
| Radhakrishnan | "from the months to the world of the fathers, from the world of the fathers to the moon. Reaching the moon they become food." |
| Śaṅkara (bhāṣya ad loc.) | *evam enāṃś candraṃ prāptān karmiṇo bhṛtyān iva svāmino bhakṣayanti **upabhuñjate** devāḥ* — "thus the gods 'eat', i.e. **make use of**, those ritualists who have reached the moon, as masters make use of servants." |

**WHERE THE TRANSLATION HARDENS.** On this verse it largely does not. All three
standard translations are close. The hardening in the English tradition around
this passage happens one level up, in the *summaries and notes*, where "reaching
the moon" becomes "travelling to the moon" and "become food" becomes "are eaten
by the gods on the moon".

## 3. The commentators

### 3.1 Śaṅkara on Chāndogya 5.10 — three separate moves

**(a) The stations are deities, not places.** On 5.10.1–2:
*te sarve 'rciṣam **arcir-abhimāninīṃ devatām** abhisambhavanti pratipadyante*
— "they all attain *arcis*, that is, **the deity that identifies itself with
the flame**." On 5.10.3: *te … dhūmaṃ **dhūmābhimāninīṃ devatām**
abhisambhavanti … **tayātivāhitāḥ*** — "they attain smoke, i.e. the deity
identifying itself with smoke; **conducted onward by her** …" and the
six months are *dakṣiṇāyana-ṣaṇmāsābhimāninīr devatāḥ*, "the deities
identifying themselves with the six months of the southern course".

**(b) But the moon reached is the visible moon.** On 5.10.4:

> कोऽसौ यस्तैः प्राप्यते चन्द्रमा य एष **दृश्यतेऽन्तरिक्षे** सोमो राजा ब्राह्मणानाम्

*ko 'sau yas taiḥ prāpyate candramā? ya eṣa **dṛśyate 'ntarikṣe**, somo rājā
brāhmaṇānām* — "Which moon is it that is reached by them? **The one that is
seen in the sky**, King Soma of the brahmins."

**OBSERVATION.** This is unambiguous, and it is Śaṅkara's, not the śruti's.
Śaṅkara identifies the *candramas* of CU 5.10.4 with the visible luminary. The
Upaniṣad itself supplies no such identification and no such qualifier.

**(c) But the eating is not eating, and the body is a new one.**

> न हि ते **कवलोत्क्षेपेण** देवैर्भक्ष्यन्ते । किं तर्हि **उपकरणमात्रं** देवानां भवन्ति ते
> स्त्रीपशुभृत्यादिवत् ।

*na hi te **kavalotkṣepeṇa** devair bhakṣyante; kiṃ tarhi **upakaraṇa-mātraṃ**
devānāṃ bhavanti te strī-paśu-bhṛtyādivat* — "for they are not eaten by the
gods **by the tossing up of morsels**; rather, they become **merely a means**
for the gods, like wives, cattle, servants and the like." He cites the
attested idiom *striyo 'nnaṃ paśavo 'nnam*, "women are food, cattle are food".

> शरीरं च तेषां सुखोपभोगयोग्यं **चन्द्रमण्डल आप्यमारभ्यते** ।

*śarīraṃ ca teṣāṃ sukhopabhoga-yogyaṃ **candramaṇḍala āpyam ārabhyate*** —
"and a body for them, fit for the enjoyment of pleasure, **is begun in the
lunar orb, made of water**."

> अन्त्यायां च शरीराहुतावग्नौ हुतायामग्निना दह्यमाने शरीरे तदुत्था आपो धूमेन सहोर्ध्वं
> यजमानमावेष्ट्य **चन्द्रमण्डलं प्राप्य** कुशमृत्तिकास्थानीया **बाह्यशरीरारम्भिका** भवन्ति ।

*antyāyāṃ ca śarīrāhutāv agnau hutāyām agninā dahyamāne śarīre tadutthā āpo
dhūmena sahordhvaṃ yajamānam āveṣṭya **candramaṇḍalaṃ prāpya**
kuśa-mṛttikā-sthānīyā **bāhya-śarīrārambhikā** bhavanti* — "and when the body
is offered in the final oblation and burnt by fire, the waters that rise from
it, going up with the smoke and enveloping the sacrificer, **reach the lunar
orb** and, standing in the place of the *kuśa* grass and clay [of a ritual
construction], become **the material from which an outer body is begun**."

**OBSERVATION.** Śaṅkara's own account is therefore: the earthly *śarīra* is
destroyed on the pyre; the water-element released from it ascends; at the lunar
orb a *new, watery, pleasure-capable body* is constituted. **On Śaṅkara's own
reading, no body travels to the moon.** A body is *made* there. He also uses
frank ascent vocabulary of the arriver — *candramaṇḍalam ārūḍhaḥ*, "having
climbed to the lunar orb" (ad 5.10.5) — and *uṣitvā* he glosses as dwelling
there *yāvat karmaṇaḥ kṣayaḥ*, "as long as the karma is not spent"; and
*kṣaṇamātram api tatra sthātuṃ na labhyate*, "one cannot remain there even a
moment [longer], the karma that caused the stay being spent" — the duration is
measured by karma, not by distance or by any clock.

### 3.2 Brahmasūtra 4.3.4 with Śaṅkara — the dispute is exactly the one at issue

BS 4.3.4: **आतिवाहिकास् तल्लिङ्गात्** — *ātivāhikās tal-liṅgāt*, "[they are]
conductors, because of the indication of that."

Śaṅkara opens by naming the three options in so many words:

> तेष्वेव अर्चिरादिषु संशयः — किमेतानि **मार्गचिह्नानि**, उत **भोगभूमयः**, अथवा
> **नेतारो गन्तॄणाम्** इति ।

*teṣv eva arcirādiṣu saṃśayaḥ — kim etāni **mārga-cihnāni**, uta
**bhoga-bhūmayaḥ**, athavā **netāro gantṝṇām** iti* — "Regarding these
*arcis* and the rest there is a doubt: are they **landmarks of a road**, or
**realms of enjoyment**, or **conductors of the travellers**?"

He then states the landmark option in its strongest, most geographical form,
as the *pūrvapakṣa*:

> यथा हि लोके कश्चिद्ग्रामं नगरं वा प्रतिष्ठासमानोऽनुशिष्यते — "गच्छ इतस्त्वममुं गिरिं,
> ततो न्यग्रोधं, ततो नदीं, ततो ग्रामं, ततो नगरं वा प्राप्स्यसि" इति — एवमिहापि …

*yathā hi loke kaścid grāmaṃ nagaraṃ vā pratiṣṭhāsamāno 'nuśiṣyate — "gaccha
itas tvam amuṃ girim, tato nyagrodhaṃ, tato nadīṃ, tato grāmaṃ, tato nagaraṃ
vā prāpsyasi" iti — evam ihāpi …* — "just as in the world someone setting out
for a village or a city is instructed, 'go from here to that mountain, then the
banyan, then the river, then you will reach the village or the city' — so here
too …"

And he **rejects** it, concluding:

> तस्मात्साधूक्तम् — **आतिवाहिका देवतात्मानोऽर्चिरादय** इति ।

*tasmāt sādhūktam — **ātivāhikā devatātmāno 'rcirādaya** iti* — "therefore it
is well said: *arcis* and the rest are **conducting deity-selves**."

**OBSERVATION — load-bearing.** The reading of the *arcirādi* sequence as a
physical route with landmarks is not a modern misreading invented by English
translators. It is a position that the classical tradition formulated
explicitly, in the vocabulary of roads and villages, **and then rejected** in
the mainstream Advaita commentary. The tradition's own settled answer is that
the stations are neither places nor waypoints but conducting intelligences.

**UNKNOWN / commentators diverge.** BS 4.3.6, *kāryaṃ bādarir asya
gaty-upapatteḥ*: Bādari holds that what is reached is the *kārya* (effected,
lower, qualified) brahman, **because going to it is intelligible** —
*asya hi kārya-brahmaṇo gantavyatvam upapadyate **pradeśavattvāt***, "for
being a destination is intelligible of the effected brahman, **because it has
spatial extension**"; whereas of the supreme brahman *na … gantṛtvaṃ
gantavyatvaṃ gatir vā avakalpate sarvagatatvāt*, "no going, goer, or
destination can obtain, because it is all-pervading." Śaṅkara adopts Bādari
(4.3.7, appealing to the **plural** *brahmalokān* in BU 6.2.15). Jaimini's
opposing view (BS 4.3.12) takes the goal to be the supreme brahman. Vaiṣṇava
commentators follow the Jaimini side. So the classical tradition is itself
split on whether the terminus of the devayāna is a located thing.

**OBSERVATION.** Śaṅkara's principle here is worth extracting because it is a
general statement about the semantics of going: *gati* presupposes a
*pradeśavat* (spatially extended) goal, and therefore where the goal is not
spatially extended, the going-language cannot be literal. This is the
tradition's own criterion, and it cuts both ways: it licenses a located
lower-brahman world **and** it de-literalises going-language wherever the goal
is not *pradeśavat*.

## 4. Kauṣītaki Upaniṣad 1.2 — the one passage that plainly says "go"

> स होवाच — ये वै के चास्माल्लोकात्प्रयन्ति **चन्द्रमसमेव ते सर्वे गच्छन्ति** । तेषां
> **प्राणैः** पूर्वपक्ष **आप्यायते** । … एतद्वै स्वर्गस्य लोकस्य **द्वारं** यश्चन्द्रमाः । तं यत्
> प्रत्याह तम् **अतिसृजते** । अथ य एनं [न] प्रत्याह तम् इह **वृष्टिर्भूत्वा वर्षति** । …
> तमागतं पृच्छति **कोऽसि** इति ।

*sa hovāca — ye vai ke cāsmāl lokāt prayanti **candramasam eva te sarve
gacchanti**. teṣāṃ **prāṇaiḥ** pūrvapakṣa **āpyāyate** … etad vai svargasya
lokasya **dvāraṃ** yaś candramāḥ. taṃ yat pratyāha tam **atisṛjate**. atha ya
enaṃ [na] pratyāha tam iha **vṛṣṭir bhūtvā varṣati** … tam āgataṃ pṛcchati
**ko 'si** iti.*

**Textual note.** The Wikisource text reads *athāparapakṣe **na** prajanayati*
and drops the *na* in *ya enaṃ na pratyāha*; the printed editions and the sense
require *tān aparapakṣeṇa prajanayati* and *ya enaṃ na pratyāha*. Bodewitz
(*Jaiminīya Brāhmaṇa I*, 1973, 54–61 — the discussion Olivelle cites) treats
these lines as textually and semantically difficult, and holds that in the
Jaiminīya Brāhmaṇa 1.18 parallel, which is probably the original context, the
verses are addressed to **the seasons**, not to the moon.

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *candramasam* | m. acc. sg. | "the moon" |
| *eva* | particle | "just, precisely" |
| *te sarve* | m. nom. pl. | "they all" |
| *gacchanti* | 3 pl. pres. act., *√gam* | "they go" |
| *teṣāṃ prāṇaiḥ* | gen. pl. + **instr. pl.** of *prāṇa* | "by their breaths / vital airs" |
| *āpyāyate* | 3 sg. pres. mid., *ā-√pyai* | "swells, is filled out" |
| *dvāram* | n. nom. sg. | "door, gate" |
| *atisṛjate* | 3 sg. pres. mid., *ati-√sṛj* | "releases past, lets pass on" |

**OBSERVATION — load-bearing.** This is the **only** one of the four passages
that uses the plain finite verb *gacchanti* with the moon in the accusative.
Here the ordinary construction of bodily motion toward a goal is present, in
its ordinary form. If any passage in the corpus supports a straightforwardly
locomotive reading, it is Kauṣītaki 1.2 — not Chāndogya 5.10 and not
Bṛhadāraṇyaka 6.2.

**OBSERVATION.** What arrives, and what the moon takes in, are specified
differently here: *teṣāṃ prāṇaiḥ pūrvapakṣa āpyāyate*, "the waxing fortnight
swells **by their prāṇas**", instrumental. The moon is fed by *prāṇa*.
*Prāṇa* is the only technical psychophysical term that any of these four
passages names as the thing transacted, and it appears in the **instrumental**
— as the means by which the fortnight fills, not as a subject that travels.

**OBSERVATION.** *ko 'si*, "who are you?" The accepted answer ends
*ṛtur asmy ārtavo 'smi … ko 'si? **tvam asmi** iti* — "I am the season, I am
born of the season … Who are you? **I am you**." The gate opens on an identity
claim, not on a credential of place. Whatever the *dvāra* is, what passes it is
determined by a statement about identity.

**UNDETERMINED.** *dvāra* is a plain noun for a physical door, and is also the
standard Sanskrit metaphor for any threshold or means of access (*sūryadvāra*,
Muṇḍaka 1.2.11, "the door of the sun"; *mokṣadvāra*). The passage supplies no
disambiguator.

## 5. Ṛgveda — soma, *candramas*, and what the moon is said to do

### 5.1 RV 10.85.1–5 (Śākala saṃhitā-pāṭha)

> सत्येनोत्तभिता भूमिः सूर्येणोत्तभिता द्यौः । ऋतेनादित्यास्तिष्ठन्ति **दिवि सोमो अधि
> श्रितः** ॥१॥
> सोमेनादित्या बलिनः सोमेन पृथिवी मही । अथो **नक्षत्राणामेषामुपस्थे सोम आहितः** ॥२॥
> सोमं मन्यते पपिवान्यत्सम्पिंषन्त्योषधिम् । **सोमं यं ब्रह्माणो विदुर्न तस्याश्नाति कश्चन** ॥३॥
> आच्छद्विधानैर्गुपितो बार्हतैः सोम रक्षितः । ग्राव्णामिच्छृण्वन्तिष्ठसि **न ते अश्नाति
> पार्थिवः** ॥४॥
> **यत्त्वा देव प्रपिबन्ति तत आ प्यायसे पुनः** । वायुः सोमस्य रक्षिता **समानां मास
> आकृतिः** ॥५॥

*satyenottabhitā bhūmiḥ sūryeṇottabhitā dyauḥ / ṛtenādityās tiṣṭhanti divi somo
adhi śritaḥ ||1||
somenādityā balinaḥ somena pṛthivī mahī / atho nakṣatrāṇām eṣām upasthe soma
āhitaḥ ||2||
somaṃ manyate papivān yat sampiṃṣanty oṣadhim / somaṃ yaṃ brāhmaṇo vidur na
tasyāśnāti kaścana ||3||
ācchadvidhānair gupito bārhataiḥ soma rakṣitaḥ / grāvṇām ic chṛṇvan tiṣṭhasi
na te aśnāti pārthivaḥ ||4||
yat tvā deva prapibanti tata ā pyāyase punaḥ / vāyuḥ somasya rakṣitā samānāṃ
māsa ākṛtiḥ ||5||*

Gloss of 10.85.5:

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *yat* | conj. | "when" |
| *tvā* | 2 sg. acc. | "you" |
| *deva* | m. voc. sg. | "O god" |
| *prapibanti* | 3 pl. pres., *pra-√pā* | "they drink up" |
| *tataḥ* | adv. | "thereupon" |
| *ā pyāyase* | 2 sg. pres. mid., *ā-√pyai* | "you swell / fill out" |
| *punaḥ* | adv. | "again" |
| *vāyuḥ somasya rakṣitā* | nom. + gen. + nom. agent noun | "Vāyu is the guardian of Soma" |
| *samānām* | **f. gen. pl.** of *samā*, "year" | "of the years" |
| *māsaḥ* | **m. nom. sg.** | "month" (from *mās*; the same stem also = "moon") |
| *ākṛtiḥ* | f. nom. sg., *ā-√kṛ* | "shape, form, figure, appearance" |

**WHERE THE TRANSLATION HARDENS — a clean case.** Griffith renders the fourth
pāda "**The Moon is that which shapes the years.**" The Sanskrit is *samānāṃ
māsa ākṛtiḥ*. Two separate hardenings are stacked in six English words:

1. **"The Moon" for *māsaḥ*.** The stem *mās-* does carry both "moon" and
   "month", and the derived *māsa-* is primarily and overwhelmingly "month".
   The unambiguous Ṛgvedic word for the visible moon is *candramas-*, and it is
   **not** used here — although it *is* used fourteen verses later in this same
   hymn (10.85.19, *pra candramās tirate dīrgham āyuḥ*). The poet had the word
   and did not use it in verse 5. "Month" is the plain reading; "moon" is a
   defensible but non-obvious lexical choice.
2. **"that which shapes" for *ākṛtiḥ*.** *Ākṛti* is a **result-noun**: shape,
   form, figure, appearance. It is not an agent noun. "That which shapes"
   converts a form into a former, a shape into a shaper — and thereby converts
   a static predication into an act of cosmic agency.

Literal: "**the month is the figure of the years**" — i.e. the month is the
form by which years are constituted. Griffith's line, which the book quotes
twice as a doctrinal statement about Luna, is a translation artefact at both
ends.

**OBSERVATION.** RV 10.85.1–5 does state, of Soma: that he is *divi … adhi
śritaḥ*, "set above in heaven" (loc.); that he is *nakṣatrāṇām … upasthe
āhitaḥ*, "placed in the lap of these constellations" (loc.); that when the gods
drink him he fills out again. These are the substantive Ṛgvedic grounds for the
soma–moon identification, and they are real. What the hymn nowhere does is
**say it**: there is no clause in RV 10.85 of the form "Soma is the moon".
The nominal equation *somo vai candramāḥ* is a Brāhmaṇa-and-later formula; in
the Upaniṣadic corpus it is stated outright at CU 5.10.4 (*ākāśāc candramasam;
eṣa somo rājā*) and presupposed at BU 6.2.16 and KsU 1.2. **Those Upaniṣadic
statements, not the Ṛgveda, are where the equation is on the page.**

**OBSERVATION.** 10.85.3–4: *somaṃ yaṃ brāhmaṇo vidur na tasyāśnāti kaścana*,
"the Soma that the brahmins know — of that nobody eats"; *na te aśnāti
pārthivaḥ*, "no earthly one eats of you." The verb is *√aś*, "eat/consume",
not *√pā*, "drink" — even here, where drinking (*prapibanti*, v. 5) is
available and used one verse later.

### 5.2 RV 10.90.13 (Puruṣa-sūkta)

> **चन्द्रमा मनसो जातश्चक्षोः सूर्यो अजायत** । मुखादिन्द्रश्चाग्निश्च प्राणाद्वायुरजायत ॥१३॥

*candramā manaso jātaś cakṣoḥ sūryo ajāyata / mukhād indraś cāgniś ca prāṇād
vāyur ajāyata ||13||*

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *candramāḥ* | m. nom. sg. | "the moon" |
| *manasaḥ* | n. **abl.** sg. | "from the mind" |
| *jātaḥ* | m. nom. sg., pp. *√jan* | "born" |
| *cakṣoḥ* | n. **abl.** sg. | "from the eye" |
| *sūryaḥ* | m. nom. sg. | "the sun" |
| *ajāyata* | 3 sg. impf. mid., *√jan* | "was born" |

**OBSERVATION.** Accurate as quoted in the book, and accurately translated by
Griffith. Ablative of source, past passive/middle of birth. No hardening.
Note only that the syntax is *X-ablative → Y-nominative born*: the mind is the
**source** of the moon, which is a genealogical claim about the cosmogonic
body, not a claim that the moon *is* mind. The book's Luna chapter heading
"She is mind" is a step beyond the verse; the verse says she is *born from*
mind.

### 5.3 RV 10.90.3–4

> एतावानस्य महिमातो ज्यायाँश्च पूरुषः । पादोऽस्य विश्वा भूतानि **त्रिपादस्यामृतं दिवि** ॥३॥
> **त्रिपादूर्ध्व उदैत्पुरुषः** पादोऽस्येहाभवत्पुनः । ततो विष्वङ्व्यक्रामत्साशनानशने अभि ॥४॥

*etāvān asya mahimāto jyāyāṃś ca pūruṣaḥ / pādo 'sya viśvā bhūtāni tripād
asyāmṛtaṃ divi ||3||
tripād ūrdhva ud ait puruṣaḥ pādo 'syehābhavat punaḥ / tato viṣvaṅ vy akrāmat
sāśanānaśane abhi ||4||*

**OBSERVATION.** *tripād asyāmṛtaṃ divi* — "three-quarters of him [is] the
immortal, in heaven" (*divi*, loc. sg. of *dyu/div*). *amṛtam* is a neuter
noun/adjective, "the deathless / immortality". Griffith's "three-fourths
eternal **life** in heaven" supplies "life". Minor, but it converts a
substantive-abstract into a biological predicate. **Hardening, small.**

**OBSERVATION.** *ud ait* (3 sg. impf., *ud-√i*) in v. 4, "went up", is a
genuine literal verb of ascent, of Puruṣa, in the Ṛgveda. Where the Vedic
poets want unambiguous ascent language, this is what it looks like. It is not
what CU 5.10.4 looks like.

### 5.4 RV 9.1.1, 9.1.6 and RV 1.91.1

> स्वादिष्ठया मदिष्ठया पवस्व सोम धारया । इन्द्राय पातवे सुतः ॥१॥
> **पुनाति ते परिस्रुतं सोमं सूर्यस्य दुहिता** । वारेण शश्वता तना ॥६॥

*svādiṣṭhayā madiṣṭhayā pavasva soma dhārayā / indrāya pātave sutaḥ ||1||
punāti te parisrutaṃ somaṃ sūryasya duhitā / vāreṇa śaśvatā tanā ||6||*

**OBSERVATION.** *punāti* is 3 sg. **present indicative** active, "she
purifies". Griffith renders "**may** Sūrya's Daughter purify" — an optative.
The Sanskrit states a fact of the rite; the English turns it into a wish.
Small, but it is a modality change.

> **त्वं सोम प्र चिकितो मनीषा त्वं रजिष्ठमनु नेषि पन्थाम्** । तव प्रणीती पितरो न इन्दो
> देवेषु रत्नमभजन्त धीराः ॥१॥

*tvaṃ soma pra cikito manīṣā tvaṃ rajiṣṭham anu neṣi panthām / tava praṇītī
pitaro na indo deveṣu ratnam abhajanta dhīrāḥ ||1||*

**OBSERVATION.** *rajiṣṭham anu neṣi panthām* — "you lead along the
straightest path" (*panthām*, m. acc. sg.; *neṣi*, 2 sg. pres. *√nī*). This is
the Soma-as-guide motif and is the closest Ṛgvedic anticipation of the *pathin*
language of CU 5.10. RV 1.91 is addressed to **Soma the deity/plant**; the hymn
contains no lunar term. Its use as an epigraph for a Luna chapter imports the
identification from elsewhere.

### 6.1 KhV 1.65–72

Gloss of 1.70ab:

| word | form | sense |
|---|---|---|
| *sampītvā* | absolutive, *sam-√pā* | "having drunk fully" |

**WHERE THE TRANSLATION SOFTENS.** Mallinson renders *upavaset* "should **curb
his diet**". The dedicated Sanskrit sense of *upa-√vas* in this register is
**to fast** (*upavāsa* = fasting, a *vrata*), and the verse pairs it with
*vratasthaḥ*, "standing in a vow". The literal reading is: "having drunk
[the nectar], being sound in himself, **he should fast, keeping the observance,
for twelve years.**" The Sanskrit prescribes a **discipline**; Mallinson's
"curb his diet" reads as a practical adjustment.

**OBSERVATION.** There is **no lunar term whatever** in KhV 1.65–72. No
*candra*, *soma*, *śaśin*, *indu*, or *kalā*. The locus is *trikūṭa*, "the
three-peaked", located *bhrūmadhye*, "between the eyebrows", in the
*brahmāṇḍa*. The nectar is *paramāmṛta* and *amṛtodaka*. The lunar material of
the Khecarīvidyā is in **chapter 2** — e.g. 2.18c *tatra somakalāś cāṣṭau*,
2.20a *śaśini cāṣṭamī sarvāḥ*, 2.22d *tat proktaṃ somamaṇḍalam*, 2.23a
*kalācatuṣkaṃ tatroktam* — which the book's extract does not contain.

single clearest case in the audit of the book asserting a Sanskrit reading that
the Sanskrit does not have.

| CU 5.9.2 (the corpse) | *haranti*, *√hṛ*, obj. *pretam*, goal *agnaye* | **Physical, explicitly — and it terminates at the pyre.** |
| CU 5.10.1–2 (devayāna) | *abhi-sam-√bhū*, then a **verbless** abl.→acc. chain; the one finite motion verb (*eti*) has the **sun** as subject; the one *√gam* (*gamayati*) takes **brahman** as goal | **UNDETERMINED, leaning non-physical.** Five of nine stations are units of time. The physicality in English renderings is supplied by the verb the translator inserts into the verbless chain. |
| CU 5.10.3 (*naite saṃvatsaram abhiprāpnuvanti*) | *abhi-pra-√āp* with **"the year"** as accusative object | **Demonstrably non-spatial** in this clause, and therefore the same construction elsewhere in the passage cannot be assumed spatial. |

| CU 5.10.8 (third *sthāna*) | *sthānam* glossed by two imperatives *jāyasva mriyasva* | **UNDETERMINED between place and state**, with the appositional gloss favouring state. |

Moon absent from this path. |
| BU 6.2.16 (moon) | *te candraṃ prāpya annaṃ bhavanti*; *enāṃs … bhakṣayanti*; simile marked *yathā somaṃ rājānam* | **UNDETERMINED, with a strong internal constraint:** the identical construction is used two clauses later of **reaching the earth and becoming food** there. Whatever kind of arrival is meant of the moon is the same kind meant of the earth. |
| KsU 1.2 | *candramasam eva te sarve **gacchanti***; *dvāram*; *atisṛjate*; moon fed *prāṇaiḥ* (instr.) | **Physical language at its strongest in the corpus** — the ordinary verb of going with an accusative goal. Still not decisive, because *dvāra* is also the standard metaphor and the gate is passed by an **identity statement**, not by arrival. |

| KhV 1.65–72, 3.8–14 | *pibet*, *siñcantī*, *prayāti* — all **within the body** | **Physical, but the geography is anatomical**: *brahmāṇḍa*, *bhrūmadhya*, *suṣumnā*, *śivadhāma*. No celestial travel is at issue, and no lunar term occurs. |

## 9. Where the English hardened the Sanskrit — consolidated list

Ordered by how load-bearing the hardening is.

1. **The supplied verb in the verbless chain (CU 5.10.1–4; BU 6.2.15–16).**
   The Sanskrit gives ablative–accusative pairs with **no verb**. Every English
   translation inserts one, and the inserted verb determines whether the reader
   sees an itinerary or a metamorphosis. Radhakrishnan's "they **go** to light"
   is the strongest hardening; Olivelle's and Hume's "pass into" is the mildest.
   None flags the ellipsis.
2. **"They become the food of the gods" read into CU 5.10.4.** The Chāndogya
   says *tad devānām annam; taṃ devā bhakṣayanti* — of **the moon**. The
   proposition about the dead is Bṛhadāraṇyaka's (6.2.16), and Śaṅkara's.
3. **"Drunk by the gods".** Neither Upaniṣad uses any verb of drinking. Both
   use *√bhakṣ* and *anna*. The drinking frame is Ṛgvedic and has been carried
   across.
4. **Griffith, RV 10.85.5: "The Moon is that which shapes the years"** for
   *samānāṃ māsa ākṛtiḥ*. Two hardenings stacked: *māsaḥ* "month" → "the Moon",
   and *ākṛtiḥ* "form" → "that which shapes" (result-noun → agent).
5. **Olivelle, Muṇḍaka 1.2.10: "atop the **firmament**"** for *nākasya pṛṣṭhe*.
   English "firmament" descends from Latin *firmamentum* rendering Greek
   *stereōma*, "a solid thing" — it is a translation of a translation carrying
   an explicit solidity claim. *Nāka* carries no such claim. "On the back of
   heaven" is literal. The book quotes this rendering.
6. **Olivelle, Muṇḍaka 2.1.5: "From the **moon** comes rain"** for *somāt
   parjanyaḥ*. The Sanskrit ablative is *somāt*, "from Soma". Substituting
   "moon" imports the CU 5.10 identification into a verse that does not make it.
   The book quotes this rendering and treats it as the compressed statement of
   the lunar cycle.
7. **Griffith, RV 10.90.3: "three-fourths eternal **life** in heaven"** for
   *tripād asyāmṛtaṃ divi*. "Life" is supplied to *amṛtam*.
8. **Griffith, RV 9.1.6: "**may** Sūrya's Daughter purify"** for the present
   indicative *punāti*. Indicative → optative.
9. **Mallinson, KhV 1.70: "should **curb his diet**"** for *upavaset*, "should
   fast", in a line that also says *vratasthaḥ*, "keeping the vow". A softening
   in the direction the book then builds on.
10. **Śaṅkara's *antarikṣe* and *ārūḍhaḥ*.** Not a translation issue but the
    same phenomenon inside the tradition: the commentator supplies "seen in the
    sky" and "having climbed up", neither of which is in the śruti. He also
    supplies the counterweight — *na kavalotkṣepeṇa*, "not by tossing morsels";
    *upakaraṇamātram*, "merely a means"; and a **new watery body constituted at
    the lunar orb**, which means that on his own account nothing bodily
    travelled there.

## 10. The furnished world beyond the moon — and how it is crossed

The point is worth isolating because it bears directly on the question.
Kauṣītaki 1.3–1.4 is by far the most **concretely furnished** passage in the
whole corpus. After the moon-gate the traveller reaches, in the accusative,
with the plain verb *āgacchati*:

> स एतं देवयानं पन्थानमासाद्य **अग्निलोकम् आगच्छति** स वायुलोकं स वरुणलोकं स
> आदित्यलोकं स इन्द्रलोकं स प्रजापतिलोकं स ब्रह्मलोकम् । … **विरजा नदी**, **इल्यो वृक्षः**,
> **सालज्यं संस्थानम्**, **अपराजितम् आयतनम्**, इन्द्रप्रजापती **द्वारगोपौ**, **विभु प्रमितम्**,
> **विचक्षणा आसन्दी**, **अमितौजाः पर्यङ्कः** …

*sa etaṃ devayānaṃ panthānam āsādya agnilokam āgacchati, sa vāyulokaṃ, sa
varuṇalokaṃ, sa ādityalokaṃ, sa indralokaṃ, sa prajāpatilokaṃ, sa brahmalokam
… virajā nadī, ilyo vṛkṣaḥ, sālajyaṃ saṃsthānam, aparājitam āyatanam,
indraprajāpatī dvāragopau, vibhu pramitam, vicakṣaṇā āsandī, amitaujāḥ
paryaṅkaḥ …*

**OBSERVATION.** These are not "halls". *Virajā* is a **river** (*nadī*),
*Ilya* a **tree** (*vṛkṣa*), *Sālajya* a **meeting-place** (*saṃsthāna*),
*Aparājita* a **dwelling** (*āyatana*), *Vibhu* a **hall** (*pramita*),
*Vicakṣaṇā* a **throne/seat** (*āsandī*), *Amitaujas* a **couch**
(*paryaṅka*), with Indra and Prajāpati as **door-keepers** (*dvāragopau*) and
five hundred *apsarases* with powder, fruit, unguent and garlands in their
hands. This is a furnished palace, described in ordinary concrete nouns.

**OBSERVATION — load-bearing.** And the text says how the traveller gets
across it:

> स आगच्छत्यारं ह्रदं **तन्मनसात्येति** … स आगच्छति **विरजां नदीं तां मनसैवात्येति** ।

*sa āgacchaty āraṃ hradaṃ **tan manasātyeti** … sa āgacchati virajāṃ nadīṃ
**tāṃ manasaivātyeti**.*

*manasā* (n. **instr.** sg., "by mind") + *eva* ("alone, precisely") +
*atyeti* (3 sg., *ati-√i*, "crosses over"). **"He crosses the lake Āra by mind;
he crosses the river Virajā by mind alone."** The Upaniṣad that furnishes the
beyond most concretely is also the one that states, in the instrumental, that
the crossing of that furniture is done **with the mind**. That is the text's own
signal about the ontological register of its own scenery, and it is in the
Sanskrit, not in an interpretation.

### 11.1 The five-fire table is a Chāndogya/Bṛhadāraṇyaka conflation

`moon-as-soul-threshold.md` presents one table under the heading "the doctrine
appears in almost identical form in two early prose Upaniṣads". The table is a
mix, and the differences it smooths over are exactly the ones that matter for a
lunar argument:

| fire | book's table | Chāndogya 5.4–5.8 | Bṛhadāraṇyaka 6.2.9–13 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | fuel sun, smoke rays, flame day, **coals moon**, sparks stars | fuel *āditya*, smoke *raśmayaḥ*, flame *ahar*, **coals *candramāḥ***, sparks *nakṣatrāṇi* | fuel *āditya*, smoke *raśmayaḥ*, flame *ahaḥ*, coals *diśaḥ*, sparks *avāntaradiśaḥ* |
| 2 | **fuel the year**, smoke clouds, flame lightning, coals thunderbolt, sparks hail | **fuel *vāyu***, smoke *abhram*, flame *vidyut*, coals *aśani*, sparks *hrādanayaḥ* | **fuel *saṃvatsara***, smoke *abhrāṇi*, flame *vidyut*, coals *aśani*, sparks *hrāduṇayaḥ* |
| 3 | **fuel earth, smoke fire**, flame night, **coals moon**, sparks regions | fuel *saṃvatsara*, smoke *ākāśa*, flame *rātri*, coals *diśaḥ*, sparks *avāntaradiśaḥ* | fuel *pṛthivī*, smoke *agni*, flame *rātri*, **coals *candramāḥ***, sparks *nakṣatrāṇi* |
| 4 | **fuel open mouth**, smoke breath, **flame speech**, coals eye, sparks ear | fuel *vāc*, smoke *prāṇa*, flame *jihvā*, coals *cakṣus*, sparks *śrotram* | fuel *vyāttam*, smoke *prāṇa*, flame *vāc*, coals *cakṣus*, sparks *śrotram* |
| 5 | fuel vulva, **smoke hair**, flame vagina, coals penetration, sparks pleasure | fuel *upastha*, smoke *yad upamantrayate*, flame *yoni*, coals *yad antaḥ karoti*, sparks *abhinandāḥ* | fuel *upastha*, smoke *lomāni*, flame *yoni*, coals *yad antaḥ karoti*, sparks *abhinandāḥ* |

The table is **Bṛhadāraṇyaka's for fires 2–5** and **Chāndogya's for fire 1**,
with "sparks the regions" in fire 3 matching neither. The visible effect of the
merge is that **the moon appears as the coals of two different fires**, which
happens in neither recension: CU puts it in the heavenly fire, BU in the earthly
fire, and neither puts it in both. If the book wants the moon at the top of the
cosmic fire, that is Chāndogya; if it wants the moon in the earthly fire, that is
Bṛhadāraṇyaka; it cannot have both from one text.

Also: "the far world (*dyuloka*)". The Sanskrit is *asau lokaḥ*, "yonder world".
*Dyuloka* is Śaṅkara's gloss word, not the śruti's.

### 11.2 "From that offering arises King Soma — **the moon**"

CU 5.4.2 reads *tasyā āhuteḥ somo rājā sambhavati*. The words "the moon" are
supplied by the book. The identification is standard and Śaṅkara makes it, but
it is not in the sentence quoted.

### 11.3 "The **soul** travels this circuit. In death **it ascends** to the moon"

Neither element is in the Sanskrit. CU 5.10 contains no occurrence of *ātman*,
*jīva*, *prāṇa*, or *śarīra* (verified by exhaustive search of 5.10.1–10). The
subject is *te*, "they", resolved to *ye … upāsate*, persons defined by
practice. And there is no verb of ascent in CU 5.10.4 — *ārūḍha* and
*āroha* are Śaṅkara's, and Śaṅkara pairs them with the statement that a **new**
watery body is constituted at the lunar orb.

### 11.4 "become Soma, are **drunk** by the gods"

No verb of drinking occurs in CU 5.10 or BU 6.2. Both use *bhakṣayanti*, "they
eat", and *anna*, "food". BU marks the eating as a **simile** (*yathā somaṃ
rājānam*). Śaṅkara denies the literal sense outright: *na hi te kavalotkṣepeṇa
devair bhakṣyante*, and glosses *anna* as *upakaraṇamātra*, "merely a means".
The book's stronger phrasing — "The souls of the dead *are* what the gods
drink" (Notes, `moon-as-soul-threshold.md`) — is stronger than any of the
three: stronger than Chāndogya, stronger than Bṛhadāraṇyaka, and directly
against Śaṅkara.

### 11.5 "It ascends, and **arrives first at the moon**" (`2-Luna.md`),
attributed to CU 5.10, BU 6.2 and KsU 1.2 jointly

- **Chāndogya**: the moon is reached *after* the sun on the devayāna and
  *after* the world of the fathers and *ākāśa* on the pitṛyāna. Not first on
  either.
- **Bṛhadāraṇyaka 6.2.15**: the devayāna has **no moon at all**.
- **Kauṣītaki 1.2**: this and only this text says *ye vai ke cāsmāl lokāt
  prayanti candramasam eva te sarve gacchanti* — "all who depart go to the
  moon". The claim as stated in `2-Luna.md` is Kauṣītaki's alone.

Correspondingly, the framing "the moon is the sorting gate: those who answer
rightly pass, those who cannot are returned" is Kauṣītaki's structure. In
Chāndogya the moon is **on both paths**, including the one with no return.

### 11.6 "the fifteen kalās (lunar phases or aspects)" in the Kauṣītaki answer

The KsU 1.2 answer reads *… ṛtavo reta ābhṛtaṃ **pañcadaśāt prasūtāt**
pitryāvataḥ …* — "the seed was gathered … from the **fifteenfold**, from the
begotten/pressed one, from him of the fathers …". The word ***kalā* does not
occur in KsU 1.2.** *Pañcadaśa* is a numeral adjective in the ablative.

### 11.7 "the fifteen kalās of the moon are also the fifteen functions of mind
(*Bṛhadāraṇyaka* 1.5.14)"

BU 1.5.14–15 reads:

> स एष **संवत्सरः प्रजापतिः** षोडशकलः । तस्य **रात्रय एव पञ्चदश कलाः** । **ध्रुवैवास्य
> षोडशी कला** । … सोऽमावास्यां रात्रिमेतया षोडश्या कलया सर्वमिदं प्राणभृदनुप्रविश्य ततः
> प्रातर्जायते ॥ १४ ॥
> यो वै स संवत्सरः प्रजापतिः षोडशकलोऽयमेव स योऽयमेवंवित्पुरुषः । तस्य **वित्तमेव
> पञ्चदश कलाः** । **आत्मैवास्य षोडशी कला** ॥ १५ ॥

*sa eṣa **saṃvatsaraḥ prajāpatiḥ** ṣoḍaśakalaḥ; tasya **rātraya eva pañcadaśa
kalāḥ**; **dhruvaivāsya ṣoḍaśī kalā** … so 'māvāsyāṃ rātrim etayā ṣoḍaśyā
kalayā sarvam idaṃ prāṇabhṛd anupraviśya tataḥ prātar jāyate ||14||
yo vai sa saṃvatsaraḥ prajāpatiḥ ṣoḍaśakalo 'yam eva sa yo 'yam evaṃvit
puruṣaḥ; tasya **vittam eva pañcadaśa kalāḥ**; **ātmaivāsya ṣoḍaśī kalā**
||15||*

**OBSERVATION.** The sixteen-parted one is **the year as Prajāpati**
(*saṃvatsaraḥ prajāpatiḥ*), not the moon. Its fifteen *kalās* are **the
nights** (*rātrayaḥ*); the sixteenth is *dhruvā*, "the fixed one". Transposed
to the person (1.5.15), the fifteen are **his wealth** (*vittam*) and the
sixteenth is **the self** (*ātmā*). ***Manas* does not appear in either
verse.** The book's "the fifteen kalās of the moon are also the fifteen
functions of mind (BU 1.5.14) … Manas is the fifteenth" is not what the passage
says. What *is* correct in the book's note is the sixteenth as the undiminished
part — BU 1.5.15 makes it *ātman* explicitly.

This is also the one place in the audited corpus where **amāvāsyā** occurs as a
technical station: *so 'māvāsyāṃ rātrim … anupraviśya tataḥ prātar jāyate*,
"on the new-moon night, having entered all that breathes [with the sixteenth
part], he is born again from it in the morning." *Amāvāsyā* is **not** a station
on either path in CU 5.10, BU 6.2, or KsU 1.2.

### 11.8 The Kauṣītaki "halls"

`moon-as-soul-threshold.md` and `2-Luna.md` list "the halls *Vijara*, *Ilya*,
*Salajya*, *Aparājita*, *Vibhu*, *Vicakṣaṇa*, *Amitaujas*". In KsU 1.3 these
are, respectively, a **river**, a **tree**, a **meeting-place**, a **dwelling**,
a **hall**, a **throne**, and a **couch**. Flattening them to "halls" loses the
detail that matters most: they are the furniture of a palace, and the text says
the river is crossed *manasaiva*, **by mind alone** (§10).

### 11.9 Muṇḍaka renderings quoted in `death-and-the-imperishable-self.md`

- "atop the **firmament**" (1.2.10) = *nākasya pṛṣṭhe*, "on the back of
  heaven". "Firmament" imports solidity that *nāka* does not carry (§7).
- "From the **moon** comes rain" (2.1.5) = *somāt parjanyaḥ*, "from Soma, the
  rain-cloud". The Sanskrit word is *soma*, not *candramas*.
- "he is not killed, when the body is killed" (cited in the file as Kaṭha
  2.1.18) — the translation is sound; the Sanskrit is **न हन्यते हन्यमाने शरीरे**,
  *na hanyate hanyamāne śarīre* (locative absolute), verified at Sanskrit
  Wikisource `कठोपनिषत्/प्रथमोध्यायः/द्वितीयवल्ली`, verse 18. This is one of the
  very few places in the audited corpus where *śarīra* appears at all in
  connection with death and survival. It says the body is killed. It does not
  say anything travels.

### 11.12 Kaṭha citation numbers are transposed

The verses quoted in `death-and-the-imperishable-self.md` as **Kaṭha 2.1.18,
2.1.19, 2.1.20, 2.1.23, 2.1.24** are all from the **second vallī of the first
adhyāya**, i.e. **1.2.18, 1.2.19, 1.2.20, 1.2.23, 1.2.24** in Olivelle's own
numbering (and I.2.x in Radhakrishnan, 2.x in Hume). Verified against Sanskrit
Wikisource `कठोपनिषत्/प्रथमोध्यायः/द्वितीयवल्ली`, where *na hanyate hanyamāne
śarīre* stands at verse 18 of that vallī. The adhyāya and vallī digits have
been transposed in five citations. The file's other Kaṭha citations — 1.1.6,
1.1.26–28, 1.3.14, 1.3.15, 2.2.15 — are correct.

### 11.10 Khecarīvidyā extract

- The file is titled "The Lunar Nectar and the Body Sustained from Within", but
  **neither extracted passage contains any lunar term** (§6.1). The lunar
  material of the Khecarīvidyā is chapter 2 (*somakalāḥ*, *śaśin*,
  *somamaṇḍala*), which is not extracted.
- ***śīta-snigdha-madhura*** as given in the file is not the reading. KhV 3.13
  has ***śiśira-snigdha-śīta***. "Sweet" has been substituted for the second
  cold-word, and the substitution then carries the argument that links the verse
  to the "sweet ghee-and-milk substrate".
- "External food has not been *renounced* as a discipline — it has become
  *unnecessary*." KhV 1.70 says *upavaset … vratasthaḥ*, "he should **fast**,
  **standing in a vow**". The verse prescribes precisely a discipline.

### 11.11 Ṛgveda extract and `2-Luna.md`

- "The Moon is that which shapes the years" (RV 10.85.5), quoted in both files
  and load-bearing in the Luna chapter's section "She is the measure": a
  double translation artefact (§5.1). The Sanskrit is *samānāṃ māsa ākṛtiḥ*,
  "the month is the figure of the years".
- RV 1.91.1 as the Luna chapter's opening epigraph: RV 1.91 is a Soma hymn with
  no lunar term.
- Griffith's 1889 editorial headnote to RV 9.1 ("Soma, or Indu, the Moon …")
  is quoted as though it stated Ṛgvedic doctrine. It states Griffith's
  nineteenth-century synthesis. The Ṛgveda supplies the material (Soma set in
  heaven, drained and refilled) but never the equation as a proposition; the
  Upaniṣads supply the proposition (CU 5.10.4).
- "*She is Soma* … the plant is the moon descended as vegetable" — RV 10.85.3–4
  says the opposite of an identity between the pressed plant and the true Soma:
  *somaṃ manyate papivān yat sampiṃṣanty oṣadhim; somaṃ yaṃ brāhmaṇo vidur na
  tasyāśnāti kaścana*, "one thinks he has drunk Soma when they crush the plant;
  the Soma the brahmins know — nobody eats of that." The book quotes this verse
  and then asserts a fourfold identity that the verse is drawing a line
  through. The book's own note four paragraphs later ("what is physically drunk
  is only the outer analogue") gets it right; the "triple identity
  Plant=Juice=Moon=God … There is no separation" note does not.

## 12. Observations, hypotheses, unknowns

### OBSERVATIONS (grammar and lexicon only)

1. The governing verb of both paths in both Upaniṣads is *abhi-sam-√bhū*, a
   compound of the root "to be/become". No primary verb of locomotion governs
   the sequence in CU 5.10 or BU 6.2.
2. The station-chains contain **no finite verb**. Every English verb in them is
   supplied by the translator.
3. Five of the nine stations of the Chāndogya devayāna are **units of time**.
4. The one finite motion verb in CU 5.10.1 (*eti*) has **the sun** as subject.
5. The one *√gam* form in CU 5.10.2 (*gamayati*) takes ***brahman*** as its
   accusative goal. The accusative-of-goal in this passage therefore does not
   entail a place.
6. *abhi-pra-√āp*, "reach", takes ***saṃvatsaram***, "the year", as its object
   in CU 5.10.3.
7. *ātman*, *jīva*, *prāṇa*, and *śarīra* do **not occur anywhere in CU 5.10**.
   The only subject is *te*, "they".
8. ***Śarīra* is never the subject of any verb of going anywhere in this
   corpus.** The one verb of physical carrying (*haranti*, CU 5.9.2) takes the
   corpse as object and the pyre as goal.
9. The return in CU 5.10.5–6 is a chain of *√bhū* verbs, in the **singular**,
   with the plural restored only at rebirth as plants.
10. In CU 5.10.4 the food of the gods is grammatically **the moon**, not the
    dead. In BU 6.2.16 it is the dead, explicitly, and marked as a **simile**.
11. The verb is *√bhakṣ*, "eat", never *√pā*, "drink", in both Upaniṣads.
12. In CU the moon lies on **both** paths, including the non-returning one.
    In BU the moon is on the pitṛyāna only.
13. KsU 1.2 alone uses the plain *gacchanti* with *candramasam*; KsU alone
    says all the dead go to the moon; KsU alone calls the moon a *dvāra*.
14. KsU says the moon waxes ***by their prāṇas*** — instrumental, a mechanism,
    not a lodging.
15. KsU 1.4 says the beyond's river is crossed ***manasaiva***, "by mind
    alone".
16. *Nāka* carries no solidity in its stem; *pṛṣṭha* carries surface-and-top;
    *ākāśa* means unobstructed open space and is the opposite of enclosure;
    *div/dyu* is a shining-root.
17. Śaṅkara reads every station of both paths as an *abhimāninī devatā*, a
    presiding deity, and the whole sequence as *ātivāhika*, conducting; he
    explicitly rejects the road-with-landmarks reading (BS 4.3.4).
18. Śaṅkara nevertheless identifies the *candramas* of CU 5.10.4 with the moon
    *seen in the sky*, and holds that a **new watery body** is constituted at
    the lunar orb from the water released by the burnt corpse.

### HYPOTHESES (doctrinal, offered as such)

1. The Chāndogya passage is more plausibly read as a **doctrine of
   transformation through cosmic phases** than as an itinerary, because its
   verbs are becoming-verbs, its stations are half of them times, and its
   "route" does not reverse. But this is a reading, not a grammatical result.
2. The physicalisation of the doctrine happens historically **downstream** of
   the two prose Upaniṣads: Kauṣītaki supplies the door and the plain
   going-verb; Śaṅkara supplies the visible moon, the ascent verb, and the
   lunar orb as a construction-site; the Purāṇic *candraloka* supplies the rest.
   Nothing of that is required by CU 5.10.
3. The Vedic material and the Upaniṣadic material are doing different work.
   RV 10.85 places Soma in heaven with locatives, which is a cosmographic claim.
   CU 5.10 places the dead in a sequence with ablatives and accusatives and no
   verb, which is not obviously a cosmographic claim at all.

### UNKNOWNS / where the evidence does not decide

1. **CU 5.10.4 is genuinely undetermined** between a spatial and a
   non-spatial reading of *candramasam*. There is no verb to disambiguate it,
   the same construction governs a time-unit one verse earlier, and the
   commentators split.
2. **BU 6.2.16 is genuinely undetermined**, with the internal constraint that
   the moon-arrival and the earth-arrival are grammatically the same event-type.
3. *Tṛtīyaṃ sthānam* (CU 5.10.8) is undetermined between "third place" and
   "third state".
4. *Parāḥ parāvataḥ* (BU 6.2.15) is undetermined between spatial remoteness and
   temporal duration; all three standard translations pick duration silently.
5. *Māsaḥ* at RV 10.85.5 is undetermined between "month" and "moon", though
   "month" is the plain reading and *candramas* was available to the poet.
6. **Commentators diverge on the terminus**: Bādari and Śaṅkara hold the goal is
   the *kārya*, conditioned brahman, precisely **because a goal must be
   *pradeśavat*, spatially extended**; Jaimini (BS 4.3.12) holds it is the
   supreme brahman. I have verified Śaṅkara's text on both sūtras directly; I
   have **not** verified Rāmānuja's or Madhva's texts in this pass, and do not
   assert their positions here.
7. Whether the identification *soma = candramas* is present anywhere **inside**
   the Ṛgveda as a proposition, rather than as material an interpreter can
   assemble, I could not settle from the Ṛgvedic text alone. It is
   uncontroversially explicit from the Upaniṣads onward.
8. The Kauṣītaki 1.2 text is corrupt in the digital witness used here at two
   points (*na prajanayati*; the missing *na* in *ya enaṃ na pratyāha*), and
   Bodewitz holds the verses originally addressed the **seasons**, not the moon.
   I did not consult a printed critical edition of Kauṣītaki in this pass.
9. The Khecarīvidyā Sanskrit here is reconstructed from apparatus lemmata with
   OCR-stripped diacritics restored by me. The consonantal skeleton is
   Mallinson's; the diacritics are mine.

## 13. Direct answer

**Does the Sanskrit assert a physical bodily destination?**

**No — not in the two passages the argument mainly rests on, and nowhere in the
corpus is a body the thing that goes.** Chāndogya 5.9.2 disposes of the body on
the pyre before the journey section begins, and *śarīra* never again appears as
the subject of a verb of going. Chāndogya 5.10 and Bṛhadāraṇyaka 6.2 are
**undetermined** between a physical and a non-physical reading: their governing
verb is a becoming-verb, their station-chains have no verb at all, half their
stations are units of time, their one verb of reaching takes "the year" as
object, and their one verb of going takes *brahman* as goal. Kauṣītaki 1.2 is
the one passage that uses the ordinary language of bodily travel — *candramasam
eva te sarve gacchanti* — and even it says the beyond is crossed *manasaiva*,
by mind alone, and that the gate opens on the words *tvam asmi*, "I am you".
The Ṛgveda does make plain cosmographic claims about Soma with locatives
(*divi … adhi śritaḥ*) but never says Soma is the moon.

**Is the physicality in the English an artefact of translation?**

**Substantially yes, but not entirely, and not uniformly.** The single largest
artefact is structural: the Sanskrit gives a verbless cascade of ablatives and
accusatives, and every English translation inserts a verb of motion into it.
Radhakrishnan's "they go" is the hardest; Olivelle's and Hume's "pass into" is
the mildest; none flags that the verb is supplied. On top of that sit the
specific hardenings listed in §9 — "firmament" for *nāka*, "the Moon" for
*māsaḥ*, "that which shapes" for *ākṛtiḥ*, "from the moon" for *somāt*.

But the hardening is not only a European habit. Śaṅkara supplies "seen in the
sky" and "having climbed to the lunar orb" — and in the same breath supplies the
counterweight, that the eating is not eating and that a **new** body is
constituted at the moon rather than an old one arriving there. And the classical
tradition put the geographical reading on the table in so many words
(BS 4.3.4: "are these landmarks of a road?") **and rejected it**.

**Where the Sanskrit is undetermined, it is left undetermined here.** CU 5.10.4,
BU 6.2.16, *tṛtīyaṃ sthānam*, *parāḥ parāvataḥ*, and *māsaḥ* at RV 10.85.5 are
listed as unknowns in §12 and are not resolved in either direction.

The one thing the Sanskrit does say plainly, in every passage without exception,
is that whatever goes to the moon **becomes** something on the way and
**becomes** something else on the way back. *Abhisambhavanti*, *sambhavati*,
*bhūtvā … bhavati*, *annaṃ bhavanti*, *bhūtāni bhavanti*. The corpus is built
out of *√bhū*.
