# Luna in the Greek: a philological audit

Question put: English renderings of Plutarch's *De facie* and of the Homeric
Hymns have been read as asserting that the moon is a physical, terrained place
that souls bodily travel to. Does the Greek say that?

This file separates **OBSERVATION** (what the Greek grammatically and lexically
says), **HYPOTHESIS** (what it may imply doctrinally), and **UNKNOWN**
(genuinely ambiguous or textually contested). Where the Greek is undetermined
between a physical and a non-physical reading it is left undetermined.

## Summary answer, before the evidence

| Passage | Physical bodily destination? | Soul passage? | Verdict |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *De facie* 933D, 935C | — | — | Moon **predicated** as γῆ ("earth"), securely, but as a dialectical θέσις; its terrain is stated only in ὥσπερ-similes |
| *De facie* 935D | — | — | The famous "holy earth" — **γῆ is an editorial conjecture**; MSS have no word for earth here |
| *De facie* 942F | no | yes | Destination defined **optically** (terminus of earth's shadow cone), not as terrain |
| *De facie* 943A | no | no motion at all | Sun/moon/earth are **sources of constituents**, not places |
| *De facie* 943B | no | yes | Locative ἐν + dative; **σῶμα is explicitly left behind in the earth** |
| *De facie* 943C–D | no | yes | ψυχή wanders in a locative dative region; body already gone |
| *De facie* 944B | — | — | **Simile** (ὥσπερ … οὕτως) with earth's gulfs |
| *De facie* 944C | no | yes | ψυχαί cross with εἰς + acc. — a real spatial accusative. But "hemisphere", "side" and "House of" are **not in the Greek** |
| *De facie* 944D | no | yes | Daemons descend to earth; a daemon is νοῦς+ψυχή, never σῶμα |
| *De facie* 945B | **decisively no** | — | Moon is the **στοιχεῖον** of soul; souls **ἀναλύονται εἰς** her as corpses dissolve into earth |
| Homeric Hymn 31 | no | — | A god drives horses; the solar "return arc" rests on a **supplied line** |
| Homeric Hymn 32 | no | — | A goddess bathes and drives; **no** emission/reception contrast with Helios in the Greek |
| *Theogony* 371–382 | no | — | Pure genealogy. No place, no travel, no body |

**Nothing in any of these three texts describes a departure from the earth by a
σῶμα.** Every departure is by ψυχή, by νοῦς, by δαίμων, or by a god. This is
not a matter of interpretation; Plutarch states the contrary explicitly twice
(943B, 945B–C).

## PART A — Plutarch, *De facie*

### A1. Is the moon called γῆ? (933D, 935C–D)

**Instance 1 — 933D, secure predication.** Lamprias to Aristotle:

> ὅλως γάρ, ὦ φίλε Ἀριστότελες, **γῆ μὲν οὖσα** πάγκαλόν τι χρῆμα καὶ σεμνὸν
> ἀναφαίνεται καὶ κεκοσμημένον, ὡς δ' ἄστρον ἢ φῶς ἤ τι σῶμα θεῖον καὶ
> οὐράνιον δέδια μὴ ἄμορφος ᾖ καὶ ἀπρεπὴς καὶ καταισχύνουσα τὴν καλὴν
> ἐπωνυμίαν.

*holōs gar, ō phile Aristoteles, gē men ousa pankalon ti chrēma kai semnon
anaphainetai…*

Literal: "for on the whole, dear Aristotle, **being earth** she shows forth as
an altogether fine and august and well-ordered thing, but as a star or a light
or some divine and heavenly body I fear she may be shapeless and unseemly and
a disgrace to the fair title."

- γῆ οὖσα is a circumstantial participle in **predicative** position, not a
  simile. OBSERVATION: Plutarch's speaker does predicate γῆ of Selene.
- But the sentence is explicitly an **argument from decorum** — "she looks
  better as an earth than as a star" — not a physical demonstration.

**Instance 2 — 935C, secure predication, but marked as a θέσις.**

> πολλοῦ δὲ δέομεν ἄνθρωποι τὴν σελήνην, **γῆν οὖσαν ὀλυμπίαν**, ἄψυχον
> ἡγεῖσθαι σῶμα καὶ ἄνουν … ὥστε μηδὲν οἰώμεθα πλημμελεῖν **γῆν αὐτὴν
> θέμενοι**

*pollou de deomen anthrōpoi tēn selēnēn, gēn ousan olympian, apsychon
hēgeisthai sōma kai anoun … hōste mēden oiōmetha plēmmelein gēn autēn
themenoi*

Word by word: πολλοῦ δὲ δέομεν "we fall far short" | ἄνθρωποι "as men" |
τὴν σελήνην "the moon" (acc.) | γῆν οὖσαν ὀλυμπίαν "being an Olympian earth"
(predicative participle) | ἄψυχον … σῶμα καὶ ἄνουν "a soulless and mindless
body" | ἡγεῖσθαι "to consider" ‖ ὥστε "so that" | μηδὲν οἰώμεθα πλημμελεῖν
"let us not think we err at all" | **γῆν αὐτὴν θέμενοι** "in **positing** her
[as] earth".

- Cherniss: "the moon, because she is a celestial earth, is a body [not]
  without soul or mind… So let us not think it any offence to hold her to be
  earth."
- OBSERVATION: γῆν οὖσαν ὀλυμπίαν is in **E and B without emendation**. This is
  the securest "the moon is earth" statement in the treatise.
- OBSERVATION, and it is load-bearing: the operative verb is **θέμενοι**,
  aorist middle participle of τίθημι. LSJ τίθημι B.II.2 (middle): "lay down as
  a principle, assume, posit"; τίθεσθαι + double accusative = "reckon/count X
  as Y". This is the technical verb for advancing a **thesis** in a
  dialectical argument. It is not εἶναι, not ἐστίν. Plutarch's speaker asks
  permission to *classify* the moon under the head "earth", against the Stoic
  classification "fire".
- The dispute at 935C–D is therefore about **which of two ontological
  categories** the moon belongs to (γῆ vs πῦρ), not about whether it has
  terrain.

**Instance 3 — 935D, the "holy earth". This one is largely editorial.**

Cherniss's printed text:

> τό γε μὴν τίμιον οὐκ ἀπόλλυσι τῆς δόξης οὐδὲ τὸ θεῖον ἡ σελήνη,
> **⟨γῆ⟩ τις ⟨ὀλυμπία καὶ⟩ ἱερὰ** πρὸς ἀνθρώπων νομιζομένη μᾶλλον ἢ πῦρ
> θολερὸν ὥσπερ οἱ Στωικοὶ λέγουσι καὶ τρυγῶδες.

Apparatus (Cherniss, ad loc., notes 5 and 6):

- `⟨γῆ⟩ τις` — **Emperius**. **E and B read ἥτις** ("she who").
- `⟨ὀλυμπία καὶ⟩` — **Bernardakis**, filling a lacuna of 9 letters in E,
  13 in B. Bernardakis justified it by reference forward to the genuine
  γῆν οὖσαν ὀλυμπίαν at 935C.

The Greek Wikisource text prints the honest form: `γῆ τις ... ἱερὰ` — accepting
Emperius but leaving the lacuna unfilled.

**What the manuscripts actually give:**

> τό γε μὴν τίμιον οὐκ ἀπόλλυσι τῆς δόξης οὐδὲ τὸ θεῖον ἡ σελήνη, **ἥτις**
> … **ἱερὰ** πρὸς ἀνθρώπων νομιζομένη μᾶλλον ἢ πῦρ θολερὸν … καὶ τρυγῶδες.

Literal from the MSS: "the moon does not lose the honour of her repute nor her
divinity, she **who** is reckoned by men [as] **holy** … rather than a turbid
and dreggish fire."

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #1.** Cherniss's "a (celestial and) holy earth" —
> which the book quotes at *passages.md* line 12, and builds the note "**Holy
> earth, not a fire**" on (line 56) — contains **two editorial supplements and
> no manuscript word for earth**. The parentheses in the Loeb English mark
> "(celestial and)" as supplied; they do **not** mark "earth", which is
> Emperius's conjecture replacing MSS ἥτις. The manuscript antithesis is
> **holy** vs. **turbid fire**, not **earth** vs. **fire**.
>
> The conjecture is a good one — 935C two sentences later genuinely has
> γῆν οὖσαν ὀλυμπίαν, and that is why Emperius and Bernardakis proposed it. But
> the sentence cannot be cited as independent evidence that Plutarch called the
> moon a holy earth, because its "earth" was put there by editors reasoning
> from the other sentence.

**Instance 4 — the terrain descriptions are all inside ὥσπερ-similes.**

935B–C, the passage the book quotes first (*passages.md* line 9):

> οὐ γὰρ ἄπιστον οὐδὲ θαυμαστόν, εἰ μηδὲν ἔχουσα διεφθορὸς ⟨ἐν⟩ ἑαυτῇ μηδ'
> ἰλυῶδες … **κάλλη τε θαυμαστὰ κέκτηται τόπων ὄρη τε φλογοειδῆ καὶ ζώνας
> ἁλουργοὺς ἔχει, χρυσόν τε καὶ ἄργυρον οὐκ ἐν βάθει διεσπαρμένον, ἀλλὰ πρὸς
> τοῖς πεδίοις ἐξανθοῦντα πολὺν ἢ πρὸς ὕψεσι λείοις** περιφαινόμενον.

*ou gar apiston oude thaumaston, ei … kallē te thaumasta kektētai topōn orē te
phlogoeidē kai zōnas halourgous echei…*

Literal: "**for it is not incredible nor astonishing, if** — having nothing
corrupt or slimy in herself … — she has acquired wonderful beauties of places
and has fire-coloured mountains and sea-purple zones, and gold and silver not
scattered in the deep but blooming out abundantly by the plains or shown all
round on smooth heights."

- OBSERVATION: the whole terrain-clause hangs on **εἰ** in an
  οὐ … ἄπιστον οὐδὲ θαυμαστόν frame. The grammatical force is "it would not be
  incredible **if** she has …". It is a **concessive possibility**, not an
  assertion.
- Cherniss's English keeps the conditional ("It is in fact not incredible or
  wonderful that the moon, if she has nothing corrupted … **has got** open
  regions…"), so the Loeb is fair here. But the book's own note (*passages.md*
  line 56) converts it: "**she is** 'a celestial and holy earth' **with**
  regions, mountains, plains, metallic veins. Luna **has** substance, weight,
  locus — a world." That is a flat assertion built from a Greek conditional.
- Immediately following, 935C:
  **ὥσπερ ἡ παρ' ἡμῖν ἔχει γῆ κόλπους τινὰς μεγάλους, οὕτως ἐκείνην
  ἀνεπτύχθαι βάθεσι μεγάλοις** — "**just as** our earth here has certain great
  gulfs, **so** she has been opened out with great depths". ὥσπερ … οὕτως is a
  formal simile frame; ἀνεπτύχθαι is an infinitive in indirect statement.

**Verdict A1.** Plutarch's speakers **do predicate γῆ of Selene** (933D, 935C:
secure). They do **not** describe her terrain outside conditional or simile
frames, and the single sentence most often quoted for "holy earth" (935D)
contains no manuscript word for earth at all.

### A2. 942F — the Elysian plain is defined optically, not geographically

> ἐπεὶ τόν γ' Ἅιδην ἀπολιπεῖν ἀδύνατόν ἐστιν αὐτήν, **τοῦ Ἅιδου πέρας οὖσαν**.
> ὥσπερ καὶ Ὅμηρος ἐπικρυψάμενος οὐ φαύλως τοῦτ' εἶπεν
> «ἀλλά ⟨σ'⟩ ἐς Ἠλύσιον πεδίον καὶ πείρατα γαίης».
> **ὅπου γὰρ ἡ σκιὰ τῆς γῆς ἐπινεμομένη παύεται, τοῦτο τέρμα τῆς γῆς ἔθετο καὶ
> πέρας.** εἰς δὲ τοῦτο φαῦλος μὲν οὐδεὶς οὐδ' ἀκάθαρτος **ἄνεισιν**, οἱ δὲ
> χρηστοὶ μετὰ τὴν τελευτὴν **κομισθέντες** αὐτόθι ῥᾷστον μὲν οὕτως βίον, οὐ
> μὴν μακάριον οὐδὲ θεῖον ἔχοντες ἄχρι τοῦ δευτέρου θανάτου διατελοῦσι.

*hopou gar hē skia tēs gēs epinemomenē pauetai, touto terma tēs gēs etheto kai
peras. eis de touto phaulos men oudeis oud' akathartos aneisin, hoi de chrēstoi
meta tēn teleutēn komisthentes autothi…*

Word by word on the definition: ὅπου "where" | ἡ σκιὰ τῆς γῆς "the shadow of
the earth" | ἐπινεμομένη "spreading/grazing outward" (ἐπινέμομαι, of fire or
pasture spreading) | παύεται "ceases" | τοῦτο "this" | τέρμα … καὶ πέρας
"terminus and limit" | τῆς γῆς "of the earth" | ἔθετο "he [Homer] set/posited"
(τίθημι mid. again — the same stance-verb).

- **OBSERVATION, and it is the most important single fact about the geography
  of this myth.** The Ἠλύσιον πεδίον is defined here as **the place where the
  earth's conical shadow ends**. That is a purely **optical and geometrical**
  locus — the apex of the umbra. It has no terrain by definition; it is a
  boundary surface in space.
- Verbs of motion here: **ἄνεισιν** (ἄνειμι, "goes up") with **εἰς + acc.**
  (εἰς τοῦτο) — a genuine spatial accusative of goal; and **κομισθέντες**,
  aorist **passive** participle of κομίζω, "having been conveyed / carried".
  LSJ κομίζω I: "take care of, provide for"; II "carry away, convey"; pass.
  "be conveyed, journey, travel". So the motion vocabulary is ordinary and
  physical. But the subject of ἄνεισιν is a **person-class** (φαῦλος οὐδείς,
  οἱ χρηστοί) after death, and the passive κομισθέντες removes self-propulsion.
- UNKNOWN: whether αὐτόθι ("on the spot, there") in 942F designates the moon
  itself or the shadow-apex just short of it. Cherniss's own note ad loc.
  concedes the difficulty: "In the present passage Plutarch does not say why
  his interpretation of Homer's line justifies him in calling the moon τοῦ
  Ἅιδου πέρας … Nevertheless, in 944C infra the Elysian plain is said to be the
  part of the moon that is turned to heaven." Cherniss flags this as a
  potential **internal inconsistency in Plutarch**. It is not resolvable from
  the Greek.

### A3. 943A — the tripartition: no motion of any kind

> **τριῶν δὲ τούτων συμπαγέντων, τὸ μὲν σῶμα ἡ γῆ τὴν δὲ ψυχὴν ἡ σελήνη, τὸν
> δὲ νοῦν ὁ ἥλιος παρέσχεν εἰς τὴν γένεσιν** ⟨τἀνθρώπῳ ὥσπερ αὐ⟩τῇ τῇ σελήνῃ
> τὸ φέγγος.

*triōn de toutōn sympagentōn, to men sōma hē gē tēn de psychēn hē selēnē, ton
de noun ho hēlios pareschen eis tēn genesin ⟨tanthrōpōi hōsper au⟩tēi tēi
selēnēi to phengos.*

Word by word: τριῶν δὲ τούτων συμπαγέντων "these three having been **welded
together**" (genitive absolute; συμπήγνυμι = "fasten together, make solid,
curdle") | τὸ μὲν σῶμα "the body" (acc.) | ἡ γῆ "the earth" (nom.) | τὴν δὲ
ψυχήν "the soul" | ἡ σελήνη "the moon" | τὸν δὲ νοῦν "the mind" | ὁ ἥλιος "the
sun" | **παρέσχεν** "furnished / supplied" (aor. of παρέχω, one verb governing
all three) | εἰς τὴν γένεσιν "for the coming-to-be" | ⟨τἀνθρώπῳ⟩ "for the man"
| ὥσπερ αὐτῇ τῇ σελήνῃ τὸ φέγγος "just as [it furnishes] the light to the moon
herself".

Apparatus: `⟨τἀνθρώπῳ ὥσπερ αὐ⟩` — **Bernardakis** and **Raingeard**, filling a
lacuna of 7 letters in E, 11 in B after γένεσιν. The core clause is unemended.

- **OBSERVATION.** There is **no verb of motion in this sentence at all**. The
  three bodies are **grammatical subjects of παρέχω** — suppliers of the three
  constituents. εἰς τὴν γένεσιν is **purposive** ("for the purpose of
  generation"), not directional.
- **OBSERVATION.** The construction places sun, moon and earth in the role of
  *material/efficient source*, exactly parallel to "the sun furnishes light to
  the moon". Nobody supposes the moon travels to the sun to fetch its light.
- Cherniss's English ("earth furnishes the body, the moon the soul, and the sun
  furnishes mind to man") is accurate and adds nothing.
- The book quotes this correctly (*passages.md* lines 22, 25). Its gloss "Luna
  is the **middle substance**" (line 53) is well founded — see A9 below, where
  Plutarch says exactly that with στοιχεῖον.

### A4. 943B — the two deaths: locative, and the body is left behind

> ὃν δ' ἀποθνήσκομεν θάνατον, ὁ μὲν ἐκ τριῶν δύο ποιεῖ τὸν ἄνθρωπον ὁ δ' ἓν ἐκ
> δυεῖν, καὶ ὁ μέν ἐστιν **ἐν τῇ ⟨γῇ⟩ τῆς Δήμητρος** … ⟨ὁ⟩ δ' **ἐν τῇ σελήνῃ
> τῆς Φερσεφόνης**. καὶ σύνοικός ἐστι τῆς μὲν χθόνιος ὁ Ἑρμῆς τῆς δ' οὐράνιος.
> **λύει** δ' αὕτη μὲν ταχὺ καὶ μετὰ βίας τὴν ψυχὴν ἀπὸ τοῦ σώματος, ἡ δὲ
> Φερσεφόνη πράως καὶ χρόνῳ πολλῷ τὸν νοῦν ἀπὸ τῆς ψυχῆς…

*hon d' apothnēskomen thanaton, ho men ek triōn duo poiei ton anthrōpon ho d'
hen ek dueīn, kai ho men estin en tēi ⟨gēi⟩ tēs Dēmētros … ho d' en tēi selēnēi
tēs Phersephonēs.*

Word by word on the load-bearing clause: ὁ μέν "the one [death]" | ἐστιν "is" |
**ἐν** "in" | **τῇ ⟨γῇ⟩** "the ⟨earth⟩" (dative) | τῆς Δήμητρος "of Demeter"
‖ ὁ δέ "the other" | **ἐν τῇ σελήνῃ** "in the moon" (dative) | τῆς Φερσεφόνης
"of Persephone".

Apparatus:

- `⟨γῇ⟩` — **Madvig**. **E and B read simply ἐν τῇ τῆς Δήμητρος**, with no noun.
- After Δήμητρος, **a lacuna of 20 letters in E, 26 in B**. Cherniss fills it
  with his own conjecture `⟨διὸ τελευτᾶν λέγεται τὸν βίον⟩` — "wherefore 'to
  make an end' is called 'to render one's life [to her]'". This whole
  parenthesis in the English is **Cherniss's supplement**, marked in the Loeb
  by angle brackets in the English too.
- `⟨ὁ⟩ δ'` — **Kaltwasser**; E and B read τὸ παλαιὸν δὲ ἐν.

Observations:

- **ἐν + dative is locative and static.** It marks *where an event occurs*, not
  a destination reached. The sentence says the first death **happens in** the
  earth and the second **happens in** the moon. There is no verb of arrival.
- The separating verb is **λύει** (λύω, "loose, unbind, dissolve") — a verb of
  *undoing a bond*, taking ἀπό + genitive of separation twice
  (ἀπὸ τοῦ σώματος, ἀπὸ τῆς ψυχῆς). Not a travel verb.
- **The body is not transported anywhere.** It is the term *from* which the
  soul is loosed. The whole architecture is subtractive.
- The book's rendering of this passage (*passages.md* lines 30, 33) reproduces
  Cherniss faithfully. Its note (line 54) — "The first death is at Demeter's
  earth: body separates from soul. The second death is at Persephone's moon" —
  is correct, and "at" is a better English preposition for ἐν + dat. than any
  motion word.

> **TRANSLATION SOFTNESS, not hardening, worth flagging.** The book's note at
> line 54 calls the second death "the more gradual, more gentle, **more
> elective** dissolution". "Elective" has no basis: the Greek is πράως καὶ
> χρόνῳ πολλῷ, "gently and over much time". Nothing about choice.

### A5. 943C–D — the soul wanders; the body is already gone

> **Πᾶσαν ψυχήν, ἄνουν τε καὶ σὺν νῷ, σώματος ἐκπεσοῦσαν εἱμαρμένον ἐστὶν
> ⟨ἐν⟩ τῷ μεταξὺ γῆς καὶ σελήνης χωρίῳ πλανηθῆναι χρόνον οὐκ ἴσον**, ἀλλ' αἱ
> μὲν ἄδικοι καὶ ἀκόλαστοι δίκας τῶν ἀδικημάτων τίνουσι, τὰς δ' ἐπιεικεῖς, ὅσον
> ἀφαγνεῦσαι καὶ ἀποπνεῦσαι ⟨τοὺς⟩ ἀπὸ τοῦ σώματος ὥσπερ ἀτμοῦ πονηροῦ
> μιασμούς, **ἐν τῷ πραοτάτῳ τοῦ ἀέρος, ὃν λειμῶνας Ἅιδου καλοῦσι**, δεῖ
> γίνεσθαι χρόνον τινὰ τεταγμένον.

*pasan psychēn, anoun te kai syn nōi, sōmatos ekpesousan heimarmenon estin ⟨en⟩
tōi metaxy gēs kai selēnēs chōriōi planēthēnai chronon ouk ison…*

Word by word: πᾶσαν ψυχήν "every soul" (acc., subject of the infinitive) |
ἄνουν τε καὶ σὺν νῷ "both mindless and with-mind" | **σώματος ἐκπεσοῦσαν**
"having fallen out **of** the body" (ἐκπίπτω + genitive of separation) |
εἱμαρμένον ἐστίν "it is fated" | **⟨ἐν⟩ τῷ … χωρίῳ** "**in** the region"
(dative) | μεταξὺ γῆς καὶ σελήνης "between earth and moon" | **πλανηθῆναι**
"to wander" | χρόνον οὐκ ἴσον "for an unequal time".

Apparatus: `⟨ἐν⟩` — **Wyttenbach**; E and B read ἐστὲ τῷ μεταξύ. Even without
Wyttenbach's ἐν, a bare dative τῷ … χωρίῳ is locative, not directional.
`ἀφαγνεῦσαι καὶ ἀποπνεῦσαι ⟨τοὺς⟩` and `μιασμούς` — **Emperius** (E, B:
ἀποπνεῦσαι ἀπό … αἰτίου). `⟨εἶθ'⟩` at the next clause — **Basiliensis**.

Observations:

- **The subject is ψυχή throughout, and the body has already been shed** — the
  first thing the sentence does is separate them (σώματος ἐκπεσοῦσαν).
- **πλανηθῆναι** (aor. pass. inf. of πλανάω, with intransitive force) is not a
  destination verb. LSJ πλανάω II (pass.): "wander, roam, stray; go astray".
  It takes no goal.
- The region is **τὸ πραότατον τοῦ ἀέρος** — "the mildest part **of the air**".
  Not the moon, not a surface. The λειμῶνες Ἅιδου ("meadows of Hades") is a
  name applied to a region of **air**, and it is introduced with **καλοῦσι**
  ("they call") — reported nomenclature, not Plutarch's own predication.

Then 943D, the arrival at the moon:

> πολλὰς γὰρ **ἐξωθεῖ καὶ ἀποκυματίζει γλιχομένας ἤδη τῆς σελήνης**, ἐνίας δὲ
> καὶ τῶν ἐκεῖ περικάτω τρεπομένας οἷον εἰς βυθὸν αὖθις ὁρῶσι καταδυομένας.
> αἱ δ' **ἄνω γενόμεναι καὶ βεβαίως ἱδρυθεῖσαι** πρῶτον μέν, ὥσπερ οἱ
> νικηφόροι, περιίασιν ἀναδούμεναι στεφάνοις πτερῶν εὐσταθείας λεγομένοις…

Word by word on the key participles: **γλιχομένας … τῆς σελήνης** "clinging
to / grasping after the moon" (γλίχομαι + genitive — LSJ: "cling to, long
for"; a verb of **desire**, not of arrival) | **ἐξωθεῖ** "she thrusts out"
(ὠθέω) | **ἀποκυματίζει** "washes off like surf" (ἀποκυματίζω, a rare verb
from κῦμα, "wave") | αἱ δ' ἄνω γενόμεναι "those that have come to be above" |
βεβαίως ἱδρυθεῖσαι "firmly settled/seated" (ἱδρύω pass.).

- OBSERVATION: **γλίχομαι + genitive** is the verb of the soul's relation to
  the moon at the moment of arrival. It is a verb of **yearning**, and the
  genitive is the genitive of the thing desired, not an accusative of goal.
  Cherniss's "even as they are in the act of clinging to the moon" is a fair
  rendering, but the English "clinging" reads as physical contact where the
  Greek is primarily appetitive.
- OBSERVATION: `ἱδρυθεῖσαι` (Wyttenbach; **E and B read ἱδρύθησαν**, a finite
  verb) — the participial "firmly settled" construction is editorial.
  `περιίασιν` (Wyttenbach; E περιίστασιν, B περιιστᾶσιν) is likewise emended.
  This is one of the most heavily reconstructed sentences in the myth: Cherniss
  prints **eight** emendations in these four lines (Madvig ×2, Xylander,
  Wyttenbach ×4, Hutten, Sandbach).

### A6. 943E–944A — the moon's substance: a blend, and "they say"

> **Ἐφορῶσι** δὲ πρῶτον μὲν αὐτῆς σελήνης τὸ μέγεθος καὶ τὸ κάλλος καὶ τὴν
> φύσιν οὐχ ἁπλῆν οὐδ' ἄμικτον, ἀλλ' **οἷον ἄστρου σύγκραμα καὶ γῆς οὖσαν**.
> ὡς γὰρ ἡ γῆ πνεύματι μεμιγμένη καὶ ὑγρότητι μαλακὴ γέγονε … οὕτως τῷ αἰθέρι
> **λέγουσι** τὴν σελήνην ἀνακεκραμένην διὰ βάθους ἅμα μὲν ἔμψυχον εἶναι καὶ
> γόνιμον, ἅμα δ' ἰσόρροπον ἔχειν τὴν πρὸς τὸ βαρὺ συμμετρίαν τῆς κουφότητος.
> καὶ γὰρ αὐτὸν οὕτως τὸν κόσμον ἐκ τῶν ἄνω καὶ τῶν κάτω φύσει φερομένων
> συνηρμοσμένον **ἀπηλλάχθαι παντάπασι τῆς κατὰ τόπον κινήσεως**.

Word by word: **οἷον** "a sort of" — an explicit hedge | **σύγκραμα** "a
blend, a compound" (σύγκραμα < συγκεράννυμι, "mix together") | ἄστρου καὶ γῆς
"of star and earth" ‖ **λέγουσι** "**they say**" — the whole physical account
of the moon's constitution is put in reported speech ‖ ἀπηλλάχθαι παντάπασι
τῆς κατὰ τόπον κινήσεως "to be **entirely released from local motion**".

- OBSERVATION: the moon here is not "an earth" simpliciter but **οἷον
  σύγκραμα ἄστρου καὶ γῆς** — hedged, and a *mixture*, not a member of the
  class γῆ. This qualifies A1.
- OBSERVATION: the last clause is a striking anti-spatial statement inside the
  same paragraph — the κόσμος as a whole is *freed from* κατὰ τόπον κίνησις,
  "motion with respect to place". Plutarch is deliberately holding the physical
  and the non-spatial in the same frame here.

### A7. 944B–C — Hecate's Recess, the Gates, the Elysian plain

This is the single most textually damaged sentence bearing on the question, and
the passage the book leans on hardest.

**944B, the simile:**

> ἔστι δ' οὐ τοιοῦτον, ἀλλ' **ὥσπερ ἡ παρ' ἡμῖν ἔχει γῆ κόλπους βαθεῖς καὶ
> μεγάλους**, ἕνα μὲν ἐνταῦθα διὰ στηλῶν Ἡρακλείων ἀναχεόμενον εἴσω πρὸς
> ἡμᾶς, ἔξω δὲ τὸν Κάσπιον καὶ τοὺς περὶ τὴν Ἐρυθρὰν θάλατταν, **οὕτως βάθη
> ταῦτα τῆς σελήνης ἐστὶ καὶ κοιλώματα**. **καλοῦσι** δ' αὐτῶν τὸ μὲν μέγιστον
> **Ἑκάτης μυχόν**, ὅπου καὶ δίκας διδόασιν αἱ ψυχαὶ καὶ λαμβάνουσιν ὧν ἂν ἤδη
> γεγενημέναι δαίμονες ἢ πάθωσιν ἢ δράσωσι…

- OBSERVATION: **ὥσπερ … οὕτως** — a formal simile. The predication "βάθη
  ταῦτα τῆς σελήνης ἐστὶ καὶ κοιλώματα" ("these are depths and hollows of the
  moon") *is* an assertion, but its content is that the **dark markings** are
  depths, which is the treatise's standing astronomical thesis (cf. 935C–D),
  arrived at by analogy with terrestrial gulfs.
- **μυχός**: LSJ μυχός, "innermost part, recess, nook; the inmost corner of a
  house; a bay or creek". Cherniss's "Recess" is exact. Cherniss's own note
  observes that "Plutarch himself associates μυχός with the 'punishments in
  Hades' (*De Superstitione* 167A)" — i.e. the word is at home in cult and
  underworld language as much as in topography.
- **καλοῦσι** again: the name is reported, not asserted.

**944C, the crux. Cherniss's printed text:**

> τὰς δὲ δύο μακρὰ ⟨τὰς Πύλας⟩. περαιοῦνται γὰρ αἱ ψυχαὶ δι' αὐτῶν, νῦν μὲν
> **εἰς τὰ πρὸς οὐρανὸν τῆς σελήνης**, νῦν δὲ πάλιν **εἰς τὰ πρὸς γῆν**.
> **ὀνομάζεται** δὲ τὰ μὲν πρὸς οὐρανὸν τῆς σελήνης **Ἠλύσιον πεδίον**, τὰ δ'
> ἐνταῦθα **Φερσεφόνης οἶκος ἀντίχθονος**.

**Cherniss's apparatus for this one sentence** (notes 1–5 ad loc.):

| Printed | Manuscript / source |
| --- | --- |
| `δύο μακρά` | **Leonicus**; E and B read τὰς δὲ δύο μακράς |
| `⟨τὰς Πύλας⟩` "the Gates" | **Cherniss's own supplement**; "**no lacuna indicated in E or B**" |
| `ὀνομάζεται` (indicative) | **Cherniss (H.C.)**; **E and B read ὀνομάζεσθαι** (infinitive) |
| `πεδίον` | **B**; **E reads παιδίον** ("little child") |
| `οἶκος` | **Cherniss, after von Arnim (who read οἶκον, keeping the infinitive); E and B read οὐκ** |

**What the manuscripts actually give for the naming clause:**

> ὀνομάζεσθαι δὲ τὰ μὲν πρὸς οὐρανὸν τῆς σελήνης Ἠλύσιον πεδίον, τὰ δ' ἐνταῦθα
> Φερσεφόνης **οὐκ** ἀντίχθονος.

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #2 — the largest one in the corpus.** Cherniss's
> "The side of the moon towards heaven is named 'Elysian plain,' the hither
> side 'House of counter-terrestrial Phersephoné'" — quoted verbatim in the
> book at *passages.md* line 43 and generalised in its note at line 55 —
> contains **three things that are not in any manuscript**:
>
> 1. **"House of"** (οἶκος / οἶκον) is a **conjecture** replacing the
>    transmitted **οὐκ**. Both readings are single short words and the
>    palaeographic step is easy, but the transmitted text is a negation, not a
>    building.
> 2. **"The Gates"** (τὰς Πύλας), which the book does not quote but which
>    supplies the whole "souls pass through gates" image, is **Cherniss's own
>    invention filling a lacuna that neither manuscript marks**. His apparatus
>    says so in as many words: "no lacuna indicated in E or B."
> 3. **"is named"** as an independent assertion: the manuscripts have the
>    **infinitive ὀνομάζεσθαι**, which continues the chain of indirect
>    discourse reporting what the stranger's informants said. Cherniss's
>    indicative converts a reported name into an authorial statement. Von Arnim
>    saw this and kept the infinitive; Cherniss changed it to justify the
>    nominative οἶκος.
>
> On the manuscripts as transmitted, the clause can be construed
> "…and [they say] that the parts here are Persephone's, **not** the
> counter-earth's" — i.e. as an explicit **denial** of the Pythagorean
> identification. That reading is awkward, and I do not assert it; but it is
> what E and B contain, and it points the opposite way from the printed text.
> **UNKNOWN, and it should be recorded as unknown.**

**"Side" and "hemisphere" are not in the Greek at all.**

- The Greek is **τὰ πρὸς οὐρανόν** and **τὰ πρὸς γῆν / τὰ ἐνταῦθα** — a bare
  substantivised neuter plural of the article + prepositional phrase: "the
  [things/parts] facing heaven", "the [things/parts] facing earth", "the
  [things] here". Greek has ordinary words for both concepts and uses neither:
  **πλευρά** ("side") occurs once in the whole treatise and not here;
  **ἡμισφαίριον** ("hemisphere") occurs **once in the entire treatise**, at
  931, of the **earth's shadow**, and never of the moon.
- Cherniss's "side" is a reasonable idiomatic English for τὰ πρός + acc. The
  book's note at *passages.md* line 55 escalates it to "**Hemisphere** toward
  heaven = Elysium; **hemisphere** toward earth = Persephone's house … Luna is
  explicitly two-faced cosmologically."

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #3.** "Hemisphere" is the book's own word, not
> Cherniss's and not Plutarch's. Nothing in the Greek asserts that the two
> regions are halves, that they are of equal extent, or that they are
> geometrically opposed. τὰ πρὸς οὐρανόν / τὰ πρὸς γῆν is orientational, not
> partitive. The book's further gloss "the ancient root of later traditions
> about the moon's 'dark side'" therefore rests on a word supplied in English.

**ἀντίχθων: LSJ and attested range.**

- LSJ **ἀντίχθων, -ονος, ἡ**: the "counter-earth" of the Pythagoreans — a body
  postulated to move parallel to the earth on the far side of the central fire,
  bringing the count of moving bodies to ten. Loci classici: Aristotle,
  *De Caelo* 293a24 and 293b20; *Metaphysics* 986a12; Simplicius, *In De Caelo*
  511–512. Secondary sense in the plural, **ἀντίχθονες** = "dwellers on the
  opposite side of the earth, antipodeans".
- Cherniss's own note ad loc. records that Plutarch uses ἀντίχθων **in the
  standard Pythagorean sense** elsewhere (*De An. Proc. in Timaeo* 1028B; cf.
  *Placita* 891F, 895C, 895F), and that the identification of the moon with
  the counter-earth is ascribed to "certain Pythagoreans" by Simplicius,
  *In De Caelo* 512.17–20 (with Asclepius, *In Metaph.* p. 35.24–27).
- **Grammar at 944C.** ἀντίχθονος is **genitive singular**, agreeing with
  **Φερσεφόνης**. It is therefore an **epithet of the goddess**, not a name of
  a place — "Persephone the counter-earthly" / "counter-earth Persephone".
  Cherniss's "counter-terrestrial Phersephoné" is exact.
- The book's note at line 60 — "Plutarch's phrase *Phersephone antichthonos* —
  Persephone who is *counter-earth*" — is **grammatically correct**, and better
  than most popular treatments, which turn ἀντίχθων into the moon's name. Its
  further gloss "The moon is another earth, reversed" is a defensible
  hypothesis (Simplicius attests the identification) but is not stated in this
  sentence, whose subject is the goddess.

**The verb of crossing.**

> **περαιοῦνται** γὰρ αἱ ψυχαὶ δι' αὐτῶν, νῦν μὲν **εἰς τὰ πρὸς οὐρανόν** …
> νῦν δὲ πάλιν **εἰς τὰ πρὸς γῆν**

- **περαιόω**: LSJ — active "carry to the opposite side, ferry across";
  mid./pass. "cross over, pass over", regularly of crossing water, of armies
  crossing straits, of ferrying. Its home register is unambiguously **bodily,
  naval transit**. It is used with **δι' αὐτῶν** ("through them") and with
  **εἰς + accusative of goal**, twice.
- **OBSERVATION, and this is the strongest point on the physical side of the
  ledger.** Here the Greek does use an ordinary verb of bodily travel with a
  genuine spatial accusative. This is not a metaphor-marked construction.
- **OBSERVATION qualifying it.** The subject is **αἱ ψυχαί**. And Plutarch uses
  περαιόω of soul-transit twice in this myth on either side of the human/divine
  divide: at 941B the human envoys to Ogygia are said to be
  **ὑπὸ πνευμάτων ἤδη περαιοῦσθαι** ("to be ferried by the winds") — that one
  *is* bodily, but it is a sea voyage on the earth, not a departure from it.

### A8. 944D — the daemons descend. Still no body

> Οὐκ ἀεὶ δὲ **διατρίβουσιν ἐπ' αὐτῆς** οἱ δαίμονες, ἀλλὰ χρηστηρίων δεῦρο
> **κατίασιν** ἐπιμελησόμενοι … **ὠθοῦνται** γὰρ αὖθις **ἐπὶ γῆν
> συνειργνύμενοι σώμασιν ἀνθρωπίνοις**.

Word by word: διατρίβουσιν "spend time / linger" | **ἐπ' αὐτῆς** "upon her" |
δεῦρο "hither" | **κατίασιν** "they go down" (κάτειμι) | ὠθοῦνται "they are
thrust" (ὠθέω, **passive**) | ἐπὶ γῆν "onto earth" (acc. of goal) |
**συνειργνύμενοι σώμασιν ἀνθρωπίνοις** "being shut up **together with** human
bodies" (συνείργνυμι + dative of accompaniment).

Apparatus: `ἐπ' αὐτῆς` — **Bernardakis**; **E and B read αὐτήν** (accusative);
**Wyttenbach** read αὐτῇ (dative). The Wikisource/Bernardakis-family text prints
`ἐπ' αὐτὴν`. So even the preposition governing "upon the moon" is contested
across all three cases.

- OBSERVATION: κάτειμι and ὠθέω with ἐπὶ γῆν are ordinary physical motion verbs
  with accusatives of goal. Daemons **do** move between moon and earth.
- OBSERVATION: **συνειργνύμενοι σώμασιν** settles what a daemon is. It is
  **shut up *with* a body**, in the dative of accompaniment — therefore it is
  **not itself a body**. The bodies are supplied at the terrestrial end.
- The book's rendering (*passages.md* line 48) is faithful. Its note (line 58),
  "Luna *circulates*: she sends her population down and receives them back",
  is well supported by the Greek — with the caveat that the population is
  δαίμονες, i.e. νοῦς + ψυχή.

### A9. 945B–C — the decisive passage: the moon is the *element* of soul

This passage is **not in the book's extract file**, and it is the one that most
directly answers the question put.

> **Τούτων δ' ἡ σελήνη, καθάπερ εἴρηται, στοιχεῖόν ἐστιν. ἀναλύονται γὰρ εἰς
> ταύτην, ὥσπερ εἰς τὴν γῆν τὰ σώματα τῶν νεκρῶν**, ταχὺ μὲν αἱ σώφρονες …

*toutōn d' hē selēnē, kathaper eirētai, stoicheion estin. analyontai gar eis
tautēn, hōsper eis tēn gēn ta sōmata tōn nekrōn…*

Word by word: τούτων "of these [souls]" | ἡ σελήνη "the moon" | καθάπερ εἴρηται
"as has been said" | **στοιχεῖόν ἐστιν** "**is the element**" |
**ἀναλύονται** "they are **resolved / dissolved**" (ἀναλύω pass.) |
**εἰς ταύτην** "into her" | **ὥσπερ εἰς τὴν γῆν τὰ σώματα τῶν νεκρῶν** "just as
the bodies of the dead [are dissolved] into the earth".

And at 945C:

> Χρόνῳ δὲ κἀκείνας **κατεδέξατο εἰς αὑτὴν** ἡ σελήνη καὶ κατεκόσμησεν, εἶτα
> τὸν νοῦν αὖθις ἐπισπείραντος τοῦ ἡλίου τῷ ζωτικῷ δεχομένη νέας ποιεῖ ψυχάς,
> **ἡ δὲ γῆ τρίτον σῶμα παρέσχεν**.

**κατεδέξατο εἰς αὑτήν** = "received down **into herself**".

- **OBSERVATION.** στοιχεῖον is the technical term for a **material element or
  constituent** (LSJ στοιχεῖον II: "element" in the physical sense, of the four
  elements; Plato *Tht.* 201e, Arist. *Metaph.* 1014a26). To say the moon is
  the στοιχεῖον **of souls** is to say souls are *made of moon-stuff*.
- **OBSERVATION.** ἀναλύονται εἰς ταύτην is the vocabulary of **chemical or
  material resolution**, and the explicit comparison is to **corpses rotting
  into the earth**. εἰς here is not the εἰς of arrival at a country; it is the
  εἰς of dissolution into a substance — the same εἰς that governs
  εἰς τὴν γῆν τὰ σώματα τῶν νεκρῶν.
- **HYPOTHESIS.** This is why the treatise can hold together the astronomical
  moon (a body with hollows) and the eschatological moon (where souls go)
  without contradiction: the soul's relation to the moon is **not primarily
  residence but constitution**. Souls "go to" the moon in the sense in which a
  body "goes to" the earth when it decays. The spatial language at 944C is
  therefore embedded in a frame that is explicitly material-elemental, not
  geographical.
- If the book wants one passage to anchor "Luna is the middle substance"
  (*passages.md* line 53), **this is it** — Plutarch says it in one word,
  στοιχεῖον, and 945D closes the ring:
  ἡ ψυχὴ … **τοῦτον ἄρα πρὸς ἥλιον ἔχουσα τὸν λόγον ὃν ἔχει γῆ πρὸς σελήνην**
  ("the soul stands to the sun in the ratio that earth stands to the moon") —
  a **proportion**, λόγος, not a map.

### A10. 944E–F — where the νοῦς goes: nowhere, by any verb of motion

> Τυγχάνουσι δ' οἱ μὲν πρότερον οἱ δ' ὕστερον, ὅταν **ὁ νοῦς ἀποκριθῇ τῆς
> ψυχῆς**. **ἀποκρίνεται δ' ἔρωτι τῆς περὶ τὸν ἥλιον εἰκόνος** … **λείπεται**
> δ' ἡ τῆς ψυχῆς φύσις **ἐπὶ τῆς σελήνης** οἷον ἴχνη τινὰ βίου καὶ ὀνείρατα
> διαφυλάττουσα.

Word by word: ἀποκριθῇ / ἀποκρίνεται "is separated off" (ἀποκρίνω pass. —
LSJ: "separate, set apart", used of the separating-out of elements in
Presocratic cosmogony) | **ἔρωτι** "**by love**" — dative of cause | τῆς περὶ
τὸν ἥλιον εἰκόνος "of the image about the sun" | λείπεται "is left behind".

- **OBSERVATION.** The νοῦς is never said to **travel** to the sun. It is
  **separated off** (ἀποκρίνεται) — a chemical/cosmogonic verb — and the
  agency is **ἔρως**, love, in a dative of cause. There is no verb of motion
  and no accusative of goal anywhere in the second-death sentence.
- Apparatus: `ἐπὶ τῆς σελήνης` (genitive, "upon the moon") is **Cherniss's own
  conjecture (H.C.)**; **E and B read ἐπὶ τὴν σελήνην** (accusative);
  **Wyttenbach** read ἐπὶ τῇ σελήνῃ (dative). The Wikisource text prints the
  manuscript accusative.

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #4, in the opposite direction.** Cherniss's "The
> substance of the soul is **left upon** the moon" removes a **spatial
> accusative** that the manuscripts have. On the transmitted text the phrase is
> ἐπὶ τὴν σελήνην — directional. Cherniss's genitive is a defensible repair
> (λείπεται naturally takes a locative), but it means that the one place in the
> myth where an editor has *reduced* the physicality is invisible in the
> English. Recorded here for symmetry: the emendations do not all run one way.

### A11. Term file — ψυχή / νοῦς / σῶμα, and which goes to the moon

Plutarch is unusually explicit, and consistent, across the myth:

| Constituent | Source (943A) | First death (943B) | Second death (943B, 944E–F) | Final state (945B–C) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **σῶμα** | ἡ γῆ παρέσχεν | loosed off, **stays in the earth** | — | ἀναλύεται εἰς τὴν γῆν (945B, explicit) |
| **ψυχή** | ἡ σελήνη παρέσχεν | wanders in the air (943C) | loosed from νοῦς **ἐν τῇ σελήνῃ** | ἀναλύεται **εἰς τὴν σελήνην**; the moon is its στοιχεῖον |
| **νοῦς** | ὁ ἥλιος παρέσχεν | carried with the soul | **ἀποκρίνεται ἔρωτι** — separated by love | rejoins the sun's sphere; no motion verb used |

- **OBSERVATION.** **σῶμα never leaves the earth in this text.** At 943B it is
  what the soul is loosed *from*; at 945B it is what dissolves into the earth;
  at 945C the earth "furnishes a third body" for the next incarnation
  (ἡ δὲ γῆ τρίτον σῶμα παρέσχεν). There is no sentence in *De facie* in which a
  σῶμα goes to or is on the moon.
- The only things predicated of the moon's own surface are **βάθη and
  κοιλώματα** — depths and hollows, i.e. the visible markings.

### A12. Textual health of the myth as a whole — UNKNOWNS

This must be stated plainly, because it changes how much weight the eschatology
can carry.

- The entire direct tradition of *De facie* is **two Paris manuscripts, E and
  B**, and **B is generally held to derive from E**. In practical terms there
  is **one witness**.
- E is badly damaged in exactly the myth section. Cherniss's apparatus marks
  lacunae of **7, 9, 11, 13, 20 and 26 letters** in the passages quoted above
  alone, plus one lacuna he posits where the manuscripts mark none (944C).
- Consequently: the tripartition sentence (943A), the two-deaths sentence
  (943B), the Demeter etymology (943B), the "House of counter-terrestrial
  Persephone" (944C), and "the Gates" (944C) all contain **editorially supplied
  Greek**. Every one of these is in the book's extract file.
- **UNKNOWN.** Whether Plutarch's myth is internally consistent about where
  Elysium is. 942F locates it at the terminus of the earth's shadow; 944C
  locates it on the moon's heaven-facing parts. Cherniss himself raises this
  ("though this does not explicitly contradict the present passage, it might
  still seem to suggest…") and does not resolve it. It is not resolvable from
  the Greek.
- **UNKNOWN.** Whether the myth is Plutarch's doctrine at all. It is narrated
  by **Sulla**, reporting a **stranger**, reporting **the attendants of
  Cronos** — three removes of indirect discourse, most of it in accusative +
  infinitive — and it closes (945D) with Sulla saying

### B1. The solar arc rests on a line that is not in the manuscripts

**Evelyn-White's own Greek page** (Loeb, p. 457) prints, between 15 and 16:

```
15  ἔνθ' ἄρ' ὅ γε στήσας χρυσόζυγον ἅρμα καὶ ἵππους,
15a [αὐτοῦ παύεται ἄκρον ἐπ' οὐρανοῦ, εἰσόκεν αὖτις]
16  θεσπέσιος πέμπῃσι δι' οὐρανοῦ Ὠκεανόνδε.
```

The **square brackets** are Evelyn-White's own, and mark a supplied line.
Allen's Oxford text (followed by Perseus) prints no such line: the transmitted
hymn runs straight from 15 to 16.

Literal gloss of the transmitted couplet:

| Greek | Gloss |
| --- | --- |
| ἔνθ' ἄρ' ὅ γε | "there, then, he" |
| στήσας | "having halted / having stationed" (aor. ptcp. of ἵστημι, transitive) |
| χρυσόζυγον ἅρμα καὶ ἵππους | "the golden-yoked chariot and horses" (acc.) |
| θεσπέσιος | "divine, wondrous" (nom. masc. adj., agreeing with the subject) |
| **πέμπῃσι** | "**he sends**" — 3 sg. **present subjunctive** of πέμπω |
| δι' οὐρανοῦ | "**through** heaven" (gen.) |
| **Ὠκεανόνδε** | "**Ocean-ward**" — the allative enclitic **-δε** |

Evelyn-White renders 15–16: "Then, when he has stayed his golden-yoked chariot
and horses, **he rests there upon the highest point of heaven, until** he
marvellously **drives them down** again through heaven to Ocean."

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #5.** Everything from "he rests there upon the
> highest point of heaven, until" is the **supplied line 15a**, and
> Evelyn-White's *English* carries **no bracket** to mark it — the reader of
> the facing page sees brackets in the Greek and none in the English. So the
> whole image of *a solar culmination at the zenith followed by a descent* —
> which the book's note calls "the daily solar arc" and "the solar return"
> (*hymn-31-to-helios.md* line 18) — is a modern conjecture.
>
> Additionally, in the transmitted line 16 there is **no "down"** and **no
> "again"**: δι' οὐρανοῦ Ὠκεανόνδε is "through heaven, Ocean-ward", flat. And
> **πέμπω is "send", not "drive"**.

**Why the conjecture exists — UNKNOWN, honestly stated.** The transmitted text
is syntactically broken: πέμπῃσι is a **subjunctive with no subordinating
conjunction and no ἄν**. Something has dropped out. Editors supply a
εἰσόκεν-clause because that is the cheapest repair. But *what* dropped out is
unrecoverable, and one cannot cite the repaired text as evidence for archaic
solar cosmography. **The Greek is undetermined here and should be left so.**

### C1. The "Selene embraces, Helios pierces" contrast is an artefact of the English

This is the book's central lunar claim from the Hymns
(*hymn-32-to-selene.md* line 19): "Where Helios (Hymn 31) 'pierces,' 'beams,'
'dazzles,' Selene's light 'embraces earth' … The verbs are receptive and
enveloping: diffusion around rather than emission outward."

**The Greek uses the same emission vocabulary for both.**

| Feature | Hymn 31, Helios | Hymn 32, Selene |
| --- | --- | --- |
| rays | **ἀκτῖνες** (10) | **ἀκτῖνες** (6) |
| "gleam" | **στίλβουσι** (11) | **στίλβει** (5) |
| "bright" | **λαμπραί** (10, 12) | **λαμπρόταται** αὐγαί (12) |
| "far-shining" | πρόσωπον **τηλαυγές** (13) | εἵματα **τηλαυγέα** (8) |
| radiance | αἰγλῆεν (11) | **αἴγλη** (3, 5), αἰγλήεντας (9) |

Both are ἀκτῖνες-emitters, both στίλβει, both τηλαυγής. The lexical register is
identical. The book's contrast has no basis in the Greek and comes entirely
from Evelyn-White's word choices ("piercingly he gazes" for σμερδνὸν δέρκεται
vs. "embraces earth" for γαῖαν ἑλίσσεται).

### C2. Line 3, the load-bearing clause

> ἧς ἄπο αἴγλη **γαῖαν ἑλίσσεται** οὐρανόδεικτος / κρατὸς ἀπ' ἀθανάτοιο

| Greek | Gloss |
| --- | --- |
| ἧς ἄπο … κρατὸς ἀπ' ἀθανάτοιο | "from whose immortal head" |
| αἴγλη | "radiance, gleam" (nom.) |
| **γαῖαν** | "the earth" — **accusative** |
| **ἑλίσσεται** | "**winds/coils/revolves** [round]" — mid. of ἑλίσσω |
| οὐρανόδεικτος | "heaven-shown" (rare compound; LSJ: "shown from heaven") |

**ἑλίσσω**, LSJ: "turn round or about, roll, wheel, whirl"; mid./pass. "move to
and fro, circle round, revolve". The accusative γαῖαν is the accusative of the
space traversed or circled.

Literal: "from whose immortal head a heaven-shown radiance **wheels round the
earth**."

Evelyn-White: "From her immortal head a radiance is shown from heaven and
**embraces** earth".

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #6, of a different kind — a softening.**
> ἑλίσσεται is a **rotational** verb; "embraces" makes it affective and
> receptive. This one word is the whole textual basis for the book's "she
> embraces rather than pierces" doctrine. The Greek says her light **circles**
> the earth — which is a statement about the moon's *motion*, not her
> temperament. Note also that Evelyn-White splits οὐρανόδεικτος off into its
> own clause ("is shown from heaven"), which reads as a claim about the source
> of her light; the Greek compound is a single attributive adjective of αἴγλη.

### C3. Line 11, ὄγμος — "orbit" is a modernisation

> ἑσπερίη, διχόμηνος· ὃ δὲ πλήθει **μέγας ὄγμος**

**ὄγμος**, LSJ: "the furrow or swath made by reapers or mowers as they cross a
field"; hence "a line, a track". Homeric usage is agricultural (*Il.* 11.68,
18.546). Applied to a heavenly body it means the **swath/track** it cuts.

Evelyn-White: "then her great **orbit** is full."

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #7.** "Orbit" imports a geometric-astronomical
> concept (a closed path, a defined curve) into a word whose sense is a
> **reaper's swath**. πλήθει is "is full/is at the full" — of the moon's disc,
> or of the swath's width. The image is a mown strip broadening, not a
> calculated ellipse. The book quotes "her great orbit is full"
> (*hymn-32-to-selene.md* line 8) and glosses it as "Full orbit at mid-month".
> Nothing physical hangs on this for the present question, but it is a clear
> case of the English supplying astronomical concreteness the Greek lacks.

### C4. Line 7, the bath in Ocean

> εὖτ' ἂν **ἀπ' Ὠκεανοῖο λοεσσαμένη χρόα καλόν**

| Greek | Gloss |
| --- | --- |
| εὖτ' ἄν | "whenever" |
| **ἀπ' Ὠκεανοῖο** | "**from** Ocean" (ἀπό + gen. — source or separation) |
| λοεσσαμένη | "having washed [herself]" (aor. mid. ptcp. of λούω) |
| χρόα καλόν | "her fair flesh/skin" — accusative of respect |

Evelyn-White: "having bathed her lovely body **in the waters of** Ocean".

> **TRANSLATION HARDENING #8, minor.** "in the waters of" renders ἀπό, which is
> "from". The construction is compatible with immersion (Homeric λούομαι
> ποταμοῖο) but the preposition chosen is **ἀπό**, marking Ocean as the
> **source of the water** or as the place she comes **up from**. Whether Selene
> immerses her body or draws water from Ocean is **UNDETERMINED in the Greek**.
> The book's note (*hymn-32-to-selene.md* line 20) — "Her brightness is
> preceded by **immersion** in the primordial water… The moon's relation to
> water is not metaphor; it is ritual sequence" — chooses immersion and then
> makes it a doctrine. The εὖτ' ἄν … ἐλάσῃ construction is a **generalising
> temporal clause** ("whenever she has bathed … and yoked … and driven"), which
> does support the "sequence" reading; but "immersion" specifically is not
> secured.

Also note **χρόα** — Selene has a *body* in this hymn. But she is a **goddess**,
and her body is not a human σῶμα, and it does not depart from the earth: it
comes up from Ocean, which is where gods and stars come up from in archaic
cosmography.

### C5. Two claims in the book's notes with no textual basis

- *hymn-32-to-selene.md* line 23: "**Crown of gold, silver limbs**. 'White-armed
  goddess' + 'golden crown' — the moon is described with **silver** for her body
  and gold for her light-ornament." — **There is no silver in Hymn 32.**
  λευκώλενος (17) is the standard formulaic epithet "white-armed", used of Hera
  and of mortal women throughout Homer; it denotes skin colour, not metal.
- Same file, line 24: the note on τανυσίπτερος correctly quotes Evelyn-White's
  own footnote. Fine. **τανυσίπτερος**, LSJ: "with outspread/long wings", of
  birds (*Od.* 5.65, 22.468; Hes. *WD* 212). Applied to the moon it is
  **unparalleled**, which is exactly why Evelyn-White hedged. Genuinely
  **UNKNOWN**.
- Line 21: "*τέκμωρ δὲ βροτοῖς καὶ σῆμα*" is quoted accurately (line 13).
  τέκμωρ, LSJ: "fixed mark, boundary, end; sign, token". σῆμα: "sign, mark,
  token, portent". The verb is **τέτυκται**, perf. pass. of τεύχω, "has been
  wrought/made". The book's "the moon *is* the measure" is a fair paraphrase.

Literal, 371–374: "And **Theia** bore great **Helios** and bright **Selene**
and **Eos**, who shines for all the earth-dwellers and for the immortal gods
who hold wide heaven — bore [them] subdued in love of **Hyperion**."

- γείναθ' = γείνατο, aor. mid. of γείνομαι, "bore".
- ὑποδμηθεῖσ' = ὑποδμηθεῖσα, aor. pass. ptcp. of ὑποδάμνημι, "subdued beneath".

**The passage is pure genealogy.** There is no place, no motion verb, no body,
no travel. It is irrelevant to the physicality question except negatively: the
oldest Greek text on Selene says nothing whatever about her being a place.

### D1. λαμπρά — the book's note overreaches on one historical point

The book's note (*theogony-sun-moon-dawn.md* line 15): "Luna in Hesiod is not
dim or reflected — she is luminous in her own description… The archaic Greek
imagination did not yet contain the later (**post-Aristarchus**) physics of a
non-luminous reflecting moon; Selene simply *shines*."

- **The epithet claim is right.** λαμπρός, LSJ I: "bright, brilliant, radiant",
  of the sun, of stars, of fire. Applied to Selene at 371 it is straightforwardly
  "radiant". No implication of borrowed light.
- **The history is wrong, and Plutarch himself is the witness against it.** The
  doctrine that the moon shines by borrowed light is **Presocratic**, not
  post-Aristarchan. Plutarch quotes **Parmenides** on exactly this in the same
  treatise the book is using:

  > εἴ γε τῶν ἐν οὐρανῷ τοσούτων τὸ πλῆθος ὄντων **μόνη φωτὸς ἀλλοτρίου
  > δεομένη** περίεισι, κατὰ Παρμενίδην «**ἀεὶ παπταίνουσα πρὸς αὐγὰς
  > ἠελίοιο**»
  > — *De facie* 929B

  "…if alone among all the many things in heaven **she goes about needing an
  alien light**, as Parmenides says, 'ever gazing toward the beams of the sun'."
  Plutarch goes on to name **Anaxagoras** in the next sentence. Aristarchus is
  third century BCE; Parmenides is early fifth. The book's parenthesis should be
  corrected or dropped.

### D2. The relative clause at 372–373 belongs to Eos alone

ἣ (fem. sg. nom.) follows Ἠῶ immediately and agrees with it. Evelyn-White's
"and Eos (Dawn) who shines upon all that are on earth, and upon the deathless
Gods" is correct. The book's note (line 17) says "Eos — **and by implication the
whole cluster of luminaries** — shines upon *both*". The implication is the
book's, and the book says so. Acceptable as flagged inference; it is not
Hesiod's grammar.

## PART E — Ὠκεανός: bounded or unbounded, and what governs the sun's return

### E1. In the two Hymns

- Hymn 31.16: **Ὠκεανόνδε** — accusative + the allative enclitic **-δε**
  ("to Ocean"). Ocean is a **destination**. Nothing is said about its shape.
- Hymn 32.7: **ἀπ' Ὠκεανοῖο** — Ocean is a **source**. Nothing about its shape.
- **Neither hymn calls Ocean encircling.** No περίρρυτος, no ἀμφιρύτη, no κύκλῳ.
- The verb governing the sun's return is **πέμπῃσι** (Hymn 31.16), present
  subjunctive of πέμπω, "send". As shown in B1, **the subjunctive is orphaned**
  in the transmitted text — there is no conjunction governing it — which is the
  whole reason line 15a is conjectured. **UNDETERMINED.**

### E2. In the *Theogony*

Every occurrence (20, 133, 215, 242, 265, 274, 282, 288, 292, 294, 337, 362,
364, 368, 383, 389, 507, 695, 776, 789, 816, 841, 908, 956, 959, 979):

- Ocean is a **river**, not a sea: **Ὠκεανοῖο τελήεντος ποταμοῖο** (242, 959),
  "of Ocean the perfect river"; his sons are the Ποταμοί (337, 368).
- Epithets: **βαθυδίνης** "deep-eddying" (133), **βαθυρρείτης** "deep-flowing"
  (265), **ἀψόρροος** "**back-flowing**" (776).
- **Ocean is bounded and crossable.** Three times something lies **πέρην κλυτοῦ
  Ὠκεανοῖο**, "**beyond** famous Ocean" — the Hesperides (215), the Gorgons
  (274), Geryon's steading (294). And at 292 Heracles is
  **διαβὰς πόρον Ὠκεανοῖο**, "having **crossed the ford/passage** of Ocean".
  A πόρος is a crossing-place.
- At 789 Ocean has a **κέρας**, a "horn" or branch, of which a tenth part is
  apportioned to Styx. At 816 the Titans' houses stand
  **ἐπ' Ὠκεανοῖο θεμέθλοις**, "at the **foundations** of Ocean".

> **OBSERVATION.** The *Theogony* does **not** describe Ocean as encircling the
> earth. The circularity that modern handbooks attribute to Hesiod's Ocean is
> read in from **ἀψόρροος** (776), which literally means "flowing back", and
> from the Homeric shield of Achilles (*Il.* 18.607–608), a different poem.
> On Hesiod's own words Ocean is a **great river with a far bank**, a ford, a
> horn and foundations — i.e. **bounded, with a beyond**. The book's note
> (*hymn-31-to-helios.md* line 18) — "the descent into the great **encircling**
> water" — imports the encircling from outside these texts.

### E3. Plutarch's own encircling language, for contrast

*De facie* 941A, of the inhabited world:

> ὡς καὶ **κύκλῳ περίρρυτον** οὖσαν ὑπὸ τῆς θαλάσσης

"as being **flowed round in a circle** by the sea." Plutarch has the vocabulary
and uses it — of the **earth**, in the Ogygia digression, not of the moon.

### G3. The one substantive cross-file inconsistency

The book asserts a **Sol-emits / Luna-receives** polarity as "the archaic Greek
signature" (hymn-31 note line 17; hymn-32 note line 19). The Greek of the two
hymns does not support it — they share a vocabulary of emission. If the book
wants a Greek warrant for Luna-as-receptive, the warrant it actually has is
**Plutarch, not the Hymns**: *De facie* 945C, σελήνη δὲ καὶ **λαμβάνει** καὶ
**δίδωσι** — "the moon both **takes** and **gives**", against ἥλιος δὲ
**λαμβάνει μὲν οὐδέν** — "the sun takes nothing". That is an explicit
receive/emit contrast in the Greek, and it is in a passage the book has not
extracted.

## PART H — Direct answer, passage by passage

**Does the Greek assert a physical bodily destination, a soul passage, or is it
undetermined?**

1. ***De facie* 933D, 935C** — Neither. These are claims about the moon's
   **ontological category** (γῆ rather than πῦρ), advanced with **θέμενοι**,
   "positing". The moon is a physical body; the treatise's whole first half
   argues that on optical and astronomical grounds. But "physical body" is not
   "destination", and no soul is mentioned.

2. ***De facie* 935B–C (the terrain)** — **Undetermined, and conditional.**
   "It would not be incredible **if** she has beautiful regions and flaming
   mountains…" The English keeps the condition; the book's note drops it.

3. ***De facie* 935D** — **Cannot bear weight.** The word "earth" is a
   nineteenth-century conjecture. The manuscript antithesis is holy vs. turbid
   fire.

4. ***De facie* 942F** — **Soul passage, to an optically defined locus.** The
   destination is where the earth's shadow-cone terminates. Verbs: **ἄνεισιν**
   with εἰς + acc. (genuine goal), **κομισθέντες** (passive, "conveyed").
   Whether αὐτόθι means the moon or the shadow's apex is **undetermined**, and
   Cherniss says so.

5. ***De facie* 943A** — **Neither. No motion of any kind.** Earth, moon and
   sun **furnish** (παρέσχεν) body, soul and mind. εἰς τὴν γένεσιν is purposive.

6. ***De facie* 943B** — **Soul passage, stated locatively.** ἐν + dative
   twice; the verb is **λύει**, "loosens". **The body is explicitly what is
   left behind.**

7. ***De facie* 943C–D** — **Soul passage.** πλανηθῆναι in a locative dative
   region **of air**; the region's name (λειμῶνες Ἅιδου) is introduced with
   **καλοῦσι**. Arrival at the moon is expressed by **γλίχομαι + genitive**,
   a verb of **yearning**, not of arrival.

8. ***De facie* 944B** — **Simile.** ὥσπερ … οὕτως. The predication that
   follows ("these are depths and hollows of the moon") is a real astronomical
   claim about the lunar markings, and it is **secure in the manuscripts**.

9. ***De facie* 944C** — **Soul passage with a real spatial accusative** —
   περαιοῦνται … εἰς τὰ πρὸς οὐρανόν. This is the **strongest single point for
   the physical reading in the whole corpus**: περαιόω is the ordinary verb for
   ferrying bodies across water. **But** the subject is αἱ ψυχαί; the two
   regions are named with bare neuter plurals (**no** "side", "half" or
   "hemisphere"); and the "House of counter-terrestrial Persephone" and "the
   Gates" are **editorial**. The transmitted text has **οὐκ ἀντίχθονος**, which
   may be a **denial**. **Undetermined, and textually contested.**

10. ***De facie* 944D** — **Soul/daemon passage.** κατίασιν and ὠθοῦνται ἐπὶ
    γῆν are ordinary motion verbs with accusatives of goal, so the daemons
    genuinely move. But **συνειργνύμενοι σώμασιν** — shut up **with** bodies —
    proves the daemon is not one.

11. ***De facie* 944E–F** — **Neither.** The mind is **ἀποκρίνεται ἔρωτι**,
    "separated off by love". **No motion verb, no goal accusative anywhere.**

12. ***De facie* 945B–D** — **Decisively not a physical destination.** The moon
    is the **στοιχεῖον** of soul; souls **ἀναλύονται εἰς** her exactly as
    corpses dissolve **εἰς τὴν γῆν**; she **κατεδέξατο εἰς αὑτήν**, "received
    them down into herself". The relation is **material constitution**, and the
    closing sentence makes it a **λόγος**, a ratio: soul : sun :: earth : moon.

13. **Homeric Hymn 31** — **Neither.** A god drives horses through heaven
    Ocean-ward. The culmination-and-descent arc is a **supplied line**.

14. **Homeric Hymn 32** — **Neither.** A goddess washes, yokes and drives. Her
    light **ἑλίσσεται** — wheels round — the earth.

15. ***Theogony* 371–382** — **Neither.** Genealogy only.

### The short form

- **A physical bodily destination: nowhere.** Not once in these texts.
- **A soul passage: yes, throughout the *De facie* myth**, and expressed with
  ordinary spatial verbs and, at 944C and 942F, with real accusatives of goal.
  Plutarch is not being coy about location.
- **What is undetermined** is whether that located passage is a *journey to a
  country* or an *absorption into a substance*. Plutarch supplies both frames
  and never reconciles them — spatial at 944C, elemental at 945B. **The Greek
  does not settle it, and it should be left unsettled.**
- **What the English hardened** is a separate matter, and it is substantial:
  "holy **earth**", "**House** of", "the **Gates**", "**hemisphere**", the
  solar **zenith and descent**, the **encircling** Ocean, Selene's **embrace**,
  her **orbit** and her **silver**. Every one of these supplies concreteness
  the Greek leaves open, and four of them are supplied by **editors of the
  Greek text**, not by translators.

## Recommendations, if the book wants the Greek behind it

1. **Add *De facie* 945B–D to the extract file.** It is the strongest Greek
   warrant the book has for its own thesis ("Luna is the middle substance"),
   and it is currently missing. στοιχεῖον, ἀναλύονται εἰς ταύτην, λαμβάνει καὶ
   δίδωσι, and the closing λόγος-proportion are all there in one page.
2. **Mark 944C as textually contested** wherever it is used, as the file already
   does for 943C. One clause, three conjectures.
3. **Replace "hemisphere" with the Greek's own orientation language** — "the
   parts facing heaven" / "the parts facing earth".
4. **Reconcile the hymn-31 haplography note with the hymn-31 solar-arc note.**
   The file already knows line 15a is restored; the note two lines above uses
   the restoration as if secure.
5. **Drop or fix "post-Aristarchus"** in the Theogony note. Parmenides and
   Anaxagoras are quoted **by Plutarch in this very treatise** (929B) for the
   borrowed-light doctrine.
6. **Drop the "silver limbs" claim** on Hymn 32 and the emission/reception
   contrast between the two hymns; move the latter to *De facie* 945C, where
   the Greek actually says it.
7. **Note that νοτεροῦ ("moist") at 935B is the reading Cherniss printed but
   doubted**, proposing νοεροῦ ("intellectual"). The ghee/Soma bridge should
   acknowledge this.
